Australia is arguably the greatest beach country on the planet — tens of
thousands of kilometres of coast and a culture built around it. Narrowing it to
ten is unfair, but these are the ones that define it.

## 1. Whitehaven Beach, Whitsundays

Seven kilometres of silica sand so pure it squeaks, swirling into turquoise at
Hill Inlet — frequently called the best beach in the world.

## 2. Bondi Beach, Sydney

The most famous urban beach in Australia — surf, the Bondi-to-Coogee walk, and
beach culture distilled.

## 3. Cable Beach, Broome

22 km of red-rock-backed sand where camel trains cross at sunset over the
Indian Ocean.

## 4. Wineglass Bay, Tasmania

A perfect granite-framed crescent in Freycinet, earned by a short hike over a
saddle.

## 5. Lucky Bay, Esperance

Blinding white sand, kangaroos on the beach, and impossibly clear water in WA.

## 6. Turquoise Bay, Ningaloo

Snorkel straight off the sand onto a world-class fringing reef.

## 7. Burleigh Heads, Gold Coast

A legendary point break and a relaxed headland-backed beach.

## 8. Hyams Beach, Jervis Bay

Squeaky white sand and gin-clear shallows a few hours from Sydney.

## 9. Bells Beach, Victoria

The spiritual home of Australian surfing on the Great Ocean Road.

## 10. Four Mile Beach, Port Douglas

Palm-fringed tropical sand where the reef meets the rainforest.

## How Australian beaches work

Most popular beaches are patrolled by surf lifesavers in season — always swim
between the red-and-yellow flags. Australia's surf is powerful and rip currents
are the real danger, not sharks. Many remote WA and Tasmanian beaches are
unpatrolled; treat them with respect and never swim alone.

## When to go

It is a continent — seasons flip by region. The tropical north (Whitsundays,
Broome, Ningaloo) is best in the dry season (May–October), with marine stinger
risk in the wet. The southern beaches (Sydney, Victoria, Tasmania) peak in the
Australian summer, December–February.

## Stay safe and respectful

Swim between the flags, check for rips, slip-slop-slap against fierce UV
(shade is rare), heed marine stinger and crocodile warnings in the far north,
and take all rubbish with you. Many beaches sit on Aboriginal land — respect
signage and cultural sites.
