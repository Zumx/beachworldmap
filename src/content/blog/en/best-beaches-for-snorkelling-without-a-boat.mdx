The overwhelming majority of the world's best reef snorkelling requires a boat. Healthy coral systems tend to grow in deeper, cleaner water away from the sand disturbance and freshwater runoff of beaches, and the standard model for reef tourism — day boat from a resort pier to a mooring buoy above the reef — reflects this reality. But there is a smaller subset of beaches worldwide where the underwater topography and reef position allows a snorkeller to swim directly from the sand into exceptional marine life. These beaches are disproportionately valuable for independent travellers, families with children, and anyone who wants to snorkel on their own timetable rather than a tour boat's schedule.

## What Makes a Beach Directly Snorkellable

Several conditions need to align. The reef or rocky substrate needs to extend close enough to the beach that a moderate swimmer can reach it without exhaustion. The water over the sandy seafloor between the beach and the reef needs to be shallow enough to feel safe but deep enough that the swimmer's fins do not disturb the bottom. There should be no significant rip current pulling swimmers away from the reef or around headlands. And ideally, the snorkelling entry point should be sheltered enough from waves that surface conditions are calm.

Rocky coves, lava coastlines with fringing reef, and headlands that shelter a sandy beach on one side while fronting reef on the other are the most common configurations. The Caribbean islands with fringing reef right up to the shoreline — the leeward sides of Bonaire, St Croix, and parts of Barbados — produce the most consistent shore-entry snorkelling.

## Trunk Bay, St John, US Virgin Islands

Trunk Bay on the north coast of St John in the US Virgin Islands is administered by the National Park Service and has a marked 200-metre underwater snorkel trail that runs along a healthy fringing reef in 2 to 5 metres of water, accessible directly from the beach. The trail has underwater plaques identifying major coral species and the fish that use them. The beach itself is one of the most beautiful in the Caribbean — white sand, clear water, palm-backed — and the park service manages it carefully. Entry fees are charged.

## Anse Chastanet, St Lucia

Anse Chastanet on the southwest coast of St Lucia, below the Piton mountains, has a rocky section of beach where the reef extends to within metres of the shore and drops to 6 metres or more. The Anse Chastanet reef is considered one of the best shore-accessible reefs in the Eastern Caribbean, and the diversity of coral species (including considerable hard coral growth that survived the bleaching events of the late 1990s) and fish life — trumpetfish, French angelfish, spotted drums — is impressive. The beach requires a steep descent by road and is dominated by the luxury Anse Chastanet resort, but day visitors can access it.

## Hanauma Bay, Oahu, Hawaii

Hanauma Bay is the most visited snorkelling site in Hawaii and perhaps the most famous shore-entry snorkelling site in the Pacific. The bay is the collapsed crater of an ancient volcanic cone, and the reef inside the crater's horseshoe is close enough to the beach that visitors wade from the shore directly over the reef in water that ranges from knee-deep to 3 metres. The species density — green sea turtles, honu (Hawaiian green turtles) regularly resting on the reef, large schools of convict tang and Moorish idols — is extraordinary. Visitor numbers are now strictly capped and reservations are required to protect the reef.

## Amed, East Bali, Indonesia

The fishing village coast of Amed in east Bali has rocky beaches and immediate drop-offs into the Lombok Strait, one of the world's most oceanographically significant passages where the Pacific and Indian Ocean water masses meet. Shore-entry snorkelling here reaches large coral heads, some of the best-preserved in Bali, within a 5-minute swim. The Japanese Shipwreck — a supply vessel sunk in shallow water near Amed during World War II — lies in about 30 metres at its deepest but the upper deck is accessible to snorkellers at 3 metres. Lion fish, bumphead parrotfish, and giant trevally are all regular sightings.

## Gili Meno, Lombok, Indonesia

The smallest and quietest of the three Gili Islands, Gili Meno has a turtle sanctuary that contributes to one of the highest densities of green and hawksbill sea turtles per square kilometre of any accessible diving destination in the world. The fringing reef around the island starts immediately off the beach in less than a metre of water and the turtles are so habituated to snorkellers that they continue grazing on seagrass from a few metres away. The island has no motor vehicles and is reached by boat from Lombok or Bali.

## Eilat, Gulf of Aqaba, Israel

The northernmost coral reef in the world lies in the Gulf of Aqaba at Eilat, where the Red Sea system extends to the latitude of the Negev Desert. The Eilat Underwater Observatory Marine Park and the adjacent public beach have fringing reef accessible from shore in 1 to 4 metres of water. The species diversity of the Red Sea is extraordinary — the Gulf of Aqaba coral systems have been among the most resistant to bleaching in the world, surviving temperature anomalies that devastated reefs in the Indian Ocean, possibly due to the resilience traits of Gulf of Aqaba corals in an already thermally variable environment.

## Bonaire, Netherlands Antilles

Bonaire has built its entire tourism economy around shore-entry diving and snorkelling. The island's fringing reef extends along almost the entire western coastline and the reef edge is typically 10 to 50 metres from the shore. Yellow stones painted on the coastal road mark the established shore-entry points, each numbered and mapped. The island has a marine park that covers the entire coastal zone, with no take-anything policies enforced for decades, and the result is an abundance of large fish species including barracuda, Nassau grouper, and queen triggerfish that have been absent from most Caribbean reefs for years.

## Kealakekua Bay, Hawaii

Kealakekua Bay on the Kona coast of the Big Island is reachable by kayak (launched from the adjacent beach) or a long hiking trail, but the reef itself at the historic Captain Cook Monument is accessible from shore at the monument location. The bay has one of the densest populations of spinner dolphins in Hawaii, and the reef is exceptionally healthy. The isolation that keeps visitor numbers lower than Hanauma Bay has preserved a level of marine life abundance that more accessible sites cannot match.
