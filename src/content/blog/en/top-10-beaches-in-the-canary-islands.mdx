The Canary Islands sit in the Atlantic Ocean about 100 kilometres off the northwest African coast, and despite being a Spanish archipelago, their geography is as much shaped by the Sahara desert and volcanic activity as by any European landscape. The islands form two distinct groups: the eastern islands of Lanzarote and Fuerteventura, which are low-lying and arid, receiving the full force of the trade winds and Saharan sand; and the western islands of Gran Canaria, Tenerife, La Palma, La Gomera, and El Hierro, which are higher, greener, and more volcanic in character. Beach conditions vary accordingly: Fuerteventura has Europe's finest wind-sport beaches, Gran Canaria has its extraordinary dune system, and the western islands offer dramatic black-sand shores framed by sea cliffs.

## 1. Playa de las Canteras, Gran Canaria

Las Canteras in Las Palmas de Gran Canaria is arguably Spain's finest urban beach — a three-kilometre arc of golden sand right in the heart of the island's capital city. A natural submerged rock reef called La Barra runs parallel to the shore about 100 metres out, breaking the Atlantic swell and creating calm water suitable for swimming along the entire length of the beach. The surrounding neighbourhood has a genuine city feel: bookshops, restaurants, local fish markets, and an architecturally interesting old quarter within walking distance. Las Palmas is also a transit hub, and spending time at Las Canteras while waiting for a connecting flight or ferry is time genuinely well spent.

## 2. Playa del Inglés, Gran Canaria

Playa del Inglés sits at the southern tip of Gran Canaria and is the centre of mass tourist infrastructure on the island, backed by an extensive hotel and apartment complex that has been growing since the 1960s. The beach itself is wide and sandy, curving around the bay with consistent conditions for sunbathing and swimming. What sets it apart is its proximity to the Maspalomas Dune System, which begins at the western end of the beach and continues for about three kilometres. Walking from the hotel strip into the dunes as they grow higher and the crowds thin represents a surprisingly dramatic landscape transition for a beach that begins at a resort strip.

## 3. Maspalomas Dunes, Gran Canaria

The Maspalomas Dunes form a protected Natural Monument covering roughly 400 hectares at the southern tip of Gran Canaria. The dunes are formed from golden sand blown in from the Sahara and shaped by the persistent northeast trade winds into a landscape of crests and hollows that shifts slightly with each season. The beach at the base of the dunes — Playa de Maspalomas — is wide, flat, and generally calmer than Playa del Inglés to the northeast. A freshwater lagoon, the Charca de Maspalomas, sits at the western end and is an important migratory bird stopover point. Walking the full length of the dune system to the lighthouse at Maspalomas Point takes about 90 minutes.

## 4. Playa de Papagayo, Lanzarote

The Papagayo beaches are a group of small coves at the southern tip of Lanzarote within the Los Ajaches Nature Reserve. Each cove is different in character — some with fine sand, some with coarser gravel, all with clear water — and the surrounding landscape of volcanic black rock and sparse thorn scrub gives them a wild, almost lunar quality. Access requires paying a small conservation fee at the reserve gate. The most famous cove, Papagayo itself, is accessible by a dirt road, but the others require 15 to 20 minutes of walking across bare volcanic terrain. ## 5. Playa de Famara, Lanzarote

Famara is Lanzarote's long, windswept northwestern beach — approximately eight kilometres of grey-gold sand backed by the dramatic 600-metre Famara cliffs. The beach faces north-northwest and receives consistent Atlantic swell, making it the island's premier surf beach and one of the most reliable surf locations in the Canaries. The Famara surf school complex at the northern end is well-established. The wind is persistent and strong, which keeps the beach cool even in midsummer and makes swimming challenging for non-strong swimmers. The fishing and surf village of La Caleta de Famara sits at the northern end with a handful of seafood restaurants serving locally caught fish.

## 6. Playa de Cofete, Fuerteventura

Cofete occupies the entire western coast of the Jandía Peninsula at the southern tip of Fuerteventura — a 14-kilometre beach of wild Atlantic sand, accessible only via a rough, slow dirt road over the Jandía mountain spine. The beach is completely undeveloped, with no facilities beyond a small bar-restaurant near the hamlet of Cofete. The Atlantic swell here is strong and the undertow significant; swimming is for confident ocean swimmers only. The scale is extraordinary: the beach disappears into haze in both directions, backed by the barren Jandía mountains. A large villa rumoured to have been built for a senior Nazi official during the Franco era sits on the hillside above the hamlet.

## 7. Playa del Médano, Tenerife

El Médano at the southern end of Tenerife is the premier windsurfing and kitesurfing beach in the Canary Islands and one of the most consistently wind-powered beaches in Europe. The bay faces south toward Africa and the Montaña Roja volcanic cone creates a geographic funnel that accelerates the trade winds. The town has a small, unpretentious character compared to the larger resorts of Playa de las Américas nearby, with local-feeling restaurants and a working fishing port. The sand is natural volcanic brown rather than the imported Saharan sand used on some Canarian resort beaches.

## 8. Las Teresitas, Tenerife

Las Teresitas sits in a sheltered bay just north of Santa Cruz de Tenerife, the island's capital, and is the most accessible beach on the northern side of the island. The sand was imported from the Western Sahara in the 1970s and is the characteristic golden colour of Saharan dunes, in contrast to the dark volcanic sand found elsewhere on Tenerife's north coast. An artificial reef of concrete tetrapods protects the bay from Atlantic swell, keeping the water calm for swimming. Palm trees line the promenade behind the beach. The drive from Santa Cruz city centre takes about 15 minutes on a coastal road.

## 9. Playa de Benijo, Tenerife

Benijo sits in the far northeast corner of Tenerife, near the Anaga Rural Park, and is accessible via a winding road through the Anaga mountains — one of the most ancient volcanic formations in the archipelago. The beach is dark volcanic sand and pebble, backed by dramatic eroded cliff faces, with powerful Atlantic surf. Offshore sea stacks called the Roques de Anaga add to the dramatic visual character. There are no facilities at the beach itself, and the road is narrow with limited parking. This is not a beach for swimming; it is a beach for watching the raw Atlantic meeting ancient volcanic rock.

## 10. Playa de la Tejita, Tenerife

La Tejita is a naturist beach in the far south of Tenerife, east of El Médano, backed by the distinctive flat-topped volcanic cone of Montaña Roja. The beach is long and broad, facing south, with the wind conditions that make the El Médano area consistently excellent for wind sports. Montaña Roja is a protected natural monument and the walk to its summit takes about 30 minutes from the beach. The sand here is the natural dark volcanic brown of southern Tenerife rather than imported Saharan sand, and the beach has minimal development — a small car park and no permanent bars.
