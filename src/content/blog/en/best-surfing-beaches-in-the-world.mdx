Surfing's geography is determined by swell, bathymetry, and wind. The best surfing beaches in the world are not where the scenery is most beautiful or the weather most pleasant — they are where the ocean floor, the swell period, and the local wind pattern combine to produce the wave type that the sport depends on. Some of the world's best waves are in bleak, cold, or remote places. That the surfing world's most prestigious events are held there rather than at more comfortable destinations is the point.

## 1. Pipeline, Oahu, North Shore, Hawaii

The Banzai Pipeline at Ehukai Beach Park on Oahu's North Shore is the most written-about wave in surfing. It is a hollow, left-breaking tube that detonates over a very shallow reef in 2 feet of water. The wave's power-to-size ratio is unlike any other famous wave: a relatively modest 6-to-8-foot day at Pipeline will kill an unprepared surfer. The peak season runs from November through February when north Pacific swells arrive with clockwork regularity. The Vans Triple Crown of Surfing — the sport's most prestigious event series — is held here. Spectators watch from the beach at Ehukai. For experienced surfers only: this is not a learner wave.

## 2. Teahupo'o, Tahiti, French Polynesia

Teahupo'o (pronounced Cho-po) is regularly described as the most dangerous wave in the world, a thick, heavy, left-hand tube breaking over a reef that nearly dries in places. The wave is defined by its mass: Teahupo'o produces relatively low wave faces by global standards but the thickness and volume of water moving at speed over a shallow reef creates a hydraulic force that is extreme. The Billabong Tahiti Pro is the site's WSL event. Spectators watch from boats moored in the channel. The break is accessible by boat from Taravao on the Tahiti Iti peninsula.

## 3. Jeffrey's Bay, Eastern Cape, South Africa

J-Bay is the world's most famous right-hand point break — a long, speeding wave that peels for up to 700 metres in good conditions across a series of sections (Boneyards, Supertubes, Point). The Supertubes section is the competition zone for the WSL event held here each July. The wave quality is consistent and predictable; the water is cold year-round (15 to 18 degrees Celsius, requiring a 3/2mm wetsuit minimum). J-Bay is a surfing town first and a tourist destination second — the infrastructure serves surfers rather than general visitors.

## 4. Hossegor, Landes, France

Hossegor is Europe's best beach break and the home of European high-performance surfing. The beaches at Les Culs Nuls, Les Estagnots, and La Gravière produce hollow, powerful beachbreak waves that change day to day and require surf forecasting skills to work well. The Quiksilver Pro France WSL event is held here in autumn when the Atlantic swell season begins. The Landes coast is a long, flat, pine-forested beach landscape — aesthetically monotonous but functionally excellent for surfing. Water temperatures require a 4/3mm wetsuit from October.

## 5. Bells Beach, Victoria, Australia

Bells Beach near Torquay in Victoria is a right-hand point break on the Southern Ocean. The Rip Curl Pro, the longest-running pro surfing contest in the world, is held here every Easter — and the surf does not always cooperate, which is part of the event's identity. The wave is a slow, powerful, peeling right that rewards experienced regular-foot surfers. The Rip Curl Pro has been held on back-up sites in years when Bells was too small. The surrounding Great Ocean Road makes the area a major tourist corridor independently of surfing.

## 6. Uluwatu, Bali, Indonesia

Uluwatu is a reef break at the base of limestone cliffs on Bali's Bukit Peninsula. The wave is a left-hand tube that works on small and large swells, and the lower sections of the reef are accessible by a steep stairway through a cave. The Pura Uluwatu temple on the cliff above the break is Bali's most important sea temple. Uluwatu has become busy since the 1970s when it was a backpacker discovery, but the wave quality remains exceptional. Padang Padang, a few kilometres away, is a tight, hollow left that works only in larger swells.

## 7. Superbank, Coolangatta, Queensland, Australia

The Superbank at Snapper Rocks, Coolangatta, produces one of the world's longest rideable waves when conditions align — a continuous right-hand sandbank point break that can run from Snapper through Rainbow Bay and Greenmount for more than 2 kilometres. The wave is the product of sand dredging from the Tweed River mouth, which caused an accidental sandbank formation in the late 1990s. It is now heavily crowded whenever it is good. The Gold Coast's surf culture, infrastructure, and consistent waves make it the most functional surf destination in Australia.

## 8. Mundaka, Basque Country, Spain

Mundaka in the Basque Country is Europe's best rivermouth left — a hollow, tubular wave that forms at the mouth of the Urdaibai estuary when a large Atlantic swell meets the sandbars deposited by the river. The wave is highly inconsistent: it requires a specific swell direction, size, and river sand configuration that can be absent for entire seasons, then appear for days of exceptional conditions. When Mundaka fires, it is arguably the best wave in Europe. The town is small and beautiful; the estuary behind is a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve.

## 9. Playa Cabarete, Dominican Republic

Playa Cabarete on the Dominican Republic's north coast is the Caribbean's best surf beach and one of its best kite spots. The morning trade wind is offshore, producing glassy conditions for surfing in the early hours; by midday the onshore wind picks up for kitesurfing. The beach break at Cabarete and the reef break at Encuentro, 3 kilometres west, offer two distinct wave types. Encuentro is the better and more consistent surf wave; Cabarete beach is the social centre. The surf and kite culture here is genuinely international, with a year-round traveller community drawn by the consistent conditions. Playa Cabarete is in the database.

## 10. Arugam Bay, Sri Lanka

Arugam Bay on Sri Lanka's eastern coast is the Indian Ocean's most famous right-hand point break, working from April through October when the southwest monsoon generates consistent swell. The main point break at Arugam Bay itself is a long, mellow right suitable for intermediate surfers; the surrounding breaks — Whiskey Point, Elephant Rock, Pottuvil Point — range up to considerably more powerful waves. Arugam Bay has developed a well-established surf town character with good guesthouses, food, and surf hire infrastructure. The east coast's dry season coincides with the southwest monsoon, keeping the town functional and the surf reliable simultaneously. Arugam Bay is in the database.

## For intermediate surfers

The beaches above include some of the world's most powerful and technically demanding waves. For intermediates wanting quality surf in accessible conditions, Arugam Bay in Sri Lanka, Taghazout in Morocco (October through April), the beaches around Raglan in New Zealand, and Playa Hermosa in Costa Rica provide consistent quality with a broader range of ability levels in the water and good surf instruction infrastructure.

The single most important variable for an intermediate surfer choosing a surf destination is the wave type: point breaks and reef breaks produce predictable, repeating waves that are easier to learn on than beach breaks, which shift position and character with sand movement and swell direction. Arugam Bay's right-hand point, Taghazout's Panoramas, and Raglan's Manu Bay are all classic long-period point breaks that allow multiple attempts at the same wave pattern on consecutive rides.
