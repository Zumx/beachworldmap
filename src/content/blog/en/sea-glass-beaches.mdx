Sea glass is manufactured glass — from bottles, windows, tableware, and industrial containers — that has been broken and then tumbled by surf action over years or decades until the sharp edges are worn smooth and the surface develops a characteristic frosted patina produced by chemical etching. The result is something that straddles the boundary between natural and human-made: material derived from human activity, but transformed by geological process into objects with their own beauty and collectability. The combination of historical interest, colour variety, and tactile pleasure has made sea glass collecting one of the most popular forms of beachcombing, and certain beaches — those with historical industrial activity nearby or positioned to receive ocean-transported glass from distant sources — have become internationally known among collectors.

## How Sea Glass Forms

The process requires three ingredients: glass, wave action, and time. When glass reaches the sea, it begins a transformation driven by the mechanical abrasion of tumbling against other glass fragments, stones, and sand particles, combined with the chemical leaching of the surface by seawater. Soda-lime glass — the standard composition of most bottles and windows — contains sodium, calcium, and silicon as major components. Seawater, which is slightly alkaline and contains dissolved salts, attacks the calcium and sodium ions at the glass surface, leaching them out and leaving behind a silica-rich surface layer that scatters light diffusely rather than reflecting it clearly. This is the frosted, matte surface texture that distinguishes genuine sea glass from glass that has merely been broken.

The time required depends on the aggressiveness of the wave environment. A beach exposed to strong Atlantic swell and with a rocky, cobbly foreshore can produce well-frosted sea glass in 20 to 30 years. A sheltered harbour with minimal wave action might require a century to produce the same degree of weathering. The best sea glass beaches tend to be those with enough wave energy to tumble glass actively, but not so much that pieces are pulverised into fragments too small to collect.

## Glass Colors and Their Historical Sources

Common sea glass colours reflect the most frequently produced glass types in recent centuries. Brown is the most abundant colour in most collections — the colour of beer bottles, which were manufactured in vast quantities from the 19th century onward. Bottle-glass green (the colour of wine bottles) is the next most common. Clear or white glass comes from soft drink bottles, food jars, and windows.

Rarer colours represent less commonly manufactured glass. Cobalt blue was used for medicine bottles and certain cosmetics packaging — Milk of Magnesia bottles, Bromo-Seltzer bottles. Aqua or seafoam blue was the standard colour of American Mason canning jars from the late 19th through mid-20th century. Medium purple glass — amethyst sea glass — was produced when manganese was used as a decolouriser in glass manufacture; exposure to UV light over years causes the manganese to turn purple, so bottles manufactured roughly between 1880 and 1920 in the United States will turn amethyst when exposed to sunlight. Finding amethyst sea glass sets an approximate date on the original object.

Orange sea glass is often described as the rarest collectible colour because orange glass was rarely manufactured for household use. The few sources include Carnival glass from the early 20th century and certain specialty items. Red glass, from older warning lanterns and some decorative ware, is also rare. Genuine red sea glass is difficult to distinguish from pink by casual examination; holding it to bright light reveals a distinct red rather than pink transmission colour.

## The Most Productive Sea Glass Beaches

Sea Glass Beach at Fort Bragg, California, is probably the most famous dedicated sea glass collecting site in the world. The beach, now part of MacKerricher State Park, occupies the site of the town dump, which operated from 1906 until the mid-1960s and was positioned at the cliff edge above a rocky beach. Glass, pottery, metal, and domestic waste were dumped down the cliff face directly into the surf. Decades of wave action have ground the material against the rocky foreshore, producing enormous quantities of sea glass in a wide variety of colours, including pieces from the full span of early 20th century glass manufacturing. The beach is now managed for conservation, and collecting in large quantities is restricted.

Seaham Beach in County Durham, England, is another world-famous site, though the source material is different — not a dump site but a glass factory. The Londonderry Bottleworks operated at Seaham from 1855 and discharged waste glass directly into the sea until it closed in 1921. The result is a beach that produces distinctive glass fragments in unusual colours that originated from the glass factory's product range, including some rare colours from 19th-century production runs.

Bermuda's south coast beaches receive glass from two sources: historical dumping sites on the island and ocean-transported material from the North Atlantic. The isolation of Bermuda and the prevailing current patterns mean some of the sea glass arriving on Bermudian beaches has travelled very long distances. Blue-green glass in unusual shades can represent glass from manufacturing traditions in Europe, the Caribbean, or the eastern United States.

Beaches on the western coast of Japan, particularly on the Sea of Japan, receive material carried by currents from Chinese and Korean industrial coastlines, producing glass in colours and patterns that reflect Asian manufacturing traditions different from the Western European and American glass that dominates Atlantic-coast collecting.

## Identifying and Dating Pieces

Collectors develop a vocabulary for categorising sea glass quality. A grade is given to well-frosted, smoothly rounded pieces with no remaining sharp edges or transparent windows in the surface; C grade pieces still have patches of their original surface visible. The shape of fragments gives clues to the original object: curved fragments from spherical bottles, flat fragments from windows or plates, thick pieces from industrial glass or early hand-blown bottles. Embossed letters or numbers on fragments are particularly valuable for dating — bottle manufacturers and pharmaceutical companies embossed their names and patent numbers on glass from the 1880s onward.

Sea pottery — ceramic fragments processed by the same surf-tumbling mechanism — often accompanies sea glass at productive sites. Transfer-printed pottery from the 19th century produces fragments with partial blue-and-white patterns that are highly collectible.
