South Korea has coastlines on the Yellow Sea (west), the Korea Strait (south), and the East Sea (east, also called the Sea of Japan). Each coast has a distinct character: the west coast has extensive tidal flats (getbol) and a flat, sandy beach character with dramatic tidal ranges; the south coast has more irregular shoreline with islands and archipelagos; the east coast has cleaner water, a more direct Pacific character, and generally better swimming conditions. Jeju Island, south of the peninsula and accessible by flight or ferry, has volcanic geology and a distinct beach character quite unlike the mainland coasts.

The Korean beach season is compressed into July and August, when the humidity and heat peak and the sea is warm enough for comfortable swimming. This brevity creates a beach culture that is intense and social — entire families migrate to coastal resorts for the summer holiday period, beach umbrellas are rented in organised rows, and lifeguard systems are well-staffed. Outside peak season, most beach facilities close entirely and visitor numbers drop dramatically. This is worth knowing if planning a visit in June or September: the beach may be pristine and empty, but the infrastructure that makes it a full-day comfortable visit will not be there.

Korea's beach culture has also been shaped by the influence of Jeju island, which has promoted its volcanic black sand beaches and uniquely warm microclimate as distinct from the mainland coast. The Haenyeo — the female free-divers of Jeju who harvest seafood by breath-hold diving, a tradition listed by UNESCO — are one of the most visible elements of Korean coastal culture that distinguishes the island from the mainland resort strips.

## 1. Haeundae Beach, Busan

Haeundae is South Korea's most famous beach and the most visited in the country. The 1.5-kilometre white sand beach in Busan's Haeundae district receives millions of visitors in July and August, when the sea temperature reaches 26 degrees Celsius. The beach is backed by a wall of high-rise hotels and apartments — a fully urban beach. Haeundae hosts the Busan International Film Festival nearby and has a comprehensive resort infrastructure. Lifeguards operate throughout summer.

## 2. Gwangalli Beach, Busan

Gwangalli, 3 kilometres west of Haeundae, is smaller and backed by a more relaxed café and restaurant zone. The Gwangan Bridge is visible and lit at night from the beach, creating a backdrop unique in Korean beach culture. Gwangalli is a known fireworks festival venue in October.

## 3. Jungmun Saekdal Beach, Jeju

Jungmun on Jeju Island's south coast is one of the few South Korean beaches with a wave suitable for surfing — a consistent shore break generated by the open Pacific exposure on the southern coast. The beach is backed by the Jungmun resort complex. Jeju's basalt volcanic geology creates dark rock formations at each end of the beach that contrast with the dark-sand beach.

## 4. Hamdeok Beach, Jeju

Hamdeok on Jeju's north coast is known for clear water and a sheltered character. The beach is family-friendly, has good snorkelling on the rocks, and receives consistent Blue Flag certification by Korean standards. A café strip behind the beach has made it a social destination.

## 5. Naksan Beach, Gangwon

Naksan Beach on the east coast of Gangwon Province, below the Naksansa Buddhist temple complex on the cliff above, has a distinctive religious and cultural context that distinguishes it from Korea's purely resort beaches. The sunrise view from the beach — the East Sea to the east, the temple on the cliff above — is one of the most known sunrise spots in Korea.

## 6. Mangsang Beach, Gangwon

Mangsang Beach at the south end of Gangneung in Gangwon Province is a long east coast beach with consistent summer surf and clean water. The pine forest behind the beach and the lower development density compared to Busan make it appealing to Korean travellers looking for a quieter east coast option.

## 7. Daecheon Beach, Chungcheongnam

Daecheon Beach on the Yellow Sea coast of Chungcheongnam Province is one of the west coast's best beaches and the site of the Boryeong Mud Festival in July — when millions of visitors come to roll in the coastal mudflats. The tidal range is enormous on the west coast (up to 9 metres); the beach at low tide extends hundreds of metres and reveals the getbol tidal flat ecology.

## 8. Eurwangni Beach, Incheon

Eurwangni Beach on Ganghwa Island near Incheon airport is the closest beach to Seoul, accessible by train and bus in about 90 minutes. The beach faces the Yellow Sea and has the tidal character typical of the west coast. It is a functional day trip option from Seoul rather than a destination beach, but the practical value for Seoul residents is significant.

## 9. Woljeong-ri Beach, Jeju

Woljeong-ri on the northeast coast of Jeju is known for its turquoise water — the clearest on the island — and for the photogenic stone wall fishing weirs (woljeong-ri) that extend into the sea. The beach has become one of Jeju's most photographed due to the water colour and the traditional stone structures. Café culture along the Jeju coast has made Woljeong-ri a stop on the island circuit.

## 10. Gyeongpodae Beach, Gangwon

Gyeongpodae Beach near Gangneung in Gangwon Province is a long sandy beach on the East Sea with pine forests behind. The 2018 Winter Olympics in Pyeongchang were close by; the improved infrastructure from that event has made the wider Gangneung area more accessible. Gyeongpodae Lagoon directly behind the beach adds a freshwater swimming option.

## Planning Korean beaches

The practical beach trip to South Korea divides between two circuits: the Busan southeast coast, centred on Haeundae and Gwangalli, and the Jeju Island circuit. Both are accessible by direct flights from international hubs — Busan through Gimhae International Airport, Jeju through Jeju International Airport, one of the busiest airports in South Korea by domestic traffic.

Busan is a major port city with an active food and arts scene. Haeundae's beach infrastructure is urban and comprehensive; staying in the Haeundae district gives immediate beach access combined with the city's excellent seafood markets (Jagalchi market is the largest in Korea), the Gamcheon Culture Village, and the Haedong Yonggungsa temple built on the coast north of Haeundae. The east coast beaches of Gangwon Province (Sokcho, Naksan, Gangneung) require a separate trip from Seoul — the KTX express train from Seoul to Gangneung takes 2 hours since the 2017 extension. The east coast is popular with Seoul residents for the combination of clean water, relatively uncrowded beaches, and seafood culture.

Jeju Island is a self-governing special province with a distinct volcanic geology, culture, and regional identity from the mainland. A rental car is essential on Jeju; the island is 73 kilometres east to west and public transport is limited for reaching the best beaches. A 4 to 5 day Jeju circuit, driving the island's Olle trail coastal walking route sections and visiting the key beaches (Hamdeok, Woljeong-ri, Jungmun, the black sand beaches), the Hallasan national park interior, and the lava tube caves (Manjanggul), is one of the most varied short trips available in East Asia.

The beach season is genuinely compressed: mid-July through August is the practical window for comfortable sea swimming on the mainland. Jeju is milder and extends the season slightly both directions. Outside this window, the beaches are empty and the facilities closed; the landscape is beautiful in the off-season but the beach experience as such has ended. Korean domestic tourism in peak season is intense — Haeundae at peak can see 100,000 visitors per day — so some planning around mid-week visits or the early and late ends of the season improves the experience considerably.
