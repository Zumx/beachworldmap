The Pacific Northwest coast — Oregon and Washington state — is among the most dramatic and least conventionally beachy coastlines in the United States. Water temperatures rarely exceed 14°C even in August, owing to the California Current upwelling that draws cold deep water to the surface. This cold water, combined with pervasive Pacific fog, frequent coastal storms from September through April, and surf that can reach extreme size during winter swell events, means that the beach activities here are defined by walking, wildlife watching, storm-watching, and rugged outdoor recreation rather than the sunbathing and swimming model of the south. The compensation is extraordinary: sea stacks rising 70 metres from the surf, headlands carpeted in Sitka spruce down to the waterline, rivers cutting through to the beach from old-growth forest, and a biodiversity of the intertidal zone — sea stars, anemones, chitons, sea urchins — that rivals any in the Pacific. Oregon's entire 580-kilometre coast is public by state law; Washington's coast is partly wilderness and partly tribal land.

## 1. Cannon Beach, Clatsop County, Oregon

Cannon Beach is the most visited and most photographed Oregon beach, defined by the 72-metre-tall Haystack Rock monolith standing 90 metres from the tideline. Haystack Rock is a designated marine garden — collecting anything is prohibited — and supports nesting tufted puffins from April through August, visible from the beach below through binoculars. The beach is seven kilometres long and wide even at high tide, backed by the historic resort town of Cannon Beach with its art galleries, bookshops, and the independently owned Coaster Theatre. The beach becomes extraordinary in winter storms, when waves crash against Haystack Rock in plumes of spray 20 metres high.

## 2. Manzanita Beach, Tillamook County, Oregon

Manzanita Beach runs six kilometres south of Neahkahnie Mountain along the base of the Nehalem Bay spit, backed by the small village of Manzanita — fewer than 700 residents — and the Nehalem Bay State Park campground. The beach is wide, clean, and dominated by the looming presence of Neahkahnie Mountain, whose forested basalt face drops almost to the beach at the north end. This is a productive razor clam and crab beach; clamming at low tide is a serious pastime. Manzanita is consistently rated among Oregon's most livable beach towns for its balance of natural beauty and absence of commercial overdevelopment.

## 3. Pacific City Beach, Tillamook County, Oregon

Pacific City is uniquely the only place on the Oregon coast where you can watch dory fishing boats launch directly off the beach through the surf — a tradition that dates back to the 1940s when flat-bottomed dory boats were adapted to allow fishermen to escape the limited harbour space and launch from the open beach into the surf zone. Cape Kiwanda, an enormous sandstone headland immediately north of the beach, provides shelter and a launch ramp of smooth sandstone. Hang gliders launch from the top of the cape. The Pelican Brewing Company, the original Oregon beach brew pub, occupies the landward end of the parking area.

## 4. Ruby Beach, Olympic National Park, Washington

Ruby Beach is the northernmost and most visually spectacular of the Olympic National Park beaches accessible by road, sitting where the Hoh and Queets river systems have deposited driftwood in quantities that pile the upper beach ten metres deep in some storm seasons. The beach is named for the garnet crystals occasionally found in the black sand. Sea stacks of resistant basalt and sandstone in the surf zone provide roost sites for bald eagles and Brandt's cormorants. The Hoh Rainforest, one of the only temperate rainforests in the United States, begins a few kilometres inland, and the Hoh River runs to the beach through old-growth Sitka spruce. No facilities beyond a parking lot; the experience is deliberately wild.

## 5. Rialto Beach, Olympic National Park, Washington

Rialto Beach is divided from the town of La Push by the Quillayute River and reached by a short bridge, placing it at the mouth of the river system that drains much of the Olympic Peninsula's western slope. The beach is long, grey-white pebble and sand, with massive driftwood logs that form a natural berm at the upper beach. Hole-in-the-Wall — a sea arch worn through a basalt headland about two kilometres north — is the primary destination for beach walkers. Tidal pools at the base of the headlands are among the most accessible and species-rich on the Washington coast. The Quileute Nation's La Push community administers the adjacent lands.

## 6. Kalaloch Beach, Olympic National Park, Washington

Kalaloch Beach is a long, flat stretch of sand and small pebble on the Washington coast within Olympic National Park, backed by a forested bluff with the historic Kalaloch Lodge on the cliff above. The lodge has operated since the 1950s and is among the few places on the Olympic coast where you can sleep directly above a wild Pacific beach. The beach system — numbered Kalaloch 1 through 6 — runs for about 10 kilometres and is traversable at low tide. Brown pelicans, formerly nearly extinct, now roost on the offshore rocks in numbers in late summer. The nearby Kalaloch Creek empties onto the beach, and the hollow Kalaloch Tree — a Sitka spruce suspended over an eroding creek bank — is a notable natural curiosity.

## 7. Second Beach, Olympic National Park, Washington

Second Beach near La Push is reached via a 30-minute trail through old-growth Sitka spruce and western red cedar before emerging at the top of a bluff above the beach. The descent reveals a dramatic scene: a crescent of sand framed by sea stacks, the largest of which rise 60–70 metres from the water and are draped in vegetation maintained by spray. At low tide the rocks are accessible on foot across tidal flat, and the tide pools contain giant green anemones, sea stars of half a dozen species, purple sea urchins, and ochre sea stars recovering from the sea star wasting disease that devastated populations from 2013 to 2020.

## 8. Crescent Beach, Clallam County, Washington

Crescent Beach sits on the Strait of Juan de Fuca rather than the open Pacific — a significant distinction. The strait's protected waters bring calmer surf and modestly warmer summer temperatures than the outer coast, and the beach faces north toward Vancouver Island and the British Columbia mountains. This outlook is consistently spectacular in late afternoon light. The beach is backed by a small resort area and accessed via a county road from the Highway 101 through Port Angeles. Views of the Olympic Mountain snow peaks behind and the Canadian Rockies across the strait are available from the same viewpoint.

## 9. Shi Shi Beach, Olympic Peninsula, Washington

Shi Shi Beach is the northernmost beach on the contiguous Olympic coast, accessible only by a 5.5-kilometre trail through the Makah tribal lands from Neah Bay. The approach requires a Makah recreation permit, obtainable at tribal offices in Neah Bay. Point of the Arches, at the south end of the beach, is a formation of eroded rock arches, tunnels, and sea stacks through which the tide swirls at low water — one of the finest intertidal landscapes in the Pacific Northwest. Backpacking on the beach and the adjacent Ozette Triangle coastal route is popular; sites are dispersed and fires are prohibited. Arrive during a minus tide for full access to the arch formations.

## 10. Bandon Beach, Coos County, Oregon

Bandon Beach at the mouth of the Coquille River in southern Oregon is distinguished by an extraordinary field of sea stacks — dozens of isolated basalt columns of varying heights scattered along a three-kilometre section of coast, accessible at low tide by walking around the base of the main beach. Face Rock, a sea stack with a profile resembling a woman's face in legend, is the most photographed. The Coquille River Lighthouse, decommissioned in 1939 but restored, stands on a spit at the river mouth. Bandon town is known as the cranberry capital of Oregon and the location of Bandon Dunes Golf Resort, one of the most highly rated links courses in the United States.
