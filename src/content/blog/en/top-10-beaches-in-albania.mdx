Albania's Riviera, the stretch of Ionian coast between Vlora and Saranda, has emerged as one of Europe's most compelling beach destinations. The combination of clear Ionian water, relative underdevelopment compared to Croatia or Greece, low costs, and dramatic limestone landscapes produces beaches that are genuinely exceptional by any standard. The infrastructure is developing rapidly; the window in which these beaches can be experienced without heavy tourism pressure is closing but has not yet closed.

## 1. Gjipe Beach, Himara

Gjipe is in a canyon at the mouth of the Gjipe River where it meets the Ionian Sea. The approach requires a 40-minute hike through a limestone canyon from the road above — or a short boat ride from Himara town. The beach is a narrow stretch of pebble and sand enclosed by canyon walls, with clear turquoise water. There is a basic seasonal camp with food and overnight tents. The canyon hike is part of the experience and keeps casual visitor numbers low. Plazhi i Gjipes is in the map database.

## 2. Ksamil Beach, Ksamil

Ksamil is a small village south of Saranda, almost on the Greek border, with four offshore islands and a series of sheltered bays. The main Ksamil beaches face south into the Ionian and have remarkably clear, shallow water — the sand is white and the offshore depth gradual enough to wade far from shore. The island configuration creates natural swimming pools between the main beach and the islets. Ksamil has developed rapidly; some sections have become crowded in peak season, but the small offshore islands remain accessible by swimming or kayak.

## 3. Drymades Beach, Himara

Drymades is a 2-kilometre beach south of Himara with grey-white sand and crystal Ionian water in a relatively sheltered bay. Behind the beach, olive groves and small guesthouses form the village. The beach has a Blue Eye character — several freshwater springs emerge directly on the sea floor near the shore, creating temperature variations felt while swimming. It is quieter than Ksamil and has better-preserved olive grove character behind the beach.

## 4. Palasa Beach, Himara

Palasa is a remote beach near the village of Palasa accessible by a rough track, with a long pebble beach and dramatic cliffs on each side. The small village church visible from the beach dates from the Byzantine era. The beach is barely developed and receives far fewer visitors than the Saranda area; a summer season brings a few cafés but little else. The landscape around Palasa is typical of the Albanian Riviera at its most intact — terraced slopes, dry stone walls, and the persistent scent of wild herbs.

## 5. Jale Beach, Himara

Jale is a south-facing cove between Himara and Saranda with clear water and a pebble-and-sand mix. A cluster of beach bars and small accommodation has developed here while maintaining the informal character of a young traveller destination. The snorkelling around the rocky headlands on either side of the cove is good. Jale hosts a summer festival and has a livelier evening character than most Albanian Riviera beaches.

## 6. Borshi Beach, Himara

Borshi, north of Himara, is one of the longest beaches on the Albanian Riviera — approximately 7 kilometres of shingle and sand with the Borsh castle ruins visible on the hillside above. The length of the beach means that self-distribution of visitors keeps it feeling uncrowded even at the peak of summer. The village behind the beach produces good olive oil and basic accommodation.

## 7. Buneci Beach, Ksamil

Buneci is a small beach south of Ksamil near the Greek border with shallow, extraordinarily clear water and a private-feeling character. The surrounding area is the Butrint National Park — the beach is within walking distance of the UNESCO World Heritage Site archaeological remains of Butrint, a Greek, Roman, and Byzantine city. The combination of a good beach and genuinely significant archaeology a kilometre away is unusual.

## 8. Karaburun Peninsula

The Karaburun Peninsula north of Vlora is accessible only by boat from Sazan island or from Vlora port. The wild western coast of the peninsula has caves, sea caves, and beaches accessible only by water. The Haxhi Ali Cave is the most famous — a sea cave with a freshwater lake inside, accessible by boat. The beaches around the cave are pebble and rock with no facilities. This is wilderness coastline by Albanian standards.

## 9. Dhermi Beach, Dhermi

Dhermi is a well-known Albanian Riviera destination with a long pebble beach below the village. The beach has a developed beach bar scene that makes it livelier than more remote options, and the water quality and clarity are typical of the Ionian here — very good. The village above the beach retains old stone architecture and is worth the walk up. Dhermi has been growing rapidly; its character is shifting toward a party beach destination.

## 10. Livadhi Beach, Himara

Livadhi is the main beach of Himara town, a long crescent of grey pebble and sand in a sheltered bay with a moderate infrastructure of restaurants and guesthouses behind. The town of Himara has Greek-Albanian character — a significant Greek-speaking minority lives here — and the cafés along the seafront are pleasant for evening meals. The beach is more oriented toward local life than tourist spectacle, which distinguishes it from more internationally marketed Albanian beaches.

## Season and planning

The Albanian Riviera is best from June through September. The roads connecting the coastal villages improved significantly after 2020, though some tracks to remote beaches remain unpaved. Saranda is the base town for the southern section; Himara for the central section. Most international visitors fly into Tirana's Mother Teresa Airport and drive south — the SH8 coastal road from Vlora to Saranda is one of the most scenic in the Balkans, though it narrows to a single-lane mountain road in places and the driving requires patience. The journey from Tirana to Saranda takes 3.5 to 4.5 hours depending on traffic and stops.

## Albania versus Greece and Croatia

The practical case for choosing the Albanian Riviera over the more established Ionian Greek islands or the Croatian Adriatic is primarily cost and development pressure. Albanian prices for accommodation, food, and beach services are substantially lower than equivalent quality in Greece or Croatia — typically 40 to 60 percent less — and the most beautiful beaches (Gjipe, Ksamil, Drymades) receive a fraction of the visitor numbers that comparably beautiful Greek beaches like Navagio or Porto Katsiki absorb.

The tradeoff is infrastructure: Albania's beach facilities are developing but uneven, internet connectivity is variable in remote areas, and the range of accommodation options is narrower than in more established destinations. The best approach to the Albanian Riviera is to carry more self-sufficiency than you would need in Greece or Croatia and to treat the infrastructure gaps as the price of access to uncrowded exceptional beaches.

Ksamil specifically — the most approachable Albanian beach destination for first-time visitors, with good infrastructure and clear water — is less than 30 minutes by boat or land from the Greek island of Corfu and can be combined with a Corfu stay on a day trip. The contrast between the heavily developed Corfu beach infrastructure and the Ksamil waterfront is stark.
