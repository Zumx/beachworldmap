Switzerland has no sea coast, but it has lakes — 1,484 of them by the official federal count — and the best of these lakes offer bathing conditions that rival any coastal resort in the summer months. Lake Geneva (Lac Léman) is the largest lake entirely or partly in Switzerland, stretching 73 kilometres with the Alps reflected in its south shore and the Jura mountains behind its north shore. Lake Lucerne twists through Rigi, Pilatus, and the Bürgenstock in a complex of interconnected bays. Lake Constance forms the boundary with Germany and Austria in a flat expanse that warms quickly in early summer. Lake Maggiore and Lake Lugano descend into northern Italy with a genuinely Mediterranean climate, palm trees, and cicadas. Water temperatures in the main lakes reach 22–26°C in late July and August — comparable to the North Sea and Baltic at their best, but in Alpine scenery. The Swiss bathing culture is distinctive: a network of public Badis (outdoor bathing facilities) provides changing rooms, lawns, and diving platforms at relatively modest admission fees, making lake bathing accessible without private ownership or beach club costs.

## 1. Plage de St-Saphorin, Lake Geneva

Plage de St-Saphorin sits in the heart of the Lavaux UNESCO World Heritage vineyard district, where terraced vine terraces cascade from the Vaud hillside to the lake shore. The beach is small and public, the water of Lake Geneva reaching 22–24°C in late summer, and the backdrop of medieval wine villages and the Alps across the lake is outstanding. The village of St-Saphorin itself has a 12th-century church and wine cellars producing the crisp Chasselas whites for which Lavaux is known. This is one of the few beaches in the world where you can swim in front of a working Neolithic archaeological landscape — the Lavaux shores have yielded pile dwelling artefacts dating back 5,000 years.

## 2. Sundlauenen Beach, Lake Thun, Bernese Oberland

Sundlauenen is a small beach on the south shore of Lake Thun in the Bernese Oberland, looking directly across at the three-peaked silhouette of Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau. The beach is accessible by ferry from Thun or Interlaken — the ferries that operate Lake Thun are a scenic experience in themselves — and by car via the narrow lake-shore road from Spiez. Water here is deep and cold at depth but warms quickly at the surface to 20–22°C in July. The setting is among the most dramatic of any beach in the Alps. The nearby Beatushöhlen — a large cave system above the lake — can be combined with beach time.

## 3. Plage de Saint-Aubin, Lake Neuchâtel

Plage de Saint-Aubin is a municipal beach on the southern shore of Lake Neuchâtel, the largest lake entirely within Switzerland. The beach is sandy and well-equipped, with the Jura mountain ridge rising behind the town and the Bernese Alps visible in clear conditions across the lake. Lake Neuchâtel has one of the better water temperatures of Swiss lakes, reaching 23–24°C in July, and is notable for its remarkably clear water — visibility in the open lake reaches several metres. The town of Saint-Aubin-Sauges is a modest wine-producing community; a local white wine with a beach picnic is the appropriate lunch.

## 4. Plage de la Falaise, Lake Geneva, Lausanne

Plage de la Falaise is one of the municipal bathing areas along the Lausanne-Ouchy lakefront, set beneath the terrace of the Hotel Beau-Rivage Palace above. The name refers to the low cliff — falaise — from which the beach is accessed. This is an urban lake beach in the best Swiss tradition: clean, orderly, well-maintained, and directly connected to a major city by public transport in under 15 minutes. The Ouchy lakefront with its castle, the Olympic Museum, and the promenade connecting to Morges to the west constitutes one of the most pleasant lakeside walking environments in Europe. The lake here is warm and the backdrop of the Savoy Alps across the water is constant.

## 5. Meier's Badhüttli, Lake Zurich

Meier's Badhüttli is a historic bathing hut on the Zurich Lakeside — a small wooden pavilion structure extending over the water on Lake Zurich that represents the oldest form of Swiss urban bathing infrastructure. Bathing huts of this type, with changing cabins, ladders into the water, and wooden sundecks, have been part of Swiss lake culture since the late 19th century. The lake temperature at Zurich in August is typically 23–24°C. The hüttli system in Zurich is divided by gender at some locations, a tradition maintained with Swiss consistency. The combined lake and city backdrop — the old town, the Grossmünster and Fraumünster spires, the Albis ridge behind — is compelling.

## 6. Bibermühli, Greifensee, Zurich Canton

Bibermühli is a natural lake beach on the Greifensee — a smaller lake east of Zurich — set in the Greifensee Nature Reserve, a protected area for migratory birds and amphibians. The beach is accessed via a nature path from the village of Maur, passing reed beds and waterside meadows. The lake warms quickly due to its shallowness and regularly reaches 25°C or more in a warm summer — warmer than Lake Zurich by several degrees. This makes Greifensee one of the most comfortable swimming lakes in the canton. The reserve is significant for breeding marsh birds, and early morning visits in late spring offer extraordinary birdsong.

## 7. Rhiibadi, Rheinfelden, Aargau

The Rhiibadi at Rheinfelden is not a lake beach but a river bathing facility directly on the Rhine, where the river flows wide and relatively fast between Germany and Switzerland. The Rhine current here is strong — the entire volume of water draining from the Alps passes through this section — and experienced swimmers float downstream from the entry point to the exit ladder. The water temperature in summer reaches 20–22°C. Rheinfelden itself is known for its historic wooden covered bridge and the Feldschlösschen brewery on the Swiss bank. River bathing in the Rhine is a Swiss tradition connected to Basel and its famous Rhybadi, but the Rheinfelden facility is less visited.

## 8. Plage du Rocheray, Lake de Joux, Vaud

Plage du Rocheray is on Lake de Joux in the Vallée de Joux, a high plateau in the Jura mountains at 1,004 metres above sea level. The lake is the largest natural lake in the Swiss Jura and is surrounded by forest. At this altitude, the season is short — the beach is usable from mid-June through early September — but the mountain plateau setting, the clarity of the lake, and the complete absence of Alpine tourists who go instead to the better-known Bernese or Valais resorts make it a genuinely secluded experience. Water reaches around 20°C at peak summer. Le Sentier, the main village, has a tradition of watchmaking.

## 9. Dr. H. Spreng-Bucht, Lake Murten, Fribourg

The Dr. H. Spreng-Bucht is a named bay on the south-eastern shore of the Murten See (Lake Murten), a shallow moraine lake in the Swiss Mittelland between Fribourg and the Bernese Seenland. The bay has a small public beach of sand and gravel, with the medieval town of Murten visible at the lake's end. Lake Murten is one of the warmest lakes in Switzerland, its shallowness allowing it to reach 26°C in a warm July, and the combination of warmth, access by ferry from Murten, and the backdrop of the intact medieval wall circuit of Murten town makes this a particularly complete day trip from Bern.

## 10. Lido, Locarno, Lake Maggiore

The Lido at Locarno on Lake Maggiore is one of the most elaborate public beach and pool complexes in Switzerland, opened in the 1930s and located on a wide sandy beach at the north end of the lake. Locarno's climate is subtropical Mediterranean — palm trees, camellias, magnolias, and 2,300 hours of sunshine per year — the warmest town in Switzerland. Lake Maggiore here reaches 26–27°C in late July. The Lido complex includes an outdoor pool, a shallow children's pool, a sandy beach, tennis courts, and a restaurant, all within a ten-minute walk of Locarno's old town and the Piazza Grande, where the Locarno International Film Festival takes place every August.
