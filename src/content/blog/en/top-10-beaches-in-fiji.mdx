Fiji is an archipelago of 333 islands in the South Pacific Ocean, 2,000 kilometres north of New Zealand. The islands are divided into the main island of Viti Levu (where the airport and capital are), the second island Vanua Levu, and two major chains of smaller islands: the Mamanuca Islands (30 minutes to 2 hours from the main airport by ferry or speedboat) and the Yasawa Islands (4 to 6 hours by catamaran or 15 to 45 minutes by seaplane). The beach quality improves generally as you move north from the main island and its development.

The water temperature is 26 to 30 degrees Celsius year-round. The Coral Sea south of Fiji is one of the world's most biodiverse marine environments. The cyclone season runs from November through April; July through September is the peak tourist season with the most consistent weather.

## 1. Natadola Beach, Viti Levu

Natadola is the best beach on the main island of Viti Levu — a long crescent of white sand with a reef break producing consistent small waves on the outer edge of the bay. The inner bay is calm and suitable for swimming. The InterContinental resort is the primary accommodation. Natadola is the Fijian beach that requires the least travel: 40 kilometres west of Nadi airport, accessible by taxi or rail.

## 2. Yasawa Island, Yasawa Islands

Yasawa Island, at the northern end of the Yasawa chain, has a private feel enforced by the remote location — it is 6 hours by the Yasawa Flyer catamaran or 45 minutes by seaplane from Nadi. The Yasawa Island Resort is on a private beach with sand that is white and fine by any standard. The horse riding along the beach at sunset is a known experience at this property. The outer reef of the Yasawa chain is in good condition and the snorkelling is excellent.

## 3. Sawa-i-Lau, Yasawa Islands

Sawa-i-Lau is not primarily a beach but a limestone cave accessible by swimming into a flooded chamber — one of the most unusual experiences in Fiji. The cave has a cathedral-like inner chamber with stalactites and a skylight. A second, darker chamber requires swimming underwater briefly to enter. The scene from the Blue Lagoon film (1980) was filmed here. Boat tours to Sawa-i-Lau operate from resorts in the southern Yasawas.

## 4. Blue Lagoon, Nanuya Lailai, Yasawa Islands

The Blue Lagoon is a shallow, enclosed bay between the islands of Nanuya Levu and Nanuya Lailai. The water colour — shallow, over white sand, enclosed by the reef — is the specific turquoise blue that gives the lagoon its name. Snorkelling in the lagoon produces encounters with reef fish, sea turtles, and, on lucky days, manta rays. Accommodation at both island resorts on the lagoon's edge is available.

## 5. Malolo Island, Mamanuca Islands

Malolo Island in the Mamanuca chain has two resort operations — Likuliku Lagoon (the only Fijian resort with overwater bungalows) and Malolo Island Resort — on a beach facing a sheltered lagoon. The lagoon water is calm and warm, the sand white and fine, and the island has a tropical garden character that the smaller cay islands lack. Malolo is 30 minutes by speedboat from Port Denarau.

## 6. Tokoriki Island, Mamanuca Islands

Tokoriki is a small island at the outer edge of the Mamanuca group with an adults-only resort on a beach facing the open Coral Sea. The reef around Tokoriki is healthier than around the more trafficked inner Mamanucas and snorkelling directly from the beach produces reef encounters. The sunsets from the west-facing beach are excellent.

## 7. Nananu-i-Ra, Ra Province

Nananu-i-Ra (Rainbow Island) is a small island near Rakiraki on Viti Levu's north coast, accessible by a short boat transfer. The island has backpacker accommodation and a reputation among wind sports enthusiasts for consistent breeze. The beach has good snorkelling on the reef and the relative remoteness from the main tourist circuit keeps prices lower than the Mamanucas.

## 8. Wailoaloa Beach, Nadi

Wailoaloa Beach near Nadi is the functional beach near the airport for travellers with early morning or late evening flights who want a beach stop. The beach is long, has accommodation at every price point, and serves as the staging ground for day trips to the Mamanuca Islands. Saweni Beach and Wailoaloa Beach are in the map database.

## 9. Taveuni Waterfalls and Coast

Taveuni, the Garden Island, is Fiji's third largest island. The island's reputation is for its interior (the Bouma National Heritage Park waterfalls and bird life) rather than beaches, but the Lavena coastal walk ends at a waterfall-fed freshwater pool that meets the sea. The snorkelling at the offshore Rainbow Reef is world-class for Fiji — some of the most diverse soft coral in the Pacific.

## 10. Castaway Island, Mamanuca Islands

Castaway Island (Qalito Island) is one of Fiji's most long-established resort islands, used as a filming location for the film Castaway. The beach circles most of the small island and the property has been renovated into family-oriented accommodation. The island's proximity to Nadi (less than an hour by speedboat) makes it accessible for shorter Fiji visits.

## Planning around the Fiji islands

The Mamanuca Islands are the most practical destination for shorter stays: 30 minutes to 2 hours from Nadi airport by speedboat or water taxi from Port Denarau Marina, with daily services connecting the islands. The Yasawa Flyer catamaran runs daily from Port Denarau to the Yasawa chain, taking 4 to 6 hours to reach the northern islands. The Yasawa Flyer schedule and stops are fixed; the islands it serves are accessible no other way except by more expensive seaplane or organised small boat charters.

Accommodation in Fiji divides between the high-end international resort properties (Four Seasons on Laucala or Volivoli, Likuliku Lagoon at Malolo, Turtle Island), the mid-range island resorts at Malolo, Tokoriki, and Mana, and the budget island backpacker operations concentrated on the Yasawa chain (Octopus, Mantaray, Oarsman's Bay). The backpacker Yasawa circuit — taking the Flyer north, hopping between islands, and returning south after five to eight days — is one of the most well-organised independent travel circuits in the Pacific.

The Yasawa and Mamanuca islands have resident Fijian communities on most of the larger islands. Kava ceremonies at village visits are arranged by accommodation hosts and are a genuine part of the cultural experience rather than a tourist performance. Bringing a half-kilo of dried kava (yaqona) as a gift when visiting a village is the appropriate courtesy.

## Cyclone season and timing

The Fijian cyclone season runs from November through April. Severe tropical cyclones pass through the archipelago on average once or twice per year, though not all hit the main resort islands. July through September is the peak tourist season: the southeast trade wind is consistent, humidity is lower, and rainfall is reduced. This period coincides with European and Australian school holidays, driving accommodation demand and prices to their highest. April through June and October represent shoulder-season periods with lower prices, generally adequate weather, and reduced crowd levels. December through March is viable — the Southern Hemisphere summer produces warm weather and warm water — but cyclone risk and increased rainfall are real considerations that should factor into travel insurance decisions. Most Fijian resort properties will provide specific weather monitoring information at time of booking.
