Japan has coastline on both the Pacific Ocean and the Sea of Japan, with beaches ranging from the subtropical coral beaches of Okinawa's outer islands to the grey pebble shores of Hokkaido. The beach culture is distinct from Western tradition: Japanese beach season (海水浴, kaisuiyoku) is concentrated in July and August, with organised beach stations (umino-ie, beach huts) providing infrastructure. Outside these months, most beach facilities close and many beaches are effectively unmanaged.

Okinawa Prefecture, the island chain stretching 1,000 kilometres southwest of the main islands toward Taiwan, has the best beach quality in Japan — coral sand, warm clear water, and a tropical marine environment — and receives the majority of Japanese beach tourism.

## 1. Emerald Beach, Okinawa (Motobu)

Emerald Beach at the Okinawa Churaumi Aquarium complex in Motobu is a constructed beach on the western coast of Okinawa main island. The name is accurate: the shallow water over the coral sand is a vivid green-blue. The beach is adjacent to the world's second-largest aquarium, making it a convenient combination visit. Managed access, lifeguards, and clean facilities make it one of the most practically useful beaches on the main island.

## 2. Kondoi Beach, Taketomi Island, Okinawa

Kondoi on Taketomi Island (accessible by ferry from Ishigaki in 10 minutes) is a shallow tidal flat beach famous for star-shaped foraminifera sand — the same organism that produces pink sand elsewhere, visible here as small five-pointed shapes in the pale sand. The water retreats hundreds of metres at low tide. Taketomi Island retains traditional Ryukyuan village architecture — red-tiled roofs, shisa guardian figures — that has been lost on most other Okinawan islands.

## 3. Hateno Beach, Ishigaki Island, Okinawa

Ishigaki Island has the highest concentration of high-quality snorkelling and beach in Okinawa. Kabira Bay, on the north coast, is one of the most photographed beaches in Japan — a bay of white sand islets with clear water — but swimming is prohibited due to strong currents. Hateno Beach on the northeast coast is swimmable with good reef snorkelling accessible directly from the sand.

## 4. Yonaguni Island, Okinawa

Yonaguni, the westernmost point of Japan — closer to Taiwan than to Tokyo — has a dramatic and wild character quite different from the tourist-developed Okinawa main island. The diving off Yonaguni is famous for the Yonaguni Monument, an underwater rock structure whose origin (natural or artificial) remains debated. The beaches on the north coast are small and rocky, backed by subtropical vegetation. Hammerhead sharks aggregate at the south cape from December through February.

## 5. Itoman Beach, Okinawa Main Island

The beaches around Itoman in southern Okinawa are less developed than the resort beaches of the northern main island and have better coral quality for snorkelling. The area is also the site of significant Second World War battlefield memorials — the Battle of Okinawa's final engagements were fought here.

## 6. Shirahama, Wakayama Prefecture

Shirahama on the Kii Peninsula of Honshu is one of the few genuinely sandy beaches on the Pacific coast of the main island. The white sand was, controversially, supplemented with sand brought from Australia to maintain the beach's famous pale colour after erosion depleted it. Shirahama has an onsen (hot spring) culture alongside its beach; the combination of ocean bathing and mineral spring soaking is a Shirahama tradition.

## 7. Kerama Islands, Okinawa

The Kerama Islands (Keramashoto National Park) 30 kilometres west of Okinawa main island are known among divers for "Kerama blue" — the specific clear, vivid blue of the water around the archipelago. The beach at Zamami Island's Furuzamami Beach has fine white sand and coral reef accessible directly from the shore. Humpback whales use the Kerama waters from January through March as a calving ground; whale watching from the islands is a known winter activity.

## 8. Shirahama Beach, Shirarahama, Wakayama

A second Shirahama location — Shirahama Beach on the Izu Peninsula in Shizuoka Prefecture, south of Tokyo — has white sand and views of Oshima Island. The Izu Peninsula coast is one of the most accessible Pacific coast beach areas from Tokyo; the Shimoda area at the peninsula's tip has a series of beaches with surf potential.

## 9. Ohama Beach, Okinawa

Ohama on the east coast of Okinawa main island is quieter than the developed west coast resorts and has the calm water character of the Pacific side. The beach faces the outer Pacific and the water clarity is excellent. Local character — fishing boats, rather than tourist infrastructure — gives it a different feel from the west coast resort strip.

## 10. Itaki Beach, Miyako Island, Okinawa

Miyako Island, south of Okinawa's main island, is considered by Japanese beach enthusiasts to have the best beaches in Japan. Yonaha Maehama Beach on Miyako is a 7-kilometre stretch of fine white sand — the longest in Okinawa. Itaki Beach nearby is less known and less crowded. The Miyako Sea water clarity consistently ranks among the highest measured in Japan.

## Planning beach visits in Japan

The Japanese beach season is defined by the sea-bathing period (kaisuiyoku season), which runs officially from July 1 through August 31. Outside these dates, the beach house structures (umino-ie) that provide food, changing facilities, and sunlounger hire are closed, and most public beaches have no facilities at all. Visiting in June or September means a beach with no infrastructure.

Jellyfish (kurage) are a significant nuisance at many Japanese beaches from late July through August. The species encountered are not dangerous (unlike the box jellyfish of tropical Australia) but produce painful stings. Many Japanese beaches post jellyfish warning flags and some suspend swimming during mass bloom events.

The Okinawa Islands are the exception to the two-month season: warm enough to swim from April through October, with the subtropical climate extending the beach window significantly. Okinawa's beaches are in a different climate zone from the main islands — planning an Okinawa beach trip in April or May avoids the peak August crowds while delivering swimmable sea temperatures.

Transport to Okinawa's outer islands — Ishigaki, Miyako, Iriomote — requires flights from Naha on Okinawa main island. The inner islands of the Kerama group are accessible by ferry from Naha in 35 to 70 minutes. Accommodation on the outer islands books out quickly for the July and August season; reservations three to four months in advance are standard practice.

International visitors to Japan's beaches face a practical consideration that domestic visitors do not: the beach season's compression into July and August coincides with the hottest and most humid period of the Japanese summer. Heat index temperatures in Tokyo in August regularly exceed 40 degrees Celsius; the coasts are more bearable but beach weather in the Tokyo region combines intense heat with high humidity that many visitors find exhausting. Okinawa's subtropical climate is different — consistently warm and less oppressively humid than the Kanto or Kansai regions in summer. For international visitors specifically planning a Japanese beach component, routing through Okinawa rather than the main island beaches typically produces a more comfortable experience.
