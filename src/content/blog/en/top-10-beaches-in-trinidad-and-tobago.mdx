Trinidad and Tobago are twin islands at the southeastern corner of the Caribbean, lying just off the coast of Venezuela's Paria Peninsula. Trinidad, the larger island, sits almost on the continental shelf and has a geology and ecology more closely related to mainland South America than to the oceanic Caribbean islands to the north — which is why it has exceptional biodiversity and also why its beaches, particularly on the north coast, are backed by dense tropical forest rather than the limestone formations of the island Caribbean. Tobago, the smaller island 30 kilometres to the northeast, is more typically Caribbean in character with turquoise water, reef systems, and the kind of calm, clear-water beaches the region is known for. Together they form one of the most diverse beach destinations in the Caribbean.

## 1. Tyrico Beach, Trinidad

Tyrico Beach sits on the north coast of Trinidad near the village of Tyrico Bay, east of Maracas Bay. The north coast road — one of the most scenic coastal drives in the Caribbean — winds along the edge of the Northern Range mountains above a series of bays and beaches including Maracas, Las Cuevas, and Tyrico. Tyrico is smaller and less developed than Maracas but has the same character: Atlantic-influenced surf breaking on dark sand backed by steep, densely forested mountains. The combination of powerful surf and tropical forest backdrop is unique to Trinidad's north coast. The drive from Port of Spain through the mountains to the north coast takes about 45 minutes.

## 2. Manzanilla Beach, Trinidad

Manzanilla Beach occupies a long stretch of Trinidad's east coast, facing the Atlantic Ocean rather than the Caribbean Sea. The beach is backed by one of the longest continuous belts of coconut palms in the Caribbean — the Manzanilla-Mayaro coconut estate stretches for tens of kilometres along the coast. The east coast beaches of Trinidad receive the full Atlantic swell and are not ideal for swimming but are extraordinary for walking: the scale of the palm canopy, the grey-green sea, and the absence of development create a genuinely wild landscape within a short drive of the capital. Leatherback turtles nest at Manzanilla and the adjacent Fishing Pond area.

## 3. Macqueripe Beach, Trinidad

Macqueripe is a small, sheltered cove on the northwestern peninsula of Trinidad, near the town of Chaguaramas. The bay is protected and the water calm compared to the north-coast Atlantic beaches, making it one of the better swimming beaches on Trinidad. Chaguaramas is a significant area historically: American military bases operated here from 1941, and the architecture and infrastructure of that era is still partially visible. The Defence Force Recreational Facility manages access to the beach. The Chaguaramas peninsula has also become a base for sailing and yachting, with several marinas and boatyards serving the Caribbean cruising circuit.

## 4. Chagville Beach, Trinidad

Chagville Beach sits on the western coast of Trinidad in the Chaguaramas area, facing the Gulf of Paria rather than the open sea. The Gulf of Paria is enclosed between Trinidad's western coast and the Venezuelan mainland, and the water here is calmer and warmer than on the north or east coasts. Chagville is a local beach used by families from Port of Spain and the western suburbs — a genuine neighbourhood beach where Trinidadian beach culture is on display: cricket games on the sand, curry-flavoured street food from mobile vendors, music from car speakers, and the particular sociability of Trinidadian public space.

## 5. Toco Beach, Trinidad

Toco is a small fishing village at the northeastern tip of Trinidad, where the Caribbean Sea meets the Atlantic Ocean at the Galera Point. Toco Beach is a local beach backed by the fishing community's infrastructure — boats pulled up on the sand, nets drying, the smell of the catch. The northeast corner of Trinidad is remote from Port of Spain by road, a drive of about two hours on winding roads through the Northern Range and along the north coast. The area is known for leatherback turtle nesting — Galera Point is within the important nesting zone — and for the seabird colonies on the offshore rocks. ## 6. Vessigny Beach, Trinidad

Vessigny Beach sits on the southwestern coast of Trinidad near the village of Vessels, in the area south of Point Fortin and the La Brea Pitch Lake. The Pitch Lake is one of the world's three natural deposits of asphalt, a lake of bubbling pitch covering about 40 hectares, and is genuinely extraordinary. Vessigny beach is a south-coast beach facing the Gulf of Paria — calmer than the north coast, with warm, shallow water. The beach is managed as a recreation site and is popular with families from the southwest oil-industry towns of Point Fortin and San Fernando.

## 7. Grand Tacaribe, Tobago

Grand Tacaribe is a beach on the Atlantic-facing northern coast of Tobago, accessible from the village of Roxborough on the windward side of the island. Tobago's windward coast receives the Atlantic swell that the Caribbean-facing leeward coast is sheltered from, and the beaches here have a different, wilder character: darker sand, stronger surf, and dense forest behind. Tobago's Main Ridge Forest Reserve — the oldest legally protected forest in the western hemisphere, established by the British in 1776 — lies immediately inland, and the combination of Atlantic coast and primary forest makes the windward coast one of the most naturally impressive stretches of coastline in the Caribbean.

## 8. Petit Tacaribe, Tobago

Petit Tacaribe — Little Tacaribe — lies adjacent to Grand Tacaribe on Tobago's windward coast and is smaller and somewhat more sheltered by the headland between them. The Atlantic coast of Tobago sees dramatically fewer visitors than the Caribbean leeward coast where the major resort beaches are concentrated, and both Tacaribe beaches retain a natural character that the well-trafficked Pigeon Point and Crown Point beaches have largely lost. The road along Tobago's windward coast provides access to several fishing villages including Speyside, the base for diving in the Tobago Marine Protected Area.

## 9. Pigeon Point, Tobago

Pigeon Point is Tobago's most famous beach and one of the most recognisable in the Caribbean — a long wooden jetty over turquoise shallow water, backed by palm trees, with the reef visible beneath a glass-bottomed kayak. The beach is on the Caribbean leeward coast near Crown Point International Airport, the entry point for most Tobago visitors, and is managed as a paid admission facility by the Tobago House of Assembly. The water is shallow, calm, and exceptionally clear. The offshore reef of Buccoo, visible from the beach, is part of the Buccoo Reef Marine Park established in 1973. Glass-bottomed boat tours from Pigeon Point to Buccoo Reef have operated since the 1960s.

## 10. Englishman's Bay, Tobago

Englishman's Bay sits on Tobago's northern leeward coast, accessible via the Windward Road from Scarborough. The bay is a calm cove backed by dense tropical forest, and the water here is exceptionally clear over a sandy bottom with reef formations accessible by snorkelling from shore. Englishman's Bay is one of the quietest of Tobago's accessible beaches — it has a car park and a small bar but no hotel development — and the drive to it from Scarborough through the forested hills of the northern Tobago passes through some of the finest secondary forest on the island. The adjacent Store Bay and Pigeon Point are ten kilometres back toward Crown Point.
