The gap between what people pack for the beach and what they actually use is wide. Overpacked beach bags are heavy, drain energy on the walk in, and result in gear buried under sand and forgotten. This checklist is organised by category and honest about what earns its space and what rarely does.

## Sun protection essentials

Sunscreen is non-negotiable. Broad-spectrum SPF 30 or higher applied 20 minutes before exposure and reapplied every two hours, or after every swim regardless of the clock. The 50-millilitre tube sufficient for a single person for a day in the sun is consistently undersized; bring more than you think you need.

A wide-brim hat with a brim of at least 7 centimetres provides meaningful UV protection to the face and neck that sunscreen alone does not replicate, particularly for children. UV-protective swimwear — rash guards and swim leggings — reduces the body area requiring sunscreen application and is particularly useful for children who resist being reapplied.

Sunglasses rated UV400 or CE-marked UV400 protect against both UVA and UVB. Cheaper unrated lenses darken the scene (causing pupils to dilate) without blocking UV, which may actually increase exposure compared to wearing nothing.

A beach umbrella or pop-up shade shelter earns its weight for anything longer than two hours. A standard 2-metre beach umbrella provides roughly 4 square metres of shadow at midday — enough for two adults to sit in full shade. Cantilever designs with a side pole rather than a central one are easier to sit under and less prone to flipping. Secure the pole in sand to at least 45 centimetres depth: an unsecured umbrella in a gust of wind becomes a projectile.

## Seating and comfort

A good beach mat or towel does double duty as a clean surface and drying cloth. Quick-dry microfibre towels pack small and dry faster between uses, though some people find the texture inferior to cotton terry. Bring one towel per person plus a spare.

Beach chairs vary enormously in portability and comfort. Lightweight low-slung chairs with a carry strap are the standard; the primary tradeoff is seat height (lower chairs are more stable on sand and easier to pack but harder to get out of). For all-day stays, chairs with a headrest and a side cup holder reduce fatigue significantly. Avoid chairs with hollow aluminium legs, which collect and retain sand.

## Swimming and water safety

For children, a well-fitting buoyancy aid (rather than inflatable arm bands, which can pop or slip off) provides more reliable flotation for non-swimmers in open water. Inflatable rings and floats are fine in pools and calm, shallow conditions but should not be used beyond the depth where a child can stand: offshore winds and rip currents can carry floating children offshore faster than adults can follow.

A waterproof dry bag for phones, wallets, and car keys costs very little and avoids the single most common expensive accident at the beach. Seal it with a second twist after the first roll to ensure the closure holds. Helmet clips to beach chair arms are the simplest way to keep the bag accessible without burying it.

Reef shoes protect feet on rocky shores and reef flats where sea urchins, sharp coral, and weever fish are present. In sandy conditions they are unnecessary weight; check the beach type before you pack them.

## Snorkelling gear

A well-fitting mask is the critical piece of snorkelling equipment. An ill-fitting mask that leaks ruins any snorkelling session. To test fit without water: place the mask against your face without putting the strap on and inhale gently through your nose; a correctly fitting mask will seal and stay in place. Prescription masks are available for swimmers who normally need glasses.

Full-face snorkel masks have become popular but have a documented design flaw: CO2 build-up inside the face space during sustained exertion is possible, and some reports associate this with shallow-water blackout. Traditional separate mask and snorkel remain the safer choice for anyone swimming beyond very calm, shallow conditions.

Fins add propulsion but make walking on land awkward. Split fins or short travel fins are easier to manage on shore and in rocks. For casual reef snorkelling they are optional; for stronger currents, they make a genuine difference.

## Families with young children

Sand toys — buckets, spades, moulds — are worth bringing for children under 10 and rarely used otherwise. Bring more buckets than spades (the spade is usually the point of conflict). A large ziplock bag for wet, sandy toys saves the car from a day's worth of beach sand.

A portable changing mat or thin foam mat allows sand-free nappy changes and dressing. A waterproof bag for wet swimwear keeps the car boot dry. A small first-aid kit covering sand in eyes, minor cuts, jellyfish sting treatment, and blister plasters handles the majority of common beach incidents.

A cooler or insulated bag for drinks is valuable when the beach has limited facilities or none. Hydration is easily forgotten at the beach: the wind and sun together accelerate dehydration without the sweat feeling hot the way it does inland.

## Electronics and cameras

Waterproof phone cases allow photography in and around the water, but note that most are not rated for submersion depth beyond around 1 metre. A separate waterproof action camera on a chest mount or selfie pole is a better choice for underwater use.

Portable speakers are common on beaches; in many countries there are local ordinances governing amplified sound in public beach areas. At beaches in national parks, sound is frequently prohibited. Check before bringing one.

A portable battery pack ensures that phones and cameras remain powered for a full day — especially relevant if you are using maps and navigation on remote beaches without easy transport.

## What to leave at home

Scented body products attract stinging insects. Aerosol sunscreens are convenient but wasteful and frequently end up coating neighbouring beachgoers. Large folding tables are rarely practical in sand. Glass containers are prohibited on most public beaches worldwide for good reason. Drones require permits or are prohibited outright at many coastal locations.

## The weight calculation

The practical constraint on beach gear is weight and carrying distance. A beach accessed by a 30-minute walk from the nearest parking requires a ruthlessly edited kit; a beach directly adjacent to car parking allows more flexibility. The single most common beach logistics error is carrying too much and arriving exhausted. Prioritise by asking which items genuinely change the beach experience (shade, sun protection, hydration) versus which are comfort additions that can be left out.

Umbrella plus chairs plus cooler plus children's toys plus beach tent plus snorkel gear can easily total 25 to 35 kilograms — manageable from a car boot to a beach 50 metres away, completely impractical as a walk-in kit. The compromise: on foot-access beaches, choose a beach tent-shelter (doubles as shade and wind protection, lighter than an umbrella-and-chair combination) and bring only the most used items, leaving the rest in the car for retrieval if needed.
