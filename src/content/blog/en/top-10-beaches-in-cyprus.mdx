Cyprus is a large island in the eastern Mediterranean — the third largest — with a beach coast on all sides and a climate that delivers reliably warm, dry summers from May through October. The island's beaches split broadly between the developed resort coast around Ayia Napa and Protaras in the east, Limassol in the south, and Paphos in the west, and the wilder beaches of the Akamas Peninsula in the northwest and the Karpaz (Karpas) Peninsula in the north.

The water temperature in the eastern Mediterranean is among the warmest in Europe — 26 to 28 degrees Celsius in August. Sand beaches are the norm rather than the exception, which distinguishes Cyprus from the pebble-and-rock coasts further northwest in the Adriatic.

## 1. Nissi Beach, Ayia Napa

Nissi Beach is a broad crescent of fine white sand with shallow water and a sandbar that extends toward a small island (the "Nissi" — island). The sandbar at low tide is walkable, creating an unusual and photogenic beach geometry. The beach is the most popular in Cyprus and has a full resort infrastructure: sunloungers, beach bars, and water sports. It is busy in peak season but the physical setting — fine sand, turquoise water — is genuinely good. Nissi is in the map database.

## 2. Fig Tree Bay, Protaras

Fig Tree Bay at Protaras is considered by many visitors the best beach in Cyprus for swimming: sheltered, calm, fine sand, and shallow water suitable for children. Named for a fig tree that no longer exists. The bay has consistent Blue Flag status and is backed by a promenade with restaurants. The beach is compact — less than 500 metres — but well maintained and functionally excellent.

## 3. Coral Bay, Paphos

Coral Bay is the main beach resort area north of Paphos, a long crescent of sand on the west coast with a calm sea and a full complement of water sports and restaurants. The water colour is a distinctive deep Mediterranean blue. Coral Bay is family-oriented, well organised, and busier than Paphos town beach. Coral Bay is in the database.

## 4. Aphrodite Hills Beach, Paphos

The beach below the Aphrodite Hills resort complex, south of Paphos, is a small cove with clear water at the base of a cliff accessible by path from the resort. It is generally quieter than the public Paphos beaches and the cliff setting is attractive. Swimming and snorkelling are good in calm conditions.

## 5. Lara Beach, Akamas Peninsula

Lara Beach in the Akamas Peninsula is a protected sea turtle nesting beach — loggerhead and green turtles nest on this remote northwest coast. The beach is accessible by four-wheel drive from the Cape Drepanum road. No sunloungers, no facilities. The beach has a wild, limestone-cliff character that the resort areas of Cyprus entirely lack. Nesting takes place from May through September; night visits require permits.

## 6. Petra tou Romiou, Pissouri

Petra tou Romiou (Rock of Aphrodite) is the legendary birthplace of Aphrodite — the sea-stacks and the beach below the rocks on the coastal road between Limassol and Paphos. The beach is pebble, the water clarity excellent, and the visual backdrop of the rock formations makes it one of the most photographically striking beaches on the island. Swimming around the sea stack in calm conditions is worth the cold water at the rock base.

## 7. Governor's Beach, Limassol

Governor's Beach south of Limassol is a series of small coves below low white chalk cliffs, with the dark pebble beach contrasting visually with the pale cliff. The white chalk dust in the water gives it a milky turquoise tint in shallow areas. This stretch of coast has a distinct character from the sandy beaches of the east. The cliffs and coves are easy to walk between at low tide.

## 8. Konnos Bay, Protaras

Konnos Bay is a sheltered cove south of Protaras with fine sand and calm, clear water. It is smaller than Fig Tree Bay but similar in quality, and it is backed by a national forest reserve of pine trees that provides shade. The combination of shade, calm water, and fine sand makes it one of the best family beaches on the island.

## 9. Paramali Beach, Limassol District

Paramali is a quiet village beach on the southern coast between Episkopi Bay and Paphos. The beach is wide, relatively uncrowded, and backed by low dunes. It has a local rather than resort character and minimal infrastructure, which makes it more pleasant for those avoiding the developed beach strip.

## 10. Dimex Beach, Ayia Napa

Dimex (also known as Macronissos Beach) is north of Ayia Napa, a long sandy beach that is less built-up than Nissi despite being close to the same resort cluster. The beach has Blue Flag status and consistent water quality. The combination of sand quality, water temperature, and access without the extreme congestion of Nissi makes it a practical alternative for those who want similar conditions with fewer people.

## Season and planning

Cyprus beaches are at their best from May through October. July and August are extremely hot inland but the coast is tempered by sea breezes. April and November have warm sea temperatures (23 to 25 degrees) and much lower visitor numbers. The shoulder season months — May, early June, September, October — offer the best balance of sea temperature, manageable crowds, and lower accommodation prices.

## The north coast and the Green Line

A significant portion of Cyprus's coastline is in the northern territory administered by the Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus (TRNC), accessible through crossing points across the UN buffer zone. The Karpaz Peninsula in the north has some of the island's least developed and most beautiful coastline — isolated sandy beaches on a long finger of land extending northeast. Nangomi Beach (Golden Beach) near the peninsula's tip is considered one of the finest beaches on the island, with fine golden sand, no development, and loggerhead turtle nesting.

Crossing from the Republic of Cyprus to the north is now straightforward for EU citizens and most international visitors; a vehicle can cross at the Ledra Palace or Agios Dometios crossings in Nicosia. Carrying sufficient cash is advisable in the north as card infrastructure varies. The beach quality in the Karpaz Peninsula justifies the crossing; Golden Beach alone is worth a day trip from anywhere in the east of the island.

## Getting around

Cyprus is a driving destination — public transport between beach areas is limited and a rental car is the practical transport solution for anyone planning to visit multiple beaches. Road infrastructure is good and driving is on the left (a British colonial inheritance). Paphos, Limassol, Ayia Napa, and Larnaca all have accommodation bases with good beach access within 30 minutes. Renting from Paphos Airport or Larnaca Airport and choosing a coastal base according to the beaches of priority is the standard approach.

The motorway connecting Nicosia, Limassol, Paphos, and Larnaca covers the southern coast efficiently. Ayia Napa is accessible from Larnaca airport in 45 minutes; the Akamas Peninsula from Paphos in about 40 minutes. The extreme ends of the beach circuit (Akamas to Karpaz) take nearly 3 hours to drive, which effectively means the island requires at least 5 to 7 days to cover all regions adequately.
