Tides are one of the most consequential variables for a beach visit and one of the least understood by most beachgoers. The difference between a high and low tide can range from a few centimetres at some locations to over 16 metres at others. Knowing the tidal range at your beach and whether the tide is coming in or going out can determine whether you have access to a beach at all, whether you can reach a tidal island, whether snorkelling conditions are good or poor, and in some cases whether you survive the visit.

## What causes tides

Tides are caused primarily by the gravitational pull of the Moon on the Earth's oceans, with a secondary contribution from the Sun. The Moon's gravitational force pulls ocean water slightly toward it, creating a bulge on the side of the Earth nearest the Moon. A corresponding bulge forms on the opposite side through inertial forces. As the Earth rotates, these bulges sweep across the ocean, creating the rising and falling water levels we experience as tides.

The Sun's gravitational pull is weaker but still significant. When the Moon and Sun align — at new moon and full moon — their gravitational effects combine to produce larger-than-average spring tides. When they are at right angles — at the quarter moons — they partially cancel, producing smaller neap tides. The spring-neap cycle repeats roughly every 14 days.

## Why tidal range varies

The tidal range — the difference between high and low water — varies enormously between locations, from nearly zero in some areas to extreme ranges in others. This variation is not primarily due to proximity to the Moon. It is due to the resonant properties of ocean basins and coastal configurations.

Some bodies of water have natural resonant periods that amplify tidal motion. The Bay of Fundy in Canada, which has a tidal range of up to 16 metres — the largest in the world — is shaped and sized in a way that the natural oscillation period of the bay (about 12.4 hours) matches the tidal forcing period exactly. The result is a resonance effect that dramatically amplifies the tidal range.

The Mediterranean Sea, by contrast, is a nearly enclosed basin that is too large and complex for strong tidal resonance. Mediterranean tides are typically 20 to 50 centimetres in range. Many Mediterranean beach visitors are unaware tides exist at all, because the practical effect of a 30-centimetre tidal change on a wide sandy beach is minimal.

The Iroise Sea off Brittany, the Bristol Channel in England, the Bay of Bengal, and Cook Inlet in Alaska all have large tidal ranges due to similar resonance effects in their specific geometries.

## When tides matter for beach visits

For beaches with small tidal ranges (most Mediterranean, Caribbean, and tropical island beaches), tides have minimal practical effect on the beach experience. The beach is approximately the same width at high and low tide; swimming conditions are similar throughout the cycle.

For beaches with moderate ranges (2 to 4 metres, common on the Atlantic coast of Europe and North America), tides matter significantly. A beach that has 50 metres of sand at low tide may have only 10 to 15 metres at high tide — or may be entirely submerged. Planning a full-day beach visit requires checking the local tide table.

For beaches with large tidal ranges (5+ metres, as in parts of Brittany, Wales, the UK's southwest, the Bay of Fundy, and Australia's north coast), tides can be life-critical. Tidal islands become accessible only at low tide and are cut off by rising water. Rock ledge fishing spots that are safe platforms at low tide are covered by fast-moving water at high tide. Walking along a tidal flat at low tide and being caught by the incoming tide in a funnel-shaped estuary is a recurring cause of death on UK and Breton coastlines.

## Tidal currents

The movement of water in and out with the tide creates tidal currents. These are strongest at mid-tide (when the water level is changing most rapidly), and weakest at slack water (just after high and low tide when the tide turns). Tidal currents are distinct from rip currents: they move in predictable directions aligned with the tidal flow rather than the irregular patterns of beach rip currents.

Strong tidal currents make swimming hazardous even when the sea surface looks calm. Tidal inlets, estuary mouths, and the channels between islands and headlands can have very strong tidal flow. In the Alderney Race between the Channel Islands and France, spring tidal currents reach 12 knots — fast enough to pull a swimmer underwater against their will.

## Hot Water Beach and Iztuzu examples

Hot Water Beach in New Zealand requires visitors to dig their thermal pools within 2 hours of low tide. Missing this window means digging into water that is too cold to be geothermal at depth. The tide table is the planning document for the visit.

Iztuzu Beach in Turkey, a turtle nesting beach, restricts beach access after 8pm in nesting season. The tidal range in the eastern Mediterranean is minimal, so this is a management rule rather than a tidal issue — but it illustrates that beach access rules can be independent of tide.

## Checking tide times

Tide tables are published annually by national hydrographic offices. For practical beach planning, tide prediction apps and websites — Tides.net, Tideschart.com, and the tide tools in general weather apps — provide accurate local predictions for any location. Always specify the exact beach location rather than a nearby named port, as tidal timing can vary by 20 to 30 minutes within a short stretch of coast depending on the local shoreline geometry.

## The two-tide and one-tide day

Most coastal locations experience two high tides and two low tides per day — a semidiurnal tidal pattern. The two tides in a day are not equal in height; one high tide is typically higher than the other, and one low tide lower. This is the mixed semidiurnal pattern common on most of the world's coasts.

Some locations experience a diurnal tidal pattern with only one high and one low tide per day. Parts of the Gulf of Mexico (including the Yucatan coast of Mexico and parts of the Gulf of Thailand) have predominantly diurnal tides. At these locations, the beach will be at high water for an entire half-day and at low water for the other half, rather than cycling through two tidal periods.

Understanding which pattern applies at your destination helps with planning. A beach that has been at high water all morning will still be at or near high water in the early afternoon at a diurnal location — there is no midday low tide to reveal the sand bank you expected to walk to.

## Tidal bores

A tidal bore is a specific tidal phenomenon that occurs in funnel-shaped estuaries with large tidal ranges: the incoming tide produces a wave that moves upstream against the river current. The Qiantang River bore in China — reaching over 9 metres in height during spring tides — is the world's most dramatic. The Severn Bore in England, the Turnagain Arm bore in Alaska, and the Tidal Bore on the Petitcodiac River in New Brunswick are all surf-able by experienced board and kayak riders.

Tidal bores are not beach phenomena in the usual sense, but they occur in estuarine systems connected to beaches at river mouths. The Qiantang bore attracts thousands of spectators to the river banks annually; the Severn Bore has a dedicated following of surfers who ride it from Newnham downstream toward Gloucester.

## Rip currents and tidal interaction

Tidal state affects rip current intensity. At low tide, rip currents at beach breaks are often more intense because the water depth in the surf zone is reduced and water flowing seaward through channels is concentrated. At mid-incoming tide, rip currents can be particularly strong as large volumes of water are moving across the beach face. Lifeguards adjust flag positioning as tide changes, which is why the flags move position through the day even on a beach without changing conditions.

The combination of a falling tide with a strong offshore wind can create surface currents moving away from the beach that are stronger than a swimmer can overcome. This combined tidal-wind current is most hazardous on beaches with a significant offshore wind component — prevalent on many west-facing beaches in the afternoon when sea breezes develop and shift.
