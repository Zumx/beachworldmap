Beaches are not permanent. They are temporary accumulations of sediment — sand, gravel, pebbles — held in dynamic equilibrium between the forces that deposit material and those that remove it. Every beach that exists today was not a beach ten thousand years ago, and a significant fraction of today's beaches will not exist in their current form one hundred years from now. This is partly natural process and partly the accelerated consequence of human modification of coastlines.

Understanding beach erosion is not just useful for coastal scientists. It directly affects where and how people can access coastline, how long coastal infrastructure will remain viable, and which beaches will still exist for future generations to visit.

## How beaches form and how they move

Beach sand arrives from three main sources: river systems carrying eroded rock material to the coast, cliff erosion that breaks adjacent headlands into sand-sized particles, and offshore seabed material transported onshore by wave action. Over geological timescales, these sources keep beaches in approximate equilibrium with erosive forces.

Waves do not simply erode beaches. They also deposit material. A beach is constantly moving: sand shifts onshore during calmer swells, shifts offshore during storms, and moves along the coast in the direction of the dominant longshore drift. A beach that appears static over a human lifetime is cycling material continuously. Some years the beach is wider; other years it narrows. A single severe storm can remove the sand equivalent of decades of accretion.

The equilibrium model breaks down when something alters the sediment budget. Remove a river's sediment supply (by damming it upstream), build a seawall that intercepts longshore drift, or stiffen a shoreline with hard engineering, and the beaches downstream begin to starve and erode without replacement.

## What humans have done to sediment budgets

River dams are the largest single human modification to coastal sediment budgets. A dam traps river sediment in its reservoir: the sediment never reaches the coast. This effect is visible at multiple Mediterranean beaches downstream of major dam projects. The Nile Delta has been eroding measurably since the completion of the Aswan High Dam in 1970 cut off the enormous sediment load that had maintained the delta's coastline for thousands of years.

Seawalls and revetments protect the land behind them but eliminate the beach in front. When a seawall is built at the shoreline, waves reflect off the wall rather than dissipating on a beach slope. The reflected energy scours the seabed in front of the wall, eventually deepening the water and causing the beach to disappear entirely. This process, known as passive erosion, has been documented at seawalled beaches in Florida, the English Channel, and the Baltic.

Urban development of coastal dunes removes the sediment reservoir that beaches draw on during storms. Dunes are not decorative: they are the material store that replenishes beaches after wave erosion. Remove dunes for carparks, hotels, and golf courses, and the beach's long-term sand budget is permanently diminished.

## Sea level rise and its effects

Sea level has been rising globally at an accelerating rate — approximately 3.7 millimetres per year at current measurements, with projections ranging from 0.5 to 2 metres of additional rise by 2100 depending on emissions trajectory. For beaches, the effect of sea level rise works through a relationship known as the Bruun Rule: for every unit of sea level rise, the shoreline retreats proportionally landward as the beach profile adjusts to the new water level.

The Bruun Rule is a simplification, and shoreline response to sea level rise varies enormously depending on beach sediment size, offshore depth profile, and local sediment supply. But the general principle is well-supported: beaches in areas of rising sea level will retreat unless they receive sufficient sediment to maintain their position.

## Beach nourishment as a response

The dominant management response to eroding beaches in developed coastlines is beach nourishment: pumping sand from offshore or distant sources onto the beach to replace what waves remove. The United States has the largest beach nourishment programme in the world, spending several hundred million dollars annually to maintain beaches along the Atlantic and Gulf coasts.

Nourishment works in the short term. A well-designed and well-executed nourishment project can maintain a beach for 5 to 15 years before another cycle of nourishment is needed. The problems are cost, environmental impact (the borrow areas where sand is taken from are marine habitats), and the reality that in areas of significant sea level rise, nourishment becomes a permanent and increasingly expensive requirement rather than a one-time fix.

## Which beaches are most at risk

Small sand cays — the low-lying coral island beaches of the Maldives, Kiribati, Tuvalu, and the Marshall Islands — are among the most vulnerable. Several of these islands are already losing beach area measurably and are at existential risk from continued sea level rise without significant adaptation.

Barrier island beaches on the Gulf and Atlantic coasts of the United States face a combination of sea level rise, reduced sediment supply, and increasing storm intensity. The Outer Banks of North Carolina, the Florida Keys, and the beaches of the Louisiana coast are among the most documented cases.

The beaches of Vietnam's central coast, including areas around Da Nang and Hoi An, have experienced severe erosion accelerated by reduced Mekong sediment loads from upstream damming and by sand mining directly from riverbeds.

## Sand mining: the least-discussed driver

Illegal and legal sand mining from beaches and riverine systems is a significant but underreported driver of coastal erosion globally. Sand is the world's most consumed natural resource after water — used primarily in concrete construction. The depletion of riverbeds and nearshore seabeds to supply construction removes the sediment that would otherwise maintain beaches.

Countries with rapid construction growth and insufficient regulation — several in Southeast Asia and West Africa — have experienced severe beach loss directly linked to sand mining. Indonesia's Riau Islands have lost numerous small islands entirely to sand dredging operations that supply Singapore's land reclamation projects. Vietnam's Cua Dai Beach (near Hoi An) has retreated dramatically partly from sand mining in the Thu Bon River system upstream.

Global sand supply chains are increasingly regulated in response to documented losses, but enforcement in regions with weak coastal governance is inconsistent. The connection between the beach you stand on and the sand budget that maintains it — which can be affected by decisions made hundreds of kilometres inland at a dam site or sand pit — is a useful frame for understanding why beaches that were wide and healthy a generation ago may be significantly narrower today.

## Artificial beach restoration methods

Beyond nourishment, two other management approaches are used at eroding beaches. Beach drainage systems (installed as perforated pipes below the sand surface) alter the groundwater dynamics of the beach face and can reduce erosion rates by changing how waves interact with the sand. These are experimental at most sites and require ongoing energy input.

Nearshore artificial reefs or submerged breakwaters can reduce wave energy reaching the beach and encourage sand accretion in their lee. Their ecological impact (they create artificial reef habitat, which can be positive for marine life, or they can alter currents in ways that create unexpected erosion elsewhere) is site-specific and the long-term results vary considerably.

None of these approaches solves the underlying problem of a coastline that has lost its natural sediment budget. Management of coastal erosion ultimately requires addressing the root causes — dam management, sand mining regulation, planned shoreline retreat in the most vulnerable areas — as well as the symptoms.
