Algeria has roughly 1,200 kilometres of Mediterranean coastline, and the stretch around Algiers — the Tell region — is among the most varied and scenically diverse. The capital itself sits on a bay, and the beaches extend both east and west from the city along a coast that alternates between sheltered sandy coves, rocky promontories, and open bay beaches. Despite Algeria being the largest country in Africa and the Mediterranean's largest coastline country, international beach tourism here is minimal, making it one of the genuine undiscovered beach coasts of the Mediterranean world. Algerians themselves take their beaches seriously: summer weekends see the Algiers coastal beaches packed with local families, and the culture of the summer beach day — late breakfasts, long swims, afternoon grillades of grilled meat and fish on the beach — is deeply embedded in urban Algerian life.

## 1. Plage Timallahine, Tipaza Province

Plage Timallahine sits west of Algiers in the Tipaza Province, a coastline that includes the significant UNESCO World Heritage Site of ancient Tipaza — a Roman town that overlooks the sea, its ruins scattered across a clifftop headland with mosaics, temples, and a basilica. Camus wrote about Tipaza in essays of luminous quality, and the combination of ancient ruins, Mediterranean light, and clean sandy beach makes the area genuinely exceptional. The beach at Timallahine is part of this coastal zone and has the clean Mediterranean water and pale sand characteristic of the sheltered Tipaza bay. The drive from Algiers takes about 70 kilometres west on the coastal road.

## 2. Plage El Ouardania, Algiers Wilaya

Plage El Ouardania sits in the western suburbs of Algiers, part of the beach zone that extends along the coastline west of the city centre toward Zeralda and beyond. The Algiers western beaches are backed by the residential and resort developments that grew rapidly in the post-independence period, and El Ouardania has a broad, sandy beach that serves the western suburbs. The Mediterranean here is calm, warm in summer, and the water quality has improved significantly over the past two decades following investment in wastewater treatment infrastructure.

## 3. Plage Chatt El Hilal, Algiers area

Plage Chatt El Hilal is one of several organised beach complexes along the Algiers coastal strip that provide managed facilities for local beach-goers. Algeria's beach infrastructure model often involves paid-entry organised beach complexes — called piscines or plages aménagées — alongside public open beaches. Chatt El Hilal has facilities including changing rooms, restaurants, and supervised swimming areas. The beach itself is sandy and the water clear. The organisational approach to beach access reflects the density of Algiers' urban population and the management challenge of accommodating the demand for coastal recreation in a city of several million people.

## 4. Paradis Plage, Tipaza Province

Paradis Plage — Paradise Beach — sits on the Tipaza coast west of Algiers and takes its optimistic name from the quality of the setting: a sandy cove with the turquoise Mediterranean water characteristic of the sheltered Tipaza bay. The beach has been developed as a resort facility, with accommodation and restaurant infrastructure, making it one of the more accessible organised beach experiences near Algiers for visitors who want to stay on the coast rather than day-trip from the city. The proximity to the ancient Roman ruins at Tipaza gives the area an additional layer of interest beyond the beach itself. ## 5. Kristel Stidia, Mostaganem Province

The Kristel and Stidia beaches are part of the Mostaganem coast, approximately 350 kilometres west of Algiers on the approach to Oran. The Mostaganem coastline is known for its quality Mediterranean beaches, and Kristel in particular has a well-established reputation among Algerians as one of the finer beaches on the western coast. Stidia is the broader beach resort town nearby. The Mostaganem region is also known for its wine production — Algeria was historically a major wine-producing country during the colonial period — and the combination of quality coastline, agricultural hinterland, and the historic city of Mostaganem makes the area worthwhile for more than beach time alone.

## 6. Plage du Puits, Algiers area

Plage du Puits — Well Beach — is one of the beaches in the extended eastern suburbs of Algiers, in the area beyond the city centre toward the airport and the eastern municipalities. The eastern Algiers coast has a somewhat different character from the western approaches: the landscape flattens out toward the Mitidja plain and the beaches here are backed by lower terrain. The beach is used by families from the eastern Algiers suburbs. The name suggests a historic freshwater well or spring near the beach — a practical feature that historically determined beach settlement locations on the North African coast.

## 7. Plage de Sidi Fredj, Algiers area

Sidi Fredj is a purpose-built resort peninsula west of Algiers, developed in the late 1980s as a tourism complex with hotels, a marina, restaurants, and a sandy beach. The marina at Sidi Fredj is the largest yacht harbour near Algiers and is used as a base for sailing excursions along the Algiers coast. The beach at Sidi Fredj faces south into the Bay of Algiers and is consequently sheltered from the northern swells that affect the more exposed beaches. The resort has a somewhat dated infrastructure reflecting its 1980s construction, but the quality of the beach itself — clean, sandy, and calm — remains good.

## 8. Plage Surcouf, Algiers area

Plage Surcouf sits in the eastern coastal suburbs of Algiers and takes its name from the French corsair Robert Surcouf, reflecting the area's colonial-era naming conventions. The beach is part of the continuous coastal access east of Algiers toward Boumerdès Province, where the coastline has a series of coves and sandy stretches that function as the eastern counterpart to the Zeralda-Tipaza beaches to the west. The eastern Algiers coast is slightly more sheltered from the dominant northwest swells than the open western beaches, and the coves here tend to be calm and suitable for family swimming.

## 9. Plage de Zéralda, Algiers area

Zéralda is a town about 25 kilometres west of Algiers that has become one of the most popular beach destinations accessible from the capital. The beach at Zéralda is long, broad, and sandy, with organised beach club facilities alongside open public access areas. Zéralda International airport — a secondary airport to Houari Boumediene — is nearby. The town has a growing residential population as Algiers' western suburbs expand, and the beach infrastructure has developed to match. The coastline between Zéralda and Tipaza is one of the most attractive sections of the greater Algiers coastal zone.

## 10. Plage Azur, Boumerdès Province

Plage Azur sits in the Boumerdès Province east of Algiers, in a coastal zone that has been developing for domestic tourism since the 1990s. The Boumerdès coast was significantly affected by the May 2003 earthquake, which caused severe damage to coastal towns; the rebuilding has created a more modern infrastructure but the beach character remains good. Plage Azur is a sandy beach with clean Mediterranean water, part of the string of beaches that line the Bay of Algiers east from the capital. The coast here faces north into the open Mediterranean, and summer afternoons see strong northwesterly breezes that provide relief from the intense inland heat.
