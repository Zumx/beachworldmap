The United Kingdom has over 11,000 miles of coastline and somewhere between 400 and 700 designated beaches depending on how you count them. The country's beach landscape is far more varied than its reputation for grey skies might suggest: Cornwall's clear turquoise water rivals the Mediterranean on a calm summer day, the Outer Hebrides has machair grassland meeting white shell-sand beaches that look more like the Maldives than northern Europe, and the chalk cliffs of Sussex have shaped how artists have depicted dramatic coastlines for centuries. The British seaside experience is particular, too — candyfloss, fish and chips, the cold shock of an unheated sea — and the ten beaches below represent both the scenic and the cultural best of what the UK coastline offers.

## 1. Blackpool Sands, Devon

Despite sharing a name with the famous Lancashire resort, Blackpool Sands in Devon is an entirely different proposition: a private, arc-shaped pebble-and-shingle beach near Dartmouth owned and managed by the Mercer family since the 1930s. It curves between two wooded headlands about four miles south of Dartmouth and faces south, giving it more sun hours than most Devon beaches. The water is notably clear, and the Venus Café at the back of the beach is a proper sit-down restaurant rather than a kiosk. The beach sits in a sheltered cove that blocks prevailing southwesterly swell, making it safe for families on days when nearby breaks are surfable.

## 2. Pentireglaze Haven, Cornwall

Pentireglaze Haven is a small sandy cove at the end of a footpath on the Pentire headland near Polzeath in north Cornwall. It is only accessible on foot, a roughly 15-minute walk from the nearest car park at Pentireglaze Farm, and sits below dramatic grassy headlands. The beach is small — perhaps 100 metres across at low tide — but the cliff scenery and relative solitude make it one of the finest short walks on the South West Coast Path. The water here faces directly into the Atlantic swell funnelling up the Camel Estuary and can be rough; the RNLI operates a seasonal patrol from nearby Polzeath.

## 3. Tregardock, Cornwall

Tregardock is one of the most remote beaches on the North Cornish coast, accessible via a steep descent across private farmland (a small fee is payable at the farm) followed by a scramble down cliff steps. The beach is large at low tide — nearly a kilometre of grey-gold sand backed by dramatic slate stacks — but disappears almost entirely at high tide. This tidal character means the beach is genuinely wild: no facilities, no lifeguard, and a strong need to monitor tide tables. The drive from Camelford takes about 20 minutes on narrow lanes; parking is limited at the farm.

## 4. Long Sands, Tynemouth

Long Sands at Tynemouth in Tyne and Wear stretches nearly a mile of solid pale sand backed by Victorian and Edwardian beach houses in various states of preservation. It faces northeast into the North Sea, which brings consistent surf when the right swell directions align, and Tynemouth has a functioning surf culture with several board hire and wetsuit rental operations. The beach is accessible by Metro directly from Newcastle city centre — around 20 minutes from Monument station — making it the most public-transport-accessible beach in the northeast of England. Despite the urban access, the water quality is consistently rated excellent.

## 5. Mellor Udrigle Beach, Scottish Highlands

Mellor Udrigle (sometimes spelled Mellon Udrigle) sits on the south shore of the Gruinard Bay in Ross-shire, Highland Scotland, and in summer it looks improbably tropical: white shell sand, shallow turquoise water, and mountains behind. It faces south, protected from the prevailing westerlies, and the sea temperature — while cold by any tropical standard — is warmer than most Highland beaches. The single-track road from Laide is narrow and takes about 15 minutes to drive. A small car park, a basic campsite, and toilets exist, but no café. The beach is backed by machair grassland dotted with wildflowers in June and July.

## 6. Whitesand Bay, Pembrokeshire

Whitesand Bay (Porth Mawr in Welsh) lies directly north of St Davids on the Pembrokeshire coast and stretches about a mile of golden sand facing almost due west into the Atlantic. It is a genuine surf beach, with the St Davids Surf School operating here seasonally, and the lifeguard service covers it from May to September. The car park is large and the beach has good facilities, but it is popular: arrive early in July and August. The St Davids Peninsula juts far enough into the Celtic Sea to catch solid Atlantic swells, and the surrounding Pembrokeshire Coast National Park provides exceptional cliff walking to Carn Llidi and Penberry.

## 7. Barafundle Bay, Pembrokeshire

Barafundle Bay is reached via a 15-minute clifftop walk from the car park at Stackpole Quay — there is no road access and no facilities at the beach itself. The bay is sheltered and the water a deep clear blue over pale sand. It faces south, so it receives the most sun of any Pembrokeshire bay, and the enclosing headlands block most wind directions. The National Trust manages the surrounding Stackpole Estate, and the clifftop walk passes through a landscape of limestone arches, blowhole formations, and nesting seabirds in spring. It was voted best beach in Wales multiple times and consistently features in UK-wide rankings. [Open the map](/map) to find the Stackpole Quay parking and the walking route.

## 8. Kynance Cove, Cornwall

Kynance Cove on the Lizard Peninsula in south Cornwall is one of the most striking coves in Britain, with stacks and islets of dark serpentine rock rising from white sand and clear green-blue water. The serpentine rock — a magnesium silicate with a distinctive mottled green and red pattern — is unique to the Lizard and has been used as an ornamental stone since the 18th century. The cove is entirely covered at high tide; the National Trust car park is at the top of the cliff, and the path down takes about ten minutes. A seasonal café operates from the base of the cliff. The beach faces southwest and catches the prevailing winds, making it surf-able in winter.

## 9. Filey Bay, North Yorkshire

Filey Bay stretches nearly six miles of pale sand from Filey Brigg — a distinctive rocky promontory — southward toward Speeton Cliffs. It is one of the largest beaches in the north of England by sand area and is backed for most of its length by low grassy cliffs rather than development. Filey itself is a Victorian seaside town with a working fishing heritage and an intact Georgian crescent overlooking the sea. The beach is accessible by road and train — Filey station is on the Scarborough-Hull line — and the Filey Brigg headland provides a fine walk with views north toward Scarborough Castle.

## 10. Camber Sands, East Sussex

Camber Sands is the only sand dune system in East Sussex, a low-slung stretch of pale blown sand south of the Romney Marsh, near the village of Camber. The beach is wide at low tide — sometimes extending 300 metres from dunes to sea — and the dunes behind reach several metres high, providing good windbreaks. Camber is popular with day-trippers from London, accessible in about 90 minutes via Ashford, and is one of the few beaches in the southeast of England with genuine surf on the right days. The village has a cluster of fish-and-chip shops, cafés, and seasonal car parks.
