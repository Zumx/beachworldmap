The Atlantic hurricane season officially runs from June 1 through November 30. The definition of "officially" matters here: hurricanes have formed outside those dates, and the vast majority of destructive storms occur in a much narrower window within the season. For beach travellers, understanding the actual statistical distribution of hurricane risk — rather than the worst-case scenario implied by the full six-month season — allows much better planning of Caribbean visits.

## The Statistical Peak

The Atlantic hurricane season is not uniformly risky across its six months. June and July are early-season months when sea surface temperatures in the Atlantic are still rising and the atmospheric conditions needed for storm formation are not yet consistently present. Storms in June and July are more likely to form in the Gulf of Mexico or the western Caribbean than in the deep Atlantic, and they tend to be shorter-lived than late-season storms.

September is statistically the most active month of the Atlantic hurricane season. September 10 is typically cited as the peak of the season, based on historical storm track data going back to the satellite era. The combination of peak Atlantic sea surface temperatures, the optimal position of the Intertropical Convergence Zone, and low wind shear conditions during September creates the most fertile environment for tropical cyclone development. October remains highly active, particularly for storms forming in the southern Caribbean.

The practical implication is that Caribbean beach travel in June, and to a lesser degree July, carries considerably lower risk than travel in September and October. Early December through May is the statistical dry season for hurricane formation, and December through April represents the period when Caribbean beach travel risk from tropical cyclones is closest to zero.

## Which Islands Have the Best Historical Protection

The Caribbean's hurricane tracks are not geographically random. Storms forming in the Atlantic generally travel from east to west on trade wind steering currents before curving northward as they interact with the subtropical ridge. This means the islands at lower latitudes receive more storm traffic than islands higher in the arc. However, elevation and terrain also matter significantly.

The southern Caribbean islands — Trinidad and Tobago, Aruba, Bonaire, and Curaçao — sit below the main hurricane tracks and have historically seen very few direct hits. Trinidad, despite being the southernmost major Caribbean island, last experienced a significant hurricane in 1933. Aruba receives so few storms that its tourism industry markets itself explicitly as being outside the hurricane belt. The geographical protection is real: the islands sit south of the 12th parallel, where steering currents tend to keep tropical systems moving west into the Caribbean Sea rather than north across the Lesser Antilles.

The islands most exposed to Atlantic hurricane tracks are the Northern Lesser Antilles — Barbuda, Antigua, St Martin, St Kitts, Dominica, Guadeloupe, and Martinique — and the Greater Antilles — Puerto Rico, the Dominican Republic, Haiti, Jamaica, and Cuba. These islands have all experienced Category 4 or 5 storms in the historical record, and some, like Barbuda (devastated by Hurricane Irma in September 2017) have experienced near-total destruction from a single event.

## A Month-by-Month Framework

June represents the opening of the official season, but statistically calm. The Dominican Republic, Jamaica, and Cuba have seen early-season storms, but major activity is uncommon. Beach travel throughout the Caribbean in June carries relatively low hurricane risk, though afternoon thunderstorms increase from the May dry season. This is also shoulder season pricing for many hotels.

July adds marginally more risk than June. Gulf of Mexico storms are possible. The Yucatan coast of Mexico, Belize, and Honduras are most exposed to Gulf formation activity. The Atlantic islands remain relatively safe statistically, and crowds are substantially lower than in the high winter season.

August is when genuine caution is warranted. Tropical waves crossing the Atlantic from the African coast begin to sustain storm development at significant rates, and major hurricanes have affected the Caribbean in late August. The Caribbean coast of Colombia and Venezuela remains largely outside the primary track. The Windward Islands and northern Lesser Antilles face elevated risk.

September is the highest-risk month and the peak of the season. Travel insurance that covers hurricane disruption is strongly recommended if visiting anywhere north of the ABCs (Aruba, Bonaire, Curaçao) during September. Monitoring NOAA's National Hurricane Center seven-day forecasts is essential.

October remains active, particularly in the southern Caribbean. Several devastating storms have tracked through the central Caribbean in October — Hurricane Mitch in 1998, which caused catastrophic flooding in Central America, formed in late October. Jamaica, Cuba, and the Cayman Islands face elevated October risk.

November sees sharp statistical decline but is not zero-risk. Late-season storms are typically less intense but can still be significant. The southern Caribbean ABCs remain the safest choice.

December through April is statistically the safest window, with January through March representing the driest and calmest beach travel conditions across most of the Caribbean. This is also peak season pricing.

## Beyond Hurricanes: The Trade Wind Factor

Even outside hurricane season, trade winds shape Caribbean beach conditions. From December through April, northeast trade winds blow consistently across the Caribbean, creating ideal conditions for sailing and wind sports on the exposed Atlantic sides of islands, but keeping sea conditions rough for swimming and snorkelling on those same beaches. The Caribbean (leeward) sides of islands are generally calmer for beach swimming in winter. Calm summer conditions on the windward Atlantic coasts of islands are paradoxically associated with the higher-risk hurricane months.
