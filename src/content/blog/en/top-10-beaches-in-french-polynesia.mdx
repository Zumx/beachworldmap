French Polynesia covers an ocean area larger than Europe, with 118 islands and atolls spread across five archipelagos: the Society Islands (Tahiti, Bora Bora, Moorea, Huahine), the Tuamotu Archipelago (Fakarava, Rangiroa), the Marquesas, the Austral Islands, and the Gambier Islands. The beach geography is fundamentally shaped by island type: high volcanic islands have barrier reefs and enclosed lagoons with calm, shallow water; low coral atolls have beaches directly on the ocean and reef passes with powerful current.

Access is primarily through the international airport at Tahiti (Faa'a), with domestic flights connecting the islands. Most visitors focus on the Society Islands; the Tuamotus and remoter archipelagos require longer planning.

## 1. Matira Beach, Bora Bora

Matira Beach on the southern tip of Bora Bora is the island's only genuine public beach and consistently cited as one of the best in French Polynesia. The sand is white and fine, the water over the lagoon floor is a brilliant turquoise, and the enclosing reef keeps wave action minimal. Mount Otemanu is visible on the horizon to the north. Matira is backed by simple food stalls and a few guesthouses — less resort-intensive than the luxury bungalow properties on the island's interior. Matira Beach is in the database.

## 2. Bora Bora Lagoon, Bora Bora

The enclosed lagoon of Bora Bora as a whole — not any specific beach — is arguably the defining French Polynesian experience. Snorkelling and dive sites include populations of lemon sharks and rays in the lagoon, and the coral gardens in the southern sections of the lagoon are healthy. Overwater bungalow guests at the major resorts (Four Seasons, St. Regis, Conrad) access the lagoon from private platforms.

## 3. Temae Beach, Moorea

Moorea, 17 kilometres northwest of Tahiti by ferry or small aircraft, has a cluster of beaches on its north coast near the Cook's Bay and Opunohu Bay complex. Temae Beach on the northeast coast is a long, palm-backed beach with lagoon water and a reef providing reasonable snorkelling. Moorea has a more local and less exclusive character than Bora Bora, with guesthouses and pensions alongside the international resorts.

## 4. Fakarava Lagoon, Tuamotu

Fakarava atoll is a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve 500 kilometres northeast of Tahiti. The atoll is one of the largest in French Polynesia; the South Pass (Passe Tumakohua) is one of the world's most famous dive sites for shark aggregations. The atoll's beaches are on the motu — the small sandy islets on the reef ring — and have the white sand, clear water, and coconut palm character of pure coral atoll environment. The dark sky above Fakarava on a moonless night is extraordinary.

## 5. Rangiroa Lagoon, Tuamotu

Rangiroa (Vast Sky) is the second largest coral atoll in the world. The lagoon is so large it is effectively an inland sea — Tahiti could fit inside it. The Blue Lagoon excursion from Avatoru village visits a part of the main lagoon where the water is a specific pale blue over white sand — one of the most photogenic single spots in French Polynesia. The Tiputa Pass at Rangiroa is the reference site in the world for dolphin encounters on dive trips.

## 6. Huahine, Society Islands

Huahine is sometimes called "the island that time forgot" for its relative lack of development compared to Bora Bora and Moorea. The island has vanilla farms, marae (ancient Polynesian temples), and several good beaches on the lagoon side. Anini Beach on the north coast of Huahine Nui is a calm, shaded beach with a local character.

## 7. Tikehau, Tuamotu

Tikehau atoll, near Rangiroa, has a reputation for having the highest density of fish in French Polynesia, documented by scientific surveys. The beaches on the motu fringing the lagoon are white coral sand with clear warm water. A small resort and pension accommodation operate on the main motu near the village.

## 8. Tahiti, Society Islands (Plage de PK18, Mahina)

Tahiti itself is often bypassed by tourists in favour of the outer islands, but the east coast beach at Mahina (PK18) near Papenoo has a broad black-sand beach — volcanic in origin — with consistent surf and a distinct character from the lagoon beaches of the outer islands. The black sand against the vivid blue Pacific is one of French Polynesia's more dramatic beach landscapes.

## 9. Maupiti, Society Islands

Maupiti is the westernmost inhabited Society Island, small and slow, with a lagoon similar to Bora Bora but far less developed. The motu Auira to the north of the main island has a long white sand beach facing the open lagoon. Access is by direct flight from Bora Bora or a cargo boat from Raiatea. The limited accommodation (a small number of family pensions) keeps visitor numbers low.

## 10. Moorea Lagoon, Moorea

The Moorea lagoon, particularly the northwestern section between Cook's Bay and the hotel zone, has good snorkelling on the coral and stingray and shark feeding tours are operated by dive shops. The Intercontinental resort on the northwest coast has a beach and lagoon area. The ferry from Tahiti takes 30 minutes and the ferry crossing itself offers views of both islands.

## Planning French Polynesia

The inter-island logistics of French Polynesia require more planning than most destinations. Air Tahiti operates the domestic air network connecting the Society Islands, Tuamotu, Marquesas, and Austral Islands. Flights between islands are short — 50 minutes from Tahiti to Bora Bora — but the frequency is limited on some routes, and missing a connection due to weather can strand travellers for 24 hours or more. Building buffer days into any multi-island itinerary is not paranoia; it is practical scheduling.

The most common mistake in planning a French Polynesian trip is underestimating the inter-island air costs. An Air Tahiti Discovery Pass covering multiple islands is available to international visitors and is substantially cheaper than booking individual segments. The pass requires advance booking and constrains flexibility, but the cost savings on a multi-island itinerary typically exceed the flexibility cost.

For first visits to French Polynesia, the conventional combination of Moorea and Bora Bora from Tahiti works well logistically and covers both the social, more accessible island (Moorea) and the iconic lagoon destination (Bora Bora). A one-week itinerary with three nights Moorea, three nights Bora Bora, and a night in Tahiti on arrival and departure is achievable and exposes visitors to the full range of Society Island character.

Adding a Tuamotu atoll — Fakarava or Tikehau — requires a second flight segment and is best for travellers with ten days or more. The Tuamotus are the destination for serious divers, for those who want genuinely remote atoll atmosphere, and for anyone whose interest extends beyond the overwater-bungalow resort model to the more austere beauty of a low coral atoll with limited services.

The Marquesas are French Polynesia's most culturally and historically significant archipelago — Nuku Hiva and Hiva Oa have Polynesian heritage sites, extraordinary mountain scenery, and a raw beauty that the tourist-oriented Society Islands have softened. They are not beach destinations in the traditional sense: the beaches are small and the surf is Atlantic-scale. The Marquesas reward travellers who want something other than a lagoon resort experience.
