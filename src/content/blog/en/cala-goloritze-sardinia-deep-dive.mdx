Cala Goloritzè is a small beach at the base of limestone cliffs in the Golfo di Orosei on Sardinia's east coast. It is a natural monument of the Italian Republic, a UNESCO World Heritage Site candidate as part of the Gennargentu and Golfo di Orosei National Park, and consistently ranked among the most beautiful beaches in the Mediterranean. Access is either by boat from Arbatax or Santa Maria Navarrese, or by a hiking descent from the plateau above. There is no road.

The combination of access difficulty, geological drama, and water quality places it in a category of beach that has almost no European equivalent. It is one of those places where photographs taken by competent photographers reproduce what the eye sees with reasonable accuracy, which is rarer than it sounds.

## The geology

The Golfo di Orosei is a 40-kilometre arc of limestone coast with no road access for most of its length. The rock is Jurassic limestone, eroded by millennia of wave action and fresh water infiltration into a series of sea caves, sea arches, and coastal towers. The most distinctive feature at Cala Goloritzè is the pinnacle of the Aguglia — a limestone needle rising roughly 150 metres from the sea adjacent to the beach, one of the most famous rock climbing routes in Italy (6b maximum grade on the southwest face).

The sand at Cala Goloritzè is white pebbles and coarse gravel, not fine sand. The water begins shallow near the shore and drops to considerable depth within a short distance as the limestone cliff base continues underwater. The colour — produced by the combination of pale pebbles, limestone walls, and the depth gradients of the Tyrrhenian Sea — moves through a range of blues and greens that changes with sun angle through the day.

## How to reach it

The boat approach from Santa Maria Navarrese (25 minutes) or Arbatax (45 minutes) is the most common and the most straightforward. Multiple operators run day trips from both ports from June through September. The standard day trip includes Cala Goloritzè alongside one or two other Gulf coves — Cala Biriola and Cala Mariolu are the most common alternatives. Arrive early: the cove gets busier from 10am and the boat tour operators concentrate arrivals mid-morning.

The hiking descent from the plateau of Baunei takes approximately 2.5 to 3 hours one way. The trailhead is accessed by car from the village of Baunei on the SS125 road. The descent loses roughly 500 metres of altitude over 7 kilometres of trail through garrigue scrub and limestone rock. The trail is well-marked but demanding underfoot; solid footwear, 3 litres of water per person, and sun protection are essential. Return is the same route uphill, adding 3 to 4 hours. The Park authority permits are required for the hike and must be purchased in advance through the Baunei municipality website.

The combination route — hike down in the morning, return by boat in the afternoon — is possible by pre-arrangement with boat operators.

## What is at the beach

There is nothing at Cala Goloritzè except the beach, the rock, and the sea. No café, no sunlounger hire, no toilets (though a basic composting facility was added in recent years). Bring everything you need: food, water, shade. A beach mat or portable seating is advisable since the pebbles make lying directly on the ground uncomfortable.

The sea cave to the north of the beach is accessible by swimming. The cliff base has snorkelling and, for divers, the underwater rock formations continue the above-water drama. The clarity of the water here is exceptional by Mediterranean standards: visibility of 20 to 30 metres is common.

The beach takes roughly 100 to 150 people at the peak of the day before it feels genuinely crowded. Given the access restrictions this is occasionally approached in August but rarely exceeded.

## When to visit

June and September are the optimal months for Cala Goloritzè. The sea temperature is comfortable for swimming (22 to 26 degrees Celsius), the tourist volume is below the August peak, and the boat services operate at full frequency. July and especially August are busiest; the parking at the Baunei trailhead fills before 7am in August.

May and October are possible but the boat services reduce frequency and the water is cooler. Winter visits are possible by the hiking route only, and the descent is for experienced walkers comfortable with variable weather.

## The wider Golfo di Orosei

Cala Goloritzè is the most famous beach on the Gulf but not the only one of exceptional quality. Cala Mariolu, with white marble pebbles and electric-blue water, is frequently cited alongside it. Cala Sisine, accessible only by boat or a 4-hour hike, has fresh water from a spring feeding a stream behind the beach. Cala Luna has a sea cave 20 metres wide that can be entered by boat. Planning a boat day that includes two or three of these beaches, rather than focusing solely on Goloritzè, makes the logistics of the boat hire considerably more worthwhile.

## Rock climbing the Aguglia

The Aguglia limestone pinnacle adjacent to the beach is one of the most distinctive sport climbing routes in Italy. The southwest face carries a multi-pitch route with a maximum grade of 6b that attracts technical climbers throughout the season. The experience of climbing 150 metres above the turquoise sea and beach below, with the boat traffic moving through the gulf and the Gennargentu massif visible inland, is cited by climbers as among the most dramatic settings for a sea cliff route in Europe. The route requires appropriate multi-pitch climbing competence and gear; guided climbing days from Baunei are available through local guides.

The view of the Aguglia from the beach below — the pinnacle rising straight from the sea, occasionally with climbers visible on the face — is one of the more unusual sights at any beach in the Mediterranean.

## Practical logistics in detail

Boat tours from Santa Maria Navarrese: depart typically at 9am and 10am, return by 5pm. Book in advance in July and August — capacity is limited by national park access permits and tour operators reach their allocation by the time of popular sailing. Several operators offer a private hire option (the full boat for a group) that allows more flexible timing and a longer stay at individual beaches.

For the hiking route: park access fees (payable online via the Baunei municipality) are approximately 3 to 5 euros per person. The trailhead parking area at Baunei costs a separate fee in peak season and fills early. The 7-kilometre trail each way takes experienced walkers 2.5 hours descending (it is steep and rocky) and 3.5 hours ascending (more demanding in the afternoon heat). Start no later than 7am in summer to be at the beach before 10am and have time to swim before the return climb begins.

Accommodation nearest to the access points: Santa Maria Navarrese, Baunei, and Tortolì (where the port of Arbatax is located) all have accommodation. Baunei is the most convenient for the hiking option and has character as a traditional Barbagia village. The Arbatax resort area to the south has more hotel infrastructure but is further from the hiking trailhead.
