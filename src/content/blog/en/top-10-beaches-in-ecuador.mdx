Ecuador has approximately 640 kilometres of Pacific coastline, running from the Colombian border in the north to the Peruvian border in the south. The coast is shaped by a distinctive geographic phenomenon: the meeting of the cold Humboldt Current from the south and the warm Panama Current from the north creates a zone of ecological transition roughly at the latitude of Manta, which divides the coast into a drier, cactus-scrub north and a somewhat more arid south. The country's main beach towns — Salinas, Montañita, Canoa, Manta — are well established with Ecuadorian domestic tourism but largely undiscovered by the international beach travel market. The combination of Pacific surf, whale-watching season from June to October, and genuinely affordable local seafood makes Ecuador's coast one of the best-value beach destinations in South America.

## 1. Playa de las Ranas, Santa Elena Province

Playa de las Ranas — Beach of the Frogs — sits in the Santa Elena Peninsula, the broad westward-pointing cape at Ecuador's coastal elbow near the city of Salinas. The Santa Elena Peninsula is the driest part of Ecuador's coast, geographically and climatically reminiscent of the Peruvian desert coast rather than the tropical north. The beach is a local strand within the greater Salinas conurbation, part of the continuous coastal development that has grown along the Santa Elena coast. The name likely derives from a seasonal frog migration that historically occurred in the area when the occasional winter rains filled temporary pools near the shore.

## 2. Playa La Unión, Manabí Province

Playa La Unión sits in the Manabí Province, the central coastal province that contains the city of Manta — Ecuador's major Pacific port and tuna fishing capital. The Manabí coast has a mix of working fishing ports, beach towns, and stretches of undeveloped shore. La Unión is characteristic of the Manabí coast at its quieter end: a beach with local fishing activity, modest infrastructure, and the warm Pacific water of the northern zone where the Humboldt Current's influence is reduced. Manabí's beaches receive consistent Pacific swells from May to November and are better known to Ecuadorian surfers than to the international market.

## 3. Playa de los Monos, Manabí Province

Playa de los Monos — Monkey Beach — is a beach in the greater Pedernales area of northern Manabí, named presumably for the howler monkey populations in the adjacent dry coastal forest that extends to the clifftops above the shore. The northern Manabí coast between Pedernales and the Esmeraldas border has a number of beaches with this character: backed by forested cliffs and dry shrub vegetation, accessible via unpaved roads, and seeing minimal tourist development compared to the busier beaches around Manta and Montañita to the south. Pedernales was significantly affected by the April 2016 earthquake and has been rebuilding its coastal infrastructure.

## 4. Playa Ayampe, Manabí Province

Playa Ayampe sits at the southern end of Manabí Province, just north of the border with Santa Elena, and has developed quietly into one of the most appealing smaller beach communities on the Ecuadorian coast. The village of Ayampe is small — a few dozen houses, a handful of surf lodges and restaurants — and the beach is backed by the Ayampe River estuary, which creates a freshwater bathing option alongside the Pacific. The surf here is consistent, left-hand beach break, and the adjacent Machalilla National Park territory to the north (which contains Playa Los Frailes) keeps the surrounding landscape largely undeveloped.

## 5. Playa Los Frailes, Manabí Province

Playa Los Frailes is inside the Machalilla National Park, the only coastal and marine national park on Ecuador's mainland. Access requires paying a park entry fee and walking about two kilometres on a clearly marked footpath from the park entrance, which keeps the beach from becoming overcrowded. The beach is the finest in the park: a curved arc of pale sand in a sheltered bay, with exceptionally clear water and reef formations accessible by snorkelling offshore. The national park also protects the Isla de la Plata, an offshore island accessible by boat from Puerto López, where frigatebirds, boobies, and nesting albatrosses can be observed and humpback whales are seen from June to October.

## 6. Playa Montañita, Santa Elena Province

Montañita is Ecuador's most internationally famous surf beach, a small town at the northern tip of the Santa Elena Peninsula that has grown from a local surfing spot into a full-scale backpacker resort over the past 25 years. The surf break — a consistent right-hand point break — is excellent by South American standards, and the town around it has the characteristic surf-bohemian infrastructure: hostels, surf schools, late-night bars, beach restaurants, and a population mix of Ecuadorians, Colombians, Argentines, Europeans, and North Americans. The beach itself is a long grey-sand Pacific strand, and the surf conditions are best from December through May. [Open the map](/map) to find Montañita on the Santa Elena Peninsula coast.

## 7. Playa Salinas, Santa Elena Province

Salinas is Ecuador's primary resort city and the endpoint of the coast road from Guayaquil, about 100 kilometres west. The main Salinas beach — Malecón de Salinas — is a broad boulevard beach backed by a developed promenade and a high-rise hotel strip. The beach itself is clean and well maintained, the water warm and generally calm at this sheltered end of the peninsula. Salinas has the infrastructure and beach-resort feel of a proper seaside city: seafood restaurants, ice cream vendors, beach umbrella and sunbed rental, and the concentrated summer activity of Ecuador's beach-going middle class. The town is also a base for deep-sea fishing charters.

## 8. Playa Canoa, Manabí Province

Canoa is a beach town on the northern Manabí coast that has been developing as a surf destination since the 1990s. The beach is long — several kilometres of dark grey-brown Pacific sand — and the village has a relaxed backpacker atmosphere with hostels, cheap seafood, and a more authentic Ecuadorian coastal feel than the more resort-developed Salinas and Montañita. The surf here is beach break, best in the wet season from December to May when the Pacific swells are most consistent. The Canoa area was also significantly affected by the 2016 earthquake and several of the older beachfront buildings were rebuilt in the following years.

## 9. Playa Olón, Santa Elena Province

Playa Olón sits just north of Montañita on the Santa Elena Peninsula coast and is somewhat quieter and more family-oriented than its famously rowdy southern neighbour. The beach is long, the surf moderate, and the village has a calmer character that draws visitors who want the Pacific coast scenery without the Montañita nightlife. Olón's church — the Santuario de la Virgen de la Roca, built into a rocky promontory above the beach — is a local landmark, and the bluffs above the beach provide good views north along the coast toward the Machalilla National Park boundary.

## 10. Playa Tonchigüe, Esmeraldas Province

Playa Tonchigüe occupies the Esmeraldas Province coast in the far northwest of Ecuador, north of the town of Atacames. The Esmeraldas Province is Ecuador's most Afro-Ecuadorian region, and the beach culture here — music, food, the atmosphere of the coastal towns — reflects the strong West African cultural heritage of the population. The beach is long and sandy, and the town of Atacames nearby is the most developed beach resort in the province. Tonchigüe itself is quieter than Atacames, with a fishing-community character alongside the beach. The Esmeraldas coast receives more rainfall than the coast to the south, keeping the vegetation green and the coconut palm groves dense.
