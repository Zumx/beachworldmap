California's Pacific coastline runs approximately 1,350 kilometres from the Oregon border to the Mexican frontier at San Diego, making it the longest state coastline on the US Pacific shore. The California Current sweeps cold water south from Alaska, keeping most of the coast brisk by global beach standards — water temperatures rarely exceed 19°C north of Point Conception, and even in San Diego the August average is around 20°C. This distinguishes California's beaches from their Atlantic Gulf counterparts: swimming is a serious proposition, wetsuits are standard, and the beach culture historically centred as much on surfing, volleyball, and running as on passive sunbathing. The coast divides broadly at Point Conception: north of the point, rugged Big Sur-style cliffs and fog-shrouded strands; south, the warmer, more urban beaches of Los Angeles and San Diego counties. The Channel Islands off Santa Barbara and the kelp forests off Monterey add world-class underwater dimensions to a coast that, on the surface, ranges from crowded urban recreation at Venice Beach to complete solitude at lost beaches within the Lost Coast State Marine Reserve.

## 1. Coronado Beach, Coronado, San Diego

Coronado Beach, fronting the Coronado peninsula across San Diego Bay from downtown, is consistently ranked among the finest urban beaches in the United States. The sand is extraordinarily wide — 90 metres at the northern section — and an unusual orange-gold colour produced by heavy-mineral deposits in the source sediment. The Hotel del Coronado, a Victorian wooden grand hotel opened in 1888 and a National Historic Landmark, frames the northern end with its red-turreted silhouette. Water here is warmer than most of the San Diego coast due to the peninsula's sheltered orientation. The beach is clean, lifeguarded year-round, and connects to a flat cycling and walking path along the Silver Strand spit southward.

## 2. La Jolla Cove, La Jolla, San Diego

La Jolla Cove is a small, protected cove flanked by sea caves in the sandstone bluffs north of the village centre, within the San Diego-La Jolla Underwater Park — a marine protected area where fishing, collecting, and disturbing marine life are prohibited. The result is a snorkelling environment of extraordinary richness: bright orange Garibaldi (California's state marine fish), leopard sharks, octopus, and sea bass in water rarely deeper than six metres near the cove. California sea lions occupy the rocks to the north of the cave, and brown pelicans nest on the adjacent bluffs. The cove is tiny — no more than 30 metres wide — and crowded in summer, but the marine life more than compensates.

## 3. Malibu Beach, Malibu

Malibu Beach in Los Angeles County is inseparable from the mythology of California surf culture. Surfrider Beach, the primary surf break at the eastern end of the Malibu coastline near the pier, produced the Malibu style of surfing in the 1950s — long-board noseriding on a perfect right-hand point break over a rock and sand bottom. The beach itself is a long, flat sand strip backed by Pacific Coast Highway, with the Santa Monica Mountains rising steeply behind. The break is a World Surfing Reserve. Leo Carrillo State Park at the western end of Malibu provides camping and one of the quieter beach settings in Los Angeles County.

## 4. Zuma Beach, Malibu

Zuma Beach is Los Angeles County's largest public beach, stretching about three kilometres in a wide flat arc of sand at the northern end of Malibu. Without a point break or notable surf feature, Zuma functions as a family and recreational beach par excellence — the parking lot holds 2,000 cars, multiple volleyball courts operate seasonally, and year-round lifeguard coverage makes it genuinely safe for swimming in water that can be rough by Southern California standards. The Santa Monica Mountains loom immediately behind, and on smog-free days the view of the Channel Islands — Anacapa, Santa Cruz, Santa Rosa — across the Santa Barbara Channel is striking.

## 5. Santa Barbara East Beach, Santa Barbara

Santa Barbara East Beach is the primary beach of what is arguably the most graceful small city on the California coast. The beach runs east from Stearns Wharf, a Victorian-era pier, along a palm-lined promenade backed by the Cabrillo Pavilion, a 1927 bathhouse that now functions as a recreation centre. The Santa Ynez Mountains rise abruptly behind the city, and the Channel Islands lie 40 kilometres offshore — on clear days they form a visible archipelago horizon. Water is slightly warmer here than in Los Angeles, and fog burns off earlier. The Sunday arts and crafts market at East Beach has operated for decades and gives the beach a leisurely, civilised character.

## 6. Pismo Beach, San Luis Obispo County

Pismo Beach is a classic Central Coast resort town whose namesake beach runs flat and wide for 35 kilometres along a protected section of coast known as the Pismo Dunes. Vehicle access on the beach — a California tradition at specific designated areas — is possible through the southern Oceano Dunes State Vehicular Recreation Area. The beach takes its name from the Pismo clam, once so abundant it could be harvested by the bucketful by wading; today strict limits protect the recovering population. Monarch butterflies overwinter in the eucalyptus grove at Pismo Beach State Park from October through February, sometimes in clusters of hundreds of thousands.

## 7. Carmel Beach, Carmel-by-the-Sea

Carmel Beach at the foot of Ocean Avenue in Carmel-by-the-Sea is a white-sand crescent of remarkable natural beauty set against a backdrop of Monterey cypress and backed by the Santa Lucia foothills. The sand is fine and pure, the water cold and clear (13–15°C year-round), and the beach is leash-free for dogs — which gives weekend mornings a joyful, unruly quality. Surfing is popular at the south end near Carmel Point. The Pebble Beach golf course occupies the headland to the north, and the Point Lobos State Natural Reserve immediately south is one of the finest marine nature reserves on the US west coast.

## 8. Baker Beach, San Francisco

Baker Beach is a stretch of sand directly below the Marin Headlands cliffs in San Francisco's Presidio, with an unobstructed view of the Golden Gate Bridge from its northern end — arguably the most dramatic backdrop of any urban beach in the world. The beach is 1.6 kilometres long, backed by the steep Presidio forest, and faces the strong tidal currents of the Golden Gate directly. Swimming is not advised due to rip currents and cold water (12–14°C year-round), but the beach is a major draw for walking, photography, and sunbathing on the rare fog-free summer days. The northern section is clothing-optional by tradition.

## 9. Stinson Beach, Marin County

Stinson Beach in Marin County, across the Golden Gate from San Francisco, is the Bay Area's primary warm-weather swimming beach, reaching water temperatures of 17–18°C in late summer — warmer than any San Francisco beach. The beach is within the Golden Gate National Recreation Area, stretching over a kilometre with a village of restaurants and guesthouses behind. To reach it requires driving or busing over the Mount Tamalpais ridge on Highway 1, which limits the crowds somewhat. The adjacent Bolinas Lagoon supports harbour seals and shorebirds, and the town of Bolinas — marked by residents who repeatedly remove the highway signage — retains its counter-culture independence.

## 10. Crystal Cove, Laguna Beach

Crystal Cove State Park between Newport Beach and Laguna Beach preserves 3.5 kilometres of undeveloped Orange County coast within a rapidly urbanised region. The park contains a 1930s-era beach colony of wooden cottages — 46 structures on the National Register of Historic Places — some of which are now available as rental accommodation managed by the state park foundation. The beach has rocky reefs for snorkelling, a sandy section for conventional bathing, and the El Moro Canyon trail network inland. Water is among the warmest in Southern California in summer, and the combination of historic buildings, natural landscape, and accessible surf makes it the most varied beach park in Orange County.
