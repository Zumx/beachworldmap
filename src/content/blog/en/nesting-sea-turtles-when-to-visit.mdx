Sea turtles are ancient: their lineage is 220 million years old, predating the dinosaurs. Seven species of sea turtle exist today, and all are either vulnerable, endangered, or critically endangered. Their beach nesting behaviour — females returning to the exact beach where they were born to lay eggs, guided by magnetic sensing of the Earth's field — is one of the most remarkable phenomena in natural history. Witnessing it at a well-managed beach is one of the more profound wildlife experiences available to beach visitors.

The timing, location, and method of access matter both for the viewing experience and for the turtles themselves. Poorly managed turtle watching can disrupt nesting, cause females to abort their nesting attempt and return to sea without laying (which has fitness costs), and damage eggs through light pollution and foot traffic.

## Loggerhead turtles: Zakynthos, Greece

The Ionian island of Zakynthos is home to one of the most important loggerhead sea turtle (Caretta caretta) nesting beaches in the Mediterranean. Laganas Bay on the south coast is the primary nesting area, protected as the National Marine Park of Zakynthos. Nesting season runs from May through August; hatching is from July through October.

Night tours to observe nesting and hatching are operated by the park authority and certified local operators. Groups are small and guided by rangers who enforce a strict protocol: no white lights, maintain required distance from nesting females, no photographs with flash, follow ranger instructions without exception. The experience is controlled and the viewing is meaningful.

## Leatherback turtles: Trinidad

Trinidad has some of the most important leatherback nesting beaches in the world. Grande Rivière, a village on Trinidad's north coast, receives hundreds of leatherback females per night in peak season (March through July). The leatherback is the world's largest turtle — females can weigh 600 kilograms — and the nesting process is correspondingly dramatic.

Nesting watching tours from Grande Rivière are guided, operating after dark from the beach hotels. The scale of nesting here — which can involve females nesting so close together they disturb each other — is unlike anything in the more managed Caribbean sites.

## Green turtles: Tortuguero, Costa Rica

Tortuguero National Park on Costa Rica's Caribbean coast is the most important green turtle nesting site in the Western Hemisphere. The remote beach — accessible only by boat or small aircraft from San José or the Caribbean coast towns — receives thousands of green turtles from July through October. Lesser numbers of leatherback and hawksbill turtles also nest here.

The park enforces strict viewing protocols: guided tours only, no white lights within 50 metres of nesting areas, maximum group sizes, no physical contact with turtles or hatchlings. The town of Tortuguero has a small network of eco-lodges and the experience is one of Central America's most celebrated wildlife encounters.

## Hawksbill turtles: Cousin Island, Seychelles

Cousin Island Special Reserve, managed by Nature Seychelles, is a small island in the inner Seychelles that protects one of the Indian Ocean's most important hawksbill nesting populations. Day visits to the island are by guided tour only and include turtle viewing when season permits (nesting is year-round for hawksbills, with peak periods). The island also protects nesting seabirds in extraordinary densities. Tours depart from Praslin.

## Olive ridley turtles: Odisha, India

The Gahirmatha Marine Sanctuary in Odisha on India's Bay of Bengal coast is the site of the world's largest mass-nesting event in biology: the olive ridley turtle's arribada. Hundreds of thousands of female olive ridleys arrive simultaneously at Nasi Island to nest over a period of days. The 2024 season saw an estimated 650,000 turtles in a single event. Witnessing an arribada requires advance permitting, travel to a remote part of Odisha, and acceptance that access may be denied at short notice for conservation reasons.

## Flatback turtles: Crab Island, Australia

Crab Island off Queensland's Cape York Peninsula is a significant flatback turtle nesting site accessible only by charter aircraft or vessel from Weipa. The flatback turtle is Australia's endemic turtle species and nests exclusively in Australian waters. Ranger-accompanied visits to Crab Island are the most strictly controlled turtle experience on this list — visitor numbers are minimal and the experience is genuinely remote.

## Green turtles: Ras al-Jinz, Oman

Ras al-Jinz on Oman's eastern cape is the Arabian Peninsula's most important green turtle nesting site and one of the most organised turtle-watching programmes in Asia. Female green turtles arrive each night from May through October; the scientific reserve at Ras al-Jinz has a visitor centre, ranger-guided tours that depart at scheduled times after dark, and a strict protocol of group sizes and behaviour. The combination of accessible logistics, good facilities, and reliable sightings makes Ras al-Jinz one of the most rewarding turtle experiences for visitors who want certainty over adventure.

## Additional sites worth noting

Punaluu Black Sand Beach in Hawaii has green turtle haul-outs rather than nesting — turtles rest on the black volcanic sand in the daytime and are reliably present most days throughout the year. The green turtles at Punaluu are a distinct Hawaiian green turtle subpopulation protected under the US Endangered Species Act; observation protocol requires maintaining a 2-metre distance without active enforcement, which the local honu (turtle) community maintains effectively through social pressure.

Watamu Marine National Park in Kenya protects green and hawksbill turtle nesting on the Kenyan coast (October through March is peak nesting). The Local Ocean Conservation programme at Watamu operates a nest monitoring and protection system and offers guided beach walks during nesting season.

The Kefalonia and Zakynthos loggerhead population in Greece is one of the same Mediterranean subpopulation. Both islands have established turtle-watching tourism but Zakynthos's National Marine Park has the better-organised visitor protocol. The park's strict zoning of Laganas Bay — restricting boat traffic and beach access in the core nesting zones while allowing guided tourism in a buffer zone — is a model of balancing conservation and tourism at a popular destination.

## Ethical principles for turtle watching

Never use white light near nesting or hatching turtles: even a phone screen at close range can disorient a hatchling navigating by moonlight toward the sea. Red filtered lights are acceptable at approved distances. Do not touch, move, or pick up hatchlings — disoriented hatchlings are not lost and should not be helped into the sea. Follow guide instructions absolutely. Photograph without flash only.

Choose guided tours over unguided access at any significant nesting beach. The additional cost of a guided tour goes toward ranger salaries and nest protection programmes; it is not discretionary. At beaches like Grande Rivière and Tortuguero where the tourism revenue is a significant part of the local economy, the relationship between visitor spending and conservation is direct and transparent.
