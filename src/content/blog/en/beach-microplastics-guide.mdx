When you run sand through your fingers on a beach, you are handling a mixture of mineral grains, shell fragments, algae, diatom skeletons, and, increasingly, plastic. The plastic fraction is mostly invisible — particles below 5 millimetres in size, the threshold below which plastics are classified as microplastics, and often far smaller, down to the micron scale. The quantities documented by beach sand surveys over the past decade have changed how marine scientists, policy makers, and eventually the general public understand the global plastic pollution problem.

## What Microplastics Are

The 5-millimetre classification boundary is administrative rather than physical — plastic does not stop breaking down at 5 mm. Macro-plastic debris on beaches, from fishing nets to single-use packaging, is photodegraded and physically abraded by wave action and sand particles into progressively smaller fragments. A plastic bottle fragmented by UV light and mechanical abrasion becomes microplastic film; fishing line becomes microplastic fibres. The process is irreversible: once fragmented to the micro scale, the pieces are too small for most collection systems and too light to sink in most water bodies.

Primary microplastics are manufactured at the micro scale and were never part of a larger plastic object. Industrial plastic pellets — nurdles — are the raw feedstock for almost all plastic manufacturing and are transported globally in bulk. Spillages during transfer are a significant source of pellet contamination on beaches worldwide. Nurdles are approximately 5 mm in diameter and are dense enough to wash ashore rather than floating indefinitely. Microbeads in personal care products (now banned in several countries including the United States from 2017 and the United Kingdom from 2018) were another primary source — polyethylene spheres designed to exfoliate skin and washed directly down drains into wastewater systems.

## What Sand Surveys Have Found

Systematic beach sand microplastic surveys have been conducted across most of the world's ocean coastlines since the early 2010s, and the geographical patterns that have emerged are informative. Remote beaches, despite having no local plastic sources, consistently contain microplastics. Henderson Island in the South Pacific, an uninhabited British overseas territory accessible only by infrequent supply ship, was surveyed in 2017 and found to have among the highest surface densities of plastic debris on Earth — driven entirely by ocean current transport from distant sources. The beach faces directly into the South Pacific gyre, concentrating floating debris swept from the coasts of South America, New Zealand, and beyond.

Beaches in heavily industrialised coastal regions — the Pearl River Delta in Guangdong Province, the Po Delta in the northern Adriatic, the Houston Ship Channel coast — show the highest concentrations of industrial pellets and film fragments. Urban beaches in megacities receive direct inputs from stormwater outfalls, which collect plastic from streets and discharge it through drainage channels directly to the sea.

In terms of fibre contamination, the dominant source identified in most surveys is synthetic textiles. Polyester, nylon, and acrylic fabrics shed microfibres during domestic washing. A single washing machine load of synthetic clothing can release hundreds of thousands of microfibres, which pass through wastewater treatment systems that are not designed to capture particles at that size, and enter waterways that eventually reach the sea.

## Which Beaches Are Most Affected

The worst-affected beaches globally are those at the downstream end of large river systems draining densely populated regions. Asian rivers contribute a disproportionate share of ocean plastic by volume, partly because of population density and partly because waste collection infrastructure in many rapidly urbanising areas has not kept pace with plastic consumption growth. The beaches of Java, Vietnam's Red River delta coast, and Bangladesh's Bay of Bengal coastline receive large quantities of riverine plastic input.

In Europe, the Mediterranean is a relatively enclosed sea with limited tidal flushing, and plastic accumulates at a higher rate relative to input than open ocean coasts. Italian, Greek, and Spanish beaches consistently show elevated microplastic concentrations in sand surveys. The North Sea coast, while connected to the Atlantic, receives input from several heavily populated riverine catchments including the Rhine, Thames, and Scheldt systems.

Remote Arctic and Sub-Antarctic beaches, despite tiny local populations, receive microplastic through two mechanisms: ocean currents delivering material from lower latitudes, and atmospheric transport. Airborne microplastic particles — dried from ocean spray or lifted from urban surfaces — have been detected in Arctic snow and in rain samples from the Pyrenees, suggesting that plastic contamination has entered the atmospheric circulation.

## Citizen Science and Beach Surveys

The scale of beach microplastic contamination is partly understood because of citizen science programs that have mobilised volunteers to conduct standardised sand surveys. The MicroPlastic Sensitive project coordinated across European institutions, the CSIRO's Marine Debris Survey program in Australia, and the Tangaroa Blue network in New Zealand have trained volunteers to collect sand samples, sieve them through standardised mesh sizes, and report results through consistent methodologies.

The value of citizen science programs is not just the data they generate but the community of informed people they create. A volunteer who has personally sieved beach sand and counted microplastic particles under magnification has a different relationship to plastic consumption than someone who has only read about the problem abstractly.

## What Can Actually Be Done

The solutions operate at different scales. At the individual level, reducing plastic use and supporting take-back schemes for plastic packaging reduces the total stock of plastic available to fragment. At the industrial level, filter installation on washing machines (laundry balls and filter bags that capture shed microfibres are available commercially, and France legislated mandatory filter installation on new washing machines from 2025) addresses the textile fibre pathway. Beach cleaning events remove macro-plastic before it fragments to the micro scale.
