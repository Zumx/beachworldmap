Beaches are among the most productive fossil hunting environments on earth, and the reason is purely mechanical. Coastal erosion constantly attacks the cliff face above and the rock platform below, releasing fossils that were locked in the matrix millions of years ago and depositing them on the accessible beach surface. At the right geological exposure, this process happens continuously: a winter storm can reveal a metre of fresh cliff face and scatter a season's worth of new specimens across the beach below. Unlike quarries or inland exposures, productive fossil beaches refresh themselves without any human excavation.

The key variable is geology. Not all coastal rock yields fossils; the beach needs to be backed by or composed of sedimentary rock laid down in an environment that preserved organic material — shallow Jurassic or Cretaceous seas are the most productive, but Devonian reef complexes, Carboniferous lagoons, and Eocene estuaries also yield excellent material. Metamorphic and most igneous rocks contain no fossils at all.

## Charmouth and the Jurassic Coast, Dorset, England

The Jurassic Coast World Heritage Site stretches from Exmouth in Devon to Studland Bay in Dorset — 155 kilometres of continuous rock exposure spanning 185 million years of geological time. Charmouth, in the middle section, is arguably the most productive single-location fossil beach in Britain. The Lower Jurassic cliffs here are composed of Lias shales and limestones packed with ammonites, belemnites, ichthyosaur vertebrae, and occasional complete ichthyosaur skeletons.

The Charmouth Heritage Coast Centre provides year-round guidance on safe collecting practices, cliff conditions, and current productive areas. The beach is free to access and collecting fallen material from the beach is legal and actively encouraged — the fossils eroding out would otherwise be destroyed by the sea. The cliff faces themselves must not be dug into. Finds of significant vertebrate specimens should be reported to the Centre, which manages a site permit under the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act; complete ichthyosaur specimens legally belong to the Crown and are subject to licensing.

The best time to visit Charmouth is the week following a winter storm, when fresh material has been deposited at the base of the cliffs. A low tide between 0.5 and 1.5 metres provides the most beach exposure. Amateur collectors regularly find ammonites up to 30 centimetres in diameter and occasional ichthyosaur bones.

## Bracklesham Bay, West Sussex, England

Bracklesham Bay exposes Eocene-age London Clay and Bracklesham Group sediments on the foreshore, yielding a completely different assemblage from the Dorset Jurassic. Shark teeth — particularly from the genera Striatolamia, Otodus, and the giant Carcharocles — are the primary attraction, along with ray teeth, turtles, crocodile scales, and sea snake vertebrae. The material dates from roughly 45 to 50 million years ago when southern England was a warm subtropical sea.

The shark teeth at Bracklesham are typically small (1 to 3 centimetres) but well-preserved with enamel intact. They are found by systematic scanning of the foreshore at low tide, particularly around the base of clay exposures. The best conditions are an ebbing spring tide on a calm morning; the receding water leaves recently exposed clay surface that has not yet been worked by other collectors.

## Calvert Cliffs, Maryland, USA

The Calvert Cliffs on the western shore of Chesapeake Bay in Maryland expose Miocene-age Calvert Formation sediments — marine deposits from a warm shallow sea that covered the Mid-Atlantic region roughly 14 to 17 million years ago. The fossil assemblage is dominated by shark teeth, including large Carcharocles megalodon teeth up to 15 centimetres in length, along with whale bones, ray teeth, and the remains of over 600 species of invertebrates and marine vertebrates.

Collecting fallen material on the beach below the cliffs is legal at Calvert Cliffs State Park, which provides park access, a clearly marked two-mile trail to the beach, and information on what visitors may legally collect and keep. The cliffs are eroding at several centimetres per year, continuously releasing new material. Spring visits after winter storms typically offer the freshest specimens. Complete megalodon teeth are rare but genuine finds occur annually; more commonly, partial teeth and fragments are found in the gravel beds below the cliff base.

## Lee Creek Mine (Aurora), North Carolina, USA

The phosphate mining operations near Aurora, North Carolina, have produced some of the largest and best-preserved Miocene marine fossils in the world. A public fossil collecting beach adjacent to the mine (formerly operated as a pay-access site by Nutrien/PCS Phosphate) provides access to spoil material removed by mining, which includes shark teeth of extraordinary size and preservation, along with dugong bones, whale vertebrae, ray dental plates, and abundant invertebrates.

The geological formation is the Pungo River Formation, dating to approximately 15 million years before present. Megalodon teeth found at Aurora regularly reach 17 to 18 centimetres, and specimens in excess of 20 centimetres have been documented. The site conditions are unlike natural beach collecting — it is an active industrial operation with controlled access — but the quality and scale of material available make it one of the most celebrated fossil sites in North America.

## Joggins Fossil Cliffs, Nova Scotia, Canada

The UNESCO World Heritage Site at Joggins on the shore of the Bay of Fundy preserves some of the world's most complete Coal Age forest deposits, dating to approximately 310 million years ago. The Carboniferous-age cliffs expose upright fossil tree stumps, with the hollow interiors containing the remains of early reptiles and amphibians that fell into the stumps and became preserved. The diversity of terrestrial Carboniferous life recorded at Joggins is unmatched globally.

The cliffs are actively eroding — the Bay of Fundy's tidal range, among the highest in the world, provides enormous erosive energy — and collecting fallen material from the beach is permitted under provincial fossil management regulations. The Joggins Fossil Centre manages guided tours and research access; self-guided beach collecting is allowed within defined zones outside the core protection area.

## Legal Frameworks for Fossil Collecting

The legality of fossil collecting varies fundamentally by country and land ownership. In England and Wales, collecting from privately owned land requires landowner permission; from Crown foreshore (most tidal beaches below mean high-water), loose surface material can generally be collected without specific permission, but significant vertebrate finds should be reported under the Natural Environment and Rural Communities Act. Scotland operates under the Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003, which provides broader general access rights.

In the United States, collecting on private land requires landowner permission; on national parks and national monuments, all fossil collecting is prohibited regardless of size or significance. On Bureau of Land Management land, casual collecting of reasonable quantities of invertebrate and plant fossils for personal use is generally permitted; vertebrate fossils require a permit. State regulations vary considerably.

In most European countries, significant fossil discoveries are state property under cultural heritage law; this applies in France, Germany, Switzerland, and most of the Nordic countries. Amateur collectors are not prohibited from collecting in these jurisdictions but are required to report significant finds and cannot export them without permit.
