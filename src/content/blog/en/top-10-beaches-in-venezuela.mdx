Venezuela has approximately 2,800 kilometres of Caribbean coastline plus the vast outer archipelago of Los Roques, one of the largest coral atolls in the southern Caribbean. The country's beaches range from the iconic white-sand cays of Los Roques — accessible only by a 35-minute charter flight from Caracas — to the long, palm-lined strands of Margarita Island and the mainland coast of Miranda, Vargas, and Sucre states. Despite political and economic difficulties that have reduced international tourism sharply since the mid-2010s, the natural quality of Venezuela's coastline remains extraordinary, and the beaches of the Morrocoy National Park, Mochima, and the Paria Peninsula represent genuinely world-class tropical coast.

## 1. Playa Flores, Los Roques Archipelago

Playa Flores is one of the outer cay beaches of the Los Roques National Park, a protected marine area of 225,000 hectares of coral reefs, lagoons, and sandy islands about 160 kilometres north of Caracas. Los Roques is accessible only by light aircraft — Cessna Caravans and similar charter planes — from Maiquetía airport, and the total accommodation on the island of Gran Roque, the only inhabited island, is limited to a relatively small number of posadas (guesthouses). This capacity limitation has preserved the archipelago's character. Playa Flores has fine white sand, turquoise shallow-water lagoons, and the type of reef snorkelling that has largely disappeared from more intensively visited Caribbean destinations.

## 2. Playa Medina, Sucre State

Playa Medina lies on the Paria Peninsula in Sucre State on Venezuela's eastern Caribbean coast. The beach is long, palm-lined, and exceptionally beautiful — consistently cited as one of the finest beaches in Venezuela — set against a backdrop of dense tropical forest. The Paria Peninsula is one of the nearest points of South America to Trinidad and Tobago, and the cultural and ecological connections between the peninsula and the islands to the northeast are strong. The beach has a small resort and a cluster of cabins operated by INATUR, Venezuela's tourism authority, which has managed the site as a controlled-access nature tourism area.

## 3. Playa Grande, Vargas State

Playa Grande sits on the short coastal strip of Vargas State, the narrow band of coast between the Caribbean and the Ávila mountain range that also contains Caracas's port of La Guaira and the main international airport. This coast is the primary beach zone for the Caracas metropolitan area — tens of millions of weekend beach trips happen here annually — and Playa Grande near Naiguatá is one of the wider, sandier beaches on this stretch. The backdrop of the cloud-forest-covered Ávila mountains creates a visually dramatic setting. [Open the map](/map) to see how Playa Grande relates to the Caracas coastal access roads.

## 4. Playa Arapito, Anzoátegui State

Playa Arapito sits in the Mochima National Park, which straddles the border of Anzoátegui and Sucre states on the northeastern coast. Mochima is a marine national park protecting a series of inlets, islands, and deep-water bays carved by river erosion into the coastal mountains. Playa Arapito is accessible by boat from the town of Mochima or by a hiking trail from the coastal road, and its relative inaccessibility keeps it quieter than the more popular Playa Blanca nearby. The water clarity in Mochima is excellent, and the surrounding hills are covered in dry tropical forest that turns green during the wet season.

## 5. Playa Colorada, Anzoátegui State

Playa Colorada — Red Beach — is named for the distinctive rusty red-orange sand caused by high iron content in the local sediments. The colour contrast between the red-tinged sand, the deep turquoise water, and the green coconut palms behind it is immediately striking and justifies the beach's frequent appearance in Venezuelan travel photography. The beach sits near the town of Puerto La Cruz in Anzoátegui State and is accessible by road, making it more popular than some of the harder-to-reach Mochima beaches. The reddish sand makes the beach visually unique among Venezuelan Caribbean beaches.

## 6. Cata Beach, Aragua State

Cata is a classic Caribbean beach in the Henri Pittier National Park in Aragua State, Venezuela's oldest national park established in 1937. Henri Pittier covers a substantial section of the central Venezuelan coast and the mountain range behind it, which is one of the most biodiverse areas for birds in South America. Cata itself is a broad arc of grey-white sand inside a sheltered bay, with calm water and good swimming conditions. The road from Maracay through Henri Pittier to the coast passes through extraordinary cloud forest and is one of the finest scenic drives in Venezuela. The park protects the beach from the development that might otherwise have followed its accessibility from Caracas.

## 7. Playa El Agua, Margarita Island

Margarita Island has been Venezuela's primary beach resort destination since the 1970s, and Playa El Agua on the northeastern coast is its signature beach — a long, wide strip of pale sand with consistent Atlantic-influenced surf and a backdrop of palm trees and beach hotels. The island is a free-trade zone and has its own airport with direct international connections. Playa El Agua is roughly 12 kilometres of beach, one of the longest in Venezuela, and its consistent northeast trade winds make it excellent for windsurfing and kitesurfing as well as general beach use. The beach town around it has a well-developed tourist infrastructure.

## 8. Playa Guacuco, Margarita Island

Playa Guacuco sits on the eastern side of Margarita Island and is a favourite with Venezuelan families who want more local atmosphere than the international resort zone around Playa El Agua provides. The beach is sandy and the surf moderate, with a cluster of local restaurants serving fresh grilled fish and seafood. Margarita Island's cultural identity is deeply tied to its fishing heritage, and the eastern coast around Guacuco and the nearby town of La Asunción retains more of this traditional character than the resort-focused western and northern coasts.

## 9. Playa Parguito, Margarita Island

Playa Parguito is the surf beach of Margarita Island, facing northeast into the Atlantic swell that the trade winds carry from the open ocean. The beach is broad and the waves consistent and powerful — popular with surfers but requiring care for swimmers who are not experienced in the surf zone. Adjacent to Parguito is Playa Pargo, a shorter cove with calmer conditions that serves as a good swimming alternative. The combined Parguito-Pargo area is one of the most visually attractive sections of Margarita's coastline, with low rocky headlands providing natural definition to each beach.

## 10. Playa Los Hueques, Anzoátegui State

Playa Los Hueques sits in the greater Barcelona-Puerto La Cruz coastal zone of Anzoátegui State, part of the northeast Venezuelan coast that serves as the primary beach destination for the interior Llanos and Caracas populations during the Venezuelan holiday season from July to September. The beach is sandy and accessible by road, with the typical North Venezuelan coastal vegetation of dry forest and coconut palm. The Barcelona area has an airport with connections to Caracas and provides a base for visiting the various Mochima National Park beaches by boat, making it useful as a logistics hub for exploring the northeastern coast.
