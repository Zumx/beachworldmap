Beach glamping sits in the space between a hotel and a campsite — retaining the physical contact with a coastal environment that a walled room eliminates, while providing a mattress, linen, and often a private outdoor space that makes the experience accessible beyond the committed tent-and-groundsheet camper. Done well, it offers sunrise views from a bed, salt air through canvas, and the sound of the sea without the inconveniences of conventional camping.

The range of what qualifies as beach glamping is wide: from high-end safari tents on African shores to converted fishing camp platforms in Southeast Asia to luxury safari-style experiences on remote Australian beaches. What distinguishes the best examples is a meaningful relationship with their specific beach environment.

## 1. Soneva Jani, North Malé Atoll, Maldives

Soneva Jani is not camping in any traditional sense, but its overwater villas and beachside residences represent the apex of the idea of sleeping immersed in a water environment. The retractable roof above the master bed opens to the Milky Way. The private beach and lagoon access from the villa eliminates the resort-corridor experience entirely. The archipelago setting on Noonu Atoll means the surrounding water is within arm's length at all times. For the glamping aspiration — sleeping in nature without sacrificing comfort — this defines the ceiling of what is possible at a beach destination.

## 2. andBeyond Mnemba Island, Zanzibar, Tanzania

Mnemba Island is a small coral atoll off the northeast coast of Zanzibar, accessible only by dhow from Matemwe. The andBeyond camp on the island has twelve banda-style beach huts — open-sided thatch-and-canvas structures with wooden beds, outdoor showers, and a private section of white sand beach. There are no other guests beyond the camp's small capacity. The island is surrounded by coral reef; snorkelling from the beach is among the best in East Africa. The dhow trip and the isolation make arrival feel genuinely remote.

## 3. Longitude 131, Uluru, Northern Territory, Australia

Longitude 131 is technically desert, not beach, but it pioneered the luxury tented camp concept in Australia and its approach has been replicated at coastal locations. The company operates a related experience, Field Camp Expedition, which deploys similar tented camps at remote Australian coastal sites including Kimberley reef camping locations accessible by liveaboard charter. The combination of Australian wilderness coastal access and the established glamping infrastructure makes these among the most sought-after Australian beach experiences.

## 4. Dakhla Attitude Camp, Western Sahara

The Dakhla Lagoon kite camps in Western Sahara have evolved beyond purely sport-focused facilities to include accommodation in permanent canvas tents over the lagoon with direct access to the flat water. The Dakhla Attitude camp was one of the first to add design sensibility to what had been purely functional kite accommodation — wooden floors, real beds, outdoor showers, and food that goes beyond the camp canteen. The remoteness of the Western Saharan coast means the sky at night is extraordinary.

## 5. Phu Quoc camping, Vietnam

Phu Quoc's Long Beach (Bai Truong) area and the undeveloped north of the island near Ganh Dau cape have seen growth of semi-permanent glamping facilities run by boutique Vietnamese operators. The format is typically a platform tent above the beach with a direct view of the Gulf of Thailand, a hammock outside the entrance, and meals from a shared kitchen. Phu Quoc's national park covers a large portion of the island, which means the beaches adjacent to the forest remain relatively undisturbed. The best operators open October through April, avoiding the southwest monsoon season.

## 6. Watamu, Kenya

The Kenyan coast south of Malindi at Watamu has several eco-tent camps positioned between the forest and the beach. The Watamu Marine National Park protects the reef immediately offshore, and the beach camps have good snorkelling from the front steps. Watamu is known for sea turtle nesting, and camps typically run turtle watch programmes for guests. The tented accommodation ranges from simple mess and canvas to more established platforms with proper beds and private bathrooms. The tidal flat at Watamu is remarkable — the sea retreats several hundred metres at low tide.

## 7. Bambu Indah, Bali, Indonesia

Bambu Indah near Ubud (inland from the coast) established the model in Bali for sensitively designed eco-accommodation using local materials. Its beach-adjacent sibling operations on Nusa Penida and in the Amed area on Bali's east coast carry the same design philosophy to cliff-side and beachfront settings. The bamboo and timber structures are genuinely beautiful and the approach to site integration — minimal clearing, local materials, composting and water management — is more thorough than the broader glamping industry.

## 8. Camp Bestival's Beach Stage, Dorset, England

For festival glamping, Camp Bestival at Lulworth in Dorset gives campers a clifftop field with views over Lulworth Cove — one of England's most iconic coastal geological formations. Glamping packages in yurts, bell tents, and tipi villages are arranged close to the cliff edge. It is glamping in the British summer festival model: not particularly remote or peaceful, but the combination of music, coast, and comfortable tent living is a specific kind of experience the British summer delivers.

## 9. Lamu Archipelago, Kenya

The Shela Beach area on Lamu Island has a handful of beach camp-style properties in traditional Swahili mud-and-timber buildings that evoke a far older version of "glamping" — thick walls, carved wooden beds, coral-stone floors, and a private beach backed by the dhow harbour. The Peponi Hotel at Shela is the most established; several smaller guesthouses have a similar relationship with the beach. The absence of wheeled vehicles on Lamu (donkeys provide transport), the narrow medieval lanes of the old town a 20-minute walk away, and the Indian Ocean directly in front create a combination of historical immersion and beach relaxation unavailable elsewhere in the world.

## 10. Four Seasons Tented Camp, Golden Triangle, Thailand

The Four Seasons Tented Camp in Thailand's Golden Triangle (Chiang Rai province) is the luxury safari tent concept applied to a jungle and river setting rather than a beach — mentioned here because it established the format that many beach glamping operations have adapted. The camp has 15 tents elevated above the jungle on stilts with views of the Mekong River. The relevance to beach travel: the Four Seasons group and its imitators have exported the fully integrated tented camp experience to beach destinations in Fiji, the Maldives, and Mozambique.

## What to look for in a beach glamping camp

Proximity to water is not the same as access to water. Confirm that the tent location allows you to reach the beach quickly — some glamping sites describe themselves as beachfront when the tent is a five-minute walk from any sand. Privacy between tents matters for both sound and atmosphere. Check whether the outdoor shower is genuinely private or whether it is shared infrastructure. Ask about the season: the best beach glamping camps are often closed or substandard outside their optimal weather window.

## The difference between beach glamping and a beach hotel

The core value proposition of beach glamping is sensory connection with the environment that a solid-walled room interrupts. Canvas walls allow the sound of rain, the smell of the sea, and temperature changes that air conditioning eliminates. These are features for some travellers and bugs for others. Anyone who sleeps poorly when warm, who is significantly disturbed by sound, or who finds canvas sleeping uncomfortable in any way should choose a conventional hotel. The best beach glamping experience requires matching the format to what you actually want from a night at the beach — which is a more personal assessment than reviewing accommodation star ratings.
