Puerto Rico occupies a rectangular island about 180 kilometres long and 60 kilometres wide in the northeastern Caribbean, with the Atlantic Ocean on its north coast and the Caribbean Sea to the south. This dual-coast geography means the beach experience varies dramatically: the north coast faces prevailing Atlantic trade winds and produces consistent surf, while the southern Caribbean side is calmer and warmer. The archipelago includes the smaller islands of Vieques and Culebra to the east, which have some of the finest beaches in the entire Caribbean and escaped the hotel development that colonised the main island's most accessible shores. Puerto Rico is a US territory, which means no passport for Americans and straightforward access from the continental United States.

## 1. Playa de las Tres Palmitas, northwest coast

Playa de las Tres Palmitas is a small, local beach on the northwestern coast of Puerto Rico near Isabela, a town known as a surf hub and gateway to the wild northern beaches. The beach is named for three palm trees that long marked the path down from the road — a practical rather than poetic naming convention common on island beaches. The northwestern coast around Isabela and Aguadilla receives the most consistent Atlantic swell in Puerto Rico, and the water quality here is generally excellent, with good visibility for snorkelling around the rocky headlands at either end of the beach.

## 2. Playa Joyuda, Cabo Rojo

Playa Joyuda sits in the sheltered bay near Cabo Rojo on the southwestern corner of Puerto Rico. The bay is famous above all for its seafood restaurants — a long strip of open-air marisquerías lines the road above the beach, serving fresh mofongo with shrimp, whole grilled red snapper, and crab dishes to a mostly local clientele. The beach itself is modest, the water calm and shallow, and the appeal is as much culinary as scenic. The nearby Cabo Rojo lighthouse and salt flats are worth combining with a visit; the salt flats attract flamingos in winter and the lighthouse sits on dramatic limestone cliffs at the southwestern tip of the island.

## 3. Playa Espinar, Aguadilla

Playa Espinar lies near Aguadilla in the northwest and occupies a stretch of beach within the former Ramey Air Force Base area, which closed in 1973 and has been gradually converted to civilian use. The beach is long, sandy, and relatively uncrowded even in peak season, backed by the flat land of the former base. The area has become a hub for water sports; Crashboat Beach, immediately adjacent, is arguably more famous, but Espinar offers more space and a slightly wilder feel. The northwest Puerto Rico coast has consistent Atlantic swells year-round, with December through April producing the most powerful conditions.

## 4. Playa Caracoles, Hatillo

Playa Caracoles sits on the north coast near Hatillo, east of Arecibo, and is named for the sea snails collected from the beach. The north coast beaches near Hatillo and Arecibo have a less touristed character than the beaches closer to San Juan, and the landscape behind them — sugar cane fields and working Puerto Rican agricultural towns — gives them a more locally grounded atmosphere. The beach is broad and sandy, the surf consistent. The nearby Arecibo area has additional attractions including the Puerto Rico Karst landscape and the former Arecibo Observatory site, making the area worth a longer visit.

## 5. Playa Ocean Park, San Juan

Ocean Park sits between the tourist beach of Condado and the famous Isla Verde beach strip, sandwiched between San Juan's two major hotel zones but retaining a quieter, more residential character. The beach is backed by a neighbourhood of wooden beach houses and small guesthouses rather than high-rise hotels, and the local community actively uses it. The Atlantic surf here is consistent and the beach is popular with surfers and body-boarders. Ocean Park is walkable from Condado and within a short taxi ride of Old San Juan, making it a genuinely good alternative for those who want beach access without the high-rise resort atmosphere.

## 6. Playa de Levittown, Toa Baja

Playa de Levittown sits just west of San Juan in the municipality of Toa Baja, near the planned residential development of Levittown built in the 1950s. This is a local beach serving the San Juan metropolitan area — families from the surrounding suburbs rather than international tourists. The beach is broad and the water is the consistent turquoise of the Puerto Rican north coast, and on weekdays it offers a genuine glimpse of how Puerto Ricans actually use their beaches: extended family gatherings, loud music, coolers of food, and children in the surf. ## 7. Playa Laguna, northeastern coast

Playa Laguna sits on the northeastern coast of Puerto Rico near the Laguna Tortuguero, a coastal lagoon that is an important manatee habitat and protected natural reserve. The beach is accessed via a road through the coastal communities north of San Juan, and the proximity of the lagoon gives it a particular natural character — the border between freshwater lagoon and saltwater beach is unusually close. Manatees occasionally enter the surf zone from the lagoon. The northeastern coast receives more rainfall than the south and west, keeping the vegetation lush and the beach less crowded than the major resort zones.

## 8. Playa La Puente, Culebra

Playa La Puente is a small beach on the island of Culebra, Puerto Rico's outer island archipelago, accessible by ferry from Fajardo or a short 10-minute flight. Culebra's beaches are generally considered among the finest in the entire Caribbean: the water has exceptional clarity, the coral reefs offshore are largely intact compared to more trafficked islands, and development remains relatively low-key. La Puente is calmer and less visited than the famous Flamenco Beach on the same island, making it a good choice for snorkelling and for visitors who prefer a quieter setting.

## 9. Playa Flamenco, Culebra

Flamenco Beach on Culebra is consistently ranked among the top beaches in the world in reader surveys. The horseshoe-shaped bay encloses a kilometre of brilliantly white sand and turquoise water, sheltered from the prevailing Atlantic swell by its orientation and the surrounding headlands. It is a public beach with basic facilities — toilets, kiosks, camping permitted — and while it is the most visited beach on Culebra, the island's limited accommodation means day-tripper numbers stay manageable. Arriving on the morning ferry from Fajardo and leaving on the afternoon departure gives a full day at the beach. Camping on Flamenco is a genuinely good way to experience the beach after the day-trippers leave.

## 10. Playa Buyé, Cabo Rojo

Playa Buyé sits in the southwestern municipality of Cabo Rojo, a Caribbean-side beach facing southeast with calm, transparent water and pale sand. The southwest of Puerto Rico is the driest part of the island — the rain shadow of the central mountains keeps this area sunny when the north and east are cloudy — and the sea here is consistently calmer and warmer than the Atlantic-facing beaches. Buyé is accessible by a local road and has a small parking area, basic facilities, and a typically local Puerto Rican beach atmosphere. The Cabo Rojo salt flats and lighthouse are 20 minutes away by car.
