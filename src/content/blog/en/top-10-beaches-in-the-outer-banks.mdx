The Outer Banks are a 320-kilometre chain of barrier islands and spits running from Virginia's Currituck County south to Cape Lookout, separated from the North Carolina mainland by a series of sounds — Currituck, Albemarle, Roanoke, Pamlico, Core — that collectively form the largest lagoon system on the US East Coast. The outer face of the islands receives Atlantic Ocean swells and is one of the most storm-affected coastlines in North America: the islands are narrow (in places less than 500 metres wide), low (rarely above three metres), and historically violent in their behaviour, migrating westward at rates of up to two metres per year under storm and sea-level pressure. The graveyard quality that history has given this coast — over 600 documented shipwrecks along the Diamond Shoals alone — is matched by a wild, elemental beach landscape that the Cape Hatteras and Cape Lookout national seashores preserve in one of the most intact stretches of barrier island coast on the eastern seaboard. Summers are hot and humid; fishing and surfing are year-round pursuits; the Outer Banks Scenic Byway runs the length of the islands.

## 1. Cape Hatteras National Seashore, Hatteras Island

Cape Hatteras National Seashore, established in 1953, was the first national seashore in the United States and protects roughly 130 kilometres of the Outer Banks including Hatteras Island and the north end of Ocracoke Island. The beaches are wild Atlantic-facing strands with no commercial development in the seashore boundary — dunes, sea oats, and the sky behind, with the Cape Hatteras Lighthouse (the tallest brick lighthouse in the US at 63 metres) visible for kilometres along the beach. Vehicle access on the beach is managed by permit; the combination of beach driving, surf fishing, and solitude constitutes the classic Outer Banks experience. Sea turtle nesting and piping plover nesting cause seasonal access restrictions at specific zones.

## 2. Ocracoke Beach, Ocracoke Island

Ocracoke Island is accessible only by ferry — a 40-minute service from Hatteras to the north or a 2.5-hour service from Cedar Island and Swan Quarter on the mainland. This ferry requirement filters the day-trip crowd, giving Ocracoke a distinctly more remote character than the northern Outer Banks. The beach on the Atlantic side of the island is within the Cape Hatteras National Seashore and is maintained in a near-natural state: 24 kilometres of sand with no facilities except at the main beach access ramps. The village of Ocracoke, at the southern end of the island, is the most historically intact Outer Banks community, with a lighthouse built in 1823 and a local dialect — the Hoi Toider brogue — still spoken by some older residents.

## 3. Kitty Hawk Beach, Kitty Hawk

Kitty Hawk Beach is at the northern end of the developed Outer Banks, where the barrier island is wide enough to support a full resort town with motels, seafood restaurants, and residential vacation rentals behind the beach strand. The beach here is wide and well-maintained by the town. The adjacent Kill Devil Hills contains the Wright Brothers National Memorial at Big Kill Devil Hill, where the first powered aircraft flights took place on December 17, 1903. The combination of the historic memorial, the beach, and the string of seafood restaurants on the bypass makes Kitty Hawk-Kill Devil Hills the most practical base on the Outer Banks.

## 4. Kill Devil Hills Beach, Kill Devil Hills

Kill Devil Hills Beach runs through a town named for the rum that locals reputedly hid in the large dunes. The town beaches are managed by the municipality and maintained to consistent standards. The surf here is modest by Outer Banks standards — the continental shelf narrows near Cape Hatteras to the south, and the shoaling process that creates the Diamond Shoals conditions does not yet operate at full force this far north. The beach benefits from the Outer Banks wide, open character — no development between the back dune and the ocean, just sand, sea grass, and Atlantic light.

## 5. Nags Head Beach, Nags Head

Nags Head is the oldest resort community on the Outer Banks, dating to the 1830s when mainland planters began building summer cottages on the sound side of the island. The name's origins are disputed — possibly from the practice of walking horses with lanterns around the dunes to lure ships onto the shoals. The beach at Nags Head is typical Outer Banks Atlantic strand: wide, clean, and subject to the water temperature range of 7–27°C between January and August. Jockey's Ridge State Park, immediately behind the beach strip, protects the largest active sand dune on the US East Coast, which rises to about 28 metres and provides hang-gliding and sand-sliding.

## 6. Cape Lookout, Core Banks

Cape Lookout National Seashore is the undeveloped southern extension of the Outer Banks barrier system, accessible only by ferries operating from Beaufort, Harkers Island, and Davis on the mainland. The seashore comprises the Core Banks and Shackleford Banks, the latter home to a self-sustaining herd of wild horses that have grazed the island since at least the 16th century. The beach on the Atlantic side of Core Banks is among the most remote and undisturbed on the entire US Atlantic seaboard. The distinctive diamond-pattern black and white Cape Lookout Lighthouse, built in 1859, marks the cape where the North Carolina and South Carolina outer coasts meet.

## 7. Pea Island, Dare County

Pea Island National Wildlife Refuge occupies the northern 22 kilometres of Hatteras Island, established in 1938 as a sanctuary for waterfowl and shorebirds. The beach within the refuge is accessible and excellent — wide, undeveloped, and particularly productive for birdwatching during spring and autumn migrations when tens of thousands of shorebirds stage on the tidal flats facing the Pamlico Sound. Greater snow geese, tundra swans, and red-throated loons winter in the sound-side wetlands. An interpretive visitor centre on Highway 12 at the north end of the refuge provides trail maps and bird lists.

## 8. Coquina Beach, Cape Hatteras National Seashore

Coquina Beach is within the national seashore near the town of Nags Head on Bodie Island, and takes its name from the coquina shells — tiny bivalves — that characterise the sand at this location. It is the most developed beach access point within the national seashore on Bodie Island: bathhouses, picnic facilities, and parking. The adjacent Bodie Island Lighthouse, completed in 1872, is open for climbing in season. The beach itself is wide and faces north-east, subject to the moderate surf and swell typical of this section of the coast.

## 9. Frisco Beach, Hatteras Island

Frisco Beach, near the village of Frisco toward the southern end of Hatteras Island, is less visited than the national seashore access points further north, which makes it one of the better options for solitude on the northern Outer Banks. The beach access ramps here are used primarily by anglers and four-wheel-drive vehicles with beach permits. The offshore conditions near Frisco, where the shoaling of the diamond shoals begins to steepen waves, create a consistent and fairly powerful shore break. The village of Frisco has a small museum of native American artefacts and a good local grocery.

## 10. Salvo Beach, Dare County

Salvo Beach is a small beach community on Hatteras Island named for a cannonball incident during the Civil War, when a Union gunboat fired a salvo at the village when it appeared on no maps. The village itself is a quiet residential community; the beach is flat, wide, and backed by low dunes typical of central Hatteras Island. The sound side of Hatteras Island near Salvo and the adjacent Canadian Hole — a notorious windsurfing and kiteboarding location where the sound is reliably flat and the wind channels through a gap in the dunes — makes this stretch of Hatteras Island the kite and windsurf capital of the US East Coast.
