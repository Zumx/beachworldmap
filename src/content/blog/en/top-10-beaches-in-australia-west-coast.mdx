Western Australia has the longest beach coastline of any Australian state — over 12,000 kilometres facing the Indian Ocean. The character shifts from the tropical Kimberley coast in the north, through the turquoise water and red pindan cliffs of the Pilbara and Gascoyne, to the white quartzite sand and cold Southern Ocean character of the southern coast. The distances are large by any standard: Cable Beach in Broome and Lucky Bay near Esperance are 2,700 kilometres apart by road. Planning a west coast beach trip requires deciding on a region rather than a single circuit.

## 1. Cable Beach, Broome, Western Australia

Cable Beach is an 18-kilometre straight beach of red-orange sand at the Indian Ocean shore of Broome, a pearling town and tourist centre in the Kimberley region. The beach faces west and the sunsets are spectacular — the camel rides along the waterline at sunset are a Broome institution. The Indian Ocean at Cable Beach is warm year-round but subject to marine stingers in the wet season (November through April), when swimming is from the stinger net area only. The dry season from May through October is the standard visit window. Cable Beach is in the map database.

## 2. Lucky Bay, Cape Le Grand National Park, Western Australia

Lucky Bay, 50 kilometres east of Esperance, is consistently ranked one of Australia's top beaches: white quartzite sand, turquoise Southern Ocean water, and a population of eastern grey kangaroos that rest on the beach as casually as the human visitors. The national park setting means no permanent facilities apart from a campsite. The water is cold — 18 to 22 degrees Celsius — and the Southern Ocean swells are powerful, so swimming is for confident swimmers. Lucky Bay is in the database.

## 3. Cottesloe Beach, Perth, Western Australia

Cottesloe is Perth's most loved suburban beach, backed by the Indiana Tea House heritage restaurant and a promenade of Norfolk Island pines. The beach faces southwest into the Indian Ocean. The offshore reef system provides some swell reduction. Snorkelling on the offshore reef in calm conditions is accessible for beginners. Cottesloe is 15 minutes by train from central Perth and is the reference point for Perth beach culture — Sunday morning coffee at Indiana, a swim, watching the swans in the water.

## 4. Turquoise Bay, Cape Range National Park, Western Australia

Turquoise Bay, 55 kilometres south of Exmouth on the Ningaloo coast, is a World Heritage listed snorkelling beach where the marine diversity — coral trout, reef sharks, turtles, rays, and hundreds of reef fish species — is accessible directly from the sand. The coral begins within 10 metres of the shore. The drift snorkel — entering the water at the north end of the bay and letting a gentle current carry you along the reef edge — is one of the best snorkel experiences in Australia. Ningaloo Reef is healthier and more accessible than the Great Barrier Reef for most visitors.

## 5. Hamelin Bay, Western Australia

Hamelin Bay, south of Augusta at the tip of the Leeuwin peninsula, is a small harbour beach that is famous for a population of large stingrays that congregate in the shallows, sometimes dozens at a time. The rays are habituated to human presence and swim around wading visitors. The beach is otherwise unremarkable — a small protected bay — but the stingray interaction is one of the most unusual wildlife beach experiences on the Australian coast.

## 6. Meelup Beach, Dunsborough, Western Australia

Meelup is a small beach on the north side of Cape Naturaliste in the Margaret River wine region of Western Australia. The beach faces north into Geographe Bay, which is sheltered from the Southern Ocean swells, producing calm water suitable for families and kayaking. The bay is a significant whale calving ground from July through November: southern right and humpback whales bring calves into the bay's protected waters. The Margaret River region's wineries and food scene make Meelup the beach component of a broader regional visit.

## 7. Salmon Holes Beach, Albany, Western Australia

Salmon Holes, 10 kilometres west of Albany on the dramatic Torndirrup Peninsula, is a beach cut into granite cliffs on the Southern Ocean. It is not a swimming beach — the wave action is severe and undertow extreme — but the landscape is extraordinary and it is one of the most popular rock-fishing spots in Western Australia. The approach road through the Torndirrup National Park has multiple lookouts over blowholes and natural bridge formations.

## 8. Bremer Bay, Western Australia

Bremer Bay is a small coastal community 180 kilometres east of Albany with a broad beach and Wellstead Estuary behind. In January and February, the Bremer Canyon offshore attracts a significant orca (killer whale) population, and whale-watching boats operate from Bremer Bay during this window. The beach is good but the orca season is the primary attraction.

## 9. Lancelin Beach, Western Australia

Lancelin, 130 kilometres north of Perth, has a protected beach behind an offshore island chain that creates calm, shallow water ideal for beginner windsurfing and kitesurfing. The beach is long, the wind reliable from the south through summer, and the facilities — camping, caravan parks, a general store — are functional rather than luxurious. The massive sand dunes behind the town are used for sand boarding.

## 10. Coral Bay, Western Australia

Coral Bay is at the southern end of the Ningaloo Reef, 1,130 kilometres north of Perth. The Ningaloo Reef comes within metres of the beach here, making it the most accessible coral snorkelling in the country. The beach is a calm, shallow bay with clear water. Whale shark aggregation from March through July is the headline activity — tour boats from Coral Bay take snorkellers into the water alongside whale sharks. Coral Bay is a remote town with limited accommodation that must be booked well ahead. Coral Bay is in the database.

## Planning a Western Australia beach trip

The great challenge of the Western Australian coast is its scale. Perth to Broome is a 2,300-kilometre drive — longer than London to Istanbul. Most visitors choose one region rather than attempting the full coast. The three practical regions are:

The Perth and Southwest (Margaret River area): Cottesloe, Meelup, Hamelin Bay, and the Southern Ocean beaches east of Albany. This circuit is entirely accessible as day trips or short road trips from Perth, covers everything from urban beach culture to cold Southern Ocean drama. The Margaret River region's food, wine, and cave system adds significant non-beach content.

The Ningaloo Coast (Exmouth and Coral Bay): a 14-hour drive north from Perth or a 2-hour flight. The Ningaloo Reef, Turquoise Bay, and whale shark season are world-class marine experiences in a remote landscape. A week is needed to do justice to both Exmouth and Coral Bay. The season for whale sharks and calm weather is April through October; before and after the weather is less reliable and stinger risk increases.

The Kimberley (Broome and beyond): Broome is accessible by direct flights from Perth (2.5 hours), Sydney, and other Australian cities. Cable Beach is the base; the surrounding landscape — Gantheaume Point's red cliffs and dinosaur footprints, the Dampier Peninsula, the Horizontal Falls accessible by floatplane — constitutes a spectacular regional exploration. The dry season (May through October) is the only practical window; the wet season (November through April) involves monsoonal rain and restricted access to much of the Kimberley.

For international visitors combining a Western Australia beach experience with the rest of Australia, Perth is the main gateway. Direct flights serve Perth from London, Singapore, Kuala Lumpur, Doha, Dubai, and most major Asian hubs. The time zone (GMT+8, or GMT+9.5 in summer) means Perth is closer to Southeast Asia in travel time than to Sydney (5 hours) or Melbourne (4 hours) by air.
