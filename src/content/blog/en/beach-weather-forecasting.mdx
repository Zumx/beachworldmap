Reading weather forecasts for a beach day involves a different set of variables than reading a forecast for a city. Temperature and precipitation — the standard outputs of most weather apps — are secondary to wind direction, swell height and period, tide state, and sea temperature. A surf lifesaver at a major Australian beach assesses at least eight variables before deciding where to position the flags each morning. Understanding even a fraction of those variables transforms how you plan and experience a day at the coast.

## Wind Direction and Its Effect on Beach Conditions

Wind direction is the single most important variable for beach conditions, more important than wind speed. An onshore wind — blowing from sea toward land — pushes waves toward the beach, increasing surf height and period, and creates choppy, disorganised surface water. A strong onshore wind produces white-capping waves, spray across the water surface, and difficult swimming conditions even when the underlying swell is small.

An offshore wind — blowing from land toward sea — has the opposite effect. It holds up the face of approaching waves, producing cleaner, more organised surf, and suppresses the surface chop that makes swimming uncomfortable. For surfers, offshore winds create the groomed wave faces that make for excellent riding conditions. For casual swimmers and snorkellers, an offshore wind can produce deceptively calm surface conditions in the shore zone even when significant swell is running.

A cross-shore wind (blowing parallel to the beach) creates lateral drift along the beach — both in the surface water and for anything floating in it, including swimmers. The longshore drift that shapes beach geomorphology over geological time is driven by the same mechanism that pushes a swimmer 300 metres along a beach in an hour without them noticing.

## Swell Height, Period, and the Difference Between Them

Swell is the organised wave energy that arrives at a beach from distant storm systems, separate from the locally generated wind chop. The two key measurements are height and period. Swell height is typically expressed as the significant wave height — the average of the highest third of all waves — and ranges from under 0.5 metres for calm conditions to over 6 metres for major storm swells.

Period — measured in seconds between successive wave crests — is equally important and often underemphasised by weather apps that show only wave height. A 2-metre swell at 8 seconds is a short-period, locally generated swell that produces moderate surf with relatively little power. A 2-metre swell at 16 seconds is an ocean swell that has travelled perhaps thousands of kilometres, organised itself into long, powerful waves, and will produce surf with dramatically more energy. The longer the period, the more energy each wave carries, and the more dangerous the surf zone becomes for casual swimmers.

Forecasters use this relationship to classify swell types. Wind swell (short period, under 10 seconds) is generated by local weather systems. Ground swell (long period, over 14 seconds) arrives from distant oceanic storms — winter storms in the Roaring Forties generating surf on Australian east coast beaches, or North Atlantic winter gales producing waves on the Caribbean north shores of Dominican Republic and Puerto Rico in January and February.

## The Sea Breeze System

A sea breeze is a local circulation driven by differential heating between the land and the sea during daytime. The land heats faster than the sea in sunlight, and the warmer air over the land rises, drawing cooler marine air inland to replace it. This produces an afternoon onshore wind that can be remarkably predictable and consistent in certain coastal climates — Perth's Fremantle Doctor, the Cape Cod sea breeze, the afternoon winds at Cape Town — and which transforms beach conditions from calm in the morning to gusty in the afternoon.

The sea breeze typically starts 2 to 3 hours after sunrise and peaks in early to mid-afternoon before diminishing as the land cools. In tropical and subtropical beach destinations, the sea breeze is a reliable feature of fine weather days. Recognising when a sea breeze is developing — watching for the cloud line that often marks the sea-breeze front moving inland — allows beach visitors to predict when conditions will deteriorate and plan accordingly.

## Reading Surf Forecast Tools

Several free surf forecast services provide considerably more detail than general weather apps for beach planning. Surfline, Magicseaweed, and Windguru all provide beach-specific forecasts that include swell height, swell period, swell direction, wind direction and speed, and tidal information in the same view. Windy, which visualises meteorological model output, allows users to see the entire Atlantic or Pacific swell field and trace where the waves arriving at a particular beach originated.

These tools are free to use at basic resolution, though higher-frequency updates and proprietary nowcast models are typically subscription services. The model forecasts are most accurate within 72 hours; beyond that, swell forecasts become increasingly speculative because they depend on accurate storm track prediction many days in advance.

## Rip Current and Hazard Forecasts

The United States National Weather Service issues rip current outlooks as part of its coastal marine forecast system. When swell height and period combine with tide state and beach topography to create high rip current risk, the NWS issues elevated risk statements for specific coastal counties. These advisories appear in most major weather apps and are integrated into Google weather cards for US coastal areas.

Beach hazard ratings in Australia — the Beachsafe system operated by Surf Life Saving Australia — provide a 1-to-5 hazard rating for patrolled beaches, updated daily and based on both forecast conditions and lifeguard observations of actual conditions at each beach. The ratings integrate swell, wind, tide, and known hazard points at each beach to produce a unified assessment accessible through the Beachsafe app.

## Interpreting a Complete Forecast

A beach visitor with full forecast information is considering: overnight swell direction and height (which will shape conditions even before wind develops); morning wind direction and strength (offshore means clean surf, onshore means choppy conditions); whether a sea breeze is forecast to develop and at what time; tidal phase — rising tide generally produces better rip current conditions for fish and more active surf; and sea temperature (relevant for wetsuit decisions and for assessing jellyfish season in regions where temperature predicts bloom timing).
