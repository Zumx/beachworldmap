The Visayas are the central island group of the Philippines, stretching across the arc of sea between Luzon to the north and Mindanao to the south, encompassing the major islands of Cebu, Bohol, Negros, Panay, Leyte, and Samar, and dozens of smaller islands including Boracay, Siquijor, and Camiguin. The region is the historical and cultural heartland of the Philippines: the first Spanish contact with Southeast Asia occurred at Cebu in 1521, and the Visayan languages — Cebuano, Hiligaynon, Waray — are among the most widely spoken in the country. Beach conditions in the Visayas are shaped by the geography of the Philippine Sea to the east and the Sulu and Sibuyan seas to the west. The east-facing coasts are more exposed to Pacific typhoon paths; the west-facing and inter-island beaches tend to be calmer, warmer, and more protected. Water temperature is 28–30°C year-round in the shallow inter-island passages, and the coral biodiversity of the Visayan Sea is exceptional — the region sits within the Coral Triangle, the global centre of marine biodiversity. The dry season is roughly November through May, though microclimates vary significantly between the eastern and western sides of the major islands.

## 1. Sumagingsing Beach, Camiguin Island

Sumagingsing Beach is on Camiguin, the volcanic island province off the northern coast of Mindanao best reached from Cagayan de Oro by ferry. Camiguin is known as the island of volcanoes — it has seven, and its land area of 238 square kilometres is the highest density of volcanoes of any island in the world. Sumagingsing is a black sand beach on the southern coast, the volcanic character evident in the dark grey sand and the steaming volcanic formations visible on the hillside above. The combination of black sand, palm-shaded beach, clear warm water, and the volcano profile makes this one of the most atmospheric beaches in the Philippines.

## 2. Tagbarungis Beach, Camiguin

Tagbarungis Beach is on the western side of Camiguin, calmer and sandier than the southern volcanic beaches, with views across the Bohol Sea toward the distant Misamis Oriental coast. This section of Camiguin is the main resort area, with a string of small hotels, dive shops, and restaurants between the road and the beach. The coral reef directly offshore at several points along the west coast is in good condition and accessible by snorkelling from the beach. The sunken cemetery nearby — a graveyard submerged by a 19th-century volcanic eruption, marked by a large cross visible at low tide — is Camiguin's most unusual attraction.

## 3. Puting Buhangin Beach, Camiguin

Puting Buhangin — White Sand Beach in Cebuano — is on the islet of Mantigue off Camiguin's eastern coast, accessible by a short bangka ride from Mambajao. The islet is a marine sanctuary; fishing is prohibited around the reef. The beach on the landward side of the islet is white and powdery, the coral gardens on the seaward side exceptional, and the combination of islet snorkelling, white sand, and the Camiguin volcanic backdrop makes Mantigue one of the most complete short-trip destinations in the Visayas.

## 4. Botoon Beach, Bohol

Botoon Beach is on the southern coast of Bohol, close to the town of Anda, in an area that developed as a beach destination in the 2010s as an alternative to the overcrowded Alona Beach on Panglao Island. The beach is a long, narrow ribbon of white-cream sand backed by moderate resort infrastructure — guesthouses, dive shops, and simple restaurants. The Anda area avoided the worst damage from the 2013 Bohol earthquake. Snorkelling and diving off Anda is good, with wall dives and coral gardens accessible by a short boat ride. The Chocolate Hills — the famous karst formation in central Bohol — are a 2-hour inland drive from the coast.

## 5. Bentoan Beach, Surigao del Norte (Siargao)

Bentoan Beach is on the island of Siargao in Surigao del Norte — a different part of the Visayas region, technically in the Caraga administrative region but within the broader Visayan cultural sphere. Siargao is now internationally famous as a surf destination after decades of obscurity; the Cloud 9 break hosts the annual Siargao Surfing Cup. Bentoan is a quieter beach on the northern coast, away from the main surf break circuit, with calm water suitable for swimming and kayaking. The surrounding palm-covered landscape and the shallow tidal lagoons behind the beach give it a classically tropical Philippine character.

## 6. Papaya Beach, Siquijor

Siquijor is an island province with a folklore-rich reputation — it is traditionally associated with folk healing and witchcraft — and its beaches remain considerably less visited than Bohol or Cebu despite being accessible by ferry from Dumaguete in about an hour. Papaya Beach on the north-west coast is a long stretch of white sand and shallow reef-sheltered water, backed by coconut palms. The northeast trade wind side of Siquijor (the southern and western coasts) is calmer. Siquijor remains a genuinely off-the-beaten-track destination with basic but functional guesthouses and an island circuit navigable by motorbike in a day.

## 7. Saptap Beach, Leyte

Saptap Beach is on the island of Leyte, on the eastern side of the Visayas facing the Leyte Gulf — the body of water where the largest naval battle in history, the Battle of Leyte Gulf, was fought in October 1944. The beach is a working coastal community beach with no resort development. The surrounding landscape, which was heavily affected by Super Typhoon Haiyan (locally Yolanda) in November 2013, has largely recovered but retains visible evidence of storm surge impact in the altered coastline profile at some points. Visiting Leyte combines beach access with significant WWII and disaster resilience history.

## 8. Magiket Beach, Boracay

Magiket Beach is on the eastern side of Boracay, facing the Sibuyan Sea, in contrast to the famous White Beach on the western side. The east coast is exposed to the northeasterly Amihan wind from November through April — when White Beach is calm, the east coast is windy and good for kiteboarding — and sheltered to some extent when the southwest Habagat wind blows from May through October. Magiket has a kiteboarding school operation and attracts a sports-oriented clientele rather than the resort crowd of White Beach. The beach itself is narrow and coarser than White Beach, but the wind sports environment is world-class.

## 9. Puka Shell Beach, Boracay

Puka Shell Beach is at the northern tip of Boracay, named for the puka shells once found in abundance along its length — the shells that were fashioned into the puka shell necklaces that swept global beach fashion in the 1970s. The beach is wider and more natural than White Beach to the south, with coarser sand, fewer facilities, and a quieter character. Commercial development has slowly expanded northward but Puka retains more of old Boracay's character. It is accessible on foot or by tricycle from Station 1 in about 20–30 minutes, or by bangka from the White Beach area.

## 10. Diniwid Beach, Boracay

Diniwid Beach is a small cove immediately north of the White Beach area, separated by a rocky headland from the main resort strip. The beach is accessible through a narrow path around the headland rocks (passable at low tide) or by a 15-minute walk on the upper road. The result is a calm, intimate cove with white sand and clear water that functions as a quieter alternative to the White Beach strip immediately to the south. A handful of small boutique properties have established themselves on the headland above the cove. At high tide the headland crossing requires knee-deep wading.

## Plan Your Visayas Beach Route

The Visayas offer the broadest range of beach experiences in the Philippines — from the global resort infrastructure of Boracay to the completely undeveloped volcanic shores of Camiguin.
