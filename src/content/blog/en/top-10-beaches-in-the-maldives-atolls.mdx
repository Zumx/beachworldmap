The Maldives consists of 26 natural atolls made up of about 1,200 islands, of which roughly 200 are inhabited and around 160 are operated as resort islands. The country stretches 900 kilometres from north to south in the central Indian Ocean, straddling the equator, and lies so low that the highest natural point in the country is only about two metres above sea level. The beach experience varies significantly across the atolls: the northern atolls like Haa Alif and Shaviyani are remote, relatively little-visited, and retain the quietest character; the central Male Atoll area is the most accessible and most developed; and the southern atolls around Addu and Fuvahmulah have their own distinct geography, culture, and reef systems. Local island beaches — those on inhabited Maldivian islands rather than resort islands — have opened dramatically to tourism since 2011, making independent travel through the atolls genuinely feasible for the first time.

## 1. Bikini Beach, North Malé Atoll

Bikini Beach on the local island of Hulhumalé, adjacent to Malé, is the most accessible beach for visitors arriving at the main Velana International Airport. Hulhumalé is a reclaimed island that has grown substantially over the past two decades as a planned urban extension of the capital. Bikini Beach is designated as a section where bikinis are permitted — not standard on Maldivian local islands, where public beach rules typically require more modest clothing out of respect for the local Muslim culture. The beach is sandy, the water clear, and the proximity to the airport makes it useful for first and last day beach time without requiring additional boat transfers.

## 2. Ukulhas Beach, South Ari Atoll

Ukulhas is considered one of the best local-island beaches in the Maldives — a long, calm stretch of white sand on a small island in the South Ari Atoll, accessible by speedboat from Malé in about two hours or by domestic flight to nearby Ari Atoll airport. The island has developed steadily as a budget-friendly alternative to the resort islands that dominate the surrounding atoll, with a cluster of guesthouses, restaurants, and the kind of snorkelling from the beach — reef fish, manta rays in season, whale sharks in the deeper channels — that would cost a significant premium at a resort island.

## 3. Fulhadhoo Beach, Baa Atoll

The Baa Atoll UNESCO Biosphere Reserve is home to one of the largest congregations of manta rays in the world, gathering seasonally in Hanifaru Bay to feed on plankton-rich upwelling water. Fulhadhoo island in the Baa Atoll has a beach of exceptional quality — fine white sand, clear shallow water, and a housereef accessible from the shore — in a genuinely remote atoll that is less developed than the central Male area. Domestic flights from Malé reach the Dharavandhoo airport in the Baa Atoll in about 30 minutes, making it more accessible than its remote reputation suggests. The island has a small permanent population and a handful of guesthouses.

## 4. Maafushi Beach, South Malé Atoll

Maafushi is the pioneering local island of the Maldives independent travel revolution — the first local island to open guesthouses to tourists in significant numbers after the 2011 policy change, and still one of the most visited. The island has a long, developed strip of guesthouses, restaurants, and watersports operators on the bikini beach section, creating an infrastructure that feels almost resort-like in density while remaining on a local island. Maafushi is accessible by public ferry from Malé in about 90 minutes or by speedboat in 45 minutes. The Malé Atoll reefs surrounding the island have seen heavy snorkelling pressure over the years but remain accessible and reasonably intact. ## 5. Rasdhoo Atoll Beach, Rasdhoo Atoll

Rasdhoo is a small, circular atoll between the North Malé and Ari Atolls, with a local island of the same name and a surrounding reef system known for hammerhead shark sightings. The Rasdhoo atoll wall drops steeply into deep water, and hammerheads patrol the outer edge of the reef particularly in the early morning. The beach on Rasdhoo island is characteristic of a small local Maldivian island: a sandy shore around a palm-fringed, low-lying island with a tight-knit permanent community. The atoll has minimal resort development, making it one of the more authentic local-island experiences accessible within a few hours of Malé.

## 6. Hithadhoo Beach, Addu Atoll

Addu Atoll is the southernmost atoll of the Maldives, just south of the equator, and has a distinct geography from the rest of the country: several of its islands are connected by a causeway running for about 25 kilometres, creating one of the few places in the Maldives where it is possible to drive between islands. Hithadhoo is the main settlement and has beaches on its western coast facing into the Indian Ocean. Addu has a different cultural character from the northern atolls — its proximity to Sri Lanka and its history as a British Royal Air Force base during World War II give it a distinct identity. The Addu Atoll's reefs are among the least dived in the Maldives because of the distance from Malé.

## 7. Veligandu Island, North Ari Atoll

Veligandu is a small resort island in the North Ari Atoll known for its sandbar — a temporary white-sand strip that emerges from the sea at low tide and extends some distance from the island into the shallow lagoon. Sandbars of this type are characteristic of the Maldivian atoll system: transient formations of pure white sand surrounded by shallow turquoise water, visible from above as luminous shapes in the deep blue of the Indian Ocean. Veligandu's sandbar is reliably photogenic and has become one of the defining images of Maldivian beach photography. The resort island itself is small and intimate, with bungalows directly on the beach.

## 8. Fodhdhoo Beach, Noonu Atoll

Fodhdhoo is a local island in the Noonu Atoll, a northern atoll that receives fewer visitors than the central Maldives. The Noonu Atoll has recently gained attention for its whale shark sightings in the surrounding channels, and the local diving and snorkelling community around Fodhdhoo and neighbouring islands has developed accordingly. The beach is white sand and the water crystal clear, with the atoll reef accessible by snorkelling from shore. The Noonu Atoll is accessible by domestic flight from Malé to Maafaru Airport, a journey of about 40 minutes, making it practical as a base despite the northern location.

## 9. Mathiveri Beach, North Ari Atoll

Mathiveri is a local island in the North Ari Atoll with a beach reputation that has grown steadily through word of mouth among Maldives independent travellers. The island has a particularly long, clean beach — unusually generous in a country where local island beaches are often modest in size — and the snorkelling from the beachfront housereef is considered excellent. The North Ari Atoll has a high density of resort islands relative to local islands, making Mathiveri something of an independent traveller outlier surrounded by luxury properties.

## 10. Dhigurah Beach, South Ari Atoll

Dhigurah is one of the longest local islands in the Maldives — the name means "long island" in Dhivehi — and its beach stretches for nearly three kilometres, an extraordinary length by Maldivian standards. The South Ari Atoll is one of the best places in the Maldives for whale shark encounters, with aggregations occurring particularly around the southern tip of the atoll from June to November. Dhigurah's guesthouses have built their business model partly around these whale shark trips, which operate year-round. The beach is wide, sandy, and the water quality excellent; the combination of beach access, long swimming strand, and world-class wildlife encounters makes Dhigurah one of the most compelling local island destinations in the country.
