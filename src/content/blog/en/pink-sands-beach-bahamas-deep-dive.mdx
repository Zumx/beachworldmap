Pink Sands Beach on Harbour Island (locally called Briland) is 5 kilometres of Atlantic-facing beach on the east side of a 5-kilometre island 3 miles off the north tip of Eleuthera in the Bahamas. The sand is pink. Not faintly pink in photographs taken at golden hour, not pinkish if you look very carefully — actually, visibly pink in full midday light, confirming what all those travel magazine lists have been saying about it for decades.

It is a genuinely exceptional beach in almost every respect: the colour, the water quality, the Atlantic surf character, the width of the beach at any tide, the consistency of the pinkness from one end of the 5 kilometres to the other, and the relative tranquility maintained by the island's limited accommodation capacity.

## Why the sand is pink

The pink colouring comes from Foraminifera — specifically, from a species called Homotrema rubrum, a microscopic single-celled marine organism that builds a pink-to-red hard shell and lives on the underside of coral reef structures. When these organisms die, their shells accumulate on the ocean floor, are broken down by wave action, and mix with white coral and quartz sand to produce the pink colour.

The Foraminifera concentration in the waters around Harbour Island is high enough, and the mixing ratio with white sand is right enough, that the beach achieves a consistent and visible pink. Wet sand at the waterline shows the colour most clearly; dry sand higher on the beach is paler but still noticeably tinted compared to a white sand beach.

This is not unusual sand tinted by minerals — pink sand beaches of this type exist in perhaps a dozen locations worldwide (Bermuda, Elafonisi in Crete, and a few others), but the Harbour Island example is consistently the most vivid and the most extensive.

## How to get there

Harbour Island is small and car-free for practical purposes — the island is 5 kilometres long and golf carts are the standard transport. Getting there involves flying to North Eleuthera airport (ELH), accessible from Nassau (30 minutes by Bahamasair or charter) or from several US cities including Miami and Fort Lauderdale with small charter carriers. From North Eleuthera airport, a water taxi to Harbour Island takes about 10 minutes from the ferry dock at Three Island Dock.

Alternatively, drive or take a taxi from ELH down to the ferry dock near the Governor's Harbour area and take the ferry across.

From Nassau by ferry, the journey is longer (the mail boat runs twice weekly and takes several hours); the water taxi is the standard visitor option.

## The beach character

Pink Sands Beach faces due east into the Atlantic. The surf is Atlantic-grade — consistent small to moderate waves in ordinary weather, larger sets after storms in the Bahamas. The beach is 50 to 80 metres wide at normal tide and does not narrow significantly at high tide, which gives it a spacious quality even at busy times. The length of 5 kilometres means that self-distribution of visitors leaves long stretches relatively empty outside the stretch immediately in front of the main hotels.

The water is typically very clear, Bahamian blue-green in the shallows and deep blue offshore. There is no lagoon between the beach and the open sea — this is ocean-facing Atlantic beach, which means genuine surf character and waves to body-surf in on any day with swell.

Undertow and rip currents exist as at any Atlantic beach. The beach is not patrolled by lifeguards; swimming awareness is the visitor's responsibility.

## Harbour Island itself

Harbour Island's western side faces a calm harbour and the island of Eleuthera. Dunmore Town, the island's main settlement, is a colonial village of pastel-painted clapboard houses, a couple of good restaurants, and shops. The island has been a discreet upmarket destination since the 1970s, and the boutique hotels that define its accommodation are the Pink Sands Resort (which gave the beach its international name), Rock House, the Landing, and a number of private villa rentals.

The island's food scene — concentrating in Dunmore Town and at the hotel restaurants — is above average for a small Bahamian island: fresh seafood, good cocktails, and a colonial-Caribbean character in the architecture that is becoming rare in the wider Bahamas.

Golf cart hire is the standard way to get around the island.

## When to visit

The Bahamas season runs November through April, when the northeast trade winds are consistent, humidity is lower, and the risk of tropical storms is minimal. Harbour Island is pleasant year-round — the trade winds moderate summer heat — but June through November is the Atlantic hurricane season and while the island is not frequently directly struck, monitoring conditions before travel is advisable.

December through March is the peak season; book accommodation well in advance.

## Accommodation and cost

Harbour Island is a boutique luxury destination by any measure. The Pink Sands Resort, which helped establish the island's international reputation in the 1990s, occupies a beachfront position and has remained the aspirational address on the island. Rock House and the Landing are smaller properties with characterful colonial architecture facing the western harbour rather than the Atlantic beach. A number of private villas are available for weekly rental through local agents; these are the most cost-effective option for groups.

Budget accommodation is not the Harbour Island model. The island's accommodation economics reflect both its remoteness (everything arrives by boat or small aircraft, inflating supply costs) and its deliberately low-density tourism approach (the island has limited accommodation capacity, which keeps demand for available rooms high). If the budget is a primary constraint, Eleuthera on the mainland — accessible by the same ferry from the North Eleuthera ferry dock — has more affordable options with day-trip access to Harbour Island.

## Comparison to other pink sand beaches

Harbour Island consistently occupies the top position when pink sand beaches are ranked globally, and the comparison to competitors supports this ranking. Bermuda's beaches are pink and beautiful but require navigating Bermuda's own accommodation costs and limited inter-island transport for those not staying in the immediate Hamilton area. Elafonisi in Crete has a subtler pink hue and is accessible by road from Chania — a more affordable destination overall, but the beach experience is less exclusive. Budelli's Spiaggia Rosa in Sardinia has been closed to landing since 1994. The Komodo Pink Beach in Indonesia is accessible but requires a multi-day trip from Bali or Labuan Bajo.

What Harbour Island offers that no other pink sand beach matches is the combination of intensity of pink, length of continuous beach (5 kilometres), quality of Atlantic surf conditions, and a supporting destination — Dunmore Town — that justifies the journey independently of the beach. The pink sand is the primary draw but not the only one.

## Practical details

Golf carts are rented by the half-day or full day from several operators near the ferry dock. The island is small enough to walk end-to-end in about an hour, but a golf cart makes it easy to transport beach gear and to reach the quieter southern end of the beach where the crowd thins. Bicycles are also available for hire.

The beach has no permanent commercial infrastructure — no food vendors or hire operations set up on the sand itself. Beach towels, supplies, and food must come from the hotel or from stalls in Dunmore Town. This is part of what keeps the beach in the condition it is in; the absence of concession infrastructure means the sand is clean and the experience is quiet compared to heavily commercialised resort beaches.
