Coral reefs are among the most biodiverse ecosystems on Earth, occupying less than 1 percent of the ocean floor while supporting an estimated 25 percent of all marine species. They are also among the most sensitive ecosystems to human contact, temperature change, and chemical pollution. The world's reefs are under significant stress from ocean warming, ocean acidification, and local human impact including physical damage from tourism.

Snorkelling is a low-impact activity compared to most beach tourism, but at scale — with millions of snorkellers visiting reef systems annually — the cumulative effect of individual contact events, sunscreen chemicals, and anchor damage is significant. What individual snorkellers do or choose not to do matters.

## The buoyancy problem

The single most common cause of reef damage by snorkellers is standing on coral or grabbing at coral heads for support. This is almost always unintentional: a snorkeller who is not comfortable in the water reaches down to steady themselves and puts their weight on a coral head. A single contact event can break off coral branches that took decades to grow. Coral tissue crushed by physical contact does not regenerate in the spot where the damage occurred.

The solution is to achieve neutral buoyancy before entering the reef area and to develop the ability to hover without touching the bottom. This sounds technical but is achievable with a small amount of practice in a pool or calm, open water before approaching a reef. The specific adjustments: wear less weighting (or no weight belt), practise slow, shallow fin kicks that do not stir up sediment, and keep hands close to the body to avoid instinctive grabbing.

A snorkel vest or buoyancy aid maintains horizontal position in the water and prevents inexperienced swimmers from sinking to the reef. Most dive and snorkel operations in well-managed reef areas now provide vests as standard rather than optional equipment.

## Fin technique near the reef

Fin kicks near coral produce two problems: the physical risk of fin-tip contact with coral, and the turbulence that lifts sediment onto coral tissue. Sediment smothers coral polyps and can kill a coral head if the covering is sustained. A technique called the flutter kick — small, fast kicks with a relatively straight leg — produces less turbulence than the wide, slow fin sweeps used in open water. When approaching close to coral, tucking fins up and using hands for slow directional control reduces both contact risk and turbulence.

Never stand on the reef, even on apparently dead or rubble sections. Dead coral rubble may support recovering juvenile coral recruits that are not visible to the eye from above water.

## Sunscreen and reef chemistry

Oxybenzone and octinoxate are chemical UV filters used in most conventional sunscreens that have been shown in laboratory conditions to have toxic effects on coral larvae and zooxanthellae (the symbiotic algae that live in coral tissue) at concentrations substantially lower than those found in reef areas with heavy visitor traffic. Hawaii, Palau, the US Virgin Islands, and several other jurisdictions have banned sunscreens containing these chemicals in response to this evidence.

Mineral sunscreens using titanium dioxide or zinc oxide as their active UV-blocking ingredients do not have the same documented toxicity to coral. They are less water-resistant, thicker to apply, and often leave a visible white film, but they are the responsible choice for reef snorkelling.

The practical response is to apply sunscreen at least 20 minutes before entering the water (most chemical sunscreen rinses off as soon as you enter), and to choose a certified reef-safe formulation using only mineral active ingredients. UPF-rated swim shirts provide an alternative solution for body coverage that requires no sunscreen on covered areas.

## Equipment handling

Ensure all snorkel equipment is properly adjusted before entering the water — a mask that needs repositioning or a snorkel that is clearing water constantly causes the snorkeller to reach for support on the reef. Rented fins that fit poorly create handling problems in the water. Take time to adjust equipment in shallow, sand-bottomed areas before reaching the reef.

Do not feed fish on reef snorkelling sites. Feeding fish changes their behaviour patterns in ways that disturb the reef ecosystem and habituates them to human presence in ways that can increase aggressive behaviour toward later snorkellers.

## The cumulative impact question

Research on popular reef snorkelling sites including Australia's Great Barrier Reef, the Thai Similan Islands, and Hawaiian reef systems documents measurable physical degradation at high-traffic sites compared to low-traffic sites with similar water quality. The damage is not spectacular — it is the gradual wearing away of coral at the snorkeller-depth zone, the fragmentation of branching coral by repeated contact, and the smothering of recruitment surfaces by sediment.

Well-managed snorkel sites rotate access between reef sections, limit the number of snorkellers at a given location at one time, and have trained guides who both teach the techniques above and physically prevent reef contact when they see it occurring. Choosing reef access through operators with certified reef management practices rather than unguided access makes a measurable difference at sites under pressure.

## At-risk reefs to know about

Maya Bay in Thailand was closed from 2018 to 2022 specifically because of tourist damage to the reef; it has partially recovered. Lady Elliot Island on the Great Barrier Reef operates a strict snorkel management programme that has maintained reef condition under significant visitor numbers. The house reefs at Maldivian resorts vary enormously in condition depending on how much boat anchor damage and snorkeller contact they have absorbed.

## Coral bleaching and what to do if you see it

Coral bleaching is the expulsion of zooxanthellae (the symbiotic algae in coral tissue) triggered by stress — primarily elevated sea temperature. A bleached coral appears white or pale and is in a weakened state; it can recover if the stressful conditions pass, or die if the stress continues. Widespread bleaching events, caused by marine heatwaves, have affected the Great Barrier Reef, the Maldives, and reefs throughout the Pacific since the 1980s with increasing frequency and severity.

If you observe widespread bleaching while snorkelling, report it to the dive operator, the marine park authority, or to citizen science platforms like CoralWatch or the Reef Check programme. Systematic documentation of bleaching events — timing, extent, depth — provides data that informs reef management decisions. CoralWatch's Coral Health Chart allows snorkellers and divers to record coral colour against a standardised chart and submit observations to a global database.

## Choosing reef access responsibly

The simplest reef protection decision a snorkeller can make is to choose guided snorkel trips over unguided access at reef sites where monitoring is in place. Well-run guided snorkel operations at significant reef sites — Ras Mohammed in Egypt, the Similan Islands in Thailand, Lady Elliot in Australia, Ningaloo Reef in Western Australia — brief all participants on behaviour, have guides in the water monitoring for contact, and rotate snorkel sites to reduce pressure on specific reef sections.

The fee for a guided trip includes not just the guide's time but the regulatory framework that limits access, invests in reef monitoring, and funds ranger operations. At busy reef sites worldwide, the economic model that funds protection comes partly from visitor fees — choosing guided access rather than independent entry is not just ecologically better; it actively funds conservation.
