Pebble beaches occupy a distinct niche in Mediterranean beach culture. Visitors accustomed to sand often overlook them — incorrectly. Pebble beaches in the Mediterranean have two qualities that sandy beaches frequently cannot match: the water is typically clearer, because fine sand is not kicked into suspension by swimmer movement, and they tend to attract fewer mass-tourism crowds, because lying on stones is less immediately comfortable than lying on sand.

For snorkelling, cliff-side beauty, and a beach character that feels more elemental than a groomed resort beach, the Mediterranean's pebble and cobble beaches offer some of the region's best experiences.

## 1. Cala Goloritzè, Sardinia, Italy

Cala Goloritzè is reachable only by boat from Arbatax or Santa Maria Navarrese, or by a 2-hour hiking descent from the plateau above. The beach is a small crescent of white pebbles and boulders at the base of limestone cliffs, with a sea arch at the north end. The water is a transparent blue-green owing to the limestone geology and the depth immediately offshore. It is a protected natural monument and one of the most photographed landscapes in Sardinia. The pebbles are fine enough to sit on comfortably with a mat; the water entry over the rocks is awkward but the snorkelling around the base of the cliffs is exceptional. Cala Goloritzè is listed in the GeoJSON database.

## 2. Plaka, Naxos, Greece

Plaka on the west coast of Naxos is a long beach of fine white sand that grades to larger pebbles and smooth cobbles at the northern end near the village of Orkos. The pebble section receives fewer visitors than the southern sand, the water stays clearer through the afternoon, and the mix of sand and pebble gives it a more varied character than either type alone. Naxos's fertile interior provides good food supplies to the beach tavernas that distinguish the island's food scene from the more tourist-dependent islands. Plaka is in the map data.

## 3. Praia da Marinha, Algarve, Portugal

Praia da Marinha is not strictly Mediterranean — it is on the Atlantic coast of the Algarve — but its geology, water clarity, and character place it in the pebble-and-rock-pool category rather than the wide-sand Atlantic beach category. The beach sits at the base of red and ochre limestone cliffs and consists of smooth pebbles and rock platforms with sea caves on either side. The snorkelling around the rock formations is among the best on the Portuguese coast. Access is via 200 steps from the clifftop car park.

## 4. Vernazza and Manarola, Cinque Terre, Italy

The five villages of the Cinque Terre on the Ligurian coast of Italy have small pebble beaches and concrete swimming platforms at the base of terraced vineyards. Vernazza's small pebble beach below the castle is the most naturally beautiful; Manarola's rock platform with a jumping board is the most used. These are not beaches for lounging — they are small, busy, and surrounded by the infrastructure of a village. They are, however, beautiful in the way that the Cinque Terre entire is beautiful, and swimming in the Ligurian Sea from them in evening light is a specific experience.

## 5. Stiniva, Hvar, Croatia

Stiniva is a small cove on the south coast of Hvar island, accessible by boat or by a steep 20-minute descent from the plateau above. The narrow entrance to the cove is framed by limestone walls; inside, the beach is pebbles in an enclosed bay. The water colour inside Stiniva — sheltered from open sea movement, over a pale pebble floor — is the defining turquoise of the Adriatic. It is crowded in peak season; arriving by boat before 9am or after 4pm provides relative quiet. Stiniva is in the map database.

## 6. Nice and the Côte d'Azur, France

Nice's Promenade des Anglais is one of the world's most famous urban beaches and it is entirely pebbles — smooth, fist-sized grey and white pebbles deposited by alpine rivers. This is the defining aesthetic of the Côte d'Azur. The Niçoise have adapted: pebble mats, inflatable kneeling cushions, and water shoes are standard beach gear. The water in the Baie des Anges is exceptionally clear in summer and warm by mid-July. The pebble character keeps the beach cleaner than comparable sandy urban beaches and the water visibility is consistently good.

## 7. Lubenice Beach (Sveti Ivan), Cres, Croatia

Lubenice on the island of Cres is a medieval hilltop village above the Adriatic. The beach below — Sveti Ivan — is accessed by a 30-minute descent through olive groves and is a broad pebble bay with exceptionally clear water. Cres is one of the less-visited larger Croatian islands, and Lubenice's relative inaccessibility keeps it quieter than comparable beaches on Hvar or Brac. The combination of the medieval village visible from the water, the surrounding limestone cliffs, and the green-blue Kvarner Gulf water makes it one of the most beautiful places in Croatia.

## 8. Elafonisi, Crete, Greece

Elafonisi is technically a tidal island on Crete's southwest coast, reached by wading through shallow water less than knee-deep. The beach itself has both sand and smooth pebble sections, and the sand has a pinkish tint from crushed shells. The pebble areas are on the eastern side of the island. The water colour here — shallow, over white and pink pebble, with the light of the Libyan Sea — is exceptional. Elafonisi is listed in the database and is reached from Chania by bus or rental car, about an hour's drive on the south-coast road. It gets extremely busy in July and August.

## 9. Sakarun Beach, Dugi Otok, Croatia

Sakarun on the island of Dugi Otok in the Zadar archipelago is a beach of fine white pebbles and some sand in a sheltered bay with remarkably clear water. Dugi Otok ("Long Island") is accessible by ferry from Zadar, and Sakarun is at the island's northern end. The water is so clear and shallow that boats can be seen to float above the bottom from aerial photographs; the gradual depth gradient and the sheltered bay make it excellent for snorkelling and gentle swimming. The island has limited accommodation, which keeps visitor numbers lower than on the Dalmatian islands further south. Sakarun is in the map database.

## 10. Ksamil, Albania

Ksamil's beaches are pebble-and-sand mix with some sections predominantly smooth white stones. The shallow water between the beach and the offshore islands of Ksamil archipelago has a clarity that rivals the best of the Adriatic and Aegean — partly because the area around Butrint National Park has limited industrial development, partly because the limestone and clean Atlantic-influenced water creates good conditions. Ksamil is consistently less expensive than comparable beaches in Croatia or Greece, which is a practical reason to visit alongside the beach quality.

## The case for pebble

Water shoes and a portable pebble mat resolve the main discomfort of pebble beaches. Once those adaptations are made, the experience competes directly with sand beaches on most criteria and exceeds them on water clarity and crowd density. The Mediterranean's finest pebble beaches are also frequently its most scenically dramatic.

A practical packing note: a thin inflatable sit-mat — designed for camping — converts any pebble beach into a comfortable surface for lying down, adds almost no weight to a beach bag, and lasts for many seasons. Combined with water shoes for rocky entry, it makes the entire pebble beach category as comfortable as sand. The clear water that pebble beaches routinely produce is a genuine quality advantage that no amount of sand can replicate in a busy summer bay.
