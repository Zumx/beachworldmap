The Côte d'Azur — the French Riviera — stretches approximately 115 kilometres from Toulon in the west to the Italian border at Menton in the east, encompassing the most globally recognizable beach culture in continental Europe. The name, coined by writer Stéphane Liégeard in 1887, was already attached to a coast that Queen Victoria wintered on, Matisse painted, and Fitzgerald immortalized. Today the Riviera oscillates between superyacht spectacle, democratically crowded public beaches, and quiet calanque coves accessible only by boat or footpath. The water is Mediterranean: warm (25–27°C in August), clear, and tideless. Sand and pebble beaches alternate depending on geology; the eastern coast from Nice to Menton is predominantly pebble while the western stretches around Saint-Tropez are sandy. Most public beaches are free; the private beach clubs, which have occupied concessions since the 1930s, charge for sun loungers and service. The season runs late April through October, though the resort towns retain year-round life.

## 1. Plage de Pampelonne, Saint-Tropez

Plage de Pampelonne is one of the most famous beaches in the world — the 4.5-kilometre strand south of Saint-Tropez that Brigitte Bardot made internationally famous when Roger Vadim filmed parts of And God Created Woman here in 1956. The beach is divided between public areas and a string of private clubs — Club 55, Nikki Beach, Tahiti Plage — that charge for service and maintain the tradition of see-and-be-seen beach culture. The sand is golden and fine, the water warm, and the views south toward Cap Camarat are handsome. Saint-Tropez itself is reached by ferry from Saint-Raphaël in peak season, as road access to the peninsula becomes impossibly congested in July.

## 2. Plage de Paloma, Saint-Jean-Cap-Ferrat

Plage de Paloma is a small, sheltered cove on the Saint-Jean-Cap-Ferrat peninsula between Nice and Monaco, reachable via a path from the upper village. The peninsular setting means the beach faces east across the bay toward Beaulieu-sur-Mer, providing calm conditions and early morning sun. Water clarity here is exceptional — the depth of the bay and the absence of any river run-off contribute to visibility of 15 metres or more. A small floating platform is anchored offshore in summer. The peninsula is ringed by the most expensive private real estate in France, occupied by Russian oligarchs, European royalty, and American billionaires, giving even a walk to the public beach an unusual quality.

## 3. Plage de Passable, Saint-Jean-Cap-Ferrat

Plage de Passable is on the western side of the Cap-Ferrat peninsula, facing Nice Bay rather than the open sea, which produces calmer water and gentler conditions than the eastern beaches. The beach is managed as a partly private club at its southern end, with public access on the northern section. A wide concrete terrace above the beach offers tables for lunch, and the afternoon view of Nice's white tower blocks across the blue of the Baie des Anges — Bay of Angels — is as quintessential as the Riviera gets. The coastal footpath circling the entire Cap-Ferrat peninsula takes about 90 minutes and connects both main beaches.

## 4. Plage de la Garoupe, Antibes

Plage de la Garoupe on the Cap d'Antibes peninsula was where Gerald and Sara Murphy — the American couple who inspired Fitzgerald's Tender Is the Night — first cleared seaweed and began sunbathing on the French Riviera in the 1920s, effectively inventing the summer season at a time when the coast was used only in winter. The beach, sheltered in a bay on the south-east side of the cape, remains one of the finest on the Riviera: wide sandy arc, clear water, private clubs on both sides of a public strip, and views south to the Esterel mountains. The lighthouse and chapel of Notre-Dame de la Garoupe above are worth the short walk from the beach.

## 5. Plage de la Croisette, Cannes

Plage de la Croisette is the sandy boulevard beach running below the famous Croisette promenade in Cannes, stretching about 1.5 kilometres from the Palais des Festivals to the Palm Beach casino. It is backed by the grandest hotels on the French Riviera — the Carlton, Majestic, and Martinez — whose private beach clubs take up most of the strand. The Palme d'Or film festival every May fills the beach with celebrities and cameras; the rest of the summer fills it with determined sun-seekers. Public beach sections are available between the clubs. The Lérins Islands — Île Sainte-Marguerite and Île Saint-Honorat — are reachable by ferry in 15 minutes.

## 6. Plage de Monaco, Monte Carlo

The beaches associated with Monaco are small, managed concessions set into rocky terrain with few sandy beaches of natural origin. The principal organised beach — attached to the Monte-Carlo Beach Hotel at Roquebrune-Cap-Martin, just east of the principality — has been operating since 1929 and maintains an Olympic pool, private club service, and access to a small sea terrace on a rocky point. An artificial sandy beach extension provides conventional bathing. The setting, with the Monte-Carlo skyline visible to the west and the Italian coast to the east, is unique. Access is by shuttle or taxi from Monaco.

## 7. Plage de Juan-les-Pins, Antibes

Juan-les-Pins is a resort on the southern side of the Cap d'Antibes bay, and its beach was, alongside Pampelonne, the original setting for the Mediterranean summer culture the Murphys introduced to American expatriates in the 1920s. The beach curves in a long arc of coarse sand, lined by umbrella pines and seasonal clubs, with views across the bay toward the old town of Antibes and its Vauban fortifications. The town hosts the internationally known Jazz à Juan festival each July, during which performances take place in the pine grove immediately behind the beach. Water is calm thanks to the bay's south-facing orientation.

## 8. Plage de Beaulieu, Beaulieu-sur-Mer

Beaulieu-sur-Mer is a small resort between Nice and Cap-Ferrat that benefits from the most temperate microclimate on the French Riviera — the mountains above funnel warm air down while blocking northern cold, making it measurably warmer in winter than Nice. The beach is a sequence of private clubs along a narrow pebble and sandy-pebble strand, backed by Belle Époque hotels and the extravagant Villa Kérylos, a reconstruction of a classical Greek villa completed in 1908. ## 9. Plage de Rayol, Le Rayol-Canadel

Plage de Rayol at Le Rayol-Canadel in the Var, west of Saint-Tropez, is one of the least-spoiled sandy beaches on the entire Riviera, backed by the Domaine du Rayol — a 20-hectare botanical garden of global coastal plants managed by the Conservatoire du Littoral. The combination of a clean, quiet beach and an extraordinary garden immediately adjacent creates an experience quite different from the resort beaches further east. Snorkelling trails have been laid out in the bay, where posidonia meadows and rocky reefs support a rich fish population. The Var coast around Le Rayol remains relatively low-key by Riviera standards despite being only 30 kilometres from Saint-Tropez.

## 10. Plage de Cassis, Cassis

Plage de Cassis sits at the western edge of the Riviera proper, in a small port town below the white Calanques plateau between Marseille and Toulon. Three small beaches flank the working fishing harbour, which still dispatches boats daily for urchins and rockfish. Cassis produces the most celebrated white wine in Provence — its AOC Cassis blanc, made mainly from Marsanne and Clairette, is drunk locally with bouillabaisse and grilled sea bass. The Calanques National Park begins immediately east of town, where the limestone inlet beaches are accessible by foot, boat, or kayak. The 20-kilometre coastal footpath to the first calanque, En-Vau, is one of the finest day walks in southern France.
