The Gulf of Mexico coastline of the United States runs from the Texas-Mexico border at Brownsville northward around the Texas arc, through Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, and down through the Florida Panhandle and peninsula — a total of roughly 2,700 kilometres. The Gulf is fundamentally different from the US Atlantic coast in its physics: it is an almost enclosed sea, the tides are weak (often less than 60 centimetres diurnal range), the water is warmer (reaching 30°C or more in summer in shallow areas), and the wave energy is considerably lower. The result is a beach experience defined by flat water, high heat, fine quartz sand on the Florida and Alabama sections, and a culture of fishing, boating, and unhurried family recreation that has changed relatively little since the 1950s. Texas's coast is browner and more wind-exposed, backed by barrier islands and flat coastal prairie; Louisiana's coast is largely marshy and its beaches are few; Mississippi and Alabama have modest but underrated strands; Florida's Panhandle, from Pensacola to Panama City, contains the finest white-sand beaches in the continental United States.

## 1. Matagorda Beach, Matagorda Bay, Texas

Matagorda Beach occupies the barrier peninsula enclosing Matagorda Bay, one of the few stretches of the Texas coast accessible without crossing private ranch land or military reservations. The beach is wide, flat, and driven by wind rather than waves — windblown sand accumulation has created low dunes behind the upper beach. Fishing is the primary activity: red drum, flounder, and speckled trout are taken from the surf year-round by Texas anglers who drive trucks directly onto the beach. The Colorado River enters the bay a few kilometres to the north, and the nutrient-rich mix of fresh and salt water makes the area biologically productive. Beach access is via State Highway 60 through Bay City.

## 2. University Beach, Galveston, Texas

University Beach is a stretch of Galveston Island's Gulf-facing shore managed by the University of Texas Marine Sciences Institute, which uses the adjacent wetlands and near-shore area for research into Gulf Coast ecology. The beach itself is open to the public and retains a more natural character than the heavily developed sections of Galveston Island to the east. Sand here is medium-brown quartz with a significant shell component, the water is warm but carries the low-visibility brown characteristic of the Texas Gulf coast. Seabirds are plentiful, and the adjacent Galveston Island State Park preserves a remnant of the coastal prairie ecosystem that once backed the entire Texas shoreline.

## 3. Rockport Beach Park, Rockport, Texas

Rockport Beach Park on Aransas Bay — technically a bay rather than open Gulf — is a designated Blue Wave beach: one of the cleanest and most managed in Texas. The sheltered bay setting means water is warmer and calmer than the open Gulf beaches, with conditions comparable to a large lake. The town of Rockport is a serious birding destination: the Aransas National Wildlife Refuge across the bay hosts the only naturally occurring flock of whooping cranes in the world during the winter migration from October through March. Combining beach time at Rockport with the wildlife refuge and the excellent Gulf Coast seafood in town makes for a more varied day than most Texas coast visits.

## 4. MacFadden Beach, Sea Rim State Park, Texas

MacFadden Beach is within Sea Rim State Park near Port Arthur in extreme south-east Texas, at the edge of the Gulf where it meets the Louisiana marshes. The park encompasses both a narrow Gulf beach and an extensive marshland interior, and visiting during the spring shorebird migration (April through May) is one of the finer natural history experiences on the Texas coast. The beach itself is narrow and sometimes storm-battered, but the solitude — the park is lightly visited compared to Galveston — and the marshland setting create a genuinely wild coastal atmosphere. A small campground with basic facilities overlooks the Gulf.

## 5. Lighthouse Beach, Port Aransas, Texas

Lighthouse Beach in Port Aransas on Mustang Island is one of the most popular day beaches on the Texas coast, accessible by a free ferry crossing from the mainland. The beach is wide, with brown-white sand and the warm, shallow Gulf water that characterises this section of coast. The town of Port Aransas is a working fishing community with a long history of sport fishing — the record black drum taken from Texas Gulf waters was caught nearby. Seasonal rentals of beach chairs, umbrellas, and ATVs are available near the lighthouse, and the beach is one of the few in Texas where driving on the sand is legal and commonly practised.

## 6. Corpus Christi North Beach, Corpus Christi, Texas

North Beach in Corpus Christi occupies a narrow strip of land between the Corpus Christi Bay and the Nueces Bay, across the Harbour Bridge from downtown. It is an urban beach in the truest sense — steps from the USS Lexington museum and the Texas State Aquarium — and functions as a swimming and recreation area backed by city infrastructure. The bay water is calm and warm, and the beach is managed for cleanliness. The Corpus Christi Bayfront, across the ship channel, is the more famous city vista; North Beach serves the more practical purpose of putting visitors in the water within minutes of the downtown hotels.

## 7. Quarantine Shore, Galveston Island, Texas

Quarantine Shore is on the bay side of Galveston Island near the western end, where the 19th-century quarantine station for ships entering the port was located. The bay shore is flat, shallow, and very warm in summer, favoured by families with young children because of the lake-like water conditions. Sunset views from the bay side of Galveston — facing west across the water toward the mainland — are among the best on the Texas coast. The Galveston Island State Park adjacent includes beach camping, kayak access, and wildlife observation platforms over the coastal marsh.

## 8. Shell Beach, Cameron Parish, Louisiana

Shell Beach on the shore of Lake Borgne in Cameron Parish, Louisiana, is a saltwater lake beach rather than an open Gulf beach, and it sits at the edge of one of the most ecologically important coastal wetlands in North America. Access requires navigating the coastal road networks of the Louisiana delta, which were largely rebuilt after hurricane damage from Katrina (2005) and Ida (2021). The beach is used primarily by local fishing communities; recreational beach use is secondary to the fishing and crabbing culture that defines this stretch of coast. The surrounding Biloxi Marsh and the nearby Chandeleur Islands constitute extraordinary wildfowl habitat.

## 9. Hackberry Beach, Cameron Parish, Louisiana

Hackberry Beach near the town of Hackberry in Cameron Parish is a quiet Gulf-adjacent beach in a region of Louisiana that sees almost no tourism. The surrounding landscape is flat coastal prairie and marsh, the beach is backed by the Intracoastal Waterway levee system, and the water is warm, shallow, and brown. The area is better known for sport fishing — the Cameron Prairie area is one of the finest duck hunting territories in the US — but the beach exists and provides a genuinely local coastal experience unlike anything on the Florida or Texas shores. Gas, food, and basic facilities are available in Hackberry town, about 10 minutes from the beach.

## 10. Cypremort Point Beach, St. Mary Parish, Louisiana

Cypremort Point State Beach at the end of the Cypremort Point peninsula in St. Mary Parish is the most accessible Gulf beach in central Louisiana, reached via Highway 83 through cane fields and live-oak forests draped in Spanish moss. The beach itself is a narrow strip of brown sand on a protected bay — conditions are lake-like rather than oceanic, the water is warm and shallow, and the setting in the surrounding bayou landscape is distinctly Louisianian rather than conventionally beachy. A small state-managed facility with restrooms and picnic shelters operates in season. Summer weekends attract families from Lafayette and Morgan City.
