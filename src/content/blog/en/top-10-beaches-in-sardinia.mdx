Sardinia is the second largest island in the Mediterranean and arguably the one whose beaches most consistently justify the superlatives heaped upon them. The island's granite, limestone, and basalt geology has been sculpted over millions of years into an extraordinary variety of coastal forms: caves that collect turquoise water under limestone arches, white quartz-sand beaches backed by pink granite boulders, and sea stacks where peregrine falcons nest above the surf. Water clarity is among the best in the whole Mediterranean — visibility of 30 metres or more is reported in several bays — owing to a combination of minimal river discharge, deep offshore currents, and relatively low population density. The coastline runs approximately 1,850 kilometres, and access ranges from resort infrastructure rivalling anywhere in Europe (the Costa Smeralda) to beaches reachable only by hiking or small boat. The peak season runs June through September. In May and October the beaches are usable and far less crowded. The Mistral wind, arriving from the north-west, creates chop on the north and west coasts while keeping conditions calmer on the east and south.

## 1. Cala Goloritzé, Gulf of Orosei

Cala Goloritzé is accessible only on foot via a trail descending from the plateau above — the walk takes about 90 minutes one-way from the trailhead at Baunei — or by small boat from the port of Santa Maria Navarrese. This inaccessibility has kept it in extraordinary condition: white pebbles and sand, water the colour of lapis lazuli, and a slender white spire of rock called the Aguglia rising 143 metres from the cliff edge above the beach. It was declared a natural monument in 1993 and is managed by the regional authority with strict visitor limits in summer. Rock climbers attempt the Aguglia; swimmers and snorkellers are limited to the beach and the immediate shallow water.

## 2. Cala Luna, Gulf of Orosei

Cala Luna is a crescent of white sand and pebble at the mouth of a gorge carved by a small freshwater stream, backed by dramatic arched caves where fresh water seeps through the limestone. The caves are walk-in at low tide and contain stalactites that have grown through millennia of water action above sea level. The beach is reachable by boat from Cala Gonone (20 minutes) or by a five-hour coastal hike along the famous Selvaggio Blu route. The combination of caves, cliffs, fresh water, and turquoise sea makes Cala Luna one of the most distinctive beach environments in the Mediterranean.

## 3. Spiaggia del Principe, Costa Smeralda

Spiaggia del Principe — the Prince's Beach — on the Costa Smeralda north of Porto Cervo gets its name from the Aga Khan IV, who effectively created the Costa Smeralda resort complex in the 1960s and is said to have considered this beach his personal favourite. It lies between pink granite headlands, the sand is exceptionally fine and pale, and the water moves through a spectrum from transparent turquoise at the shore to deep blue offshore. July and August bring boats anchoring in the bay and beach clubs in operation; May and September are considerably quieter. The Costa Smeralda marina at Porto Cervo is about 10 minutes by car.

## 4. La Pelosa, Stintino

La Pelosa at Stintino, on the north-western tip of Sardinia, looks genuinely Caribbean: a shallow lagoon of turquoise water, sand so pale it is almost white, and the silhouette of the Torre della Pelosa — a 16th-century Spanish watchtower — on an islet just offshore. Because the lagoon is very shallow over a wide area, the water warms quickly and stays unusually warm by Sardinian standards. Demand has grown so dramatically that the municipality introduced a daily visitor limit and advance booking requirement after 2020 to protect the sand from erosion. Access is via the coast road from Stintino, where a shuttle bus operates in peak season.

## 5. Cala Mariolu, Gulf of Orosei

Cala Mariolu is a south-facing cove on the Gulf of Orosei's limestone coast, accessible by boat from Cala Gonone in about 25 minutes. The beach divides into two sections separated by a rocky promontory, one predominantly white pebble and one mixed sand and pebble. The water here has been described by divers as some of the clearest in the Mediterranean, with visibility that allows the bottom to be seen at considerable depth simply by looking from the surface. Snorkelling around the rocky headlands reveals grouper, barracuda, and occasionally dolphins. Like most Gulf of Orosei beaches, it has no road access, which is entirely the point.

## 6. Cala Brandinchi, San Teodoro

Cala Brandinchi near San Teodoro on the north-eastern coast is nicknamed the Little Tahiti of Sardinia — an exaggeration, but understandable given the vivid turquoise of the shallow lagoon formed between the sand spit and the forested interior. The beach is backed by junipers and tamarisks that provide natural shade, a rarity on exposed Sardinian beaches. Water is calm and shallow over a wide area, making it one of the most child-friendly beaches on the island. A short path leads to the adjacent Lago Orientale di San Teodoro, a freshwater lagoon where flamingos feed in spring and autumn. San Teodoro town has good restaurants and nightlife.

## 7. Is Arutas, Oristano Province

Is Arutas is a beach composed almost entirely of quartz granules — not grains but actual small pieces of quartz crystal that make the beach white-bright and produce a crunching, almost glass-like texture underfoot. Removing sand is strictly prohibited, enforced by police checks at exits in summer, because the beach was being gradually depleted by souvenir collectors. The water is turquoise and shallow, set within the protected Sinis Peninsula. The Sinis is a fascinating peninsula with Phoenician and Nuragic ruins nearby, including the ancient city of Tharros. Access is from Riola Sardo, and shuttle buses from the village operate at peak times.

## 8. Cala Domestica, Buggerru

Cala Domestica is a fjord-like inlet on Sardinia's south-western coast, between Fluminimaggiore and Buggerru, enclosed by steep cliffs that shelter it from every wind direction except directly offshore. The name — Domestic Cove — reflects its sheltered, almost interior character. The beach is mixed sand and fine gravel, the water a vivid teal, and the cliffs above contain the ruins of a World War I lookout station and a cable railway used to load ore from the inland mines. A 20-minute walk from the road over a low headland leads to the equally fine Portixeddu beach to the south. Both beaches are considerably less visited than the Orosei and Costa Smeralda beaches.

## 9. Spiaggia di Tuerredda, Teulada

Spiaggia di Tuerredda near Teulada in the far south of Sardinia is widely rated as the finest beach in the island's southern half: an arc of white-pink sand in a broad bay sheltered by the Capo Teulada military zone headland, with water that runs from transparent crystal at the shore to deep blue at the 200-metre line. A small island — Isola di Tuerredda — sits 100 metres offshore and is reachable by wading or swimming at low tide. The beach was declared off-limits to construction and is accessible via a gravel road from the SS195. Shuttle buses from the parking area at high season.

## 10. Porto Giunco, Villasimius

Porto Giunco is a double beach on the headland of Capo Carbonara near Villasimius in south-eastern Sardinia, where the land narrows to a slender strip between the sea and the Notteri lagoon, a flamingo habitat protected as part of the Capo Carbonara Marine Reserve. The beach faces south into calm water framed by two headlands, and the combination of pink-white sand, the flamingo lagoon immediately behind, and the rocky reserve islands offshore constitutes one of the most visually layered settings in Sardinia. Snorkelling in the marine reserve requires a permit in peak season; the beaches are free.
