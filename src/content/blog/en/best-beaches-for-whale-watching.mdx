Watching whales and dolphins from the shore, without a boat and without a tour operator, is possible from a surprisingly wide range of beaches and cliff headlands worldwide. The conditions that bring cetaceans close to land are predictable: shallow submarine canyons that approach the coast and concentrate prey, headlands that deflect ocean currents and create upwellings of nutrients, and seasonal migrations that bring animals along predictable routes within sight of land. Finding these locations, arriving at the right time of year, and knowing where and how to look rewards patience with sightings that are completely free and entirely on the animal's terms.

## Hermanus, South Africa — Southern Right Whales

Hermanus on the Western Cape of South Africa is one of the most celebrated shore-based whale watching destinations in the world, operating with a designated town whale crier who walks the streets with a kelp horn to alert residents when whales are sighted in Walker Bay. Southern right whales (Eubalaena australis) enter Walker Bay from approximately June through November to calve in the sheltered bay, and they bring their calves to the surface so frequently and so close to the cliff path that binoculars are optional. The 12-kilometre cliff path above the town provides elevated viewing points across the entire bay.

Southern right whales are among the most conspicuous cetaceans to watch from shore. They are large — females reach 17 metres — slow-moving in calving areas, and prone to behaviours that are highly visible at distance: breaching (jumping clear of the water), lobtailing (slapping the tail surface repeatedly), and sailing (holding the flukes vertically in the wind to drift). The calves, born from June through August, stay at the surface for nursing and rest, and mother-calf pairs often drift within a few hundred metres of the cliff path.

## Patagonia — Peninsula Valdés, Argentina

Peninsula Valdés on the Patagonian coast of Argentina is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and probably the world's premier shore-based whale watching location after Hermanus. Southern right whales arrive in the Golfo Nuevo and Golfo San José from June through December, with peak calf numbers in September and October. Punta Piramides is the main base, and the beach at Punta Piramides allows whale watching directly from the shore at close range.

The peninsula is also home to the only population of killer whales (orcas) in the world known to intentionally strand themselves on beaches to catch South American sea lion and fur seal pups. The intentional stranding behaviour occurs from January through March at Punta Norte, where a narrow beach hosts a sea lion colony. The orcas launch themselves onto the beach from the surf zone, grab pups, and then work their way back off the beach with the waves. This is a learned behaviour passed from mother to calf and has been documented at this single location for decades.

## Kaikoura, New Zealand — Sperm Whales Year-Round

Kaikoura on the east coast of New Zealand's South Island is one of the few places in the world where sperm whales (Physeter macrocephalus) are reliably present year-round close to shore. The reason is a submarine canyon — the Kaikoura Canyon — which begins in very shallow water just offshore and drops to over 1,000 metres within a few kilometres of the coast. This depth gradient concentrates the deep-water squid on which sperm whales feed at a point that brings the whales into surprisingly close proximity to land.

From the Point Kean Peninsula and the clifftops south of town, sperm whales can occasionally be seen at the surface between dives, though the long dive intervals (sperm whales dive for 40 to 60 minutes to depths exceeding 1,000 metres) mean that sightings from shore require patience and a good pair of binoculars. Dusky dolphins are far more reliably seen from shore year-round and often come into the bay in groups of hundreds.

## Big Sur Coast, California — Grey Whales and Blue Whales

The California coast south of Monterey, particularly the elevated viewpoints along Highway 1 in Big Sur, offers one of the best land-based whale watching environments in North America. Grey whales (Eschrichtius robustus) make the longest migration of any mammal — from their summer feeding grounds in the Bering and Chukchi Seas to calving lagoons in Baja California and back — and they pass the California coast twice annually. The southbound migration peaks in January, the northbound migration in March through May. The elevated clifftops of Big Sur provide unobstructed views across the migration corridor.

Blue whales (Balaenoptera musculus), the largest animals on Earth, feed off the California coast from July through October, attracted by the upwelling krill concentrations that make the California Current one of the most productive marine ecosystems in the Pacific. Point Reyes, north of San Francisco, and Piedras Blancas south of Hearst Castle are reliable blue whale viewing points during peak season.

## Baja California — Shore-Based Grey Whale Watching

The lagoons of Baja California — San Ignacio Lagoon, Scammon's Lagoon (Laguna Ojo de Liebre), and Magdalena Bay — are where grey whales calve between January and March, and all three can be watched from the shore without a boat. The grey whales of Baja are famous for their habit of approaching small pangas (fishing boats) and presenting themselves for touching — so-called friendly whales — but shore-based viewing from the lagoon edges also provides remarkable encounters as whales surface within metres of the bank.

## Pico Island, Azores — Multiple Species from Cliffs

The island of Pico in the Azores, once the centre of the Portuguese and American whaling industry in the North Atlantic, has transformed its cetacean relationship entirely. Vigias — stone watchtowers built by whalers to spot spouts from clifftops — are now used by whale watching guides to locate whales for eco-tourism boats. But the vigias are also accessible to independent visitors, and the whale sighting rate from these elevated coastal viewpoints is high enough that the Azores deserves mention as a shore-based watching destination. Fin whales, blue whales, sperm whales, and multiple dolphin species are all regularly seen from the high cliffs of Pico and Faial.
