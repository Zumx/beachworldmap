Ireland has approximately 2,500 kilometres of coastline, the great majority of it facing the open Atlantic Ocean, and the beaches that result from this exposure are a particular type: broad, windswept, wave-battered, and frequently spectacular in a way that sun-drenched Mediterranean resorts rarely are. The Irish beach experience is an outdoor one in the fullest sense — even on fine summer days, the air is cool enough for a jumper at most locations, and the sea temperature in August rarely exceeds 16 or 17 degrees Celsius. The beaches on the Wild Atlantic Way — the official driving route that follows the western coastline — are the foundation of Ireland's coastal tourism, and the best of them are among the finest unspoiled beaches in Europe.

## 1. Glenmore Strand, County Donegal

Glenmore Strand sits in the remote southwest corner of Donegal, near the town of Killybegs, and is accessed via a narrow road that ends at a small car park above the beach. The strand is long and south-facing, partially sheltered from the prevailing northwest winds by the headland at Kilcar. The combination of relative shelter and fine white sand makes it one of the more swimmable beaches in Donegal, where powerful Atlantic exposure often makes the sea too rough for anything but surfing. The surrounding landscape is classic Donegal: green hills, stone walls, and a scattering of whitewashed cottages.

## 2. Ballybrassil Beach, County Waterford

Ballybrassil is a quieter beach on the Waterford coast in the southeast of Ireland, in a county whose coastline is often overlooked in favour of the more dramatic Wild Atlantic Way. The southeast of Ireland receives more sunshine hours than the west coast — the Waterford coast averages notably better summer weather than Donegal or Clare — and the sea here is fractionally warmer, being the Celtic Sea rather than the open Atlantic. The beach is sandy and accessible, suitable for family swimming, and the surrounding Copper Coast UNESCO Geopark includes dramatic volcanic and sedimentary rock formations along the shore.

## 3. Murvagh Beach, County Donegal

Murvagh Beach occupies a peninsula south of Donegal Town, surrounded on three sides by the Donegal Bay, and is backed by a forest plantation that provides some shelter from the prevailing wind. The beach is broad and pale, facing south across the bay, with views toward the mountains of Sligo and the Ben Bulben plateau. Access is through the Murvagh Forest car park. The bay provides gentler conditions than the fully exposed Atlantic beaches further north on the Donegal coast, making it a more accessible option for swimmers who want calmer water without venturing to the south coast.

## 4. Dollymount Strand, County Dublin

Dollymount Strand is Dublin's own beach — a 5-kilometre stretch of sand on Bull Island in Dublin Bay, accessible across a causeway from the suburb of Clontarf. Bull Island was formed in the 19th century from sand accumulation after the construction of the North Bull Wall, which was itself built to improve tidal flushing of Dublin Port. The strand is backed by dune grassland and a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve supporting significant migratory bird populations. Kite surfers and windsurfers use the exposed bay regularly. The strand is walkable from the Dublin city bus network, making it the most accessible beach in the capital. [Open the map](/map) to locate Bull Island and the causeway route.

## 5. Raheen Strand, County Clare

Raheen Strand sits on the coast of County Clare south of Kilrush, facing into the lower Shannon estuary. The beach is sandy and tidal, part of the broader loop head peninsula landscape that culminates in Loop Head lighthouse to the west. This stretch of the Clare coast is less visited than the Burren coast to the north and the Cliffs of Moher, and the quieter atmosphere is part of its appeal. The Shannon estuary is one of the last reliable places in Ireland to see bottlenose dolphins, and the Kilrush Marina is a base for dolphin-watching boat trips that head out from nearby.

## 6. Coss Strand, County Galway

Coss Strand occupies the Connemara coast in County Galway, in the area of small islands and inlets between Clifden and Roundstone. Connemara's beaches are characterised by brilliantly white shell-sand — a Caribbean-coloured beach in a distinctly Irish-Atlantic setting — and the surrounding Connemara National Park landscape provides a dramatic backdrop of quartzite mountains. The beach is relatively small and the water is cold, but the visual quality of Connemara's beaches is extraordinary. Roundstone village has a good selection of pubs and restaurants serving fresh Connemara seafood.

## 7. Dromina Strand, County Clare

Dromina Strand is a small, undeveloped beach on the Loop Head Peninsula, one of the quietest stretches of the Clare coast. The peninsula is a dramatic strip of land projecting about 30 kilometres into the Atlantic, with sea cliffs on its southern shore and slightly more sheltered beaches on the north-facing side toward the Shannon. Dromina is a local beach, used primarily by families from the surrounding townlands, and sees minimal tourist traffic. The Loop Head drive is one of the finest short coastal drives in Ireland, with the additional interest of several early Christian sites and ringforts near the road.

## 8. Shell Beach, County Mayo

Shell Beach — sometimes listed as Carrowmore Strand — sits on the Mullet Peninsula in County Mayo, one of the remotest peninsulas on the Irish Atlantic coast. The beach is composed partly of shells and shell fragments rather than sand, giving it a distinctive pale, almost white surface. The Mullet Peninsula is one of the least-visited parts of the West of Ireland, a long thin finger of land running south from Belmullet into Blacksod Bay. The peninsula has a quiet, end-of-the-road quality — small farms, scattered houses, long views across open water — that is increasingly rare as more accessible parts of the Wild Atlantic Way become better known.

## 9. Inchydoney Beach, County Cork

Inchydoney is the finest beach on the Cork coast, sitting on a small peninsula near Clonakilty in West Cork. The beach is actually two strands divided by a headland called the Virgin Mary's Bank — a grassed dune system — and both face south toward the open Celtic Sea. The eastern strand is somewhat sheltered; the western Virgin Strand is more exposed and has consistent surf. The town of Clonakilty, a 10-minute drive away, is one of the most pleasant small towns on the Cork coast, with a strong food culture and several excellent restaurants. Inchydoney Island Lodge, perched on the headland between the two beaches, has operated a thalassotherapy spa since the late 1990s.

## 10. Enniscrone Beach, County Sligo

Enniscrone — sometimes spelled Inishcrone — sits on the southern shore of Killala Bay in County Sligo, and its beach stretches for about three kilometres of broad sandy strand backed by dunes. The town of Enniscrone has the Kilcullen's Seaweed Baths, a Victorian-era seaweed bathing institution that has operated since 1912 using the same wrack gathered from the nearby shore. The baths serve as a genuine reason to visit in themselves, combining a post-swim soak in seaweed-infused hot seawater with steam rooms — a genuinely restorative experience after a cold Atlantic dip. The beach faces north-northwest and picks up consistent swell from Donegal Bay.
