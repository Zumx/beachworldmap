Chile has one of the longest coastlines of any country on earth — approximately 4,300 kilometres of mainland Pacific coast, not counting the 84,000 kilometres of coastline that includes the fjords, channels, and islands of Patagonia and Tierra del Fuego. The Humboldt Current runs cold along most of Chile's coast, keeping sea temperatures fresh even in midsummer, and the prevailing winds blow consistently offshore in the afternoons, creating surf conditions. The most popular beaches are concentrated between La Serena in the north and Concepción in the south, within reach of Santiago. Further south, the beaches become wilder, colder, and progressively harder to reach, which is for many visitors precisely the appeal.

## 1. Playa Guaylandia, Coquimbo Region

Playa Guaylandia sits in the Coquimbo region of northern Chile, in the broad latitude band around La Serena that receives reliable summer sunshine from December to March. The beach is sheltered by a headland and the sand is fine and pale — lighter than many Chilean beaches where the sand tends toward grey or brown. The Coquimbo region is known for its clear night skies as much as its beaches; several international astronomical observatories operate in the hills behind the coast, and the combination of beach days and stargazing nights makes it a distinctive Chilean travel combination.

## 2. Playa El Tabito, O'Higgins Region

El Tabito sits on the central Chilean coast south of San Antonio, in the O'Higgins Region, and represents the kind of relaxed beach village that feels increasingly rare on the Chilean coast as development accelerates northward from Viña del Mar. The beach is a long grey-sand strand with consistent onshore wind conditions and a small local community. The drive from Santiago takes about two hours, and the beach sees mostly Chilean families rather than international visitors. The seafood at the restaurants along the seafront is excellent — fresh crab, sea urchin, and Chilean sea bass prepared with local simplicity.

## 3. Playa Guayópolis, Valparaíso Region

Playa Guayópolis sits near the small coastal town of El Quisco in the Valparaíso Region, part of the stretch of coast around Algarrobo that serves as the summer playground of Santiago's middle class. The beach is sandy and relatively sheltered compared to the more exposed strands north and south. Pablo Neruda's famous beach house, La Isla Negra, sits on the rocks just south of El Quisco and draws visitors who come as much for the literary pilgrimage as for the beach. The surrounding coast is lined with weekend houses and summer rental properties.

## 4. Playa Solitaria, Atacama Region

Playa Solitaria lives up to its name — the Solitary Beach — occupying a stretch of the Atacama coast that has minimal access road development. The Atacama Region in northern Chile is world-famous as the driest non-polar desert on earth, and the coastal towns here — Chañaral, Caldera, Bahía Inglesa — sit at the edge of this desert, where the near-zero rainfall produces a striking landscape of bare brown hills meeting a cold, clear Pacific. Playa Solitaria is reached from Chañaral and offers the combination of dramatic desert backdrop and genuinely clean, clear water found along this stretch of coast.

## 5. Playa Poza El Batro, Atacama Region

Poza El Batro sits near Bahía Inglesa, which has developed a modest reputation as one of the more attractive beach destinations in northern Chile. The water along this coastline is notably clear despite the cold Humboldt Current, and the surrounding desert creates a spare, almost surreal visual contrast between the bright turquoise sea and the brown arid landscape. Bahía Inglesa is accessible from Copiapó, the regional capital, and the town has grown incrementally to accommodate summer visitors from Santiago while retaining a distinctly small-town character. ## 6. Playa Camping Olga, Biobío Region

Playa Camping Olga occupies the Biobío coast south of Concepción and is named for an informal camping area that has served generations of Chilean families for weekend escapes. The Biobío Region is Chile's most populous after the Santiago metropolitan area, and the beaches here function as the primary summer destination for Concepción's two million residents. The coast around here has grey-brown volcanic sand, consistent afternoon wind, and significant surf potential. The campsite character of the beach means it is busiest in January and February during the Chilean summer holiday period.

## 7. Playa Chépica, Los Lagos Region

Playa Chépica sits in the Los Lagos Region in southern Chile, in the latitude band around Valdivia and Osorno where the beaches begin to take on a distinctly different character. The forest here is temperate rainforest — dense, green, and wet — rather than the dry scrub of central Chile, and the beaches are backed by native coihue and arrayán trees. The sea is colder here than in the north, and mist and overcast skies are common even in summer. Playa Chépica is a local beach used primarily by residents of nearby towns, relatively undiscovered by tourism infrastructure.

## 8. Playa Blanca, Atacama Region

Not to be confused with the many Playa Blancas across Latin America, this Playa Blanca sits in the Atacama coastal area and is characterised by pale calcareous sand — unusual for Chilean beaches — creating a lighter, almost white appearance against the characteristic blue-grey of the Humboldt Current water. The beach is backed by the desert landscape typical of this latitude, and the combination of white sand and clear water makes it visually one of the most striking beaches in northern Chile. Access is by a coastal road from the Pan-American Highway.

## 9. Playa Brava, Tarapacá Region

Playa Brava sits near Iquique, one of Chile's most northern cities, and is a surf beach that faces directly into the consistent Pacific swell funnelled down the South American coast. Iquique has developed a significant surf culture over the past 20 years, partly driven by the consistency and quality of its beach breaks and partly by the city's free-trade zone status, which has made surfboard and equipment importing straightforward. The beach is long and exposed, with strong winds in the afternoon — the same conditions that make Iquique excellent for paragliding from the adjacent coastal cliffs.

## 10. Playa Grande, La Araucanía Region

Playa Grande lies on the Araucanía coast in southern Chile, in the broad latitude band that corresponds roughly to the traditional Mapuche heartland. The Araucanía coast has some of the most dramatic coastal scenery in central-south Chile: volcanic sand, powerful surf, and a backdrop of Araucaria forests inland. The Mapuche people have a strong cultural presence in this region, and several coastal communities maintain traditional fishing practices. Playa Grande near Tirúa is a wide beach with powerful surf conditions — popular with experienced Chilean surfers — and significant natural character from the surrounding protected forest areas.
