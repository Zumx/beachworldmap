The line of dark material stranded at the high-tide mark of almost every beach on earth is one of the most overlooked ecological features in coastal environments. Strandline wrack — a mix of seaweed, driftwood, invertebrates, and terrestrial debris — represents the daily delivery of organic material from sea to land, and the wrack zone supports a dense community of organisms that in turn feed shorebirds, small mammals, and the sand-dune vegetation that stabilises beaches against erosion. The seaweed component of this wrack comes from a variety of sources and changes character entirely depending on the oceanographic conditions driving it onshore. Understanding these sources tells you a great deal about the sea beyond what is visible from the shore.

## Wracks and Kelp: What Grows on the Shore

The intertidal zone of rocky shores in temperate and cold climates supports several species of brown macroalgae collectively known as wracks. In the North Atlantic, the dominant species from low to high tide level are bladderwrack (Fucus vesiculosus), knotted wrack (Ascophyllum nodosum), and channelled wrack (Pelvetia canaliculata), along with the larger forms like serrated wrack (Fucus serratus). These plants grow attached to rock, reproduce in the floating gelatinous masses that appear at certain times of year, and detach — typically after storms — to wash ashore.

Bladderwrack is immediately recognisable by the paired air bladders along its midrib, which children have been popping for as long as both have coexisted. The bladders provide buoyancy when the plant is covered by tide, keeping fronds in the photic zone for photosynthesis. Ascophyllum nodosum grows more slowly than Fucus species and individual plants can be decades old; it is harvested commercially in Ireland, Scotland, Norway, and Iceland for seaweed meal and as a soil conditioner.

Kelp — most commonly Laminaria digitata and L. hyperborea in the North Atlantic, and various Macrocystis species in the Pacific — grows in the subtidal zone, typically at depths of 2 to 30 metres, and forms dense forests that are among the most productive marine ecosystems on earth. Kelp most commonly arrives on beaches after storm swell detaches stipes from the rocky substrate; large fresh-cut kelp is easy to identify by its leathery texture, dark brown colour, and the solid stipe that was attached to rock. Dried kelp forms a crisp brittle material that is a common strandline feature on Atlantic beaches from Cornwall to Nova Scotia.

## The Sargasso Sea and Caribbean Sargassum Events

Sargassum is a genus of brown pelagic macroalgae that is unique among seaweeds in spending its entire life cycle free-floating in open ocean. Two species — Sargassum natans and S. fluitans — form the floating ecosystem of the Sargasso Sea, a gyre-defined region of the North Atlantic east of the Caribbean. The Sargasso ecosystem supports an extraordinary array of life adapted specifically to the floating mat environment: the Sargassum anglerfish, filefish, pipefish, juvenile sea turtles, and numerous invertebrates live in, on, or around floating Sargassum.

From approximately 2011 onward, the Caribbean and Gulf of Mexico began experiencing mass Sargassum inundation events on an unprecedented scale. The Great Atlantic Sargassum Belt — a body of floating algae that by 2023 extended from West Africa to the Gulf of Mexico in a mass exceeding 13 million tonnes in peak season — has deposited rotting Sargassum on Caribbean beaches from Barbados to the Yucatan Peninsula in quantities that have overwhelmed tourism infrastructure at multiple destinations.

The ecological driver of the increase is debated. Elevated nutrient input — particularly nitrogen and phosphorus from the Amazon and Congo river systems, which have seen increased agricultural runoff — is the leading hypothesis for the explosion in biomass. Sea surface temperature increases in the tropical Atlantic may also be contributing to enhanced growth rates. Neither of these factors is expected to reverse in the near term.

For beach visitors and tourism operators in affected areas, the practical consequences are significant. Fresh Sargassum arriving on beaches has a pungent hydrogen sulphide odour as it begins to decompose, the mass can reach depths of one metre or more in severe events, and buried wet Sargassum creates anaerobic conditions that release hydrogen sulphide at concentrations measurable above safe health thresholds in confined areas. Beach hotels and resorts in Cancun, Playa del Carmen, Tulum, and throughout the Windward and Leeward Islands have spent hundreds of millions of dollars on mechanical removal equipment over the past decade.

There is an ecological counterpoint. Beached Sargassum is a food source for isopods, amphipods, sand hoppers, and other strandline invertebrates; it provides cover for ghost crabs and nesting shorebirds; and as it decomposes, it delivers nutrients to coastal dune vegetation. The conflict between these services and the tourism economy is real and will not resolve itself.

## Sea Lettuce, Gut Weed, and Nutrient Indicator Species

Green algae in the genus Ulva — sea lettuce (U. lactuca) and gut weed (U. intestinalis) — are indicator species for nutrient enrichment. Their growth is limited by nitrogen and phosphorus availability in low-nutrient coastal waters, but where nutrient loading from sewage, agricultural runoff, or fish farm effluent raises local nitrogen levels, Ulva can produce dense blooms that smother other intertidal life and accumulate on beaches in thick mats.

Ulva blooms — often called green tides — are most severe in sheltered estuaries and bays with restricted tidal exchange. The Brittany coast of France, particularly the bays around Saint-Brieuc and the Baie de la Forêt, experiences significant green tide events each summer attributable to intensive pig and poultry farming in the catchment. The rotting algae produces hydrogen sulphide in confined bays; at Saint-Michel-en-Grève in 2009 and 2016, gas concentrations in decomposing algae killed horses and dogs traversing the beach. France has since implemented agricultural nutrient reduction programmes targeting these specific watersheds, with partial success.

## The Ecological Value of Strandline Wrack

The temptation of beach management authorities is to treat stranded seaweed as a nuisance to be mechanically removed for the comfort of tourists. This creates real ecological damage. The wrack zone — the band of stranded material at and above the high-tide mark — is the primary habitat for sand hoppers (Orchestia gammarellus in European waters), which are present in densities that can exceed 25,000 per square metre of wrack. Sand hoppers are a key food resource for turnstones, sanderlings, dunlins, and other shorebirds that work the strandline.

Wrack that is left to dry and decompose above the storm tide mark contributes organic material to the developing sand dune soil profile, supporting the early colonisation by sea rocket (Cakile maritima) and saltwort (Salsola kali) that begin the succession toward stable dune vegetation. Beaches from which strandline wrack is routinely removed have impoverished dune vegetation as a measurable consequence.

The most ecologically sound beach management approach — practised on nature reserve beaches in the UK, the Netherlands, and Scandinavia — removes mechanical machinery from the upper beach and allows the wrack zone to function naturally, with clearing limited to areas of highest pedestrian footfall immediately adjacent to beach access points.

## Using Seaweed to Read Coastal Conditions

The composition and character of beach wrack is genuinely informative about recent conditions. A beach covered in freshly detached Laminaria with intact holdfasts indicates recent deep-water wave energy reaching the subtidal zone — a storm swell within the past 48 hours. Bleached, dried Ascophyllum with abundant attached coralline algae suggests a prolonged period of warm stable weather. A beach with no wrack at all on a reflective, sand-only shore suggests a beach with high wave energy that regularly flushes the strandline.
