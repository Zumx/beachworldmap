The question of who owns the beach — and who has the right to walk along it — is one of the most contested intersections of property law, environmental protection, and democratic access in the world. The answer varies dramatically by country, and often within countries, depending on which legal tradition shaped the nation's property law, how its constitution treats natural resources, and how aggressively property developers have lobbied legislatures over the decades. A beach that would be completely accessible in Norway can be legally private and fenced in the United States. A shoreline that belongs to the state in Mexico might be sold in parcels in certain Caribbean jurisdictions.

## The Roman Law Foundation

Most beach access law in the Western world traces back, at least partially, to Roman legal principles. The Roman jurist Marcianus, writing in the second century AD, articulated a principle that has survived in fragments through two millennia: the seashore is common to all by the law of nature. Specifically, Roman law recognised three categories of common things: res communes (things common to all, like air and the sea), res publicae (things belonging to the Roman people, like rivers and harbours), and res nullius (things belonging to no one, like wild animals). The shore fell into res communes — available to everyone for navigation, fishing, and the use that the sea itself required.

This Romanist foundation influenced the legal systems of France, Spain, Portugal, Italy, and the Latin American nations that inherited their civil law traditions from Spain and Portugal. In France, the domain public maritime — roughly, the public maritime domain — includes all land between the lowest tide and the highest ordinary tide, and extends into a maritime perimeter beyond that. You cannot privatise the French shoreline. In Spain, the 1988 Ley de Costas established a similar coastal public domain including a 100-metre protection zone above the high tide line where private construction is severely restricted.

## The Public Trust Doctrine in the United States

The United States inherited a version of this Roman principle through English common law: the public trust doctrine, which holds that certain natural resources — navigable waters, tidal lands, and their shores — are held by the state in trust for the public. The doctrine was articulated in American constitutional jurisprudence in the 1892 Supreme Court case Illinois Central Railroad v. Illinois, in which the court held that the state of Illinois could not transfer control of Chicago's lakefront to a private railroad.

However, the public trust doctrine applies differently across American states, and the definition of what the public can actually do on beaches varies considerably. In Hawaii, the state constitution explicitly guarantees public access to all beaches below the high water mark. In Texas, the Open Beaches Act of 1959 grants public access to all Gulf Coast beaches below the line of vegetation. But in states like New Jersey, the mean high water line is technically the limit of public beach access — everything above it, including the dry sand above the tide line, can be privately owned. New Jersey beach towns have been embroiled in access disputes for decades, with beachfront property owners charging access fees for the dry sand while conceding the wet sand below the tidal line.

## Nordic Everyman's Right

Scandinavia operates on a fundamentally different philosophical basis. The concept of allemansrätten in Sweden (and equivalent rights in Norway, Finland, and Denmark) is a traditional right to access land regardless of ownership, with the condition that you do not damage crops, enclosed gardens, or disturb the landowner's reasonable enjoyment of the property. This right is codified into Swedish law and constitutionally protected in Norway.

In practice, this means that any person can walk along any beach in Sweden or Norway, including beaches fronting private villas, can swim from them, and can camp for one or two nights on them without permission, as long as they stay at a reasonable distance from a residence and leave no trace. The right extends to forests, farmland, and mountains as well as the coast. The philosophical underpinning is different from the public trust doctrine: allemansrätten is not a state guarantee of access to a state resource but a civic compact between citizens that predates modern property law.

## English Common Law and the English Shore

England's position is complex and somewhat anomalous. The Crown owns the foreshore — the land between mean high and mean low tide — by default, though much of it has been granted to private parties over centuries and the Crown Estate now manages what remains. The public has certain common law rights over the foreshore for navigation, fishing, and the passage of the tide, but no general right to walk along beaches that have been granted to private ownership. In practice, most beaches with significant visitor numbers in England are managed by local authorities or major charities like the National Trust and are effectively open. But in principle, a private beach in England is legally private in a way that would be impossible in France or Sweden.

Scotland is different: the Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003 established a comprehensive right of responsible access for all land in Scotland, including all coastal land and shores. Scotland effectively adopted a version of the Nordic model, and coastal access in Scotland is as free as in Norway.

## Australia and the Public Shore

Australia's states vary, but the general principle inherited from English law and reinforced by colonial legislation holds that the foreshore is Crown land and available to the public. The controversy in Australia has been less about foreshore access and more about the access paths to beaches — where private development has blocked the tracks through private land that lead to Crown foreshore. The legal battles over foreshore access in New South Wales, Queensland, and Western Australia have often been about whether the public can reach the beach rather than whether they can use it once there.

## Private Beach Controversies

The most aggressive privatisation of beach access has occurred in some Caribbean and Central American nations where foreign investment in luxury resorts was encouraged by legislation that allowed fencing of beachfront. Countries like the Dominican Republic technically guarantee public access to the beach in their constitutions (the shoreline being part of the national maritime domain), but enforcement is inconsistent and resort development has sometimes created de facto private beaches. In contrast, Mexico's Ley de Bienes Nacionales explicitly defines a 20-metre public access zone above the high tide line on all Mexican beaches, and resort hotels are required to provide public access corridors even where they otherwise occupy the beachfront.
