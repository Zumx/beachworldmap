El Salvador is the smallest and most densely populated country in Central America, with a Pacific coastline of about 300 kilometres. Every beach in the country faces west or northwest into the Pacific Ocean — El Salvador has no Caribbean coast. The coastline is characterised by black and dark grey volcanic sand, a consequence of the volcanic activity that dominates the country's geology; the volcanoes visible from inland are directly responsible for the composition of the beaches below them. The surf is the primary draw for international visitors: the point break at El Sunzal, the beach break at El Tunco, and several other consistent spots have built El Salvador a strong reputation in the surf world. But the country's beaches have much more to offer than surf alone.

## 1. Barra de Santiago

Barra de Santiago sits in the far western corner of El Salvador near the Guatemalan border and is one of the country's finest undeveloped beach areas. The beach is a long sandy peninsula that separates the Pacific from a large estuary and mangrove lagoon system behind. The lagoon is rich in wildlife — mangrove birds, caiman, and a range of fish species — and boat tours through it can be arranged in the small village. The beach faces northwest into the Pacific and receives solid swell for surfing, but the broader beach is wide enough that the surfing section coexists with swimming and walking. The area remains largely undeveloped by tourism infrastructure, and the village has a working fishing economy.

## 2. Playa Torola

Playa Torola sits in the eastern department of La Unión, near the Honduran border, and is one of the few beaches in El Salvador that has developed resort infrastructure beyond the surf-focused communities of the central coast. The beach is sandy and palm-lined in the tropical manner, and the eastern coast of El Salvador tends to be slightly quieter than the more accessible beaches near La Libertad, which is only about 35 kilometres from San Salvador. Torola has a cluster of beach hotels and restaurants that serve the local Salvadoran holiday market, with the broad Pacific beach and reliable sunshine as the primary draws.

## 3. Playa El Cuco

El Cuco is a beach town in the eastern department of San Miguel, the largest beach destination in eastern El Salvador. The beach stretches about three kilometres and is dark volcanic sand, with consistent beach-break surf and calm sections suitable for swimming. The town has built up gradually into a modest resort with restaurants, hotels, and hostels catering to both local Salvadoran families and a growing number of foreign visitors. The eastern location means it is less frequented by the San Salvador weekender crowd that concentrates on the La Libertad beaches to the west, and the atmosphere is somewhat more relaxed for it.

## 4. Playa El Tablón

Playa El Tablón lies in the central Salvadoran coast zone, part of the cluster of beaches accessible from the Costa del Sol resort area in the La Paz department. The Costa del Sol is El Salvador's most developed beach tourism zone, a narrow peninsula separating the Bay of Jiquilisco from the Pacific Ocean. El Tablón is one of the quieter sections of this coast, where the hotel development is sparser and the beach retains a more natural character. The bay side of the peninsula, facing the protected estuary, has calmer water than the Pacific-facing beach and is used for kayaking and fishing.

## 5. Playa El Tamarindo

El Tamarindo sits in the La Unión department near the Honduran border and is named for the tamarind trees that once shaded the coastal path here. The beach is long and sandy, with gentle surf compared to the more powerful breaks of the western coast. El Tamarindo is a local fishing community beach that has seen modest development for Salvadoran tourists but remains genuinely low-key by regional standards. The area around La Unión has the additional interest of the Gulf of Fonseca immediately to the east — a triangular gulf shared between El Salvador, Honduras, and Nicaragua — with its own distinct geography and fishing culture.

## 6. Playa Espiritu Santo

Playa Espiritu Santo is part of the Bay of Jiquilisco complex in the Usulután department, one of the most ecologically important coastal areas in El Salvador. The Bay of Jiquilisco is a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve with the largest remaining mangrove forest in Central America on the Pacific coast, and the beaches within and around it have a particular wild quality resulting from this protection. Nesting sea turtles — primarily olive ridleys — come ashore here between July and December, and a locally managed conservation programme operates on parts of this coast. Access involves boat travel across the bay.

## 7. Playa Mizata

Playa Mizata lies in the Sonsonate department near the western end of El Salvador's surfing coast and is a lesser-known point break that produces longer, more consistent waves than the more crowded breaks at Punta Roca to the east. The beach is backed by a steep coastal escarpment, and the small community here has a working fishing operation alongside the surf facilities. The point break at Mizata peels left over a rocky bottom for considerable distances on the right swell — experienced surfers who find the famous breaks at El Sunzal overcrowded tend to know about Mizata. ## 8. Playa El Zonte

El Zonte is a small cove about 10 kilometres west of El Tunco, with a beach break that is slightly less powerful than its more famous neighbour and consequently somewhat more suitable for intermediate surfers. The community of El Zonte gained international attention in 2021 when it became one of the early adopters of Bitcoin as a local payment currency — the so-called Bitcoin Beach project — and the story attracted a wave of tech-oriented visitors alongside the surf crowd. The beach itself is dark volcanic sand with rocky outcrops at either end, and the village has a good selection of hostels, surf schools, and restaurants.

## 9. Playa El Tunco

El Tunco is the most internationally famous surf beach in El Salvador, a small rocky cove about 43 kilometres west of San Salvador with a beach break and a rock formation shaped like a pig — tunco in Salvadoran Spanish — that sits offshore. The wave here breaks both left and right, is powerful and hollow, and is best suited to surfers with real experience. The village around the beach has developed a well-organised tourist infrastructure with international hostels, surf shops, bars, and restaurants. On weekends and holidays it is genuinely crowded; arriving midweek gives a calmer experience both in the water and out.

## 10. Playa La Paz

Playa La Paz sits within the Costa del Sol area in the La Paz department, part of the same peninsula complex that contains several of El Salvador's most accessible resort beaches. The beach is sandy, the surf moderate, and the palm-tree-backed setting is classic Central American Pacific coast. The Costa del Sol peninsula is about 65 kilometres southeast of San Salvador on a good road, making it the primary beach day-trip destination for the capital's population. The inner bay side of the peninsula — facing the mangrove lagoon rather than the open Pacific — is considerably calmer and warmer, used for fishing, kayaking, and gentle swimming.
