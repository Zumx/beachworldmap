The beach is the highest UV exposure environment most people encounter. Sand reflects approximately 15 to 25 percent of UV radiation back upward, so that a person sitting under an umbrella still receives a fraction of the UV dose that the direct sky delivers. Water reflects and scatters UV, and the submerged swimmer receives UV through the water column. Altitude is not relevant at beach level, but latitude, time of year, time of day, and cloud cover are all highly significant.

Understanding what the UV index means and how to respond to it practically is more useful than any single product recommendation.

## The UV index

The UV index is a standardised measure of the intensity of UV radiation at the Earth's surface. It runs from 0 (night, polar winter) to 11+ (extreme, tropical midday). A UV index of 3 (moderate) is the threshold above which sunburn protection is recommended for most skin types. A UV index of 6 to 7 (high) is typical of a Mediterranean summer day between 10am and 2pm. A UV index of 11+ (extreme) occurs at tropical latitudes near noon in summer.

The critical variable is time to sunburn: at UV index 11, fair skin can burn in under 10 minutes of unprotected exposure. At UV index 3, the same skin might take 30 to 40 minutes to burn. These numbers are affected by reflected UV from sand and water, which can add 15 to 25 percent to effective UV dose.

UV index forecasts are published daily by national weather services in most countries and are included in weather apps. Checking the UV index before a beach day and using it to calibrate the day's sun protection routine is more rational than applying the same routine regardless of conditions.

## Sunscreen: what SPF means

SPF (Sun Protection Factor) is a measure of how much UVA and UVB radiation is blocked by the sunscreen. SPF 30 blocks approximately 97 percent of UVB; SPF 50 blocks approximately 98 percent. The incremental gain from SPF 50 to SPF 100 is small. The far more important variable is whether the sunscreen is applied correctly.

A full-body application for an adult requires approximately 35 ml of product — roughly 2 tablespoons. Most people apply one-third to one-half this amount, which significantly reduces effective SPF. The stated SPF is derived from laboratory testing at the full 2mg per cm² application rate; applying half that amount gives far less than half the stated protection.

Apply sunscreen 20 minutes before sun exposure to allow the chemical filters to bind to skin. Reapply every 2 hours and after every swim, regardless of "water resistant" labelling. Water resistant means the SPF holds for 40 or 80 minutes of water immersion — not all day.

## Broad spectrum and chemical versus mineral

"Broad spectrum" sunscreen blocks both UVA (which penetrates skin deeply and causes ageing and DNA damage without always causing visible burning) and UVB (which causes sunburn and is the primary driver of skin cancer). Use only broad-spectrum products.

Chemical sunscreens absorb UV and convert it to heat. Mineral sunscreens (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) reflect and scatter UV physically. Both are effective. The reef concern with chemical filters oxybenzone and octinoxate is covered in the coral reef article; at beach locations with sensitive reef systems, mineral sunscreen is the appropriate choice.

## The shadow rule and timing

The shadow rule is a reliable real-world indicator of UV intensity: when your shadow is shorter than your height, UV radiation is at its highest. In summer, at most European and North American latitudes, this corresponds to the window from roughly 10am to 3pm.

During this window, shade is the most effective single UV protection measure. Seeking shade for the 10am to 3pm period and being in the water or walking rather than lying in sun during this time can reduce overall UV dose substantially more than even excellent sunscreen application.

## Children and UV exposure

Children's skin is more sensitive to UV damage than adults'. Sunburn in childhood is a significant risk factor for melanoma in later life. For children under 12 months, keep out of direct sunlight entirely during beach visits; a hat, long sleeves, and shade are the appropriate protection. For older children: SPF 50+ broad-spectrum sunscreen, reapplied frequently, combined with a hat with a brim and a UV rash guard covering the torso, provides substantially better protection than sunscreen alone.

The rash guard is one of the most effective and underused sun protection tools for children. A UPF 50 rash guard over the torso eliminates the most difficult area to cover evenly with sunscreen (the back and shoulders of a moving, water-active child) from the daily sunscreen calculation.

## Heat and dehydration

UV damage is not the only heat-related beach risk. Heat exhaustion and dehydration are common on full-sun summer beach days. Children and older adults dehydrate faster and show symptoms later than healthy adults. Drink water continuously rather than waiting for thirst — the sensation of thirst lags behind actual dehydration status. Observe children for signs of heat exhaustion (pale, clammy, weak) and heat stroke (hot, dry skin, confusion).

## Eye protection

UV damage to eyes is cumulative and underappreciated. UV-blocking sunglasses with wraparound frames that prevent peripheral UV entry provide meaningful protection. The cheapest CE-marked UV400 sunglasses block UV as effectively as expensive designer frames — the quality difference is in optical clarity, not UV blocking. Polarised lenses reduce glare from water and sand, which significantly reduces eye fatigue on a full beach day.

Not wearing sunglasses at the beach over many years increases the risk of cataracts and macular degeneration. The beach is one of the highest UV exposure environments for the eyes as well as the skin — reflected UV from sand and water reaches the eyes from angles that typical overhead-oriented sun protection does not address.

## After-sun care

Sunburn at the beach is a real outcome even with good sun protection if the reapplication schedule slips, shade is unavailable for extended periods, or the UV index is higher than expected. For mild to moderate sunburn: cool (not cold) water immersion, aloe vera gel, over-the-counter ibuprofen or paracetamol for pain and inflammation. Do not apply oil-based products (butter, cooking oil, baby oil) to sunburned skin; they trap heat. Do not burst blisters.

A sunburned area requires several days of complete sun protection before re-exposure: a single burn event does not confer immunity to the next day's exposure, and re-burning a damaged area substantially increases the cumulative biological impact. For significant sunburn — blistering over a large area, fever, significant pain — medical attention is appropriate.

## Reef-safe sunscreen

Several jurisdictions have banned sunscreens containing oxybenzone and octinoxate — including Hawaii (since 2021) and parts of the US Virgin Islands, Mexico's Caribbean coast, and Palau. These chemical filters have been shown in laboratory studies to cause coral bleaching and reef damage at concentrations found in popular reef swimming areas. At any beach with a coral reef, using mineral sunscreen (zinc oxide or titanium dioxide) rather than chemical filter products is the ecologically responsible choice and is increasingly a legal requirement.

Mineral sunscreens tend to leave a white cast on darker skin tones; formulations with micronised or tinted zinc oxide reduce this effect. For children, the concern about chemical filter absorption through skin makes mineral sunscreen the preferable choice regardless of reef considerations.
