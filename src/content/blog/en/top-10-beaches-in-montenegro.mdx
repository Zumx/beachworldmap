Montenegro has roughly 293 kilometres of coastline on the Adriatic Sea — small by regional standards, but concentrated enough that the variety is remarkable. The coast includes the UNESCO World Heritage Bay of Kotor in the north (a fjord-like inland sea surrounded by mountains), the limestone cliffs and coves of the Budva Riviera in the centre, and the wider, calmer beaches of the Bar and Ulcinj areas near the Albanian border in the south. The water is the characteristic turquoise of the Adriatic, clear and warm from June through September.

Montenegro joined the EU accession process and has been rapidly developing its tourism infrastructure since the mid-2000s. International investment in hotel development, marina construction, and road improvement has transformed access to the coast significantly. Some sections of the coast — particularly the Budva Riviera — have lost the unspoiled character they had twenty years ago; others remain relatively undiscovered, particularly the Bar area and the inland reaches of Boka Kotorska (the Bay of Kotor).

The beach season runs from June through September. July and August are the peak months with the highest visitor volume, warmest sea temperatures (24 to 26 degrees Celsius), and most congested access roads. June and September offer good conditions with significantly lower crowds. The sea at Montenegro's beaches is among the cleanest in the Adriatic — the combination of limestone geology and limited heavy industry has kept water quality consistently high.

## 1. Sveti Stefan Beach, Budva Municipality

Sveti Stefan is a 15th-century fishing village built on a tiny rocky islet connected to the mainland by a narrow sand causeway. The beach on each side of the islet causeway is pink-tinged sand with clear Adriatic water. The pink colouring comes from crushed shells mixed into the natural sand. The islet itself is operated as a luxury hotel (Aman's Sveti Stefan), and the private sections within the village walls are for resort guests; the beach areas on either side of the causeway are publicly accessible. The view of the islet from the main beach below the coastal road — the terracotta roofs, the medieval walls, the turquoise sea — is the defining image of the Montenegrin coast.

## 2. Jaz Beach, Budva

Jaz Beach is a long beach 3 kilometres northwest of Budva, wider and longer than most Budva area beaches, with a relatively undeveloped section at the far southern end near the Crvena Glava headland. The beach has hosted major outdoor music concerts in summer — Jaz is the site of one of the largest outdoor concert venues on the Adriatic. The north section has beach bar and sunlounger infrastructure; the southern end is less commercial. The combination of beach width, accessible location from Budva, and the event programming makes it the most sociable beach in Montenegro.

## 3. Mogren Beach, Budva

Mogren Beach in Budva is a two-bay beach cut into the limestone cliffs below the old town walls, accessible by a short cliff path with a short tunnel carved through the rock. Mogren 1 faces east and gets morning sun; Mogren 2 is more sheltered and often calmer. The water colour in the enclosed bays — vivid turquoise, enhanced by the pale limestone floor below — is consistent with the best Adriatic coves. Mogren 1 and Mogren 2 are both in the database.

## 4. Kamenovo Beach, Budva Municipality

Kamenovo is a small pebble cove between Budva and Petrovac, backed by a hillside of Aleppo pine trees. The pine canopy provides the shade that the more exposed Budva town beaches lack — an advantage in midsummer heat. The beach is quieter than Budva and the drive south from Budva passes through attractive coastal countryside. The water in the enclosed bay is typically calm and clear.

## 5. Petrovac Beach, Budva Municipality

Petrovac is a small resort town 15 kilometres south of Budva with a crescent beach of grey pebble fronting calm Adriatic water. A medieval Venetian-era defensive tower stands on the headland above the south end of the beach. Two small islets offshore — Katic (with a chapel) and Sveta Nedjelja (with a monastery) — add to the visual interest and are accessible by boat in summer. Petrovac has a more relaxed and local character than Budva and is a preferred base for visitors who want proximity to the Budva beaches with less resort intensity.

## 6. Ada Bojana, Ulcinj

Ada Bojana is a river island at the mouth of the Bojana River near the Albanian border, formed by two branches of the river meeting the Adriatic. The ocean-facing beach is a long strip of fine dark sand — quite different in character from the limestone pebble beaches of the Budva Riviera. Ada Bojana has been informally clothing-optional for decades and has retained that character alongside conventional beach areas at the river mouth. The freshwater river and salt sea meet at the island's tip in a mixing zone that is visible from the beach. A small fishing village and restaurant cluster on the island serves freshwater fish from the Bojana.

## 7. Plava Spilja (Blue Cave), Budva Area

Plava Spilja (Blue Cave) is a sea cave on the coast north of Budva near Žanjic peninsula, accessible by boat from the Budva marina. Inside the cave, sunlight enters through an underwater entrance and scatters off the cave walls, producing the blue-green glow characteristic of sea caves with submerged entries. The cave beach is a small pebble strip at the back of the cave. Boat tour operators from Budva include it on coastal tours of the headlands; the journey by boat from Budva takes about 30 minutes.

## 8. Perast and Our Lady of the Rocks, Bay of Kotor

Perast is a Baroque town in the Bay of Kotor — a UNESCO World Heritage Site — with a small beach and the famous view of the Our Lady of the Rocks islet, artificially constructed over centuries through a tradition of local sailors depositing stones on a natural reef to build the island for a church. The church on the islet dates from 1452. The bay water here is not open Adriatic ocean but the enclosed, brackish interior of the fjord-like bay. Swimming is calm and possible; the landscape context — the bay, the islet church, the limestone mountains on all sides — is extraordinary and specific.

## 9. Velika Plaža (Great Beach), Ulcinj

Velika Plaža at Ulcinj is one of the longest beaches on the Adriatic — 13 kilometres of dark sand extending south from Ulcinj town toward the Albanian border. The sand is dark from river mineral deposits washed down by the Bojana. The beach is wide and the development considerably lighter than the Budva Riviera; family tourism and domestic visitors dominate. The far southern end near the Bojana River mouth is quieter and less developed.

## 10. Cape Arza, Bay of Kotor Entrance

Cape Arza, on the Lustica Peninsula at the entrance to the Bay of Kotor, has a wild rocky shoreline accessible by footpath from the road and a small beach below the limestone cliffs. The view from the cape looks west across the mouth of the bay with the Adriatic beyond and south toward the Albanian mountains. The peninsula has relatively limited development; the beach access requires moderate scrambling and the solitude is in proportion to the effort. A monastery on the peninsula adds historical interest to the walk.
