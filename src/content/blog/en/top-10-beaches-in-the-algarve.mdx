The Algarve is Portugal's southernmost region, a strip of coast approximately 155 kilometres long between the Spanish border at Vila Real de Santo António in the east and Cape Saint Vincent in the west, Europe's southwesternmost continental point. The coastline divides sharply into two characters at Faro: the eastern Sotavento coast, which is flat, lagoon-fringed, and sheltered within the Ria Formosa natural park; and the western Barlavento coast, which has the dramatic cliff formations — golden and ochre limestone carved into arches, grottos, and sea stacks — that have made the Algarve one of the most recognisable beach landscapes in Europe. Both are exceptional, for different reasons.

## 1. Praia da Marinha, Lagoa Municipality

Praia da Marinha is often cited as the most beautiful beach on the Algarve, occupying a small cove between towering limestone formations on the central Barlavento coast near Carvoeiro. The approach is down a steep set of steps carved into the cliff face, and the beach itself is relatively small — perhaps 150 metres of sand at low tide — but the surrounding cliff formations, with their arches, grottos, and vertical faces of ochre, orange, and red rock, create a setting of extraordinary visual quality. The water here is clear and deep blue, snorkelling around the base of the cliffs rewarding. Arrive before 10 a.m. in July and August; the steps and the limited space fill rapidly.

## 2. Praia de Benagil, Lagoa Municipality

Benagil is one of the most photographed places in Europe: a sea cave the size of a concert hall, with a circular hole open to the sky in its ceiling and a crescent of sand on its floor. The cave is accessible only by swimming, kayaking, or by the small boat tours that depart from the Benagil beach. The beach at Benagil itself is a small sandy cove at the base of the cliffs, used as the launching point for the cave visits. The cave tours run from the beach and take about 20 minutes. The experience of entering the cave from the sea and looking up through the oculus to the blue Algarve sky is among the most memorable coastal encounters in Southern Europe.

## 3. Praia da Falésia, Albufeira Municipality

Praia da Falésia — the Cliff Beach — is one of the longest beaches on the Barlavento coast, stretching approximately six kilometres between Olhos de Água and Vilamoura. The defining feature is the continuous cliff face of deep orange and red sandstone behind the beach, eroding in vertical formations that give the beach its name. The beach is backed by hotel developments at its eastern end but grows progressively wilder toward the west. The contrast between the deep orange cliffs, the pale sand, and the blue Atlantic is visually striking, especially in the late afternoon when the sun hits the cliff faces from an oblique angle.

## 4. Praia Dona Ana, Lagos Municipality

Praia Dona Ana sits directly south of Lagos, one of the Algarve's most attractive historic towns, and is the archetype of the western Algarve beach: a small sandy cove completely enclosed by golden limestone cliffs, with rock arches and sea stacks visible offshore. The beach is accessed via steep steps from the clifftop, and the town of Lagos is a 15-minute walk along the cliff path. Lagos itself has a well-preserved historic centre, a significant slave market history as one of the first European ports engaged in the transatlantic slave trade, and good restaurants serving fresh seafood from the adjacent Atlantic. The combination of beach quality and town infrastructure makes Lagos one of the best Algarve bases.

## 5. Praia da Meia Praia, Lagos Municipality

Praia da Meia Praia is the longest beach near Lagos, a four-kilometre arc of pale sand on the sheltered northern side of the Lagos bay, facing south. The bay orientation means this beach is calmer than the fully Atlantic-facing Ponta da Piedade beaches to the southwest, and it is generally better for swimming with children. The beach is accessible on foot from Lagos town centre via the railway bridge crossing, making it unique among Algarve beaches in being genuinely walkable from a major town. A railway station at one end of the beach connects Lagos to Faro in about 40 minutes.

## 6. Praia de Odeceixe, Aljezur Municipality

Praia de Odeceixe sits at the mouth of the Seixe river on the far western Algarve coast, near the border with the Alentejo region, within the Southwest Alentejo and Vicentine Coast Natural Park. The beach is divided by the river into two sections — one saltwater Atlantic facing, one freshwater river beach — creating a choice of swimming environments on the same stretch of sand. The cliff-backed setting and the river confluence give it a natural drama absent from more accessible beaches. The village of Odeceixe is 4 kilometres inland, up the river valley, and has a character that remains genuinely village-like despite the beach's fame. ## 7. Praia do Camilo, Lagos Municipality

Praia do Camilo is a tiny cove adjacent to the Ponta da Piedade headland, reached via a long wooden staircase descending from the clifftop near Lagos. The beach is very small — perhaps 50 metres — but the surrounding cliff formations at this point are among the most dramatic on the entire Algarve: golden arches, sea stacks, and grottos that can be explored by kayak at low tide. The Ponta da Piedade lighthouse stands on the headland above and the cliff walk between Camilo and Dona Ana beach to the north is one of the best short coastal walks in southern Portugal.

## 8. Praia de Sagres, Vila do Bispo Municipality

Sagres sits at the extreme southwestern corner of the Algarve, near Cape Saint Vincent, and the beach here has a different character from the limestone-arch beaches of the central coast. The sand is darker, the Atlantic more powerful, and the wind more persistent — Sagres is one of the windiest places in Portugal. The Sagres fortress, built in the 15th century by Prince Henry the Navigator to serve as a base for Portuguese maritime exploration, overlooks the headland. The town of Sagres has a small surf culture, several surf schools, and an end-of-the-world atmosphere generated by the dramatic convergence of two open oceans at the cape.

## 9. Praia do Amado, Aljezur Municipality

Praia do Amado is the premier surf beach on the Algarve coast, a broad, westward-facing beach in the Vicentine Coast Natural Park that receives consistent Atlantic swell from multiple directions. The beach is backed by a car park and a beach bar; there is no hotel development within sight. Several surf schools operate year-round from the beach, and the water temperature remains relatively mild enough for wetsuit surfing from autumn through spring. The park status prevents any further development, and the road in from the N120 inland road passes through a landscape of low Atlantic scrub and coastal dunes.

## 10. Praia do Barranco das Belharucas, Albufeira Municipality

Praia do Barranco das Belharucas is one of the smaller, harder-to-find beaches between Olhos de Água and Praia da Falésia, accessible via a narrow path down through the orange cliffs that typify this stretch of the central Algarve. The beach is less visited than the more famous Barlavento beaches precisely because of its relative obscurity, and the experience of finding a nearly deserted cove backed by spectacular golden cliffs within a few kilometres of the busy Albufeira resort zone is genuinely surprising. The cliffs here have the deep red and orange colouration that results from iron-rich sandstone, the same geological formation as Praia da Falésia but seen in a smaller, more intimate setting.
