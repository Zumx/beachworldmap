Tide tables are among the most practically useful pieces of information available to anyone spending time on a beach, and they are routinely ignored by the majority of visitors. The consequences range from mild inconvenience — discovering at low tide that the sandy bay you drove two hours to reach has a pebble and seaweed foreshore — to serious danger: being cut off by a rising tide under a cliff face, or walking across intertidal flats in Morecambe Bay that drain and fill in ways that do not read as threatening until they are. Reading tide tables is not technically difficult, but it requires understanding a few principles that are not immediately obvious.

## The Mechanics of the Tide

Tides are caused by the gravitational attraction of the moon and sun on the earth's oceans, with the moon providing roughly twice the tidal force of the sun due to its proximity. The lunar gravitational pull produces a bulge of water on the side of the earth facing the moon, and a corresponding bulge on the opposite side due to the centrifugal effect of the earth-moon system's orbital rotation. As the earth rotates, different parts of the ocean pass through these bulges, producing the characteristic twice-daily (semi-diurnal) cycle of two high tides and two low tides that characterises most of the world's coastlines.

Not all coastlines experience semi-diurnal tides. The Gulf of Mexico and parts of Southeast Asia have diurnal tides — one high and one low per day — due to the way their basin geometries respond to the tidal forcing. The Pacific coast of the US has mixed semi-diurnal tides, with two highs and two lows per day but with the two highs at significantly different heights. Understanding which type of tidal regime applies to the coastline you are visiting is the essential first step.

## Spring Tides and Neap Tides: The Fortnightly Cycle

The spring-neap cycle governs the amplitude of the tidal range on a fortnightly period. At spring tides — occurring every 14.7 days, when the moon is new or full — the sun, moon, and earth are aligned and the gravitational forces add together, producing the highest high tides and lowest low tides of the fortnight. At neap tides — when the moon is at first or last quarter and the angle between the sun, earth, and moon is 90 degrees — the forces partially cancel, producing smaller tidal ranges.

The difference in range between springs and neaps is significant on coastlines with large tidal ranges. In the Bristol Channel, where the tidal range at Avonmouth can exceed 14 metres at spring tides, the neap range at the same location might be only 7 to 8 metres. For the practical beach visitor, this means that a beach that offers wonderful rock pools and extensive sand flats at low water springs may reveal almost nothing at low water neaps, because the water never drops far enough to expose the interesting areas.

Spring tides do not occur exactly at new or full moon: there is a lag of roughly 24 to 48 hours between the astronomical alignment and the maximum tidal response, because the ocean takes time to physically respond to the gravitational forcing. This lag — called the age of the tide — varies by location. Tide tables incorporate this lag automatically; you do not need to calculate it independently.

## Reading the Table: Heights, Datums, and What the Numbers Mean

A standard tide table lists, for each day: the times of high water and low water, and their heights above the chart datum. The chart datum is the reference level from which all tide heights are measured — in British Admiralty tables, this is typically Lowest Astronomical Tide (LAT), the lowest level that can be predicted under any combination of astronomical conditions (though actual water levels can be lower than LAT under storm surge conditions). In US NOAA tables, the equivalent reference is Mean Lower Low Water (MLLW).

The heights in the table are therefore not heights above mean sea level — they are heights above a deliberately low reference point. A high water height of 5.8 metres does not mean the water is 5.8 metres deep at the shoreline; it means the water surface is 5.8 metres above the chart datum, wherever that happens to be in relation to the beach you are standing on.

The datum abbreviations that appear in tide-related literature are consistent across UK Admiralty publications: MHWS (Mean High Water Springs), MHWN (Mean High Water Neaps), MLWN (Mean Low Water Neaps), MLWS (Mean Low Water Springs). These are the average values of the respective tidal phases over a standard 18.6-year cycle (the metonic cycle that accounts for the full lunar precession). On navigational charts, the heights of coastal features above water (cliffs, bridges) are measured from MHWS; the depth of water is measured from chart datum.

## The Rule of Twelfths: Estimating Rate of Rise and Fall

Tide tables give the heights and times of high and low water, but not the height at any intermediate time. The rule of twelfths provides a simple approximation that is accurate enough for most practical purposes. It assumes that the tidal range rises and falls in the following fractions over successive hour-long intervals:

In the first hour after low water, the tide rises one-twelfth of the total spring range. In the second hour, two-twelfths. Third hour: three-twelfths. Fourth hour: three-twelfths. Fifth hour: two-twelfths. Sixth hour: one-twelfth.

The tide rises fastest in the middle of the flood — hours three and four — and most slowly at the extremes. If the spring tidal range at your location is 6 metres, the tide rises approximately 1.5 metres in the middle two hours of the flood: 0.75 metres per hour. This is walking pace for a fit person on flat ground, but on a steeply shelving foreshore it can fill rapidly enough to cut off a climber below a cliff face who assumed they had more time.

The critical safety application: if you are on a section of beach below a cliff or in a gully that requires dry passage to escape, check your tide table, calculate when the flood will reach your position using the rule of twelfths, and allow at least double the time you think you need to return.

## Rock Pools and Timing Your Visit

Rock pools are exposed at low tide and submerged at high. The species diversity and density in a rock pool increases with how rarely it is exposed — the lowest pools, only uncovered at low water springs, support anemones, starfish, sea urchins, and large hermit crabs that require near-permanent submergence. The higher pools, exposed for long periods each day, support a more limited stress-tolerant assemblage of limpets, periwinkles, and shore crabs.

The optimal timing for rock pool exploration is therefore the lowest possible low water — typically a spring low occurring in daylight hours. On British coasts, spring tides occur in the period around new and full moon; the lowest spring lows of the year typically coincide with spring (March-April) and autumn (September-October) equinoxes, when the sun is at low declination and the tidal forcing is amplified.

## Tides and Sandbar Beaches

On beaches with complex sandbar morphology, the relationship between tide height and beach accessibility is non-linear. At some beaches, low tide exposes hundreds of metres of sand flat; at the same beach at high tide, the available beach may be only ten metres wide between the water's edge and the dunes. Planning a beach day on an unfamiliar coast without checking the tide table risks arriving at a beach that is entirely underwater, or driving two hours to find a storm beach of large boulders where the sand only exists at mid to low tide.

This is particularly relevant in macrotidal environments — coasts with tidal ranges exceeding four metres, including the Bristol Channel, Brittany, the Fundy Shore, and the Korean Yellow Sea coast. At these locations, the difference between high and low tide is not aesthetic but categorical.
