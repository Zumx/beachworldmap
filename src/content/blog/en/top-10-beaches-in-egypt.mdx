Egypt's beach geography divides along two coasts: the Red Sea coast from Sharm El Sheikh to Marsa Alam on the Sinai Peninsula and along the Eastern Desert, and the Mediterranean coast from Alexandria west toward Libya. The Red Sea is the international diving and beach resort coast; the Mediterranean has a more local character with a few resort enclaves.

The Red Sea is among the world's most significant coral reef systems. The fringing reefs along the Egyptian coast contain biodiversity that ranks among the best in the world for recreational diving. The beach resort infrastructure in Sharm El Sheikh and Hurghada has been developed for mass European tourism since the 1990s and is extensive.

## 1. Sharm El Sheikh, South Sinai

Sharm El Sheikh at the southern tip of the Sinai Peninsula is Egypt's most famous resort destination. The beaches themselves — Na'ama Bay, Sharm El Maya, Sharks Bay — are of sandy beach character with coral reefs immediately accessible by boat or from some beach access points. The Ras Mohammed National Park at the peninsula's tip protects some of the world's most famous dive sites. The resort infrastructure is comprehensive: hotels at all price points, water sports, and a developed town infrastructure.

## 2. Dahab, South Sinai

Dahab, 90 kilometres north of Sharm El Sheikh on the Gulf of Aqaba, has a different character: a more relaxed backpacker and diver culture focused on the Blue Hole — a famous and technically serious dive site — and the stretch of waterfront camps and restaurants. Dahab's beach front is a long promenade along the Gulf of Aqaba with colourful textile shops and restaurants serving fresh fish. The Blue Hole is a 130-metre sinkhole in the reef; recreational snorkellers can enjoy the outer rim.

## 3. Marsa Alam, Red Sea Governorate

Marsa Alam 220 kilometres south of Hurghada is the gateway to the southern Red Sea's best marine environments: dugong at Marsa Abu Dabbab, whale sharks at Elphinstone, and spinner dolphin populations at Sataya. The beach infrastructure is smaller than Hurghada or Sharm but growing. The marine environment here is less impacted by decades of mass tourism and the coral quality is demonstrably better than the more developed northern Red Sea.

## 4. Hurghada, Red Sea Governorate

Hurghada is Egypt's largest beach resort city and the destination for most European charter tourism. The beach infrastructure is enormous: hotels stretch for 40 kilometres along the coast. The coral quality close to the main Hurghada waterfront has declined over decades of development, but boat trips to the offshore islands — Giftun Island, Abu Ramada — deliver good snorkelling 30 to 40 minutes from the resort area. The beach town infrastructure is extremely efficient for mass tourism.

## 5. El Gouna, Red Sea Governorate

El Gouna, 25 kilometres north of Hurghada, is a planned resort town built on a series of islands and lagoons in the Red Sea. The town has a more upmarket character than Hurghada with better-maintained infrastructure, a pedestrian-friendly layout on a series of canals, and beaches on the Red Sea lagoon. The kitesurfing conditions in the lagoon are internationally known. El Gouna's design ethos — a master-planned tourism resort town — is distinctive in Egypt.

## 6. Marsa Abu Dabbab, Marsa Alam

Marsa Abu Dabbab is a bay south of Marsa Alam famous for its resident dugong population — large marine mammals that graze on the seagrass in the bay. Snorkelling here regularly produces dugong sightings. Sea turtles also rest in the bay. The beach itself is a sandy bay with basic facilities at the water's edge. The marine environment is the draw.

## 7. Ras Sudr, Sinai Peninsula

Ras Sudr on the west coast of Sinai (Gulf of Suez side) is a kitesurfing destination with flat water and consistent wind, close enough to Cairo to function as a weekend escape. The beaches are sandy, the infrastructure basic, and the Gulf of Suez is not comparable to the Red Sea for diving. But for kitesurfing and a quick Egyptian beach fix from Cairo, Ras Sudr is the practical choice.

## 8. Sahl Hasheesh, Red Sea Governorate

Sahl Hasheesh, south of Hurghada, is another planned resort complex on a 12-kilometre bay with a protected lagoon. The man-made lagoon beaches are calm and the development is less dense than Hurghada. The beach infrastructure is good and the marine environment in the outer bay is better than the Hurghada waterfront proper.

## 9. Nuweiba, South Sinai

Nuweiba on the Sinai's Gulf of Aqaba coast is quieter than Dahab or Sharm and has retained some of the Sinai's original beach camp character. The view across the Gulf of Aqaba to Saudi Arabia and Jordan is dramatic. The beach has both sand and coral sections. Nuweiba is a longer journey from the main airports but the reduced tourist density is apparent.

## 10. Alexandria Beaches, Mediterranean Coast

Alexandria's Corniche has a series of city beaches — Montazah, Stanley, Sidi Bishr — that function as genuine urban beach amenity for Alexandria's large population. The Mediterranean beaches here are sandy, the water quality variable, and the character distinctly local rather than international resort. Montazah has the most pleasant setting, within the grounds of the former royal Montazah Palace gardens.

## Planning the Egyptian coast

The practical decision for most international visitors to Egypt's beaches is between the Sinai Peninsula (accessed via Sharm El Sheikh Airport, direct flights from Europe) and the Red Sea mainland coast (accessed via Hurghada Airport, similarly well-served by European charter traffic). The two coasts are separated by the Gulf of Suez and are not easily combined in a single trip without significant additional travel.

Sharm El Sheikh and the Sinai coast is the better option for diving and snorkelling: the Ras Mohammed National Park and the Straits of Tiran sites are among the world's top dive destinations, and Dahab's Blue Hole and Canyon are on every serious diver's list. The beach resort infrastructure is comprehensive for mass tourism.

Hurghada and the mainland Red Sea coast is the better option for pure beach resort stays with good access to snorkelling: Giftun Island day trips, the El Gouna kitesurfing lagoon, and the growing Marsa Alam area offer more variety for those who want a beach base with day-trip options. Marsa Alam specifically is worth serious consideration for divers who want better coral quality than the heavily trafficked northern sites.

The water temperature in the Red Sea averages 24 to 28 degrees Celsius year-round, making it one of the most comfortable swimming environments in the world with no wetsuit requirement for recreational swimmers or snorkellers. The Red Sea has no significant tides — the tidal range is less than 60 centimetres — which means beach access and snorkelling conditions are consistent throughout the day without tide planning.

Entry formalities: Egypt operates a Sinai-only visa available on arrival at Sharm El Sheikh Airport, valid only in the Sharm El Sheikh governorate and not for travel to the rest of Egypt. For travel to Hurghada, Marsa Alam, or any mainland Egyptian destination, a full Egyptian visa (available on arrival at all major airports or in advance online) is required. This distinction catches visitors who plan to visit both the Sinai and mainland — each destination requires appropriate visa status.
