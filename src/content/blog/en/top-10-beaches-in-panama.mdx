Panama is one of very few countries where you can watch the sun rise over the Pacific and set over the Caribbean — or vice versa — from a single vantage point on the continental divide. The country connects Central and South America and has Caribbean coastline on its north and Pacific coastline on its south. The two coasts are entirely different in character: the Caribbean side, particularly the Bocas del Toro archipelago in the northwest, has the turquoise water, coral reefs, and mangrove channels associated with the tropical Caribbean. The Pacific side has warmer but often murkier water, stronger surf, and an ecology shaped by the seasonal upwelling of cold, nutrient-rich deep water that drives the exceptional marine biodiversity of the Panama Bight. The country's beaches remain significantly less trafficked than those of Costa Rica to the north, despite comparable natural quality.

## 1. Playa Serena, Chiriquí Province

Playa Serena sits in the Chiriquí province on the Pacific coast near the town of Puerto Armuelles, close to the Costa Rican border. The Chiriquí coast has remained one of the least-developed stretches of Pacific Panama, partly because the nearby Chiriquí Highlands draw visitors upland to the highlands and volcano around Boquete. The beach is characterised by the dark volcanic sand of the Pacific coast and consistent surf from the southern Pacific swell. Puerto Armuelles was historically a banana port for the United Fruit Company, and the town's history is written in its remaining plantation architecture.

## 2. Playa Torio, Veraguas Province

Playa Torio occupies the coast of Veraguas Province in central Pacific Panama, in the area around the Coiba National Park. The Coiba National Park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site protecting Coiba Island — the largest island in Central America — and its surrounding archipelago and marine environment. The waters around Coiba have exceptional shark, ray, and whale shark populations because of their relative inaccessibility over decades when the island was a penal colony. Playa Torio sits on the mainland coast facing this national park context and shares the clean, largely undeveloped Pacific coast character of the Veraguas shoreline.

## 3. Playa Reina, Veraguas Province

Playa Reina is one of the most remote beaches on the Veraguas coast, accessible primarily by boat from Santa Catalina. Santa Catalina is the surfing hub of Pacific Panama, a small town at the end of a long dirt road that has attracted a core surf community around its consistent and powerful point break. Playa Reina sits further into the gulf, requiring a boat trip from Santa Catalina or Coiba excursion boats. The beach is sandy and wild, with the Coiba National Park marine protected area immediately offshore. The combination of remote beach access and world-class snorkelling and diving makes it exceptional for prepared visitors.

## 4. Playa El Salado, Veraguas Province

Playa El Salado lies near the mouth of the Río San Pablo estuary in Veraguas Province, in an area of mangrove-fringed Pacific coast typical of the central Panamanian shore. The beach is characteristic of Pacific Panama's quieter sections: dark sand, warm water, and a backdrop of coastal vegetation and river estuary. Access is from the Pan-American Highway via secondary roads, and the beach sees primarily local Panamanian visitors rather than international tourists. The associated estuary provides habitat for crocodiles, dozens of bird species, and the marine life that moves between the estuary and the Pacific.

## 5. Playa Paraíso, Coclé Province

Playa Paraíso — Paradise Beach — sits in Coclé Province on the central Pacific coast, one of the more accessible beaches in this region given the relative proximity to Panama City via the Pan-American Highway. The Coclé coast has attracted modest beach development serving the Panama City weekend market, and Playa Paraíso has the kind of beach-bar and seafood restaurant strip that serves domestic Panamanian family beach trips. The Pacific water here is warm and the beach is broad, with the characteristic dark sand of the volcanic Pacific coast. [Open the map](/map) to find Playa Paraíso's position on the Coclé coast road.

## 6. Playa Paragüito, Los Santos Province

Playa Paragüito sits on the Azuero Peninsula in Los Santos Province, one of the most culturally distinctive regions of Panama. The Azuero Peninsula is the heartland of traditional Panamanian folklore — the pollera dress, the cumbia dance, and the elaborate Carnaval traditions of Villa de Los Santos all originate here. The coast of the Azuero Peninsula has consistent Pacific surf on its western shore and more sheltered conditions on its eastern Gulf of Panama side. Paragüito is a small beach with the typical character of the Azuero coast: fishing community at its core, modest infrastructure, consistent surf.

## 7. Playa Grande, Colón Province

Playa Grande sits on the Caribbean coast of Colón Province in eastern Panama, near the border with Kuna Yala (formerly San Blas), the autonomous territory of the Guna indigenous people. This Caribbean coast of Colón and the adjacent Kuna territory is among the most extraordinary coastal environments in Central America: hundreds of coral cays and islands, some the size of a football pitch, others barely above water, with reef systems largely intact due to the Guna's traditional territory management and restricted access. Playa Grande is a mainland access point before the Guna territory begins.

## 8. Playa San San Norte, Bocas del Toro Province

Playa San San Norte lies on the mainland coast of the Bocas del Toro province, north of the provincial capital, near the San San Pond Sak wetland. The San San Pond Sak is a protected wetland of international significance with exceptional biodiversity including manatees, caimans, and major leatherback turtle nesting activity on the adjacent beaches. The nesting season peaks between March and July, when female leatherbacks — the largest living turtle species, reaching over two metres in length and exceeding 500 kilograms — come ashore in significant numbers. The beach is part of the turtle conservation zone managed by the Panamanian environmental authority ANAM.

## 9. Playa Langosta, Bocas del Toro Province

Playa Langosta — Lobster Beach — sits on one of the outer islands of the Bocas del Toro archipelago, accessible by water taxi from the main island of Bocas del Toro (Isla Colón). The Bocas del Toro archipelago is the primary beach destination on Panama's Caribbean coast, a cluster of six large islands and dozens of smaller cays with mangrove channels, coral reefs, and the kind of turquoise shallow water associated with the best Caribbean beaches. Bocas town on Isla Colón is the hub, with water taxis radiating to the various beaches and cays. Playa Langosta was historically known for its sea turtle nesting.

## 10. Playa Malibu, Bocas del Toro Province

Playa Malibu is a beach on Bastimentos Island in the Bocas del Toro archipelago, within the Bastimentos Marine National Park. Bastimentos is the second-largest island in the archipelago and contains a large section of protected forest alongside the marine park. Playa Malibu faces the open Caribbean and receives consistent swell that makes it one of the better surf beaches in the archipelago — Bocas del Toro has a surf culture built around several breaks including this one. The water here is the clear, warm turquoise of the Caribbean, and the reef systems around Bastimentos have some of the highest coral cover in the Bocas archipelago.
