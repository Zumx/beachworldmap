The Red Sea is one of the most unusual marine environments on earth: an elongated, enclosed, geologically young ocean connecting the Indian Ocean through the Bab el-Mandeb Strait to an otherwise landlocked basin. Its water is warm — unusually so, even for its latitude — because the surrounding Sahara and Arabian deserts generate enormous atmospheric heat that is absorbed by the shallow sea. Its salinity is among the highest in the world's oceans, because the rate of evaporation far exceeds any freshwater input (no rivers of significance drain into the Red Sea), and water exchange through the Bab el-Mandeb is restricted. The combination of warmth, salinity, and nutrient-poor oligotrophic water produces the extraordinary clarity and coral richness that have made Red Sea beaches internationally famous for snorkelling and diving.

## Geography and Marine Conditions

The Red Sea stretches approximately 2,250 kilometres from the Bab el-Mandeb in the south to the Gulf of Suez and Gulf of Aqaba in the north. Its average depth is around 490 metres, but the majority of the reef systems that draw beach tourists are located in the northern and central sections, particularly along the Egyptian Sinai Peninsula and the Egyptian Red Sea coast between Hurghada and Marsa Alam.

Water temperatures along the Egyptian coast range from about 22 degrees Celsius in February at the northern beaches near Sharm el-Sheikh and Hurghada, to over 30 degrees in August. For snorkelling and diving, the autumn and winter months (October through February) often offer better conditions than summer because the lower temperatures are associated with higher visibility — thermal stratification in summer can reduce transparency in the upper water column, and winds from the north (the regulars in summer) generate surface chop that makes snorkelling from beaches less comfortable.

The Red Sea's strong tidal flows are restricted compared to many open ocean coasts because the exchange with the Indian Ocean is through a relatively narrow strait. Tidal range along the Egyptian coast is modest — typically under 1 metre — which means reef edges and snorkel conditions are relatively stable throughout the tidal cycle.

## Sharm el-Sheikh: The Sinai Peninsula's Reef Capital

Sharm el-Sheikh occupies the southern tip of the Sinai Peninsula at the confluence of the Gulf of Suez and the Gulf of Aqaba. Its diving and snorkelling industry grew rapidly from the 1980s following the return of Sinai to Egyptian sovereignty and substantial investment in resort infrastructure. By the early 2000s, Sharm was receiving over 3 million tourists annually, primarily British, German, Italian, and Russian visitors, and had become one of the largest dive tourism concentrations in the world.

The reef systems adjacent to Sharm's beaches are the reason for this success. Ras Muhammad National Park, at the very tip of Sinai approximately 12 kilometres south of the town centre, contains some of the Red Sea's most spectacular coral gardens — walls dropping hundreds of metres into the Gulf of Aqaba abyss, with fish diversity that reflects the Red Sea's status as a semi-enclosed sea with high endemism. Approximately 20 percent of Red Sea fish species are found nowhere else on earth.

Near the town itself, Naama Bay's fringing reef is accessible from the beach and has been the most heavily visited site in Sharm's reef network for decades. The reef quality here has degraded significantly from its 1980s condition due to tourist pressure, boat anchor damage, and sewage from the resort strip. The outer reefs, accessible by boat — Shark Reef, Jolanda Reef, the Blue Hole near Dahab — retain better condition.

The Blue Hole at Dahab, approximately 100 kilometres north of Sharm on the Gulf of Aqaba coast, is arguably the most famous dive site in the world beyond the Great Barrier Reef. It is a circular submarine sinkhole approximately 300 metres in diameter, dropping to over 100 metres at its centre, with an arch at 56 metres connecting to the open sea. It is also the site of an extraordinarily high concentration of technical diving fatalities — multiple deaths per year across its history — related to the arch depth and the nitrogen narcosis and decompression risks it presents. For snorkellers, the surface of the Blue Hole and the adjacent Bells beach offer spectacular shallow reef accessible without specialist equipment.

## Hurghada and the Northern Red Sea Coast

Hurghada, on the Egyptian mainland coast roughly 200 kilometres south of Suez, grew from a small fishing village in the 1960s to one of the largest beach resort complexes in Africa and the Middle East. Its development trajectory was faster and less controlled than Sharm's, and the reef system adjacent to the urban area suffered significant damage from construction, boat traffic, and sewage in the 1990s and early 2000s.

The more interesting reef diving and snorkelling is now typically accessed by boat from Hurghada's marina to the offshore islands — Giftun Island (now part of the Giftun Island Protected Area), Abu Ramada, and the reefs around the Shadwan Island area. These offshore systems remain in better condition than the nearshore reefs, and the 30 to 40-minute boat transfers provide meaningful buffer from the worst of the town's impacts.

South of Hurghada, the road along the Egyptian Red Sea coast passes through El Gouna (a planned resort development with lagoon access and its own reef), Safaga (a diving and windsurfing destination), El Quseir (a historic Ottoman port with better-preserved reefs), and Marsa Alam, where the Abu Dabbab dugong site has become one of the world's most accessible locations for snorkelling with dugongs. The population of dugongs (Dugong dugon) in the Red Sea is one of the few remaining healthy populations outside Australia.

## Aqaba and Jordan's 27 Kilometres

Jordan's entire coastline is a single 27-kilometre stretch of the Gulf of Aqaba, with the port city of Aqaba at its centre. Despite its brevity, Jordan's coral reef system is in notably better condition than many Egyptian equivalents due to lower tourism pressure and stronger regulatory enforcement. The South Beach area, managed as a marine protected area, permits snorkelling and diving with reasonable oversight of anchoring and visitor numbers. The ferry crossing to Egypt's Sinai is a common logistics element for visitors combining both coasts.

Israel's Eilat, on the northern tip of the Gulf of Aqaba, has its own reef system in comparable condition to Aqaba's. The political geography makes combining Israeli and Jordanian visits straightforward for most nationalities, though travellers holding Israeli entry stamps may face complications in certain other Middle Eastern countries.

## Practical Planning for Red Sea Beach Holidays

The Red Sea beach holiday model differs from most tropical beach destinations in one important respect: the snorkelling and diving is genuinely accessible from the beach itself at most resorts, without needing a boat. A mask, snorkel, and fins are sufficient to access coral reef systems at depths of two to five metres within swimming distance of the shore at many Sharm and Aqaba beach properties. This accessibility makes it one of the most cost-effective coral reef experiences in the world.
