The world's beaches are disappearing. A 2020 study published in Nature Climate Change analysed satellite imagery of 24 years of shoreline change across nearly the entire global coastline and found that roughly one-third of the world's sandy beaches are already eroding at measurable rates. Under high-emissions scenarios, the same research projected that more than half of global sandy beaches could be significantly reduced or lost entirely by 2100. These are not marginal or uncertain findings — they represent the integration of extensive observational data with well-established physical oceanographic modelling.

The causes are multiple, but sea level rise is the governing long-term driver. It is compounded by wave climate change, sediment supply disruption, and coastal development that has already removed the natural buffers — dunes, mangroves, seagrass beds — that would otherwise allow beaches to migrate landward as sea levels rise.

## The Physics of Shoreline Erosion Under Sea Level Rise

Sandy beaches are not static features. Under natural conditions, they exist in a state of dynamic equilibrium: sand moves onshore and offshore seasonally, is redistributed by storms, and migrates along the coast through longshore drift. The equilibrium profile — the beach's cross-sectional shape in balance with prevailing wave energy — adjusts continuously to maintain a stable planform position.

Sea level rise disrupts this equilibrium by raising the base level against which the equilibrium profile must adjust. Bruun's Rule — a simplified model proposed by Per Bruun in 1962 and still used in its essentials in coastal engineering practice — states that for every unit of sea level rise, the shoreline retreats by roughly 50 to 200 times that amount horizontally, depending on beach slope. For a moderately sloping beach with a 100:1 horizontal-to-vertical ratio, one metre of sea level rise produces 100 metres of shoreline retreat.

The IPCC Sixth Assessment Report (2021) projects global mean sea level rise of 0.28 to 0.55 metres under low-emission scenarios and 0.63 to 1.01 metres under high-emission scenarios by 2100 relative to 1995-2014 baseline. Ice sheet instabilities in Greenland and West Antarctica introduce significant upward uncertainty in the upper range estimates. For beaches already eroding, these projections are applied to a baseline that is not neutral.

## Small Island States and Existential Coastal Risk

The most acute near-term threat falls on small island developing states whose land elevation is almost entirely within the zone of sea level rise risk. The Maldives, the Marshall Islands, Tuvalu, Kiribati, and the Cocos (Keeling) Islands have average land elevations of one to two metres above sea level. Their beaches are not just tourist amenities — they are the direct substrate of their societies.

The Maldives has been particularly studied. Its 1,190 coral islands have a maximum elevation of 2.4 metres; most inhabited areas sit below 1.5 metres. Research published in Science Advances in 2018 projected that most low-elevation atoll islands will experience annual wave overtopping by the mid-21st century under current emission trajectories, rendering them uninhabitable even before permanent inundation. The Maldivian government has invested in artificial land reclamation — Hulhumalé island, constructed by dredging lagoon sediment onto a shallow reef platform — as a domestic relocation strategy.

Tuvalu's government signed a bilateral agreement with Australia in 2023 providing pathways to citizenship for all Tuvaluan residents as a managed climate migration response — the first formal international agreement explicitly framed around sea level rise displacement.

## Beach Nourishment: The Standard Short-Term Response

Beach nourishment — the artificial addition of imported sand to an eroding beach — is the dominant coastal engineering response to beach erosion in wealthy countries. The US Army Corps of Engineers manages nourishment programmes along virtually the entire US Atlantic and Gulf coasts; the Netherlands maintains the most sophisticated national nourishment programme in the world. Spain, France, and England all operate significant nourishment programmes at their most economically important beach destinations.

The economics are straightforward: a nourished beach supports tourism and real estate values that generate tax revenue far exceeding the cost of nourishment, at least in the short to medium term. Miami Beach has been nourishing its strand continuously since the 1970s; the multi-billion-dollar real estate along Collins Avenue rests on a maintained beach that would otherwise have eroded to nothing.

The engineering reality is more complicated. Nourishment sand must be compatible with the beach it is placed on — too coarse and it will not move with the natural system; too fine and it erodes faster than native sand. The sand is sourced from offshore dredging, which has its own environmental impacts on benthic communities and sediment supply chains. Most critically, nourishment is not permanent. In high-energy environments, nourished sand erodes within two to five years and must be replaced repeatedly. Each replacement cycle costs as much or more than the initial placement.

## Managed Retreat: The Structural Alternative

Managed retreat — the deliberate relocation of coastal infrastructure and property away from eroding shorelines — is technically straightforward, economically sound in many circumstances, and politically extremely difficult. The difficulty is not engineering but the intersection of property rights, political will, and the psychological resistance of coastal communities to accepting that the land they own will be surrendered to the sea.

The most successful managed retreat programmes in the world are those applied in areas with relatively low property value density and strong central government authority. In England, several sections of the Suffolk and Norfolk coast — including the village of Happisburgh, which has lost several rows of houses to erosion since the 1990s — have been placed in the lowest category of coastal defence investment, with residents compensated and the shoreline left to erode naturally. The ICZM (Integrated Coastal Zone Management) framework in England categorises coastlines into four management responses: hold the line (defend), advance the line (reclaim), managed realignment (retreat), and no active intervention.

In the United States, the political obstacles to retreat are larger because coastal property rights are more deeply embedded in state and federal law. Several US counties — most notably Dare County in North Carolina — have retreated from offering unlimited beach nourishment, acknowledging that the costs are not sustainable, but formal managed retreat at scale has not been achieved anywhere in the American coastal system.

## Which Beach Destinations Face Existential Risk by 2050

The beaches at highest risk of severe reduction or loss within the 25-year horizon are concentrated in four categories.

Micronesian and Polynesian atoll islands, including Funafuti (Tuvalu), South Tarawa (Kiribati), and Majuro (Marshall Islands), face wave overtopping risk during storm events by 2050 under mid-range sea level scenarios.

Barrier island beaches along the US Gulf Coast — particularly in Louisiana, Mississippi, and Alabama — face both sea level rise and extreme land subsidence driven by groundwater and hydrocarbon extraction. Grand Isle, Louisiana, has lost metres of shoreline per year for decades and is defended by a three-metre concrete seawall.

Low-lying Caribbean beaches on flat limestone islands — particularly in the Bahamas — are vulnerable to the combination of sea level rise and increasing hurricane intensity. The Abacos Islands were effectively destroyed as a functioning community by Hurricane Dorian in 2019.

Several Mediterranean beaches face severe erosion independent of sea level rise due to dam construction on river catchments that has reduced sediment supply to delta beaches. The Ebro Delta in Spain and the Nile Delta coast of Egypt are eroding rapidly because sediments that once nourished their beaches are now trapped behind dams upstream.
