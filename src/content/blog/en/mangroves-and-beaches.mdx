The trees that grow in saline and brackish water along tropical and subtropical coastlines are among the least photogenic coastal ecosystems and among the most functionally important. Mangroves — the collective term for some 80 species of salt-tolerant trees and shrubs found between the latitudes of approximately 25 degrees north and south — do not form the postcard beaches that drive tourism revenue. They occupy the muddy, tangled zone between land and sea, and for decades were treated as obstacles to coastal development: cleared for shrimp aquaculture, hotel construction, and port infrastructure. The consequences of their removal are now comprehensively documented and the scale of loss — the world has lost roughly 35 percent of its mangrove cover since 1980 — is a measurable contributor to beach erosion, coastal flood damage, and fisheries collapse.

## How Mangroves Stabilise Coastlines and Protect Adjacent Beaches

Mangrove root systems — particularly the arching prop roots of Rhizophora species and the pencil-like pneumatophores of Avicennia species — function as extraordinarily efficient sediment traps. As tidal water moves through the root matrix, its velocity drops and the suspended sediment load settles. Over time, this accretion builds the substrate upward. Mangrove forests literally create land, and they maintain it by continuously trapping new sediment with each tidal cycle.

The wave attenuation function of mangrove forests is significant and has been measured directly. A 2006 study published in Nature found that a 100-metre-wide stand of Rhizophora mangroves reduced wave energy by 50 to 75 percent. This energy dissipation protects not just the mangrove substrate itself but the beaches, salt marshes, and low-lying coastal land immediately behind the forest. On coastlines where mangroves have been removed, wave energy reaches the shoreline unreduced; erosion rates accelerate and beaches narrow.

This relationship was demonstrated with terrible clarity during the 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami. Villages in Tamil Nadu, India, and along the Thai Andaman coast that were backed by intact mangrove forests sustained significantly less damage than villages on equivalent topography where mangroves had been cleared for shrimp ponds. Post-event analyses published in Science and other journals noted that the degree of protection varied with mangrove density and width, but the directional effect was consistent.

## Fish Nurseries and the Connection to Ocean Fisheries

Mangroves are not marine ecosystems — they are intertidal, shifting between submergence and exposure with each tidal cycle. But their role in supporting marine fish populations is foundational. The complex root systems provide structural habitat that juvenile fish use as refugia from predators. The leaf litter and organic detritus from mangrove forests decompose into fine particulate organic matter that enters the marine food web as a primary energy source.

Studies across the Caribbean, Southeast Asia, and West Africa have found that the adult population densities of economically important fish species — snapper, grouper, barracuda, various shrimp species — are significantly higher in reef and coastal areas adjacent to intact mangrove forest than in areas where mangroves have been removed. The mechanism is the nursery: juveniles settle in the mangroves where they are safe, then migrate to reef and offshore habitats as adults. Remove the nursery, and recruitment to adult populations falls.

In the Yucatan, the mangrove systems behind the Mesoamerican Barrier Reef form an essential link in the reef fisheries that support both commercial fishing and the diving and snorkelling tourism industry. In Southeast Asia — particularly in the Philippines, Indonesia, and Vietnam — the conversion of mangroves to shrimp aquaculture ponds produced short-term productivity followed by rapid decline, as the nutrient and disease management that mangroves provided for free collapsed. Many converted areas are now abandoned due to salinisation and disease, while the mangrove cover has not recovered.

## Carbon Storage and Climate Relevance

Mangroves store carbon at rates that are exceptional even within tropical forest ecosystems. The organic-rich waterlogged soils beneath mangrove forests accumulate carbon-rich material over centuries without the decomposition that occurs in well-oxygenated soils. Studies across the Indo-Pacific estimate that mangrove soils may contain three to five times more carbon per hectare than tropical upland forests.

When mangroves are cleared, this accumulated carbon is released — both immediately through burning (common in clearance for aquaculture) and over subsequent years as the waterlogged soils drain and previously anoxic organic material decomposes. The IPCC has identified mangrove loss as a significant source of coastal greenhouse gas emissions, and mangrove restoration is now included in the carbon credit markets of multiple countries as a verified carbon sequestration pathway.

The Mikoko Pamoja project in Gazi Bay, Kenya — operational since 2013 — was the first community-based mangrove carbon credit scheme to be formally certified. It involves the protection and restoration of approximately 117 hectares of mangrove forest, generating verified carbon credits sold primarily to European buyers, with revenue funding local school and water infrastructure improvements. Similar projects have since been established in Mozambique, Madagascar, Indonesia, and Myanmar.

## The Shrimp Aquaculture Trade-Off

Between 1980 and 2005, commercial shrimp aquaculture — primarily in Thailand, Indonesia, Vietnam, the Philippines, Ecuador, and Bangladesh — was responsible for roughly 38 percent of global mangrove loss. The economics of the conversion were compelling in the short term: mangrove land was state-owned and available at low cost, clearing was straightforward, and shrimp prices were rising with growing global demand. The ecological services the mangroves provided — fisheries support, storm protection, water filtration — were not priced into the conversion decision.

The long-term results are well documented. Shrimp pond productivity typically peaks in the first three to five years before declining as disease, nutrient accumulation, and salinisation degrade production capacity. Many converted areas in Thailand and the Philippines that were cleared in the 1980s and 1990s are now abandoned. Thailand has since introduced legal protections for remaining mangrove forests and is pursuing a national restoration target of 300,000 hectares.

## Restoration: Methods, Timescales, and Challenges

Mangrove restoration is ecologically feasible but requires attention to the hydrological conditions that mangroves need. The most common restoration failure mode is planting seedlings in areas where the tidal regime is incorrect — typically too infrequently flooded or too deeply submerged — producing high mortality regardless of species selection or aftercare.

Successful restoration projects typically involve hydrological site assessment before any planting, preferring natural regeneration where propagules from nearby forests are available to human-planted seedlings. In areas where natural propagule supply is insufficient, species such as Rhizophora mucronata and Avicennia marina have shown good establishment rates across the Indo-Pacific when planted in appropriate tidal zones.

The Society for Ecological Restoration estimates that successfully established mangrove restoration reaches functional maturity — providing measurable fish nursery and sediment trapping services — within five to ten years. Full structural complexity approximating old-growth mangrove takes decades.

## Finding Mangrove Coastlines Worth Protecting

Mangrove ecosystems and beaches coexist most visibly in areas like the Florida Keys, the Yucatan coast, the Andaman Sea shores of Thailand and Myanmar, and the East African coast from Kenya to Mozambique. The presence of intact mangroves behind a beach is a direct indicator of lower erosion risk and healthier nearshore fisheries.
