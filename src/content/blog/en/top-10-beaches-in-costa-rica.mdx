Costa Rica has about 1,290 kilometres of Pacific coastline and 212 kilometres of Caribbean shore, and despite being a relatively small country — roughly the size of West Virginia — the range of beach environments is remarkable. The Pacific coast divides into the dry Guanacaste region in the north, where beaches bake under reliably sunny skies from November to April, and the rainier central and southern Pacific where humidity stays high year-round but the jungle behind the beaches is denser and the wildlife more concentrated. The Caribbean side has its own rhythm, wetter in different seasons, home to world-famous turtle nesting sites and undeveloped reef systems. The country's commitment to protected land has preserved much of what makes its coastline exceptional.

## 1. Playa Albina, Osa Peninsula

Playa Albina sits within the Corcovado National Park buffer zone on the Osa Peninsula, one of the most biodiverse places on Earth. The beach is dark grey volcanic sand backed by old-growth rainforest, and access requires either a long jungle hike or a boat from the nearby Bahía Drake. Wildlife encounters on the walk — tapir tracks in the sand at dawn, scarlet macaw flocks overhead, white-faced capuchin monkeys in the canopy — are realistic rather than occasional. The Osa Peninsula is reached from San José by a 45-minute domestic flight to Puerto Jiménez or Drake Bay, followed by boat or 4WD transport.

## 2. Playa Jícaro, Nicoya Peninsula

Playa Jícaro lies on the northern Nicoya Peninsula in Guanacaste province, north of Nosara, and is accessible via a rough dirt road that discourages casual visitors. The beach is around two kilometres of pale brown sand facing northwest, with consistent beach-break surf from May through November when the Pacific swell is most active. Jícaro is known among Costa Rican surfers as an uncrowded alternative to the more famous breaks at Nosara and Tamarindo, with the added advantage of a reef section at the river mouth that produces hollow left-hand waves during the right swell period.

## 3. Playa Coquimba, Nicoya Peninsula

Playa Coquimba is a small cove south of Nosara, accessible on foot down a steep path through dense vegetation. The beach is protected and the water calm, sitting inside a natural inlet that blocks the dominant swells. The surrounding headlands support dry tropical forest — deciduous and golden in the November-to-April dry season, bright green in the wet months — and the bird life is considerable for anyone willing to sit quietly. The Nicoya Blue Zone designation (an area associated with unusually long human lifespans) covers this part of the peninsula.

## 4. Playa Leña, Guanacaste

Playa Leña occupies a quiet stretch of the northern Nicoya coast that remains genuinely undeveloped. The beach is broad and sandy, with a dry-forest backdrop that turns sparse in the dry season and surprisingly lush during the rains. Access is by dirt road from the nearest paved route, and the lack of infrastructure keeps visitor numbers modest. The beach is used by local fishing families from nearby communities, and the same conditions that create the dry-season sunshine also make the early morning light here exceptionally good — calm water, golden sand, and clear skies.

## 5. Playa Española, Guanacaste

Playa Española sits within the Santa Rosa National Park sector of the Area de Conservación Guanacaste, one of the most important protected areas in Mesoamerica. The beach requires a 13-kilometre dirt road drive from the park headquarters followed by a further hike, which limits access effectively to those willing to make the effort. Olive ridley sea turtles nest in mass synchronised events called arribadas here, primarily between August and November, when thousands of females come ashore in a single night. The nesting spectacle is one of the most extraordinary wildlife events in Costa Rica and requires advance park permits to observe.

## 6. Playa Muerto, Osa Peninsula

Playa Muerto — Dead Beach, so named for the stillness of the air in the surrounding estuary — is a remote strand on the southern Osa Peninsula near the Panamanian border. The beach is reached by boat from Puerto Jiménez, passing through the Golfo Dulce, a warm tropical fjord. Fishing is excellent in the estuary, dolphins are commonly seen in the gulf, and scarlet macaws are almost guaranteed in the adjacent forest.

## 7. Playa Santa Teresa, Nicoya Peninsula

Playa Santa Teresa on the southern tip of the Nicoya Peninsula has become one of Costa Rica's most fashionable surf destinations over the past 15 years, with a small but sophisticated strip of boutique hotels, yoga retreats, and farm-to-table restaurants backing a long, consistent beach break. The surf works on both swell directions and is suitable for intermediate surfers; more powerful reef breaks nearby attract experienced riders. The village of Santa Teresa is reachable from Puntarenas by car ferry to Paquera, then a 90-minute drive south on improving but still partly unpaved roads.

## 8. Playa Conchal, Guanacaste

Playa Conchal is named for the billions of crushed shell fragments — conchas — that make up its beach surface instead of sand. The result is a bright white beach with a distinctive crunchy texture underfoot and water of exceptional clarity over the shell-sand bottom. It sits directly south of Playa Brasilito near Flamingo in northern Guanacaste, accessible by walking around the headland at low tide or by boat. The large Westin resort complex occupies the headland above, but the beach itself remains public under Costa Rican law. Snorkelling around the rock outcrops at either end is genuinely rewarding.

## 9. Playa Manuel Antonio, Manuel Antonio National Park

Playa Manuel Antonio sits inside Manuel Antonio National Park, one of the country's smallest but most visited protected areas on the central Pacific coast. The beach is a curved arc of pale sand inside a sheltered bay, with swimming conditions among the safest on the Pacific coast — calm water, no strong rip currents, and a lifeguard service. The park charges an entry fee and limits daily visitor numbers. Capuchin monkeys raid bags left unattended on the beach with practiced efficiency; wildlife encounters are genuinely excellent throughout the park, which hosts three-toed sloths, raccoons, and dozens of bird species within metres of the beach.

## 10. Playa Tamarindo, Guanacaste

Tamarindo is the most internationally recognised of Costa Rica's beach towns, with a long west-facing beach that picks up swells from multiple directions. The town has a well-developed infrastructure — surf schools, board rentals, restaurants, bars, a range of accommodation at every price point — and direct international flights from Miami and other gateways land at Liberia, 90 minutes north by road. The beach itself is wide and sandy with a river mouth at the southern end that creates a sandbar producing long peeling waves favoured by beginners and longboarders. Nesting leatherback turtles patrol the beach at night between October and March.
