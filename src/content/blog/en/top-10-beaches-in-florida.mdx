Florida has more coastline than any US state except Alaska: roughly 2,170 kilometres of tidal shoreline, split between the Atlantic Ocean on the east, the Gulf of Mexico on the west, and the Florida Keys threading southward to the Cuba Strait. The two coasts are profoundly different. The Gulf side — particularly the Panhandle and the barrier islands from Tampa Bay southward — has the finest sand in the continental United States: pure white quartz ground over millennia from Appalachian source rock and transported coast-long by wave and current action. Gulf water is warm, shallow, and calm, reaching 30°C in July at Clearwater. The Atlantic coast has a different energy — more exposed, slightly coarser sand, subject to nor'easters in winter and significant surf — but benefits from the Gulf Stream warming the water to 27–28°C in summer. The Keys, suspended between the two, offer coral reef snorkelling and diving in shallow turquoise water rather than conventional beach bathing. Year-round warmth makes Florida beaches usable in every month, though summer brings intense heat, thunderstorms, and tourist peaks, and autumn brings hurricane season awareness.

## 1. Clearwater Beach, Pinellas County

Clearwater Beach on the Pinellas Peninsula has been voted the best beach in the United States multiple times in various rankings, and the case is straightforward: extraordinarily fine white quartz sand, Gulf of Mexico water that is warm, shallow, and calm, a wide beach with good public facilities, and a lively but family-oriented town behind the strip. Pier 60 park at the beach extends over the Gulf and hosts nightly sunset celebrations. The water is so shallow that adults can wade 50 metres from shore without reaching chest depth. Water temperatures average 29°C in July and August. Access from Tampa airport takes about 45 minutes via the Howard Frankland Bridge and Pinellas Bayway.

## 2. Siesta Key Beach, Sarasota

Siesta Key Beach on the barrier island south of Sarasota is home to a sand so fine it is almost impossible to keep a grip on: 99 percent pure quartz crystal, ground from source rock over millions of years, staying cool even in direct August sun because quartz does not conduct heat. In 1987 Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution conducted a formal sand analysis and ranked it the finest sand in the world; subsequent analyses have confirmed its quality. The beach won the American Beach Survey award for best beach in the US in 2011. The public section is wide, heavily shaded at the land edge by pavilions and trees, and managed for cleanliness at high standard.

## 3. St. Pete Beach, Pinellas County

St. Pete Beach — distinct from the city of St. Petersburg on the Pinellas mainland — is a resort barrier island community facing the Gulf, north of Sarasota. The beach is wide, white, and calm, with the usual Gulf advantages, and backed by a coastal road of hotels ranging from mid-century motor courts to modern full-service resorts. The Don CeSar, a flamingo-pink Mediterranean Revival hotel opened in 1928, anchors the south end of the strand and is a National Historic Landmark. The Pass-a-Grille neighbourhood at the southern tip of the island is among the oldest resort communities on the Florida Gulf Coast, retaining streets of early-20th-century beach cottages largely intact.

## 4. Fort Lauderdale Beach, Broward County

Fort Lauderdale Beach on the Atlantic coast has undergone a transformation since its spring-break heyday of the 1960s and 70s. The city invested heavily in a redesigned beach boulevard — palm trees, wide sidewalks, outdoor cafés replacing the old motel strip — and positioned itself as an upscale resort destination. The beach itself is wide, clean, and Atlantic-facing, with stronger surf than the Gulf beaches. Fort Lauderdale is known as the Venice of America for its 300 miles of interconnected waterways, and combining beach time with a waterway boat tour gives a uniquely Floridian perspective. The Riverwalk arts and culture district is 15 minutes from the sand.

## 5. Miami Beach, Miami-Dade County

Miami Beach is synonymous with the globalised beach experience: South Beach's Art Deco district behind the sand, the strip of Ocean Drive where the historic pink-and-turquoise hotels have been converted to hotels, bars, and restaurants, and a beach culture that runs from early morning lifeguard training to late-night club scenes with essentially no pause. The sand is Atlantic-fine and wide, the water warm and clear at 27°C in August. The North Beach and Mid-Beach sections are considerably quieter than South Beach. Lummus Park, the public strand between the hotels and the ocean, is one of the most culturally diverse public spaces in American beach life.

## 6. Canaveral National Seashore, Brevard County

Canaveral National Seashore protects 37 kilometres of undeveloped Atlantic barrier island coast, entirely within sight of the Kennedy Space Center on the adjacent Merritt Island. The seashore is divided into two sections — Playalinda Beach at the south and Apollo Beach at the north — with no road connection between them. The protected status keeps both sections genuinely wild and uncrowded. Sea turtle nesting on the beach (loggerhead, green, leatherhead) is significant: tens of thousands of nests are recorded each season. The Archie Carr National Wildlife Refuge, which overlaps in part with the seashore, constitutes the most important sea turtle nesting beach in the western hemisphere.

## 7. Bahia Honda, Florida Keys

Bahia Honda State Park on Bahia Honda Key in the Lower Keys preserves one of the finest natural beaches in the Florida Keys — a narrow strip of white-shell sand on the Atlantic side of the key and a calmer, shallower sandy shore on the Gulf side. The Keys are not conventionally beach-focused — the reef is the main attraction — but Bahia Honda is the exception. The old railroad bridge from Henry Flagler's 1912 Overseas Railroad arches over the key above the water. Snorkelling from the beach reveals seagrass beds, queen conchs, and occasional hawksbill turtles. Camping at the state park gives access to the beach in early morning before day visitors arrive.

## 8. Smathers Beach, Key West

Smathers Beach is the main public beach on Key West — which, given that the island is a dense concentration of bars, historic architecture, charter fishing, and sunset culture, means it competes with considerable surrounding distraction. The beach is created from imported sand on an artificial fill area; the natural Key West shoreline is primarily mangrove and rock. It stretches about one kilometre along South Roosevelt Boulevard, facing east. Jet ski and parasail operations work from the beach. The water is calm and warm (28–29°C), and the beach is the starting point for swimming and kayak routes east toward Stock Island.

## 9. Grayton Beach, Walton County

Grayton Beach State Park in the Florida Panhandle's South Walton County contains the original Panhandle beach landscape: dunes up to ten metres high stabilised by scrub oak and rosemary, Western Lake — a coastal dune lake unique to this stretch of coast — and a beach of pure quartz sand facing the Gulf. Coastal dune lakes, which connect intermittently to the Gulf during storms and high rainfall, are an extremely rare landform found primarily on this section of the Florida coast. Grayton Beach the town, immediately adjacent, is one of the oldest resort communities on the Panhandle, with a collection of old Florida beach houses and a single saloon. The pace is genuinely unhurried.

## 10. Pensacola Beach, Escambia County

Pensacola Beach on Santa Rosa Island is the westernmost of the Panhandle's famous white-sand barrier island beaches, sitting at the junction of Florida's Gulf Coast culture and the cultural influence of Alabama and Mississippi just to the west. The sand is blinding white quartz, the water emerald-green, and the beach is backed by a small resort village with a pier, restaurants, and water sports hire. Pensacola Bay behind the island is a major US Navy air station town; the National Naval Aviation Museum on the mainland is one of the largest aviation museums in the world. The Fort Pickens unit of Gulf Islands National Seashore protects the undeveloped western end of Santa Rosa Island.
