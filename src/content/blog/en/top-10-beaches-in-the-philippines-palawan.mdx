Palawan is the long, thin island province that forms the western spine of the Philippine archipelago, stretching 450 kilometres from the tip of Luzon's western tail to Borneo-facing Balabac in the south. The island's geology is defined by the Palawan microcontinental block — an ancient fragment of Gondwana that was never volcanically active, unlike most of the Philippines — and by the coral sea that surrounds it. This geological history means Palawan has limestone karst formations of extraordinary scale, an underground river system (the Puerto Princesa Subterranean River, a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the New Seven Wonders of Nature) running beneath jungle to the sea, and beaches set between vertical limestone cliffs. The Bacuit Archipelago at El Nido in the north and the Calamian Group around Coron in the north-east are the principal beach destinations, where crystal-clear lagoons enclosed by karst towers constitute some of the most visually exceptional beach environments in Asia. The dry season from November through May is the main travel window; the south-west monsoon from June through October can make sea travel difficult, though beaches in sheltered coves remain accessible.

## 1. Starfish Island, Honda Bay, Puerto Princesa

Starfish Island is an accessible day-trip sandbar in Honda Bay, a sheltered bay an hour north of Puerto Princesa city, reachable by bangka boat from the CWC waterfront. The island is a low, narrow sandbank fringed with white sand and surrounded by shallows where the sea floor is carpeted with massive and cushion starfish (not the wasting-disease-threatened species found in the Pacific US, but local Philippine species). The sand is powdery white, the water turquoise and shallow, and the surrounding Honda Bay islands — Pambato, Meara, Cowrie — form a scattering of accessible day-trip beaches. Honda Bay snorkelling is modest but consistent.

## 2. Napsan Beach, Western Palawan

Napsan Beach on the western coast of northern Palawan, about an hour south of Puerto Princesa by road and boat, is a long arc of white sand fronting deep blue water of the Sulu Sea. The western coast of Palawan faces the prevailing south-west monsoon in summer and can be rough from June through October; outside this window the beach is calm and clear. Napsan has a small resort development and several basic beach cottages. The west coast road from Puerto Princesa to Napsan passes through the Palawan tribal territories and the coconut and cashew plantations that are the island's main agricultural products.

## 3. Nagtabon Beach, Northern Palawan

Nagtabon Beach is 40 kilometres north of Puerto Princesa on a west-facing bay sheltered by a headland from the worst monsoon swells. The beach is wide, the sand golden-white, and the water clear with decent snorkelling over nearby reef patches. The approach road is rough, requiring a tricycle or motorbike for the last section. The surrounding hillside is forested and home to the endemic Palawan peacock-pheasant and the Palawan hornbill, making a beach visit combinable with birding. Accommodation at Nagtabon is basic — hammock camping and simple cottages — which keeps the beach quiet and genuine.

## 4. Talaudyong Beach, Coron, Calamian Islands

Talaudyong Beach is near Coron town on Busuanga Island, one of the less-visited beaches in the Calamian Group. Coron is primarily known for its Japanese WWII shipwrecks — among the best wreck diving in the world — but the surrounding islands also have fine snorkelling beaches. Talaudyong is accessible by bangka from Coron pier in about 30 minutes and offers white sand, calm water, and proximity to the Coron coral reef areas. The wrecks offshore are too deep for snorkelling but the reefs between the islands are accessible and healthy.

## 5. Snake Island, El Nido, Bacuit Archipelago

Snake Island is a famous sandbar near El Nido that appears at low tide as an S-shaped white sand strip connecting two small islands — the snake-like shape visible from the viewpoint above. The sandbar is a highlight of El Nido's Tour B island-hopping itinerary, and the view from the hillside above the sandbar, looking down on the S-curve of white sand surrounded by turquoise water and karst towers, is one of the most iconic images in Philippine tourism. The sandbar disappears at high tide, so timing a visit around the tide schedule is essential. The surrounding Bacuit Bay has extraordinary limestone cliff formations on every horizon.

## 6. Las Cabanas Beach, El Nido

Las Cabanas Beach is on the mainland just outside El Nido town, connected to the sea by a short walk past a beach resort development. Unlike the island-hopping beaches that require boat access, Las Cabanas is walkable from town and provides a consistent daily swimming option. A zipline crosses from the mainland bluff to a small island offshore. The beach is sandy and calm in the dry season, backed by palm trees and facing west, making it one of the finest sunset beaches in El Nido. It fills up in the late afternoon with visitors converging for the sunset hour.

## 7. Port Barton Beach, Southern Palawan

Port Barton is a beach village on the western coast of Palawan midway between Puerto Princesa and El Nido, accessible by van from either point in about three hours on an improving road. The village beach fronts a sheltered bay with several small island day-trip destinations visible offshore. Port Barton was a quiet backpacker destination in the 2000s and has grown modestly since — it retains a relaxed, affordable character that El Nido has lost. The San Isidro reef to the west is accessible by bangka for snorkelling. Firefly watching on the mangrove river at night is a local evening activity.

## 8. Balinsasayaw Beach, El Nido Mainland

Balinsasayaw Beach is a small cove beach on the El Nido mainland peninsula accessible by motorbike or tricycle from El Nido town. The beach is rarely crowded because the island-hopping circuit draws most visitors offshore. The cove is backed by low limestone formation and faces north into the Bacuit Bay. It is suitable for a quiet morning swim before the island-hopping boats depart, or for an afternoon when the island crowds seem overwhelming. The water is clear, the sand coarse-white, and the atmosphere is distinctly local.

## 9. Bulalacao Beach, South Palawan

Bulalacao is a small municipality at the southern end of Palawan's main island, overlooking the Sulu Archipelago. The beach here faces east and is sheltered by small offshore islands from the open sea. This is off-the-beaten-path Palawan — most travellers turn around at El Nido or Coron — and the reward is a beach culture that has changed little. Bangka fishing is the economic base; the catch includes grouper, tuna, and flying fish. The sea around Bulalacao is part of the Cagayancillo Marine Protected Area network and has good coral cover within the protected zones.

## 10. Sibaltan Beach, El Nido Municipality

Sibaltan is a barangay in the El Nido municipality at the far north-eastern corner of northern Palawan, facing the passage to Linapacan Island and the Calamian Group. The beach fronts a long bay of calm, clear water and has become a starting point for multi-day island-hopping expeditions to the Linapacan group — a less visited but superbly clear-water area between El Nido and Coron. Sibaltan has several small guesthouses and a growing infrastructure for independent travellers willing to make the 2.5-hour road journey from El Nido town.
