Swimming in the ocean is categorically different from swimming in a pool, and even excellent pool swimmers who have never trained in open water regularly discover this when they attempt their first ocean event. The differences are not primarily about fitness — a swimmer capable of 1,500 metres in a pool is physically capable of 1,500 metres in the ocean — but about a set of skills that pools do not develop: navigating without lane ropes, managing the disorientation of swimming without a visible bottom or walls, timing a surf entry and exit, reading currents, and staying calm when physical conditions change without warning.

## The Fundamental Difference: Sighting

In a pool, swimmers navigate by the black line on the bottom and the lane rope walls. In open water, neither is available. A swimmer who does not sight — briefly lifting the head to look ahead toward the destination — will drift significantly off course even in calm water. The natural biomechanics of the human stroke have a slight asymmetry that causes most swimmers to arc left or right, and within a few hundred metres, a non-sighting swimmer can be dramatically off course.

Sighting technique involves a brief, forward-looking lift of the head (eyes at water level rather than fully lifting), coordinated with the breathing stroke so that it does not interrupt the stroke rhythm. Effective sighting happens every 6 to 10 strokes, using a fixed target — a buoy, a headland, a building — to confirm heading. The technique is trainable and most swimmers achieve good sighting within a few sessions of focused practice.

Race sighting in large events requires handling a second challenge: other swimmers. Beach start open water events pack hundreds of swimmers into a small area at the starting gun, and the turbulence and contact of mass starts is genuinely disorienting for swimmers who have never experienced it. Training in groups, and deliberately starting in the middle or outside of training groups to experience some contact, is necessary preparation.

## Surf Entry and Exit

The surf zone — where waves break on the shoreline — is the first and last obstacle in most beach-start ocean swims. Entering through surf requires timing: a swimmer must enter the water cleanly, dive through or under the breaking waves, and achieve clear ocean before being pushed back by the next set. Dolphin diving — diving forward and down through the face of a breaking wave to avoid its turbulence — is the primary technique used in surf entries up to about chest height. At larger surf heights, timing the dive to go deep under the breaking foam zone is essential.

Exit through surf is harder than entry for most swimmers because the body is fatigued and the timing is determined by the wave set rather than the swimmer's choice. Body surfing skills — using wave energy to assist the final approach to shore — are a significant advantage and are worth practicing separately. The standard technique is to take a stroke to match speed with the wave as it catches you, extend the arms forward, and let the wave carry you toward the shore.

## Current Management

Ocean currents affect open water swimming in two ways. Tidal currents — driven by the rise and fall of the tide — can run parallel to shore or toward and away from the coast depending on the coastal topography. A swimmer who does not account for a 0.5-knot longshore current will find their sighting increasingly divergent from their intended course. The correction is not to swim harder against the current but to angle slightly into it, allowing the combination of swimming direction and current direction to produce a straight line toward the target.

Understanding the current conditions before entering the water is crucial. Local lifeguards, local surf knowledge, or observing floating debris or foam lines in the water before swimming can indicate current direction. In race conditions, experienced swimmers check official current briefings and plan race line angles accordingly.

Rip currents in the beach access zone require the same management as for casual swimmers — float and signal if caught, swim parallel to exit a narrow neck. Experienced ocean swimmers recognise rip channels by their visual signature and use the seaward flow deliberately as an efficient route through the surf zone, paddling through the neck rather than fighting the broken water.

## Training Structure for Open Water

Building open water swim fitness differs from pool periodisation in emphasis. Open water swimmers need both sustained aerobic base — the capacity to swim continuously at moderate intensity for 45 to 90 minutes — and the ability to produce surge efforts during mass starts and course turns without going into oxygen debt. The combination of base and surges maps to a training structure that mixes longer, steady ocean sessions with shorter, higher-intensity pool sets.

A practical 12-week open water preparation for a beginner might run: weeks 1 through 4 emphasising pool base work (3 to 4 sessions weekly, progressively extending continuous swim blocks from 400 metres to 1,500 metres); weeks 5 through 8 introducing ocean sessions twice weekly, focusing on sighting and course navigation; weeks 9 through 12 introducing race simulations — beach entries, mass start practice, timed course swims at target pace.

## Jellyfish and Open Water Hazards

Jellyfish concentrations in coastal waters are seasonal and variable. Some ocean swimming venues have regular jellyfish seasons — Bondi Beach in Sydney, for example, sees Bluebottle Portuguese man-o'-war (technically a siphonophore rather than a true jellyfish) wash in with northeast winds during summer, producing event cancellations and swimmer stings. The standard management for jellyfish-dense water is full body cover — long wetsuit arms and legs, swim cap and goggles protecting face — and avoiding slow or breast-stroke swimming that brings more of the body in contact with trailing tentacles.

Sea lice — actually the microscopic larval stage of certain jellyfish and thimble jellyfish — cause a prickling rash on skin covered by a swimsuit or wetsuit, particularly in warm tropical water. Exposure is managed by rinsing suits before putting them on (seawater, not fresh water, which can activate nematocysts remaining on the fabric) and showering immediately after swimming.
