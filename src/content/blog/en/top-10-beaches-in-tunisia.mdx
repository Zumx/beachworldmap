Tunisia's coastline runs along the western Mediterranean and the Gulf of Gabès, from the Gulf of Tunis in the north to the Libyan border at the south. The country has 1,300 kilometres of coastline — more than any other country in North Africa. The beach culture concentrates in the Gulf of Hammamet zone — a wide, sandy, warm Mediterranean bay that contains the country's highest density of resort hotels — and on the island of Djerba in the south. The north coast around the Cap Bon peninsula and the Saharan south add significant variety to a beach geography that is largely overlooked by international visitors compared to Turkey, Greece, or Spain.

Tunisia's relative obscurity as a beach destination has the practical benefit of very competitive pricing by Mediterranean standards. The country's medieval medinas, Roman ruins (Dougga, El Jem, and Carthage), Berber villages, and Saharan oases are all accessible as day or overnight trips from the coastal resorts — which makes Tunisia the most culturally rich beach destination in the Mediterranean relative to its tourism profile.

The Mediterranean season runs from May through October; July and August are peak with very hot and dry conditions, average temperatures above 35 degrees Celsius inland, and the sea reliably warm enough for comfortable swimming from June through October. The shoulder months of May and September deliver good beach conditions with lower visitor volumes and better prices. The north coast's winter storms make beach visits impractical from November through March.

## 1. Djerba, Médenine Governorate

Djerba is the largest island in North Africa, connected to the mainland by a Roman causeway and accessible by bridge and ferry. The north and east coasts have the island's best beaches: long, flat, sandy, and fronting shallow, warm Mediterranean water. The tourist zone around Sidi Mahres and Aghir has developed resort infrastructure since the 1970s. The combination of the beach quality, the island's medina, Jewish heritage quarter (the El Ghriba synagogue is one of Africa's oldest), and the distinctive Djerban architecture (white domed houses, underground troglodyte dwellings nearby) makes Djerba Tunisia's most complete beach destination.

## 2. Hammamet Beach, Nabeul Governorate

Hammamet is Tunisia's most internationally known resort town, 60 kilometres south of Tunis on the Gulf of Hammamet. The beach is long and sandy with calm, warm water — the Gulf of Hammamet is sheltered and shallow. The old medina and the Kasbah of Hammamet above the beach give the resort character beyond the hotel strip. The beach continues north and south from the town in a largely unbroken sandy arc.

## 3. Sousse, Sousse Governorate

Sousse's beach is the longest continuous sandy beach in Tunisia, extending north from the old city (a UNESCO World Heritage Site medina) to the tourist zone at Port El Kantaoui. The old city walls reach to the beach. The combination of historic architecture directly accessible from the beach and good swimming conditions makes Sousse Tunisia's most architecturally rich beach destination.

## 4. Port El Kantaoui, Sousse Governorate

Port El Kantaoui is a purpose-built marina resort north of Sousse, constructed in the 1970s as a model of Moroccan-influenced architecture. The beach fronting the marina zone is well-maintained and has consistent tourism infrastructure. The marina village character is pleasant for evening walks.

## 5. Mahdia Beach, Mahdia Governorate

Mahdia is a small city on a headland peninsula, the oldest Fatimid capital in North Africa. The beach runs south from the peninsula on the Gulf of Hammamet and is quieter than Sousse or Hammamet while offering similar sand and water quality. The historic fatimid ramparts and the old town character make Mahdia less resort-dominated than its neighbours.

## 6. Tabarka, Jendouba Governorate

Tabarka on the northern coast is a coral and dive destination, with a coral reef offshore and clear northern Mediterranean water that is cooler than the southern beaches but better for snorkelling. The beach is backed by the Genoese fort on a headland. Tabarka's forest and cork oak character behind the coast — typical of the Kroumirie mountains — is unlike anywhere else in Tunisia.

## 7. Sidi Bou Said Coast, Tunis Governorate

Sidi Bou Said is a blue-and-white village above the sea north of Tunis, accessible by commuter train from the capital. The beach below the village is a small sandy cove used by local Tunis residents. The village's architecture — cobalt blue doors and window grilles, white walls, terracotta tiles — is among the most distinctive in North Africa.

## 8. Zarzis, Médenine Governorate

Zarzis south of Djerba is a quieter resort area with long sandy beaches and resort hotels that are less crowded than the Djerba main beach zone. The olive groves behind the coast and the Ghozala salt lake add to the landscape variety.

## 9. Cap Angela (Cap Blanc)

Cap Blanc — the northernmost point of Africa — has a rocky headland with a small beach. The significance is geographic rather than scenic. The point where the Mediterranean meets the headland produces interesting wave patterns and the location has a curiosity value that draws visitors on the north coast road.

## 10. Nabeul Beach, Nabeul Governorate

Nabeul, 12 kilometres north of Hammamet, is the pottery capital of Tunisia with beach access on the Gulf of Hammamet. The beach town character is more local than resort-dominated; the combination of the beach and the ceramics souks (markets) is specific to Nabeul.

## Planning a Tunisian beach trip

The most practical Tunisia beach itinerary for international visitors combines a Gulf of Hammamet beach base with day trips to the country's cultural sites. Hammamet or Sousse as a base gives easy access to the Medina of Sousse (UNESCO, 15 minutes), the El Jem Roman amphitheatre (one of the world's best preserved, 2 hours south), the ruins of Carthage and the Bardo Museum in Tunis (1.5 hours north), and the Berber village circuit in the mountains behind Hammamet.

Djerba requires either an internal flight (40 minutes from Tunis-Carthage) or a 7-hour drive including the mainland causeway. It is worth treating as a separate trip base rather than a day trip. A 4 to 5 day stay on Djerba comfortably covers the beaches, the medina and Jewish quarter, a visit to the Saharan zone nearby (the Ksar villages and cave dwellings of Matmata are 2 to 3 hours from Djerba), and the evening seafood restaurants that are the island's best dining experience.

Prices on the Tunisian coast are significantly lower than equivalent Mediterranean quality elsewhere. A beachfront hotel room costs a fraction of comparable quality in Turkey, Greece, or Spain. The food — fresh seafood, couscous, brik pastry, harissa — is excellent and inexpensive. The safety situation has improved substantially since the security incidents of 2015 that affected tourism numbers dramatically; the country has invested heavily in security infrastructure at tourist sites and resort areas.

The official language is Arabic (Tunisian dialect) and French is widely spoken in tourism areas. Tourist infrastructure in the main resort zones has English-speaking staff; outside those areas, French is the practical communication language for visitors who have it.
