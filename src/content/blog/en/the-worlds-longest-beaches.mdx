The world's longest beaches share a geomorphological logic: they form on coasts where rivers supply large volumes of sediment, prevailing longshore currents distribute that sediment along the shore rather than offshore, and the coastal topography is low and flat enough that the resulting barrier beaches can extend for extraordinary distances. They are concentrated in specific regions — the Bay of Bengal, the Gulf of Mexico, the Atlantic coast of North and South America — and they have in common a wildness and scale that make them different in kind from the cove beaches that attract most international tourism.

## Cox's Bazar, Bangladesh — The Longest in the World

Cox's Bazar has been widely described as the world's longest natural sea beach, extending approximately 120 kilometres along the Bay of Bengal coast in southeastern Bangladesh. The beach is part of a barrier system backed by the Chittagong Hills and fronted by the relatively shallow bay, and it runs continuously (with minimal interruption by rocky outcrops or estuaries) from Cox's Bazar town south to Teknaf at the Myanmar border. The beach is wide, gently sloping, and has been the primary domestic beach destination for Bangladeshis for generations.

The scale of domestic tourism at Cox's Bazar is difficult to convey to visitors expecting a quiet beach — on peak weekends during the November-March season, visitor numbers in the millions have been reported across the beach's length. The northern sections near the town of Cox's Bazar are heavily commercialised; the southern sections, particularly toward Himchari and Inani, retain more natural character and are easily reached by three-wheeled motorised vehicles that serve as local transport along the beach road.

## Padre Island National Seashore, Texas, United States

At roughly 113 kilometres of protected undeveloped shoreline, Padre Island National Seashore along the Gulf of Mexico coast of Texas is the longest undeveloped barrier island in the world still in anything like a natural state. The full Padre Island complex, including developed South Padre Island, extends considerably further. The National Seashore designation, established in 1962, protects the central section from the Mansfield Channel south to the Malaquite Visitor Center.

The beach here is a world apart from the resort coastlines of Florida or California. The four-wheel-drive beaches south of the Visitor Center are accessible only by vehicles with low-tyre-pressure driving capability, and camping on the beach is common for visitors prepared for extreme wind, occasional storms, and an almost total absence of facilities. Kemp's Ridley sea turtles nest here, and the national seashore runs the largest sea turtle head-start program in the United States.

## Praia do Cassino, Brazil

Praia do Cassino in Rio Grande do Sul state in southern Brazil claims to be the longest beach in the world at various times, depending on whether you measure to different endpoints along the barrier system it belongs to. The beach runs from the city of Rio Grande north to the Uruguayan border and potentially further. Estimates of its full length range from 200 to over 250 kilometres, which would make it the longest if the full continuous sand strip is measured rather than just the named section.

The beach is backed by a lagoon system — Lagoa dos Patos — that is itself the largest coastal lagoon in the world by area. The beach is highly exposed to South Atlantic swells and wind, which makes it popular with wind and kite surfers, and it receives relatively few foreign visitors despite its extraordinary scale.

## Fraser Island (K'gari), Australia

Fraser Island off the Queensland coast of Australia is the world's largest sand island at roughly 122 kilometres long and up to 22 kilometres wide. The eastern beach, which runs the full length of the island, is both a highway (the beach is used as a driving surface at low tide) and one of the most remarkable coastlines in Australia. The beach is backed by enormous parabolic dunes, some over 200 metres high, and behind them are freshwater perched lakes — Lake McKenzie and Lake Wabby among them — that form where pure sand dunes cup rainfall above the water table. The island is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## The Cape Hatteras National Seashore, North Carolina

Cape Hatteras National Seashore protects roughly 130 kilometres of the Outer Banks barrier island chain along the North Carolina coast, one of the most dynamic coastlines in North America. The Outer Banks are a geologically young barrier system, constantly shifting in response to storms, and the lighthouse at Cape Hatteras — the tallest brick lighthouse in North America at 61 metres — had to be moved 870 metres inland in 1999 as erosion brought the sea within metres of its foundations.

The beach here is exposed to Atlantic swells and produces some of the best surf on the East Coast. The area around Cape Hatteras village and Rodanthe is a major kiteboarding destination, with the shallow Pamlico Sound on the landward side providing flat-water riding conditions.

## Ninety Mile Beach, Australia and New Zealand

Both Australia and New Zealand have beaches called Ninety Mile Beach, and both are significantly shorter than 90 miles — a historical naming convention more optimistic than accurate. Australia's version, on the Gippsland coast of Victoria, runs roughly 151 kilometres along the Gippsland Lakes barrier system. New Zealand's version, on the Northland Peninsula of the North Island, runs about 88 kilometres along a remote stretch of the west coast. Both beaches are driveable at low tide and both have an emptiness that their names promise.

## The Beaches of the Guiana Coast

The coasts of Suriname and French Guiana have some of the most unusual long beaches in the world — massive, flat mud-sand beaches deposited by the Amazon and Orinoco Rivers, whose sediment plumes travel thousands of kilometres northwest along the coast. These are not white sand beaches but broad expanses of fine brown sediment that shift continuously. They are some of the most important sea turtle nesting grounds in the Western Hemisphere, precisely because their remote character has prevented development.
