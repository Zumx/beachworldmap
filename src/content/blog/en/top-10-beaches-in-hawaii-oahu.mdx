Oahu is the most visited of the Hawaiian Islands — roughly 6 million arrivals annually pre-pandemic — and its beaches are correspondingly varied: from the most internationally famous stretch of sand in the Pacific at Waikiki to the wild turtle-nesting shores of Ka'ena Point and the legendary big-wave breaks of the North Shore. The island is 56 by 44 kilometres and its beaches change character with direction. The south shore from Makapu'u to Diamond Head faces the open Pacific with moderate year-round surf and consistent warm water (25–28°C). The North Shore, facing the North Pacific, receives the swell energy generated by winter storms thousands of kilometres away in the Aleutian chain, delivering the largest surfable waves reliably produced anywhere on earth from November through February. The Windward (east) coast is calmer and greener, backed by the Ko'olau mountains and characterised by turquoise lagoons and flat-water beaches. Honolulu provides the urban and logistical base; the North Shore, 60 kilometres away, is a world unto itself.

## 1. Waikiki Beach, Honolulu

Waikiki Beach is one of the most recognizable places on earth — the 2.5-kilometre crescent of sand fronting Honolulu's resort towers, backed by the Diamond Head volcanic crater and divided by the Ala Wai Canal. It was a favoured playground of Hawaiian royalty and is credited with introducing surfing to the international public in the early 20th century, when Duke Kahanamoku performed demonstrations here for visiting American and Australian visitors. The beach is calm, south-facing, and warm — the swell is filtered by offshore reefs. Outrigger canoe rides, stand-up paddleboard lessons, and surfing on long boards in gentle waves continue year-round. Sunrise behind Diamond Head is among the most photographed moments in the Pacific.

## 2. Hanauma Bay, Honolulu

Hanauma Bay is a flooded volcanic crater whose horseshoe rim creates an almost entirely enclosed shallow lagoon — ideal for snorkelling and snorkel tours operating from the wide sandy beach inside. The bay is a marine life conservation district and has been since 1967. Daily visitor numbers are capped and advance booking is mandatory; the state park authority requires new visitors to watch an orientation video about reef care before entering the water. Despite the administrative formalities, the experience of snorkelling in 3–8 metres of clear water among parrotfish, butterflyfish, and sea turtles within sight of Oahu's south coast cliffs is exceptional. The sea turtle in particular are habituated to snorkellers and frequently approach within arm's length.

## 3. Sandy Beach, Honolulu

Sandy Beach on Oahu's south-eastern coast near Makapu'u is a wide, flat, steeply-shelved beach subject to a fast, powerful shore break — waves that dump abruptly in very shallow water with considerable force. This shore break has produced a tradition of bodyboarding and bodysurfing specific to Sandy Beach, practised by locals with precise technique to avoid the neck and spinal injuries that the beach's break profile can cause in the uninformed. The beach is not suitable for casual swimming; the lifeguard service issues daily water hazard ratings and closes certain zones frequently. It is, however, a spectacular beach to watch: the break is fast, the launches aerial, and the setting on a dramatic volcanic headland coast is outstanding.

## 4. Makapu'u Beach, Honolulu

Makapu'u Beach sits at the north-eastern corner of Oahu's south coast, below the Makapu'u Lighthouse, and offers the most reliably surfable body-boarding conditions on the island's main circuit. The beach is wide and backed by steep black basalt cliffs. The adjacent Makapu'u Tidepools — accessible via a short trail from the beach — are among the best-developed tidal pool communities on Oahu, with wana (sea urchins), honu (sea turtles), and monk seals occasionally hauling out on the rocks. The offshore Manana (Rabbit) Island and Kaohikaipu Island seabird sanctuaries are visible from the beach. Spinner dolphins frequent the bay and are occasionally seen from shore.

## 5. Lanikai Beach, Kailua, Windward Coast

Lanikai Beach is a kilometre of cream-white sand on the Windward Coast below the residential Lanikai neighbourhood, ranked repeatedly among the most beautiful beaches in the world. Two islets — the Mokulua twins — sit about a kilometre offshore, and paddleboard and kayak routes to their shore are among the most popular on the island. The water is turquoise, calm, and warm; the beach faces east-north-east and catches morning light spectacularly. Access is through residential streets and parking is deliberately limited; the neighbourhood's quiet is fiercely protected by residents. Best visited very early morning before the day-trippers arrive.

## 6. Kailua Beach, Kailua, Windward Coast

Kailua Beach is the main public beach of the Kailua town, running three kilometres in a wide curve backed by ironwood trees and a public park with picnic facilities. The trade winds arrive reliably from the north-east, making Kailua one of the best windsurfing and kiteboarding locations in Hawaii. The water is calm and shallow in the inner bay, excellent for families; the outer bay receives larger waves that the kiteboard community exploit. Former US President Obama regularly vacationed at Kailua, bringing the beach periodic Secret Service presence and media attention. Marine turtles rest on the beach, particularly in the early morning.

## 7. Sunset Beach, North Shore

Sunset Beach is the North Shore's longest and most versatile beach — a two-kilometre stretch of white sand that is a placid, family-friendly swimming spot in summer (May through September) when the North Pacific swell has subsided, and a world-class big-wave venue in winter when it transforms into one of the most photographed surf breaks in surfing. The Vans Triple Crown of Surfing event, held annually in November and December, uses Sunset Beach as the final and most prestigious event. Waves regularly exceed five metres in the surf forecast, and the beach draws international surf professionals for months at a time each winter.

## 8. Waimea Bay, North Shore

Waimea Bay is the most famous big-wave arena in the world — the place where Hawaiian surfers in the 1950s, led by Pat Curren and Greg Noll, first demonstrated that waves of extreme size (six metres or more) could be ridden, establishing the modern big-wave tradition. The Eddie Aikau Invitational event, held at Waimea Bay only when waves exceed six metres at Waimea, is one of the most mythologised events in surfing; it was last held in 2023. In summer the bay is completely calm and filled with jumping teenagers and tourists who leap from the large rock at the north end of the beach into water of extraordinary clarity.

## 9. Ehukai Beach, North Shore

Ehukai Beach is the formal name for the stretch of sand in front of the Banzai Pipeline — the most famous and most photographed surf break on earth, a hollow left-hand reef break that produces a circular barrel over a shallow coral reef in water of brilliant clarity. Pipeline waves, when they fire in November through February, offer rides of 10–15 seconds in a cylindrical tunnel before detonating on the dry reef. Watching from the beach, with the palm grove of Sunset Beach Drive behind and the Ka'ena Point mountains rising in the distance, is one of the great spectator experiences in sport. The beach itself is narrow and not suitable for swimming in winter.

## 10. Makaha Beach, Leeward Coast

Makaha Beach is on Oahu's Leeward Coast — the western, drier side of the island — historically the site of the first major international surfing competition in 1954 and still a significant wave venue in winter. The beach faces west and receives south-west swells in summer that make it one of the few Oahu beaches with good waves in the off-season for the North Shore. The Leeward Coast is predominantly residential and less tourist-heavy, giving Makaha a local character distinct from the North Shore's international surf-camp culture. The community here is strongly Hawaiian in heritage and the beach has historical significance as a traditional surfing site documented before Western contact.
