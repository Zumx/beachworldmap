New Zealand has over 15,000 kilometres of coastline across two main islands and numerous offshore islands. The beach geography is as diverse as the landscape: the white sand beaches of the Northland and Coromandel Peninsula, the black volcanic sand beaches of the West Coast, the sheltered harbours of Auckland and Wellington, the wild Southern Ocean coast of the South Island, and the remote Marlborough Sounds.

The beach season is December through March. Summer in New Zealand is warm rather than hot — 20 to 26 degrees Celsius on the coast — and the westerly wind is persistent on exposed shores.

## 1. Cathedral Cove, Coromandel Peninsula

Cathedral Cove on the Coromandel Peninsula is the most photographed beach in New Zealand. The beach is in two sections connected by a natural arch — a rock tunnel carved through the cliff between the two bays. The water is clear, the sand white, and the surrounding pohutukawa trees (which flower vivid red in December and January) add to the colour. Access is by a 35-minute walk from the carpark at Hahei, or by water taxi from Hahei in summer. Cathedral Cove is in the database as Mautohe Cathedral Cove.

## 2. Karekare Beach, Auckland

Karekare is a black sand beach on Auckland's Waitakere coast, reached by a narrow road through the Waitakere Ranges. The beach appeared in the opening sequence of Jane Campion's film The Piano (1993). The black sand is basalt from the Waitakere Ranges, the surf is powerful, and the surrounding bush-clad cliffs create a contained and dramatic landscape. Swimming is dangerous except in very calm conditions; surf lifesaving patrol operates on summer weekends. Karekare is in the database.

## 3. Hot Water Beach, Coromandel Peninsula

Hot Water Beach is unusual: geothermal water heats the beach sand directly, and at low tide visitors dig pools in the sand in which the water temperature rises to 64 degrees Celsius — so hot that most people's pools are too hot to sit in without mixing with cold sea water. The pools are hand-dug and collapse with the returning tide; the experience lasts 2 hours either side of low tide. Spades are hired at the surf shop. Te Puia / Hot Water Beach is in the database.

## 4. Ninety Mile Beach, Northland

Ninety Mile Beach on Northland's west coast is actually 88 kilometres long — the discrepancy is historic. The beach is a largely unbroken stretch of black-tinged sand dunes and ocean surf, legal as a public highway under New Zealand road law. Conventional vehicles have gotten stuck and been lost to tide; four-wheel drives with knowledgeable drivers are the recommended access method. The beach is wild, remote, and one of the longest continuous beach stretches accessible by road anywhere. Ninety Mile Beach is in the database.

## 5. Wharariki Beach, Golden Bay

Wharariki at the top of the South Island, on the coast above Cape Farewell, is a wild beach of arches, sea stacks, and shifting dunes accessible by a 20-minute walk across farmland. Seals rest on the rocks. The Archway Islands offshore are sea-level arches. The beach faces the Tasman Sea with unrestricted western fetch. This is the most raw and dramatic beach in the northern South Island.

## 6. Abel Tasman Coast Track Beaches

The Abel Tasman National Park coastal track connects a series of golden-sand beaches — Totaranui, Awaroa, Bark Bay, Onetahuti — reachable only on foot or by water taxi. The Abel Tasman is the easiest Great Walk in New Zealand and the beaches on it are consistently excellent. Totaranui has camping and facilities; Awaroa requires wading a tidal inlet. The park is best done over 3 to 5 days rather than day trips.

## 7. Piha Beach, Auckland

Piha on the Auckland West Coast is the most popular black sand surf beach accessible from the city — 40 kilometres from central Auckland. The beach is backed by bush-covered hills; the Lion Rock sea stack divides the main beach from a smaller section to the north. Strong rip currents and powerful surf are managed by Piha Surf Life Saving Club. The Iron Māori triathlon starts from Piha.

## 8. Anawhata, Auckland

Anawhata, north of Karekare on the Waitakere coast, is a beach accessible by a 20-minute walk from a small carpark. The beach is enclosed by bush-covered cliffs, black-sanded, and rarely visited compared to Piha. The walk delivers a genuine sense of arrival. Swimming is variable; the beach is primarily for the landscape.

## 9. Pakiri Beach, Northland

Pakiri Beach, an hour north of Auckland, is one of the few white sand beaches on the east coast near Auckland. The beach is long, the sand fine, and horses are available to ride along the waterline from the Pakiri Beach Horse Rides operation. The combination of white sand and Pacific views distinguishes it from the black sand west coast beaches.

## 10. Koekohe Beach, Otago (Moeraki Boulders)

Koekohe Beach near Moeraki on the Otago coast is where the Moeraki Boulders are — large spherical concretions of mudstone that weather out of the cliff face and accumulate on the beach. The boulders are up to 1.3 metres in diameter and their spherical perfection appears artificial. The beach itself is unremarkable; the boulders are the reason to visit.

## Planning New Zealand's beaches

New Zealand beach geography divides neatly into character zones that reward different visitors. For the iconic New Zealand beach experience — pohutukawa-fringed white sand coves with clear Pacific water — the Coromandel Peninsula and the Bay of Islands in Northland are the reference destinations. Both are accessible by car from Auckland; the Coromandel is 2 to 3 hours east, the Bay of Islands 3.5 hours north. Cathedral Cove and Hot Water Beach on the Coromandel can be combined in a single day from Auckland, though the walk access to Cathedral Cove rewards a slower approach.

For dramatic landscape beaches with black volcanic sand and surf culture, the West Auckland coast (Piha, Karekare, Bethells/Te Henga) delivers an experience unlike any beach in the southern hemisphere. The black sand, the bush-covered hills, the surf — this is distinctly New Zealand. These beaches are within 40 kilometres of central Auckland and are day-trip accessible, but they have a genuinely wild character that beach destinations with more development infrastructure cannot replicate.

For the South Island, the Abel Tasman National Park is the only accessible multi-beach walking destination in New Zealand. The park's coastal track connects golden-sand beaches with campsites and water taxi access — this is where New Zealand's most beautiful beach walking is concentrated. Golden Bay, at the top of the South Island, is reached by crossing the Takaka Hill (a narrow winding road) and rewards the effort with a completely different character from the Marlborough or Kaikoura coasts.

The New Zealand beach season is short by Australian standards. December through February are the reliable months; March cools quickly. The summer coincides with New Zealand's school holiday period (approximately December 20 through February 1), when the most popular beaches — Coromandel, Bay of Islands — are crowded. Visiting in late November or late February gives better crowd conditions with only slightly cooler water.

The rip current risk at New Zealand beaches, particularly on the West Coast black sand beaches, is significant. These beaches have no barrier reefs and receive unrestricted swell from the Tasman Sea. The New Zealand surf lifesaving organisation patrols approximately 90 beaches in summer; the red-and-yellow flag system is the same as Australia's. Swimming between the flags is non-negotiable at any patrolled beach with active surf conditions.
