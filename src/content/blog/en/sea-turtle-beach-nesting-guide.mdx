Seven species of sea turtle survive in the world's oceans, and all of them return to land for one purpose: to lay eggs on sand beaches. The nesting behaviour of sea turtles is one of the most remarkable in the animal kingdom. Females navigate across ocean basins using magnetic field orientation to return to the specific beach where they were born, sometimes after decades at sea. A female loggerhead may have hatched on a Florida beach in the 1990s, grown to adulthood in the Atlantic, and returned to the same 100-metre section of that Florida beach to nest for the first time as an adult. The fidelity is extraordinary and the vulnerability is obvious: the beaches these animals require must be kept intact, undeveloped, and free of the artificial light that disorients both nesting females and hatchlings.

## The Seven Species and Their Range

The loggerhead turtle (Caretta caretta) nests primarily on Atlantic and Mediterranean beaches, with the largest nesting populations in Florida, Greece, Turkey, and Cyprus. It is the most widely distributed nesting sea turtle in the northern hemisphere.

The green turtle (Chelonia mydas) nests across the tropics — the largest nesting aggregations are in Costa Rica (Tortuguero), Ascension Island in the South Atlantic, the Great Barrier Reef, and the beaches of Oman. Green turtles are named for the colour of their fat, and their diet of seagrass and algae distinguishes them from the more carnivorous loggerheads.

The leatherback (Dermochelys coriacea) is the largest sea turtle and the only one lacking a hard shell — its back is covered by a leathery skin over a mosaic of bone fragments. It nests on tropical and subtropical beaches but forages as far north as Norway and Iceland following jellyfish. Major nesting beaches include Trinidad, French Guiana, Suriname, and the Playa Grande coast of Costa Rica.

The hawksbill (Eretmochelys imbricata) nests in smaller numbers across tropical and subtropical beaches. It is critically endangered, hunted historically for its shell for tortoiseshell manufacturing. Major nesting areas include the Caribbean (particularly Barbados and the ABC Islands) and the islands of the Indo-Pacific.

The Kemp's ridley (Lepidochelys kempii) nests almost exclusively on a single beach in Mexico: Rancho Nuevo in Tamaulipas State on the Gulf of Mexico coast. It is the smallest sea turtle and the rarest, having declined to fewer than 300 nesting females by the 1980s before conservation measures allowed partial recovery.

The olive ridley (Lepidochelys olivacea) undertakes mass synchronised nesting events called arribadas — arrivals — where thousands of females come ashore simultaneously over a period of days on specific beaches. The most famous arribada beaches are Ostional in Costa Rica and Gahirmatha in Odisha, India, where tens of thousands of turtles nest in a single event.

The flatback turtle (Natator depressus) nests exclusively in Australia, primarily on the beaches of the Gulf of Carpentaria and the northern Great Barrier Reef coast.

## Nesting Calendar by Region

Florida (United States): Loggerheads nest from May through August, with peak nesting in June and July. The Archie Carr National Wildlife Refuge near Melbourne Beach is the most important loggerhead nesting beach in the western hemisphere, and the turtle patrol program run by the Sea Turtle Conservancy offers guided night nesting watches from May through October. Leatherback nesting occurs in March through July on the same beaches. Brevard County and Volusia County together host the majority of Florida's nesting activity.

Caribbean and Central America: Tortuguero National Park on Costa Rica's Caribbean coast hosts the largest green turtle nesting aggregation in the western hemisphere, with nesting from July through October. Guided tours organised through the Caribbean Conservation Corporation and accredited local operators are the standard watching format. The beach is accessible only by boat or small plane, which limits visitor numbers and has helped preserve the site. Trinidad's Matura and Grand Rivière beaches host leatherback nesting from March through July — Grand Rivière in particular sees so many leatherbacks (sometimes hundreds per night at peak season) that the beach literally runs out of dry sand and females are laying eggs on top of previously laid nests.

Greece and the Mediterranean: Loggerheads nest on several major Mediterranean beaches from May through August. Laganas Bay on Zakynthos, Greece, is the site of the National Marine Park of Zakynthos, the largest loggerhead nesting site in the Mediterranean, though it has been subject to decades of conflict between tourism development and turtle conservation. Gerakas and Daphni beaches within the national park have managed access and guided watching. Turkey's Dalyan Beach and Belek Beach on the south coast are also significant loggerhead nesting sites.

Oman: Ras al Jinz on the eastern tip of the Arabian Peninsula hosts one of the largest green turtle nesting concentrations in the Indian Ocean, with several thousand females using the beach in the peak season from July through October. The Ras al Jinz Turtle Reserve has a visitor centre and operates guided night tours, carefully managing access with pre-booked slots and guides who enforce distance and no-light rules around nesting females.

Australia: The Great Barrier Reef and Coral Sea islands provide the primary nesting habitat for green and loggerhead turtles in the southern hemisphere. Mon Repos Beach near Bundaberg in Queensland is the most accessible large nesting beach, with a conservation park visitor centre and guided watching programs from November through March. Raine Island, accessible only by tour boat from Cairns, hosts the world's largest green turtle rookery with tens of thousands of nesting females, though it is difficult to visit.

India: The Odisha coast hosts the world's largest olive ridley aggregation, with Gahirmatha Marine Sanctuary and the beaches at Rushikulya seeing mass arribadas of hundreds of thousands of turtles from January through March. The scale of the Odisha arribadas — sometimes half a million turtles over a few days — is unlike anything else in the animal world, but viewing access is managed and regulated.

## Responsible Watching Principles

The primary rules for responsible sea turtle watching are consistent across all locations. No artificial light near nesting beaches at night — turtles use the brightest horizon (normally the sea under starlight) to orient themselves, and beach lighting confuses both nesting females and hatchlings. No flash photography. Maintain a minimum distance of at least a few metres from nesting females (specific distances vary by location). Never handle eggs or hatchlings unless directed by a qualified conservation worker. Do not block the turtle's path to or from the sea. Follow all instructions from guides and wardens without question.
