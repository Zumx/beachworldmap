Beach water quality monitoring is among the most practically useful forms of environmental regulation — it directly affects whether swimming is safe for tens of millions of people — yet the data it produces is often poorly understood by the public it is designed to inform. The bacteria measured at bathing beaches are not the bacteria that make people sick. They are indicator organisms whose presence in high concentrations signals a likelihood of contamination from sewage or animal waste, which in turn implies elevated probability that pathogenic organisms — viruses, protozoa, pathogenic bacteria — are present in numbers sufficient to cause illness.

Understanding the measurement system — what is tested, what the thresholds mean, and why a beach can fail in June but pass in August — allows beach visitors to make meaningfully better-informed decisions than those relying on a simple pass-fail summary.

## The Indicator Organism Approach

Testing bathing water directly for every possible pathogenic organism — norovirus, Cryptosporidium, Campylobacter, Hepatitis A virus, Salmonella — would be technically complex, slow, and expensive. Instead, regulatory monitoring uses indicator organisms: bacteria that live in the intestinal tracts of humans and warm-blooded animals and are excreted in large numbers in faeces. Their presence in water indicates faecal contamination, which implies probable co-presence of pathogens.

The two primary indicators used in most countries are Escherichia coli (E. coli) — specifically the faecal coliform group rather than all E. coli strains — and intestinal enterococci. E. coli is used because nearly all warm-blooded animals carry it in their guts and excrete it; its detection is a highly sensitive indicator of faecal input. Intestinal enterococci (bacteria of the genera Enterococcus and Streptococcus) are used because they survive longer in salt water than E. coli, making them a better indicator in marine environments where the signal might otherwise decay before sampling.

Neither of these organisms typically causes illness in healthy adults at the concentrations found in polluted bathing water. They are measuring devices, not the hazard itself.

## The EU Bathing Water Directive: Thresholds and Classifications

The European Union Bathing Water Directive (2006/7/EC) establishes four-year rolling quality classifications for monitored bathing sites based on the 95th percentile of intestinal enterococci concentrations and the 90th percentile of E. coli concentrations across the monitoring season. Four classifications apply: Excellent, Good, Sufficient, and Poor.

For marine (coastal) bathing waters, the Excellent threshold is set at 100 colony-forming units (CFU) per 100 millilitres for intestinal enterococci and 250 CFU/100 ml for E. coli. The Good threshold is 200 CFU/100 ml and 500 CFU/100 ml respectively. Sufficient (the minimum standard for continued operation) is 185 CFU/100 ml enterococci and 500 CFU/100 ml E. coli. Concentrations above these levels on the rolling four-year assessment indicate a Poor classification; beaches rated Poor must display a warning to bathers, and if the Poor rating persists for two consecutive years, bathing prohibition is recommended.

The percentile-based approach means that a few high readings do not automatically produce a Poor classification. A beach that normally meets Excellent standards but has occasional spikes from storm overflow events may still classify as Good if the spikes fall outside the 95th percentile threshold. This is a deliberate design decision — it reflects the reality that perfect consistency is not achievable — but it also means that a beach classified as Excellent may still have days when swimming carries elevated risk.

## Sewage Overflow Events and Real-Time Monitoring

The most common source of acute bathing water contamination in developed countries is combined sewer overflow (CSO). In cities and towns with combined stormwater and sewage drainage systems, heavy rain causes the system to exceed capacity; overflow valves release a diluted mix of stormwater and raw sewage directly into rivers, estuaries, and the sea to prevent sewage backing up into homes and streets. Many beaches in England, France, and the US experience contamination spikes within 24 to 48 hours of heavy rainfall for precisely this reason.

England's water quality notification system has been a point of significant public controversy from 2020 onward, as data from event duration monitoring on sewage outfalls revealed that combined sewer overflows were discharging far more frequently than the "exceptional" circumstances the regulations envisaged. Surfers Against Sewage's Safer Seas Service provides real-time pollution risk alerts based on rainfall data and discharge notifications, and similar services operate in Ireland (Swim Ireland), Wales (Natural Resources Wales), and France (Eau et Rivières de Bretagne).

The US EPA's Clean Water Act requires monitoring at public beaches, but the national framework is enforced at state level with considerable variation. California's Beach Environmental Assessment and Coastal Health (BEACH) Act, implemented through Heal the Bay's Beach Report Card, provides one of the most granular public-access water quality datasets in the world, grading hundreds of Los Angeles and Bay Area beaches weekly on an A to F scale.

## Sources of Contamination Beyond Sewage

Agricultural runoff is the primary source of bacterial contamination at beaches remote from urban infrastructure. Cattle, sheep, pigs, and poultry all contribute high concentrations of E. coli and enterococci to surface water that drains to coastal areas after rainfall. The timing is predictable — contamination peaks occur after significant rainfall events, particularly when slurry spreading has occurred in the preceding days. Beach monitoring data from rural coasts in Ireland, Wales, Cornwall, and the US Pacific Northwest consistently shows rainfall-correlated contamination spikes attributable to agricultural sources.

Urban dog faeces is a measurable contributor at beaches where dog access is permitted. Studies from beaches in northern Spain and southwest England found that dog fouling contributed detectably to enterococci counts on beaches where dogs were allowed year-round; this is one of the regulatory rationales for the seasonal dog exclusion rules common in European beach management.

Marine wildlife — particularly gulls, which carry high loads of both indicator organisms and campylobacter — contribute background contamination that makes achieving very low baseline counts difficult on beaches with active gull populations.

## Checking Water Quality Before You Swim

The practical process for checking a beach's water quality before visiting is straightforward but requires knowing which database to query.

For EU countries, the European Environment Agency's Bathing Water Quality portal (accessible at bathing-water-quality.eea.europa.eu) provides the four-year classification and current-season sample data for every registered bathing site in member states, searchable by country and beach name. The data is updated in near-real-time as monitoring results are submitted by member state authorities.

For the UK, the Environment Agency's Swimfo portal covers England; Natural Resources Wales and SEPA cover the devolved administrations. For Northern Ireland, the DAERA bathing water monitoring portal applies.

For the United States, the EPA Beach Advisory and Closing Online Notification (BEACON) portal aggregates state beach advisory data; California's Heal the Bay Beach Report Card remains the most user-friendly state-level tool.

Australia uses the Swim Site Atlas operated by national health authorities, with state-specific supplements in New South Wales and Victoria.
