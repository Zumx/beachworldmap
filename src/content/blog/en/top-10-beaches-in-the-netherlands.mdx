The Netherlands faces the North Sea across a coast shaped almost entirely by human engineering over five centuries. The dune ridge that protects the country's low interior from storm surges is the primary natural feature; the beaches lying seaward of this dune ridge are wide, flat, and driven by North Sea tides and westerly winds rather than by warm-water tourism. Water temperatures peak around 18–20°C in August — enough for swimming but requiring commitment — and the wind is almost always present, which makes the Dutch coast one of the premier windsurfing and kiteboarding destinations in Europe. The beach culture is also distinctly Dutch in character: beach clubs called strandtenten set up seasonal structures with heating, shelter, and food service, so a cold and windy day at Zandvoort or Scheveningen is less uncomfortable than a tropical beach picture might suggest. The coast also includes the Wadden Islands — Texel, Vlieland, Terschelling, Ameland, Schiermonnikoog — whose North Sea shores are among the finest and most spacious in Europe, and whose Wadden Sea tidal flats to the south constitute a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 1. Strand van Callantsoog, North Holland

Strand van Callantsoog is on the North Holland coast roughly 10 kilometres north of the dune city of Bergen aan Zee. The beach is wide, clean, and consistently ranked among the cleanest in the Netherlands by the KIWA water quality testing authority. The village of Callantsoog itself is small and quiet — a summer holiday community without the commercial development of Zandvoort or Scheveningen. The dune system behind the beach is one of the largest in North Holland and includes several kilometres of walking paths through the Zwanenwater nature reserve, which hosts the largest spoonbill nesting colony in the Netherlands. The beach faces west and catches full Atlantic afternoon light.

## 2. Strand Julianadorp, North Holland

Strand Julianadorp is a stretch of North Holland coast adjacent to Julianadorp aan Zee, a planned beach village developed in the late 20th century on reclaimed dune land. The beach is wide, flat, and backed by new dune-planted areas that are gradually maturing. It is less visited than Callantsoog to the north and benefits from the same clean water quality that characterises this section of the North Holland coast. Access is straightforward from the N9 main road through the Anna Paulowna polder. The beach is popular with families and walkers; wind and kite conditions are consistent.

## 3. Rhederlaag, Gelderland (Lake Beach)

Rhederlaag is a gravel-pit lake beach in the Rijnstangen nature area between Zevenaar and Arnhem in the Gelderland river district — a freshwater lake formed from flooded quarried gravel, now a nature reserve with a managed beach. This type of beach — the zandwinplas, or gravel-extraction lake beach — is a common feature of Dutch inland recreation. Rhederlaag in particular has developed an excellent beach facility with supervised swimming areas, a beach bar, and surrounding walking and cycling trails through the Rhine floodplain. Summer water temperature reaches 22–24°C, warm by Dutch standards, making it genuinely comfortable for swimming.

## 4. Nude Beach Schoorl aan Zee, North Holland

The textile-free beach at Schoorl aan Zee is one of the few formally designated nude beaches on the North Holland coast, located within the dune complex of Bergen-Schoorl. It operates with the same natural dune backdrop as the conventional beaches nearby, sheltered by the dune ridge from the worst of the westerly wind. The Schoorl dunes are among the tallest in the Netherlands, rising to nearly 55 metres in the parabolic dune formations behind the beach. The area is part of the North Holland Dunes Natural Monument, and walking trails through the dunes provide access to the beach from the Schoorl village side.

## 5. Eiland 5, Loosdrechtse Plassen, Utrecht

Eiland 5 is a small lake island with a sandy beach in the Loosdrechtse Plassen, a network of shallow lakes in the Utrecht–North Holland polder landscape formed by peat extraction in the 17th and 18th centuries. The island is accessible by small boat or pedalo from the Loosdrecht shore and functions as an informal summer picnic and bathing destination for lake users. The Loosdrechtse Plassen are a significant sailing and water sports area, with several marinas and a well-established regatta tradition. Water temperatures in the shallow peat lakes are among the warmest in the Netherlands, reaching 25°C or more in a warm July.

## 6. Naaktstrand Engelgaarde, Friesland

The Naaktstrand Engelgaarde is a nudist beach on the Frisian coast facing the Wadden Sea tidal flats. This section of the Netherlands — the Frisian coast between Harlingen and Holwerd — looks directly across the Wadden mudflats to the islands of Terschelling and Ameland. The beach is set in a landscape of salt marshes, dyke grassland, and tidal creek networks. Low tide exposes an enormous tidal flat across which guided Wadlopen (mud-walking) expeditions set out to reach the islands on foot — a uniquely Dutch experience requiring proper guide and tide knowledge. The beach itself is backed by the outer dyke and protected from North Sea exposure.

## 7. Naaktstrand Noordwijk, South Holland

The naturist section of Noordwijk beach is within the resort town of Noordwijk aan Zee, which has been a North Sea bathing resort since the 19th century. Noordwijk is the beach town closest to the main Dutch bulb fields of the Bollenstreek, making it possible to combine spring tulip viewing (March through May) with beach walking. The naturist beach occupies the northern section of the town strand, beyond the beach club zone. Noordwijk has one of the best beach pavilion cultures on the Dutch coast, with year-round strandtenten offering heated indoor-outdoor dining. The European Space Research and Technology Centre (ESTEC) is located in the town — beach and rocket science in combination.

## 8. Kuifeend, Rotterdam Metro Area

Kuifeend is a lake beach within a nature area in the Botlek industrial zone west of Rotterdam — a detail that sounds unappealing until you understand that Rotterdam's port has been systematically surrounded by nature compensation areas as part of port expansion agreements. The Kuifeend lake, named for the tufted duck (kuifeend) that nests here, has a small managed beach and is surrounded by wetland and scrub that provides excellent birdwatching. It represents the Dutch approach to nature integration in industrial landscapes, and the contrast of the beach with the distant Europoort cranes on the horizon is genuinely striking.

## 9. Zandvoort aan Zee, North Holland

Zandvoort aan Zee is the most visited beach in the Netherlands, a major resort town 25 kilometres west of Amsterdam and reachable by a direct train line from Amsterdam Centraal in under 30 minutes. The beach is wide, clean, and managed with a large number of beach clubs and facilities; the Circuit Zandvoort Formula One track sits immediately behind the dunes. The combination of rapid Amsterdam access, excellent beach infrastructure, and consistent North Sea conditions — strong sun when clear, reliable westerly breeze — makes Zandvoort the natural choice for Amsterdammers seeking a beach day. Crowds can be significant on hot summer weekends.

## 10. Scheveningen Beach, The Hague

Scheveningen Beach at The Hague is one of Europe's most historic seaside resorts, established in the early 19th century when Dutch royalty and aristocracy first popularised sea bathing. The Kurhaus hotel, built in 1885, still dominates the seafront and functions as the symbolic heart of the resort. The beach is wide and sandy, the pier extends 400 metres into the sea, and the beach boulevard is lined with restaurants and cultural venues. Scheveningen's fish market at the harbour sells herring, mussels, and eel in the traditional Dutch hawker style.
