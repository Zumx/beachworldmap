The economics of beach tourism involve some of the largest flows of money in the global travel industry, some of the most distorted property markets on earth, and a structural paradox that is well understood and rarely resolved: the same popularity that makes a coastal destination economically successful generates the congestion, erosion, and environmental degradation that undermine the natural asset driving that success. Understanding the beach economy means understanding not just the revenue flows, but the mechanisms by which those flows consume themselves.

## The Scale of Global Beach Tourism

Coastal tourism accounts for a disproportionate share of global travel expenditure relative to the fraction of the earth's surface that coastlines occupy. The UNWTO has estimated that beach and coastal destinations account for over 30 percent of all international tourism arrivals, despite representing a small fraction of total land area. The Mediterranean region alone — covering the southern European, North African, and Levantine coasts — receives over 300 million international tourist arrivals annually, the large majority of them coastal.

At the national level, the dependency on beach tourism ranges from dominant to minor. For small island developing states — the Maldives, Barbados, Fiji, the Seychelles, Palau — tourism revenue from beach and dive visitors can represent 30 to 70 percent of GDP and an even higher percentage of foreign exchange earnings. This concentration creates macroeconomic vulnerability that became painfully apparent during the COVID-19 pandemic, when Maldivian GDP contracted by 33 percent in 2020 as international arrivals effectively stopped for several months.

For large economies with significant coastal infrastructure — Spain, Italy, the United States, Australia — beach tourism represents a meaningful but smaller share of GDP, typically in the range of two to five percent at national level but much higher for specific coastal regions. The Canary Islands economy is almost entirely dependent on beach tourism; the gross value added per bed night at Canarian hotels represents a significant fraction of regional GDP.

## Property Premiums: How Far Beach Proximity Travels

The most direct financial measure of beach value is the property price premium paid for proximity to the shoreline. This premium has been measured in multiple studies across multiple countries and is consistently substantial. Research on US coastal property markets has found median price premiums of 20 to 100 percent for properties within 500 metres of the ocean compared to equivalent properties further inland, with the premium declining but remaining measurable at distances up to two kilometres.

In England, the Land Registry price data consistently shows coastal town property values at significant premiums over equivalent-sized towns of equivalent economic activity in inland locations. Salcombe in Devon commands some of the highest property prices per square metre outside London precisely because of its coastal access combined with accessibility from major cities. The same dynamic operates in Sydney's eastern suburbs (Bondi, Tamarama, Coogee), Melbourne's Mornington Peninsula, Cape Cod, and the Hamptons.

The premium encodes two distinct values: the consumption value (using the beach as a resident or frequent visitor) and the option value (the ability to use it at will). Both of these decline as the beach becomes less usable — through overcrowding, pollution, erosion, or restricted access. This is the financial mechanism by which coastal degradation translates directly into property value destruction.

## Employment and the Service Economy of Coastal Tourism

Beach tourism employment is characterised by its seasonal concentration, its preponderance of low-wage service roles, and its vulnerability to weather variation, exchange rate movements, and public health events. In Mediterranean European beach destinations — Playa de Palma, Rimini, the Greek island resorts — a large fraction of employment in hotels, restaurants, watersports, and retail is seasonal: active from April through October and dramatically reduced in winter. This seasonal structure limits the economic sustainability of beach tourism employment for permanent residents of resort towns.

The multiplier effect of tourist spending varies significantly depending on what fraction of expenditure is retained locally versus leaking out of the destination economy. An all-inclusive resort model, where visitors pay a flat rate to a large hotel chain and consume most of their food and activities on the premises, typically has a lower local multiplier than a guesthouse-and-local-restaurant model, because a larger share of total expenditure ends up with the international hotel company rather than local businesses. The economic development literature on Caribbean and Pacific island beach tourism is replete with examples of high visitor arrival numbers failing to translate into broad-based local income because of this leakage problem.

Maldivian guesthouses — permitted under a 2010 policy change that allowed tourism on inhabited islands for the first time — have produced a higher-multiplier economic model than the traditional resort island approach, because spending on guesthouse accommodation, local food, and island transport stays within the local economy rather than leaking to resort company headquarters.

## The Environmental Paradox of Beach Tourism Success

The mechanism by which beach tourism destroys itself is structural and has been documented in destinations across the world. The sequence typically runs: a beach of natural quality and relative isolation attracts early visitors; infrastructure investment follows; accessibility improves; visitor numbers grow; congestion and crowding reduce the experiential quality that attracted early visitors; water quality deteriorates as sewage infrastructure is overwhelmed; sand is lost as coastal construction removes the sediment supply; and the destination shifts down-market as wealthier visitors who could access the unspoiled original condition move to the next discovery.

This lifecycle has been described for Torremolinos, Benidorm, Ayia Napa, Koh Samui, and dozens of other destinations. The destinations that avoid it — Tuscany's coast, Capri, the Algarve's western cliffs — typically do so through some combination of geographic restriction on development (no space), regulatory control (planning laws that limit mass construction), or economic positioning (price points that limit volume).

Carrying capacity in coastal tourism is theoretically quantifiable: you can define a threshold number of visitors per day at which beach quality metrics — crowding, water quality, sand loss — begin to degrade. In practice, reaching the political decision to limit visitor numbers to carrying capacity while revenues are still growing is extremely difficult. Mallorca has been more successful than most European beach destinations in this regard, having introduced tourist tax and anti-mass-tourism regulations at local level from around 2016 onward.

## Coral Reef Tourism and the Blue Economy

Reef-based beach tourism creates a specific economic structure where the value of the underlying ecosystem is enormous but poorly accounted for. The reef is the reason the beach is clear, the fish are visible, and the snorkelling experience is exceptional. Damage to the reef — from increased sea surface temperatures, from boat anchoring, from careless snorkeller contact, from nutrient runoff — destroys the asset without showing up in any immediate balance sheet.

The IUCN and World Bank have published assessments of coral reef ecosystem service values incorporating fisheries, coastal protection, and tourism contributions. The numbers are large: the Great Barrier Reef's contribution to the Australian economy has been estimated at AUD 6.4 billion annually in a 2017 Deloitte report. The Mesoamerican Barrier Reef similarly supports billions in tourism and fisheries value across Belize, Mexico, Honduras, and Guatemala. These figures create an economic case for reef protection that complements the ecological one.

## Finding Beaches That Balance Economy and Ecology

The beach destinations that best balance economic success with environmental sustainability are typically those that have adopted visitor management systems, coral reef protection regulations, sewage infrastructure adequate for peak season loads, and development controls that preserve the natural character driving tourism.
