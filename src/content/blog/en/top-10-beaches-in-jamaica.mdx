Jamaica is a Caribbean island of about 10,990 square kilometres, lying south of Cuba and west of Haiti, with roughly 1,022 kilometres of coastline. The north coast, facing the Caribbean Sea, has the island's most famous resort beaches — Montego Bay, Negril, Ocho Rios — while the south coast, facing the Caribbean's calmer waters, has less-developed strands that are predominantly local in character. The physical geography of Jamaican beaches varies: the north has white sand and relatively calm water in sheltered bays, while some south coast beaches have darker sand from river sediments draining the central Blue Mountains. The island's beach culture is vibrant and particular, shaped by centuries of fishing communities, Caribbean festival traditions, and the reggae soundtrack that seems to follow every sunset from Negril.

## 1. Doctors Cave Beach, Montego Bay

Doctors Cave Beach in Montego Bay is one of the most famous beaches in the Caribbean and one of the founding sites of Jamaican beach tourism. The beach was donated to a bathing club in 1906 by Dr Alexander McCatty, and the name derives from him — the cave portion is a local misspelling that has stuck for over a century. The water here is exceptionally clear — cleaned by the natural filtering action of the sand and the surrounding reef — and the bay is sheltered from the trade winds. The beach is managed by the Montego Bay Marine Park and has excellent facilities. Montego Bay itself has an international airport with direct connections from North America and the UK.

## 2. Frenchman's Cove Beach, Portland

Frenchman's Cove on Jamaica's northeastern coast in Portland Parish is a privately managed cove where a freshwater river flows directly into the sea — a warm freshwater stream meeting cold Caribbean salt water at the beach. This thermal mixing creates a unique swimming experience. The beach is set within a forested estate and has a small admission charge, which keeps it quieter than many of Jamaica's resort beaches. Portland Parish, based around the town of Port Antonio, is associated with some of the most beautiful coastal scenery in Jamaica: deep-water harbours, coconut groves, and the Blue Mountains rising immediately behind the coast.

## 3. Bluefields Beach, Westmoreland

Bluefields Beach in Westmoreland Parish on Jamaica's southern coast is a quiet local beach town that holds a particular place in musical history: it was the childhood home of Peter Tosh, founding member of The Wailers. The beach itself is a long stretch of palm-backed sand on the sheltered southern Caribbean coast, with calm, warm water. The area is known for excellent fishing — wahoo, marlin, and tuna are caught offshore — and the local beach bars and fishing community give it a genuinely Jamaican atmosphere. The drive from Negril takes about 45 minutes along a coastal road that passes through working fishing villages.

## 4. Turtle Beach, Ocho Rios

Turtle Beach is the main public beach of Ocho Rios on the north coast, occupying a wide bay between the cruise ship pier and the adjacent resort zone. Unlike many Jamaican beaches near cruise ports, Turtle Beach remains relatively accessible and affordable for independent travellers. The beach is wide, sandy, and backed by a promenade with facilities. Ocho Rios itself is a major cruise destination and the beach can be crowded when multiple ships are in port simultaneously; timing a visit for early morning or late afternoon avoids the peak crowds. Dunn's River Falls, Jamaica's most visited natural attraction, is a short drive away.

## 5. Burwood Beach, Trelawny

Burwood Beach sits in Trelawny Parish on the north coast, east of Falmouth, and is a well-maintained public beach that serves the local community rather than functioning primarily as a tourist attraction. Trelawny has the highest concentration of Jamaicans of African descent on the island and a distinct cultural identity. The beach is flat, sandy, and calm, sheltered by the broad platform reef that protects much of the northern coastline. Falmouth's impressive Georgian architecture — it was once Jamaica's most prosperous port — provides an interesting complement to beach time for visitors who want to explore inland as well.

## 6. Fort Clarence Beach, Kingston

Fort Clarence Beach is Kingston's municipal beach, sitting about 30 kilometres west of the capital on the Hellshire Peninsula. The beach is a major gathering point for Kingston's population on weekends and public holidays, when the sounds of Jamaican music — dancehall playing from truck-mounted speakers, dominos games, and the smell of fried fish from the Hellshire seafood shacks — create an atmosphere that is quintessentially Jamaican and entirely unlike any international resort beach experience. The Hellshire seafood shacks serve freshly fried snapper and festival (a sweet fried dumpling), and this combination of beach and local food culture makes Fort Clarence genuinely worth visiting. ## 7. Wicki Wacki Beach, Hanover

Wicki Wacki Beach sits in Hanover Parish on Jamaica's northwestern coast, in the stretch of coastline between Lucea and the Negril promontory. Hanover is a quieter parish than its neighbours St James (Montego Bay) and Westmoreland (Negril), and the beaches along its coast have a more local character. Wicki Wacki is a small but well-liked beach with the clear water characteristic of this stretch of north coast, backed by typical Jamaican coastal vegetation. The beach is accessible from the main north-coast road and functions as a neighbourhood beach for nearby communities.

## 8. Callabash Bay Beach, Clarendon

Callabash Bay Beach sits on Jamaica's southern coast in Clarendon Parish, in the Milk River area where the coastline fronts the open Caribbean. Clarendon's south coast is one of the least-visited stretches of Jamaican coastline by international travellers, partly because of distance from the main resort areas and partly because the south coast beaches are generally darker and less immediately photogenic than the north. The Milk River area is known for its mineral spa — a natural sulphur spring claimed to have medicinal properties — which provides an unusual beach-and-spa combination rarely found elsewhere in the Caribbean.

## 9. James Bond Beach, Oracabessa

James Bond Beach sits near Oracabessa in St Mary Parish on the north coast, close to GoldenEye — Ian Fleming's former estate where he wrote all fourteen Bond novels between 1952 and 1964. The beach is open to the public and takes its name from the Bond connection, which has been marketed effectively since the estate became a luxury hotel. The beach occupies a sheltered bay with calm, clear water and a small reef for snorkelling offshore. The name aside, the beach itself is a fine north coast Jamaican strand — wide, white-sanded, with the Blue Mountains visible inland. GoldenEye itself can be visited for drinks or dinner.

## 10. Seven Mile Beach, Negril

Seven Mile Beach in Negril is one of the most iconic Caribbean resort beaches — a long, near-continuous arc of pale sand and clear water on Jamaica's westernmost point. The beach faces northwest into the prevailing trade winds, which keeps it breezy and relatively cool even in the height of summer. The strip of hotels, resorts, bars, and restaurants behind the beach ranges from international all-inclusives to small Jamaican guesthouses, and the sunset from Negril's beach — over the open Caribbean, with the cliffs behind Rick's Café and its cliff divers — is one of the genuinely memorable evening rituals of Caribbean beach travel.
