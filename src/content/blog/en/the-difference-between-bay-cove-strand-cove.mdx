Coastal place names are full of terms that carry specific geographic meanings: bay, cove, inlet, strand, fjord, lagoon, sound, bight, gulf. Most of these terms are used loosely in everyday language and travel writing, where "bay" and "cove" are often interchangeable. In geography, they have distinct meanings based on size, enclosed geometry, and the process that formed them.

Knowing these distinctions helps when reading nautical charts, planning coastal walks, or understanding why two apparently similar bits of coastline are named differently.

## Beach

A beach is the most general of the coastal terms: the accumulation of loose sediment — sand, pebbles, cobbles, shells, or gravel — at the shoreline, deposited by wave action. A beach can occur at the head of a bay, along an open coast, inside a fjord, or on a river bank. It is a depositional landform rather than a geographic container. All strands are beaches; not all beaches are strands.

## Strand

Strand is the older English and Germanic word for beach. In modern usage it typically describes a long, linear beach — the strand of a sea or lake — often with a road or promenade running alongside it (the Strandweg, the Strandveien). Many Northern European beach towns retain "strand" in their local names for the beach even when the English translation would simply say "beach." Technically, a strand is a beach exposed at low tide; some older texts use it to mean the area between high and low tide marks specifically.

## Cove

A cove is a small, sheltered bay — typically enclosed on three sides by headlands or cliffs with a relatively narrow opening to the sea. The enclosure geometry of a cove creates calmer water than an open beach, which is why many famous swimming beaches are coves: the headlands absorb swell energy before it reaches the enclosed water. Coves are typically too small to have significant harbours. Cathedral Cove in New Zealand, Lulworth Cove in Dorset, and the smaller Adriatic beach bays are typical coves.

## Bay

A bay is a concave indentation of the coastline where land curves around a body of water on more than one side, with an opening wider than a cove. The size range is enormous: Chesapeake Bay, Hudson Bay, and the Bay of Bengal are all bays at vastly different scales. A bay typically has calmer water than the open coast because the concave geometry provides some shelter from the dominant swell direction. Not all bays have beaches — many have rocky shores, cliffs, or mangroves — but where bays have beaches, the beach typically has gentler wave action than exposed coastline.

## Gulf

A gulf is a large bay — specifically, a deep indentation of the coastline opening to the sea via a relatively narrow mouth. The Gulf of Mexico, the Gulf of Oman, and the Persian Gulf are classic examples. A gulf is typically larger than a bay, though the distinction in everyday usage is not strict. "Gulf" implies deep penetration of land by sea — an arm of ocean that extends well inland.

## Inlet

An inlet is a narrow body of water extending into land, usually the sea extending into a coast or the shore of a lake. Inlets are typically narrower than bays but can be long. They shade into creeks, channels, and estuaries depending on whether fresh water is involved. Many inlets with small beaches are excellent for calm, shallow swimming — the narrow opening limits wave penetration.

## Sound

A sound in geographic terminology is a wide sea passage between two landmasses or between a landmass and an island. Puget Sound in Washington State, the Sound of Islay in Scotland, and Roskilde Fjord-adjacent channels in Denmark are sounds. Sounds typically have strong tidal currents because they connect two bodies of water at different tidal states. They are not beach destinations in the traditional sense.

## Fjord

A fjord is a long, narrow, deep inlet carved by glacial action — specifically, a glacially scoured valley that was subsequently inundated by the sea as the glacier retreated and sea levels rose. Fjords are characteristic of Norway, Iceland, Greenland, New Zealand's South Island, British Columbia, and Chilean Patagonia. They have very deep water close to shore and often dramatic cliff walls. Some fjords have beaches at their heads where river sediment has accumulated; these are often dark and pebbly. Milford Sound in New Zealand and the Sognefjord in Norway are classic examples.

## Lagoon

A lagoon is a body of water partially or completely separated from the sea by a barrier — typically a barrier island, a coral reef, or a sand bar. The water inside is calmer than outside and may have different salinity, temperature, and chemistry from the open sea. Atoll lagoons in the Maldives, French Polynesia, and the Pacific are enclosed by coral reefs. Coastal lagoons like the Lagoons behind Miami Beach or the Venetian Lagoon are separated from the sea by barrier islands. Lagoon beaches are typically calmer for swimming than ocean-facing beaches.

## Bight

A bight is a curve or recess in a coastline or in a river. In practice, it is a large bay with a gentle rather than pronounced concave shape — an open curve rather than a deeply enclosed bay. The Great Australian Bight is an example: a vast, open coastal curve across the southern Australian coast. Bights typically expose their beaches to open ocean swell from the direction of the bight's opening.

## Estuary

An estuary is a semi-enclosed coastal body of water with a river outlet, where fresh water and saltwater mix. The mixing creates a distinctive brackish environment with different salinity, chemistry, and biology from either the river or the sea. Estuaries are generally poor beach destinations by conventional standards — turbid water, soft muddy banks, and tidal variation that exposes mud flats — but some estuaries have sandy beaches at their mouths or on their sandbanks that are accessed by boat.

The importance of estuaries for coastal geography is that they are the delivery point for the river sediment that maintains most of the world's beaches. Altering river flow (by dams, abstraction, or channelisation) changes what arrives at the estuary and therefore at the beach downstream.

## Ria

A ria is a long, wedge-shaped coastal inlet formed not by glaciation (which produces a fjord) but by the flooding of a river valley by a rise in sea level. Rias are characteristic of northwest Spain (the Rías Baixas of Galicia), southwest England (the estuaries of Cornwall and Devon), and parts of Portugal. They are wider at the mouth and narrower inland, with sheltered beaches on their inner shores that are calmer than the open Atlantic beaches on the adjacent headlands. The beaches of the Galician rias — particularly Playa de Rodas on the Cíes Islands — are among the most sheltered and beautiful in northern Spain.

## Peninsula

A peninsula is a body of land surrounded on three sides by water, projecting into a sea, lake, or river. Peninsulas matter for beach geography because they have two coastal aspects with different wave exposure. The Nicoya Peninsula in Costa Rica, the Baja California Peninsula in Mexico, and the Coromandel Peninsula in New Zealand each offer a Pacific-facing coast with full ocean exposure and a gulf or bay-facing coast with more sheltered water. The choice of which side of a peninsula to base yourself on is effectively the choice between ocean and sheltered water — a fundamentally different beach experience from the same landmass.
