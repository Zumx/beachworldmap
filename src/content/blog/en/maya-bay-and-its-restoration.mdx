Maya Bay on Ko Phi Phi Leh island in Krabi Province, Thailand, is one of the most documented cases of tourism-induced ecological collapse and subsequent recovery in the world. The timeline is specific enough to be instructive: a film released in 2000, a decade of exponential visitor growth, a reef system destroyed, a closure in 2018, a partial recovery, and a reopening under a new management model in 2022. What happened at Maya Bay is a case study in both the speed at which tourism can destroy a natural system and the resilience of certain ecosystems when human pressure is removed.

## Before the film

Maya Bay is an enclosed bay on the southwest coast of Ko Phi Phi Leh, the smaller of the two Phi Phi islands. The bay is surrounded on three sides by limestone karst cliffs 100 to 200 metres high; the single entrance is a channel wide enough for longtail boats. The beach inside is approximately 250 metres of white sand.

The bay was known before 2000 as one of the beautiful beaches on the Phi Phi island boat circuit, visited by a manageable number of longtail tourists from Ko Phi Phi Don and day trippers from Phuket. The coral reef in the bay supported high biodiversity and was in good health. Visitor numbers were in the hundreds per day.

## The film and its consequences

Alex Garland's novel The Beach, published in 1996, and its film adaptation starring Leonardo DiCaprio, released in 2000, filmed partly at Maya Bay, transformed it into one of the world's most sought-after beaches. The film's central premise — a hidden paradise accessible only to those who know where to look — was promotional in its effect regardless of the story's darker themes.

Visitor numbers to Maya Bay increased from several hundred per day before the film to over 4,000 to 5,000 by the late 2000s. By the mid-2010s, peak-season days saw reported numbers of 5,000 to 8,000 people in a bay of roughly 250 metres of beach. Boats anchored in the bay itself; their anchors and propellers destroyed the sea floor and the coral. Swimmers standing on the coral head completed the damage to the bottom of the bay. The coral reef had been reduced to rubble.

## The 2018 closure

The Department of National Parks, Wildlife and Plant Conservation closed Maya Bay entirely to tourism in June 2018, citing the complete destruction of the coral reef in the bay. The closure was initially announced as temporary — four months for reef rehabilitation — but was extended as the recovery proved both possible and slow, and as the management challenges of reopening became apparent.

During the closure, researchers from Kasetsart University monitored reef recovery in the bay. Within 18 months, coral recruits — juvenile corals — were colonising the rubble. Baby blacktip reef sharks, which had been absent from the bay for years, returned within the first year of closure. By 2020, the reef showed measurable new coral growth across large sections of the bay floor.

## What the closure demonstrated

The Maya Bay closure provided one of the clearest demonstrations in global marine conservation that reef recovery is possible relatively quickly when visitor pressure is completely removed, if the water quality is sufficient and larvae from nearby healthy reefs can settle. The recovery was faster than scientists had predicted, which has informed optimism about managed closures elsewhere.

It also demonstrated the political complexity of closures: the local boat operators who made their livelihoods from Maya Bay tours opposed the closure and the extended timeline. The eventual management plan required compensation mechanisms and alternative income development for boat operators.

## The current management model

Maya Bay reopened in January 2022 under a strict management protocol. Access is by boat only from Ko Phi Phi Don or Phuket; private boats and longtail boats anchor outside the bay and visitors transfer by inflatable rubber boat to the beach. A daily visitor cap is enforced by the national park authority — the specific number is adjusted by season but is in the range of several hundred persons at any given time. Boat anchoring within the bay is prohibited. Swimming is permitted only in a designated zone away from recovering coral.

Entry requires a national park fee in addition to boat transfer costs. Photographs are taken from boats in the bay rather than from the beach by visitors.

## What visitors experience today

The visual impact of Maya Bay remains extraordinary: the limestone cliffs, the enclosed turquoise water, and the white beach are intact and more pristine than at any point since the early 2000s. The coral recovery is not visible to casual snorkellers — the reef is in early-stage recovery, not the lush system it was before 2000 — but the bay's underwater environment has measurably improved.

The managed access means the experience is quieter and more controlled than the previous free-for-all. Visitor reviews consistently report the beauty of the setting and frustration at the brevity of permitted beach time, which is limited to allow the visitor cap rotation. The tradeoff is explicit and intentional.

## The broader lesson

Maya Bay is cited in tourism management literature alongside Boracay in the Philippines (closed in 2018 for infrastructure and water quality reasons) as evidence that tourism-induced degradation can be reversed if political will, scientific monitoring, and enforcement capacity are combined. The lesson is not that beaches should be closed — it is that some beaches require active population management, that this management can work, and that the beach quality on the other side of a well-managed recovery can be higher than it was at peak degradation.

## Other beaches where closures changed outcomes

Boracay's 2018 closure by the Philippine government under President Duterte was more abrupt and politically driven than Maya Bay's, but achieved similar environmental results. The six-month closure of the island addressed accumulated sewage infrastructure failures, unauthorised construction on the beach frontage, and water quality that had deteriorated to crisis levels. The beach sand and water quality on reopening were measurably improved and have been maintained under stricter development controls.

Ko Samet in Thailand's Gulf of Thailand underwent partial closure and visitor number management in subsequent years following the Maya Bay precedent. The marine park authority has applied progressively stricter boat regulations across the Andaman and Gulf of Thailand island systems.

Phi Phi Don, the larger inhabited island adjacent to Ko Phi Phi Leh, has not been closed but has faced increasing pressure to manage its own development. The contrast between the managed recovery of Maya Bay and the continued development pressure on Ko Phi Phi Don is visible and ongoing.

## Planning a visit to Maya Bay

Access currently (from 2022 onwards): organised day tours from Ko Phi Phi Don or Phuket include the national park fee, the boat transfer to the bay, and a limited period on the beach (typically 30 to 45 minutes). Booking in advance is advisable in peak season (November through March). Tours depart from Ko Phi Phi Don's main pier and from Phuket's Rassada Pier, with multiple operators competing on price and boat type.

Go early: the first boats of the day arrive in the bay before the main tourist rush and the light is better for photography. Evening tours after 3pm also see lower visitor density. The national park entry fee is separate from tour costs and is paid at the bay entrance by the national park officer on the pier or at the entrance point.

The turquoise water colour that made Maya Bay famous is at its best when the sun is at the right angle to penetrate the enclosed bay — typically from mid-morning to early afternoon. The limestone cliffs cast shadows inside the bay in the early morning.
