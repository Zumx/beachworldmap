Most beachgoers interact with coastal birds as background — a gull stealing chips, a blur of small waders running from a wave, pelicans gliding overhead in formation. But the birds at the beach are doing specific, often remarkable things: terns are conducting aerial hunts over precise patches of water; oystercatchers are applying technique refined over years to open shellfish; sanderlings are following the waveline precisely because the swash disturbs invertebrates buried in the wet sand. Understanding what each species is doing and why transforms the background into foreground.

## Gulls: Far More Variety Than Most Visitors Notice

The word "seagull" is not a scientific category and covers dozens of species, many of which share beach habitat without being closely related in their ecology. In northern European waters, the most commonly encountered species are herring gull (Larus argentatus), lesser black-backed gull (L. fuscus), black-headed gull (L. ridibundus), and common gull (L. canus). In eastern North America, the equivalent generalist positions are occupied by herring gull and laughing gull (L. atricilla); on the Pacific coast, western gull (L. occidentalis) and glaucous-winged gull (L. glaucescens) dominate.

Gulls are opportunistic omnivores and their beach behaviour reflects whatever food source is currently most available: following the plough on adjacent farmland, patrolling the strandline for invertebrates, kleptoparasitising other birds and humans, or diving to pick up fish from just below the surface. The leg-paddling behaviour sometimes seen on short grass — drumming the feet to mimic rain and bring earthworms to the surface — is also performed by some species on wet beach sand to bring sandworms upward. Herring gulls learn to drop hard-shelled prey (mussels, crabs, sea urchins) from height onto rock surfaces to crack them open; this behaviour is learned rather than innate and shows geographic variation in technique between populations.

## Sanderlings and the Wrack Line Specialists

The sanderling (Calidris alba) is arguably the most studied beach-specific wader in the world, partly because its behaviour is so visually distinctive. Groups of sanderlings run rapidly at the water's edge, advancing and retreating with each wave, probing the wet sand as the swash retreats. They are exploiting the brief moment of wave disturbance when small invertebrates — particularly sand crabs (Emerita species) and sand hoppers — are exposed or suspended in the swash. The timing of their runs is precisely synchronised with the wave cycle.

Sanderlings breed in the high Arctic — Svalbard, northern Canada, Greenland — and winter on sandy beaches throughout the temperate and tropical zones. Most of the sanderlings on a British or South African beach in December hatched in the Arctic the previous July. Their winter plumage is almost uniformly pale grey-white, making them appear almost colourless against the sand except in strong direct light. Their distinctive running behaviour and proportionally large bill relative to body size make them identifiable with experience.

The turnstone (Arenaria interpres) operates in a different zone of the same beach: the strandline. Turnstones work through wrack, using their distinctive short upturned bill to flip stones, shells, and weed in search of amphipods, isopods, and small invertebrates beneath. The name is entirely literal. In the absence of natural strandline material, turnstones have been observed working through beach detritus — cigarette ends, plastic, discarded food packaging — with the same systematic flipping behaviour. They are remarkably bold around humans and can be observed at close range on beaches where they are not disturbed.

## Terns: Precision Hunters Above the Surf

Terns (family Sternidae) are among the most aerially accomplished birds encountered at beaches. Their hunting behaviour — hovering on rapidly beating wings above a patch of water, then plunging directly downward to take a fish just below the surface — is immediately recognisable once seen. Common tern (Sterna hirundo), Arctic tern (S. paradisaea), and little tern (S. albifrons) are the most frequently encountered European species; royal tern (S. maxima) and Forster's tern (S. forsteri) occupy similar ecological roles in North America.

The patch selection for hovering is not random. Terns track disruptions to the water surface — dark patches that indicate baitfish shoals, areas of feeding activity from below (tuna and dolphins often drive baitfish to the surface, creating tern feeding opportunities) — and concentrate their hunting over these areas. Watching where terns are hovering from a beach vantage point is a reliable way to locate areas of surface fish activity.

The Arctic tern's annual migration is the longest of any animal on earth: from Arctic breeding grounds to Antarctic wintering areas and back, covering an annual round trip of roughly 70,000 to 80,000 kilometres. Many birds encountered on UK beaches in summer are on their southbound pre-migration staging, feeding intensively to build fat reserves for the flight.

## Oystercatchers: The Specialists

The Eurasian oystercatcher (Haematopus ostralegus) is one of the most distinctive shorebirds on Atlantic beaches: black above, white below, with a long orange-red bill and pink legs. Despite the name, the primary prey items are mussels, limpets, cockles, and other bivalves rather than oysters. The bill morphology and feeding technique vary between individual birds — some oystercatchers specialise in hammering mussel shells open by striking the top of the closed valve; others have learned to probe for cockles in soft substrates and open them by inserting the bill tip into the valve gap. These different techniques require different bill tip shapes, and individual birds that learned one technique in early life are committed to it for the rest of theirs.

Oystercatchers are long-lived birds: ringed individuals have been recorded at 40 years. They defend specific foraging territories on mussel beds and cockle flats within their home beach areas, and the territories are inherited by offspring if the parent dies. The competitive dynamics of oystercatcher families over decades on a single beach have been the subject of long-term studies in the Exe Estuary, the Wadden Sea, and the Solway Firth.

## Pelicans: Social Fishing on Open Beaches

Brown pelicans (Pelecanus occidentalis) on North American coasts and Australian pelicans (P. conspicillatus) on Australasian beaches are among the largest birds encountered in beach environments. Both species fish by plunge-diving — folding the wings and entering the water from heights of 5 to 10 metres, using the expandable pouch to scoop fish and water simultaneously. On Pacific and Atlantic coasts of the Americas, brown pelicans often fish in loose coordination, with multiple birds diving in sequence over the same patch.

White pelicans (P. erythrorhynchos in North America, P. onocrotalus in the Old World) typically fish cooperatively in groups, swimming in a curved line that herds fish into the shallows. They are less associated with open beaches than brown pelicans and more with estuaries and lagoons.

## Beaches as Migration Staging Posts

The ecological function of beaches in the context of bird migration is rarely appreciated by beach visitors. Sandy beaches and associated intertidal flats are critical refuelling stops for many wader species undertaking long-distance migration. The Delaware Bay shore in late May, when horseshoe crabs (Limulus polyphemus) spawn in their millions, draws hundreds of thousands of red knots, sanderlings, dunlins, and other waders in a synchronised event that ranks among the most spectacular in North American ornithology. The knots flying northward from Tierra del Fuego to their Arctic breeding grounds have timed their arrival in Delaware Bay to coincide with the horseshoe crab spawn, which is the primary energy source for the final northward leg.

The disruption of this staging function — through development that reduces accessible beach area, disturbance from recreational use during peak wader staging in May, and the long-term decline of horseshoe crab populations — directly affects the reproductive success of migratory wader populations that depend on the beach for only a few days each year.

## Where to Watch Coastal Birds

[Open the map](/map) to locate beaches with known ornithological value — estuary mouths, sandy shores adjacent to tidal flats, and beaches within protected areas that retain the undisturbed strandline wrack zones that specialist shorebirds depend on. The overlap between good birding beaches and good swimming beaches is imperfect, but many of the Atlantic and Pacific coast beaches with the best coastal bird diversity also offer excellent general beach conditions in the appropriate season.
