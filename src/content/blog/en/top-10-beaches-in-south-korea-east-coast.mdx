South Korea's east coast — the shore facing the East Sea (Sea of Japan) — is the country's primary ocean beach corridor, extending roughly 600 kilometres from the DMZ at the north to Busan's Haeundae in the south. The mountains of the Taebaek range run parallel to the coast a few kilometres inland, often visible from the beach itself, and several of Korea's most scenic national parks — Seoraksan, Odaesan, Gyeongju — lie immediately behind the coastal plain. The beaches here are characteristically Korean: wide, organised, clean, and equipped with managed facilities including shower blocks, food stalls, and seasonal lifeguard service. Water temperatures in the East Sea are considerably warmer than the West (Yellow) Sea due to the warm Tsushima Current: July and August water reaches 23–25°C at the main beach resorts. The official beach season (해수욕장 개장) runs from roughly 1 July to 20 August, during which time lifeguards are on duty, food stalls operate, and festivals and music events are held at many beaches. Outside this window the beaches are accessible but unmanned. The coastal rail line connecting Seoul to Gangneung (via the Gyeonggang high-speed line opened in 2017) has dramatically improved access from the capital.

## 1. Okgye Beach (옥계 해수욕장), Gangneung

Okgye Beach is in Gangneung city's southern district, facing the East Sea at a position slightly sheltered by a low headland. The beach is long and sandy, the approach through the Okgye village providing a more residential character than the central Gangneung resort beaches. The water at Okgye is clean — the East Sea's open circulation produces consistently good water quality along the Gangwon coast — and the July-August season sees a substantial festival programme. Gangneung is famous for its coffee culture and its traditional local specialties: Chodang tofu, made from seawater, and a long-established coffee street whose café culture predates the Korean coffee wave by decades.

## 2. Dojak Beach (도작 해수욕장), Gangwon Province

Dojak Beach is a quieter beach south of Yangyang in Gangwon Province, less well-known than the Naksan and Surfyeon beaches that anchor the Yangyang surf scene but part of the same clean, wide-sand East Sea coast. The beach is backed by low pines and a modest parking area. Outside the official summer season it is largely empty; within the season it operates with the typical Korean beach management structure of zone-designated swimming areas, food tent streets, and evening events. The proximity to Seoraksan National Park — one of Korea's most dramatic mountain landscapes — makes Dojak a sensible stop on a Gangwon road trip.

## 3. Mangsang Beach (망상 해수욕장), Donghae

Mangsang Beach is one of the largest and best-equipped beaches in Gangwon Province, with a broad camping zone behind the beach that accommodates hundreds of tents and is managed by the municipal government of Donghae city. The beach itself is wide, the sand clean, the water clear at 23–24°C in August. Mangsang Camping Beach has been operating for decades and represents the Korean tradition of seaside camping — families drive with full camping equipment for a week's beach holiday in a campsite community setting. The surrounding Donghae coastline has several sea cave formations accessible by trail at low tide.

## 4. Deoksin Beach (덕신 해수욕장), North Gangwon

Deoksin Beach is a smaller beach in the northern Gangwon coast area, closer to the DMZ border zone than most East Sea resort beaches. This northern location keeps it less visited and more local in character. The coastal road through northern Gangwon passes through a landscape of pine forest and rice fields with occasional views of the offshore islands. Deoksin functions primarily as a local recreational beach for the Goseong and Sokcho area populations. The Seoraksan mountain range visible behind the coast gives the northern Gangwon coast its characteristic pine-mountain-sea composition.

## 5. Mangyang Beach (망양 해수욕장), North Gyeongbuk

Mangyang Beach is in North Gyeongsang Province (Gyeongbuk), below Gyeongju, on the coast where the mountains of the Taebaek range finally moderate and the coastal plain widens. This section of the Korean east coast transitions from the resort beaches of Gangwon to a more fishing-community coast, though Mangyang is developed for beach tourism. The Ulleungdo Island ferry port at nearby Pohang connects to the volcanic island 87 kilometres offshore — combining an east coast beach visit with an Ulleungdo trip makes for an unusual Korean itinerary. The squid fishing culture of the North Gyeongbuk coast is reflected in the dried squid sold at every beachside tent.

## 6. Hupo Beach (후포 해수욕장), North Gyeongbuk

Hupo Beach is in Yeongdeok county, North Gyeongsang Province, on a coast famous for king crab — the Yeongdeok snow crab (영덕 대게) is considered the finest in Korea, and the Hupo and Yeongdeok coastal towns are built around the spring crab-fishing season from January through March. The beach itself is a quiet, well-maintained strand of clean sand with the backdrop of the low north Gyeongbuk coastal hills. The combination of clean beach and exceptional seafood makes Hupo a genuinely satisfying visit; the crab at the harbour-side restaurants, caught the same morning, is among the best available anywhere in Korea.

## 7. Chuam Beach (추암해수욕장), Donghae, Gangwon

Chuam Beach is adjacent to Chuam Chotdaebawi — the Candlestick Rock formation, a tall isolated sea stack of grey limestone rising from the shallow East Sea just off the beach. The rock is one of the most photographed natural features on the Korean east coast, particularly at sunrise when it is silhouetted against the first light coming over the horizon. The beach is small and the surrounding coastline dramatic with additional eroded rock formations. Chuam is on the Donghae Blue Road — a coastal walking trail following the east coast — and the walk along the cliffs above the beach provides the best views of the geological formation.

## 8. Mangsang Beach (망상해수욕장) — Extended Entry

As one of the flagship camping beaches of the east coast, Mangsang bears additional context: the Mangsang Auto Camping Site can accommodate over 200 vehicles and operates a summer reservation system that fills weeks in advance for the peak August period. Evening entertainment — fireworks, beach concerts, and lantern festivals — is coordinated by Donghae city tourism. The combination of mountain backdrop, clean sea, organised camping, and cultural programming makes Mangsang representative of how Korean municipal governments have developed east coast beach culture as a genuine family tourism infrastructure.

## 9. Dolmeori Beach (돌머리해수욕장), Jeollanam-do

Dolmeori Beach is on the south coast of Korea rather than the east, in South Jeolla Province on the Yellow Sea side — included here for the broader Korean beach context. The beach is unusual in having both a stone-head (dolmeori) natural formation and a relatively undeveloped sandy section. The south coast island culture, with its ferries to Geomundo and Heuksando, creates a different beach experience from the east coast's organised resort culture.

## 10. Songdo Beach (송도해수욕장), Busan

Songdo Beach in Busan is on the city's south-western coast, and while Haeundae is more internationally known, Songdo has been revitalised as Korea's first public beach — it opened in 1913 — and a recent cable car installation now crosses the bay from the beach to a rocky island in the middle of the bay, one of the more surprising additions to a Korean beach. The water here is warmer than the central east coast beaches at peak summer, benefiting from the Korean Strait's warming effect. Busan's extraordinary food culture — dwaeji gukbap (pork soup), haejangguk, and the Jagalchi fish market — makes any Busan beach visit a culinary event as much as a coastal one.

## Navigate Korea's East Coast Beaches

South Korea's east coast beaches are well-served by public transport, including the KTX to Gangneung and coastal buses.
