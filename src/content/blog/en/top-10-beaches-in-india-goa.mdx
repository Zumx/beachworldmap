Goa, India's smallest state, occupies roughly 3,700 square kilometres on the west coast of the Deccan Peninsula, and its roughly 105-kilometre coastline has been drawing visitors from within India and abroad since the 1960s, when travellers on the overland route from Europe discovered it as a terminus of cheap living and tropical ease. The coastline divides naturally into north and south along the Mandovi River delta, with north Goa running between Panaji and the Maharashtra border and south Goa stretching from the Zuari River south toward Karnataka. The two halves have developed very different reputations. North Goa is louder, more commercial, and more historically significant to the alternative tourism scene; south Goa is quieter, more forested, and more expensive. The best season runs from November through March, when the southwest monsoon has retreated and temperatures stay in the high 20s to low 30s Celsius. ## 1. Palolem Beach, South Goa

Palolem is almost universally considered the most beautiful bay in Goa — a crescent of pale sand about 1.5 kilometres long, enclosed by forested headlands on both sides and dotted with small islands just offshore. It is in Canacona taluka, the southernmost district of Goa, and was largely unknown to international tourists until the 1990s. The bay is calm enough for swimming year-round, and a local tradition of silent disco parties (participants wear wireless headphones) keeps the nightlife alive without disturbing the beach ambience. Fishing boats still work the bay in the early mornings.

## 2. Agonda Beach, South Goa

About 8 kilometres north of Palolem and less developed, Agonda is a long, wide strand backed by jungle hills that form part of the Western Ghats foothills. It is one of the few Goa beaches where Olive Ridley sea turtles nest, and sections of the beach are protected during nesting season from November through March. The village has resisted large-scale hotel development, and the accommodation is mostly small guesthouses and eco-huts. The surf is stronger than at Palolem and swimming requires care outside the flagged areas.

## 3. Patnem Beach, South Goa

Immediately south of Palolem and separated from it by a short rocky headland walkable at low tide, Patnem is smaller and markedly quieter. It attracts a longer-stay crowd of yoga practitioners and families with young children. The beach has a shallow sandbar that makes it unusually safe for swimming, and the handful of restaurants facing the sea have developed a reputation for quality food cooked to order rather than tourist menus.

## 4. Vagator Beach, North Goa

Vagator is split into three coves — Big Vagator, Little Vagator (sometimes called Ozran), and the smaller Chapora cove — sitting below the ruins of the 17th-century Chapora Fort. It became famous in the 1990s as the centre of the Goa trance music scene and remains associated with electronic music events, particularly around Christmas and New Year. The red laterite cliffs above Little Vagator are a striking geological feature of the north Goa coastline. The beach itself is rocky in places but the swimming coves are protected.

## 5. Anjuna Beach, North Goa

Anjuna was the original hippie-trail destination in Goa, centred on the Wednesday flea market that began in the early 1970s as a venue for travellers selling clothes to fund further travel. The market still runs and has grown into a significant tourist event. The beach is wide and backed by coconut palms, with the rocky northern end providing dramatic views at sunset. Trance music events have been held at the southern end since the 1980s, and the beach retains a self-consciously alternative identity even as it has become thoroughly commercial.

## 6. Baga Beach, North Goa

Baga is the commercial epicentre of north Goa's beach tourism, running seamlessly into Calangute at its southern end. The 1-kilometre stretch is lined with beach shacks, water sports operators, tattoo parlours, and nightclubs, and it is the most visited beach in Goa by volume of domestic tourists. During the Christmas and New Year period the beach becomes extremely crowded. The Baga creek at the northern end has a cluster of well-regarded restaurants and is less chaotic than the main strip.

## 7. Calangute Beach, North Goa

Calangute bills itself as the "Queen of Beaches" in Goa's tourism literature and was the first beach in the state to develop significant tourist infrastructure in the 1960s. Today it is large, crowded, and noisy, with an industrial quantity of beach shacks and souvenir stalls lining the sand. It is not the best beach in Goa by most measures of natural quality, but it functions as a hub for domestic Indian tourism and everything is available — jet skis, parasailing, bodyboards, and food from all over India.

## 8. Morjim Beach, North Goa

Morjim sits at the mouth of the Chapora River in the far north of Goa and has a split identity: the northern half of the beach is an Olive Ridley turtle nesting site and is protected during nesting months, while the southern half has developed into a popular destination particularly among Russian-speaking tourists since the early 2000s. The estuary behind the beach supports a healthy population of wading birds including black-naped terns. The beach is long, relatively empty by north Goa standards, and the Chapora estuary can be explored by small boat.

## 9. Mandrem Beach, North Goa

Between Morjim and Arambol, Mandrem occupies a quieter position in the north Goa coastal sequence. It has attracted a yoga and wellness community that has built a cluster of retreat centres set back from the beach in the palm groves. The Mandrem creek at the southern end creates a small lagoon that is safe for children. The beach itself is wide and clean, with fewer beach shacks than the busier stretches to the south.

## 10. Ashwem Beach, North Goa

Ashwem connects with Mandrem at its southern end and shares its understated character. It is known for its kite-surfing conditions during the winter months when winds are consistent, and several schools operate from the beach. The northern end is rocky and less suitable for swimming but offers tide pools and bouldering. A small fishing community works from here, and some of the freshest seafood in the area is available through the handful of shacks near the boat ramp.

Goa's beaches are close enough together that it is entirely possible to visit two or three in a single day by motorcycle or taxi, moving between the frenetic energy of Baga and the meditative calm of Agonda within a few hours.
