The Hawaiian Islands offer a breadth of beach types that no single island can fully represent: calm lagoons, world-class surf breaks, black sand volcanic beaches, green olivine beaches, sheltered reef-protected bays, and exposed North Pacific shore. The five main visitor islands — Oahu, Maui, Hawaii (the Big Island), Kauai, and Lanai — each have distinct beach characters.

The Pacific geography means swells arrive from three directions: north Pacific storm swells in winter (generating the famous North Shore surf), south Pacific swells in summer (bringing gentle surf to south-facing beaches), and trade wind chop from the northeast year-round. Knowing which direction your beach faces helps predict what conditions to expect.

## 1. Waikiki Beach, Oahu

Waikiki is the most visited beach in Hawaii and one of the most famous in the world. The 3-kilometre beach faces south into the Pacific and receives gentle south swells well suited to longboard surfing and outrigger canoe rides. The iconic hotel backdrop — the Royal Hawaiian, the Moana Surfrider — and Diamond Head visible at the east end frame one of the most recognisable beach views in the world. Waikiki is also practically functional: consistent surf for beginners, lifeguards, food, and accommodation all immediately accessible. Waikiki Beach is in the database.

## 2. Lanikai Beach, Oahu

Lanikai on Oahu's Windward coast is consistently cited as one of the most beautiful beaches in the United States. The beach faces east behind an offshore reef that creates a protected lagoon with exceptionally calm, clear water. The twin Mokulua islands offshore provide a visual focus and a kayak destination. The beach is short (about 500 metres) and lacks facilities — it fronts a residential neighbourhood — but the water clarity and calm are exceptional. Access is via right-of-way footpaths between the beach houses.

## 3. Hapuna Beach, Hawaii Island

Hapuna Beach on the Big Island's Kohala coast is a half-mile of white sand — one of the largest white sand beaches in Hawaii — protected on each side by black lava headlands. The water is clear and typically calm enough for good swimming except in winter when north swell wraps around the island. A state beach park with facilities (toilets, showers, pavilions) makes it practical for a full day. Hapuna Beach State Recreation Area is one of Hawaii's best-managed beach parks.

## 4. Hanauma Bay, Oahu

Hanauma Bay is an extinct volcanic crater breached by the ocean, creating an enclosed bay with a coral reef that has been protected as a marine life conservation district since 1967. It is the most popular snorkel site in Hawaii and is managed carefully: visitors must watch an educational video, numbers are capped, and entry fees support reef monitoring. The coral recovery since management was tightened is documented and visible. The bay is a 45-minute drive from Waikiki and requires early arrival or online reservation in peak season.

## 5. Napali Coast, Kauai

The Napali Coast is not a single beach but a 16-mile stretch of Kauai's northwest coast accessible only by boat, helicopter, or the Kalalau Trail — a challenging 11-mile hike. Kalalau Beach at the end of the trail is one of the most remote and dramatic beaches in Hawaii: a half-mile crescent backed by 1,000-metre fluted green cliffs. It is a camping beach (permitted) accessible for day visitors from the sea (summer only, weather permitting). The boat approach from Port Allen or Hanalei shows the full cliff spectacle.

## 6. Po'ipū Beach, Kauai

Po'ipū on Kauai's sunny south shore is one of the most reliably calm beaches in Hawaii — the south shore is protected from north Pacific winter swell and the trade wind fetch is offshore. The beach is divided by a rocky jetty into a calm side (good for children and snorkelling) and a somewhat more active side. Hawaiian monk seals, one of the world's most endangered marine mammals, occasionally haul out on Po'ipū. The sun exposure here is greater than on the wetter north shore.

## 7. Ka'anapali Beach, Maui

Ka'anapali is a 3-mile stretch of beach on Maui's west coast, backed by a continuous line of resort hotels that has been developing since the 1960s. The beach is wide, sandy, and faces west delivering good sunset views. The snorkelling at the Black Rock (Pu'u Keka'a) at the north end of Ka'anapali is among the best accessible snorkelling on Maui — the rock is sacred to Hawaiians and a regular turtle resting spot. Cliff diving from the Black Rock by cliff performers is a resort tradition.

## 8. Punaluu Black Sand Beach, Hawaii Island

Punaluu is a black sand beach on the Big Island formed by lava from Kilauea and Mauna Loa volcanoes. It is also a reliable green turtle (honu) resting beach: the turtles haul out on the black sand in numbers sufficient to guarantee sightings on most visits. Approaching turtles is prohibited under the Endangered Species Act; rangers and signage enforce the required distances. The volcanic landscape — black sand, coconut palms, fresh water springs feeding the beach through the sand — is one of the most distinctive coastal environments in the Pacific.

## 9. Big Beach (Makena), Maui

Big Beach (Oneloa) at Makena State Park on Maui's south coast is one of the longest undeveloped beaches in Maui — 3,000 feet of white sand without resort development behind it. The shorebreak at Big Beach can be severe and is responsible for a disproportionate number of serious injuries: the waves pitch steeply in the shallow sandbar and have broken necks and spines. Read the shorebreak warning signs and respect them. Entering the water on your feet only, not diving headfirst, is the rule.

## 10. Ke'e Beach, Kauai

Ke'e Beach at the end of the road on Kauai's north coast is the trailhead for the Kalalau Trail and a reef-protected snorkel beach. The lagoon inside the reef is shallow and calm in summer; in winter, north swell can make it dangerous. The view west from Ke'e across the first section of the Napali cliffs at sunset is one of Hawaii's most photographed.

## Planning a Hawaii beach trip

The five main visitor islands have distinct beach characters that suit different priorities. Oahu has the best concentration of famous beaches — Waikiki, Lanikai, the North Shore — combined with the most urban infrastructure and ease of access (Honolulu is the only major international hub). Maui is the most practical second choice: good beaches, direct flights from the mainland US, and a balance of resort development and relatively accessible wild beaches at Makena. Kauai has the most dramatic scenery and the best snorkelling reefs, but its remoteness from the other islands (no inter-island ferry; short flying times from Oahu or Maui) makes it a separate trip rather than an add-on. The Big Island's beach variety is extraordinary — white sand, black sand, green sand at Papakolea — but the distances between them require a car and planning.

North Shore Oahu (winter): The North Shore from Haleiwa to Sunset Beach is the global reference for winter big-wave surfing. The beaches are accessible by public bus from Honolulu (the 52 and 55 routes), though the journey takes about 2 hours each way. Ehukai Beach Park (Pipeline) and Sunset Beach are the spectator points for professional surfing events from November through February. The beach itself changes dramatically between summer (flat, suitable for swimming) and winter (powerful, dangerous, closed to swimming during swell events). Checking surf conditions before visiting any North Shore beach in winter is essential.

Inter-island logistics: Hawaii does not have inter-island ferries (the service was discontinued in 2009). All inter-island travel is by short-hop flight: Oahu to Maui is about 25 minutes; Oahu to Kauai is 30 minutes; Oahu to the Big Island is 45 minutes to 1 hour depending on destination. Hawaiian Airlines and Mokulele Airlines operate these routes; Southwest Airlines entered the market and has improved price competition on the main routes.
