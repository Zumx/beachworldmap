The Dominican Republic occupies the eastern two-thirds of Hispaniola, the second-largest island in the Caribbean, and has around 1,500 kilometres of coastline on four very different bodies of water: the Atlantic Ocean on the north, the Mona Passage on the east, the Caribbean Sea on the south, and the Gulf of Gonâve in the southwest. The country receives more international tourists than any other Caribbean nation, yet its beach geography is varied enough that genuinely uncrowded strands coexist with the all-inclusive resort megalopolis of Punta Cana. The north coast, facing the Atlantic, has the wind and swell that made Cabarete a world-class kite and windsurfing destination. The south coast and the Samaná Peninsula have a different character — slower, greener, more isolated.

## 1. Playa Juan Dolio, San Pedro de Macorís Province

Playa Juan Dolio is a beach town about 60 kilometres east of Santo Domingo on the southern Caribbean coast, historically the primary beach escape for the Dominican capital. The beach is sandy and the water calm, sheltered by the reef platform that runs along much of the southern Dominican coast. Juan Dolio developed through the 1980s and 1990s into a resort strip catering primarily to Dominican families and package tourists, and it retains this accessible, affordable character compared to the more expensive north-coast alternatives. The proximity to Santo Domingo — the oldest continuously inhabited European settlement in the Americas, with a colonial zone UNESCO World Heritage Site — makes Juan Dolio a useful base for combining beach and culture.

## 2. Playa Najayo, San Cristóbal Province

Playa Najayo sits west of Santo Domingo in San Cristóbal Province and is one of the primary weekend beaches for the capital's western suburbs. The beach is a popular Dominican local beach rather than an international resort destination — lively on weekends with music, food vendors, and families, quieter on weekdays. The sand is slightly darker than the fine white sand of the northeast coast, more characteristic of the south coast's composition. The town of San Cristóbal itself, a short distance inland, is the birthplace of Rafael Trujillo, and the area has a number of sites associated with the Trujillo dictatorship era that provide historical context for visitors interested in 20th-century Dominican history.

## 3. Playa Los Almendros, San Pedro de Macorís Province

Playa Los Almendros is a quieter local beach in the San Pedro de Macorís area of the southeast coast, named for the almond trees (almendros) that line its foreshore. The beach is characteristic of the southeast Dominican coast: calm, sheltered Caribbean water, fine pale sand, and a laid-back local atmosphere. San Pedro de Macorís is most famous internationally as the birthplace of a disproportionate number of Major League Baseball players — the town has produced hundreds of MLB players and the per-capita MLB representation here is extraordinary. The beach provides a pleasant break from exploring the town's baseball heritage.

## 4. Playa Guayacanes, San Pedro de Macorís Province

Playa Guayacanes sits adjacent to Juan Dolio and is part of the continuous beach strip that lines the southeast coast between Boca Chica and La Romana. The beach is named for the guayacán (lignum vitae) trees that once grew here, and it has a slightly wilder character than the more developed Juan Dolio strip. The southeast coast in this area is popular for sea kayaking and paddleboarding in the calm Caribbean water, and several operators in Juan Dolio offer equipment rentals that can be used along the connected beach strip.

## 5. Playa Chiquita, Puerto Plata Province

Playa Chiquita — Little Beach — is a small cove east of Cabarete on the Atlantic north coast, tucked into a rocky headland that provides some protection from the dominant northeast trade winds. The cove is more sheltered than the main Cabarete beach and consequently calmer, making it suitable for swimming on days when the trade winds have made the main beach rough. The rocky headlands at either end provide good snorkelling over coral formations. Cabarete and its surrounding beaches are accessible from Puerto Plata International Airport, about 45 kilometres west by the coastal road. ## 6. Playa de Cabo Rojo, Pedernales Province

Playa de Cabo Rojo sits at the extreme southwest corner of the Dominican Republic in the remote Pedernales Province, near the border with Haiti. The cape is named for its red-tinged rock, a bauxite formation that gives the cliffs and surrounding terrain an unusually warm colour. The beach is broad and exposed to the open Caribbean, with significant wave action on most days. The entire Pedernales region is remote by Dominican standards — the nearest major town is hours away by road — and its combination of dramatic coastal landscape, dry tropical forest, and genuine isolation makes it one of the most distinctive beach environments in the country.

## 7. Playa Salinas, Peravia Province

Playa Salinas lies on the southern coast in Peravia Province, near the town of Baní, and its name derives from the salt flats (salinas) in the area, which historically produced salt for the Dominican economy. The beach faces south into the open Caribbean and is exposed to the wind that sweeps across the southern coast — this wind makes the area excellent for kitesurfing and windsurfing, creating a complement to the north coast's Cabarete scene. Baní itself is known for its distinctive sweet mangos and as the birthplace of Máximo Gómez, the Cuban independence general who was born in the Dominican Republic.

## 8. Playa Real, Samaná Peninsula

Playa Real sits on the Samaná Peninsula in the northeast of the Dominican Republic, one of the most scenically dramatic parts of the country. The Samaná Peninsula is a narrow finger of land projecting eastward into the Atlantic, with a mountainous spine, lush tropical vegetation resulting from higher rainfall than the south coast, and beaches of fine pale sand in sheltered bays. The bay of Samaná receives humpback whales from January through March — the whales migrate from their North Atlantic feeding grounds to the warm Caribbean waters to calve — making winter visits here extraordinary for whale watching combined with beach time.

## 9. Playa Bávaro, La Altagracia Province

Playa Bávaro is the core of the Punta Cana-Bávaro resort complex, one of the largest beach resort areas in the Caribbean, stretching roughly 45 kilometres of near-continuous fine white sand and turquoise water along the easternmost coast of Hispaniola. The reef platform offshore keeps the water exceptionally calm. The concentration of international all-inclusive hotels along this coast is dense — there are well over 100 major resort properties — and the beach infrastructure is accordingly extensive: watersports, beach bars, and organised activities of every description. Punta Cana International Airport is one of the busiest airports in the Caribbean, receiving flights from across North America and Europe.

## 10. Playa Rincón, Samaná Province

Playa Rincón is consistently cited as one of the most beautiful beaches in the Caribbean and one of the least developed despite its fame. The beach stretches about five kilometres of pale sand between two headlands on the Samaná Peninsula's north coast, backed by dense tropical forest and accessible only by boat from Las Galeras or by a 4WD track that is often impassable after rain. Two freshwater rivers flow onto the beach from the forest behind, creating the same warm-cool mixing experience as Jamaica's Frenchman's Cove. Facilities are minimal — a handful of beach shacks serving grilled fish and coconut water — and the water is exceptional.
