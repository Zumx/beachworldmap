Jellyfish sting first aid has been a subject of genuine medical debate over the past 20 years. The previously standard advice — apply vinegar to all jellyfish stings — is now known to be wrong for certain species and potentially harmful for others. The correct treatment varies by species, and knowing which family of jellyfish is present at the beach you are visiting may determine what you put in your beach bag.

The overview below covers the main species groups and the current medical consensus on treatment.

## Box jellyfish and Irukandji (Indo-Pacific)

Box jellyfish (Cubozoans, particularly Chironex fleckeri) are the most medically dangerous jellyfish in the world. Found primarily in the shallow coastal waters of northern Australia and throughout the Indo-Pacific, a major box jellyfish sting can cause cardiac arrest within minutes. The sting produces immediate intense burning pain, visible tentacle marks on skin, and rapid systemic symptoms including cardiovascular collapse.

First aid for Chironex box jellyfish stings: call emergency services immediately. Apply generous amounts of household vinegar (4-6% acetic acid) to the sting area to prevent unfired nematocysts from firing. Remove tentacles using tweezers or a card — not bare fingers. Be prepared to perform CPR. Antivenom is available in Australia and is administered by medical personnel. Do not apply freshwater — this causes unfired nematocysts to fire.

Irukandji syndrome, caused by several small box jellyfish species, is unusual because the initial sting is often painless. Symptoms appear 20 to 40 minutes later: severe lower back pain, muscle cramps, nausea, sweating, and a feeling of impending doom. Irukandji stings require hospital treatment; the syndrome can cause severe hypertension and pulmonary oedema. Apply vinegar to the sting site; seek emergency medical care.

## Bluebottle (Pacific man o' war)

The bluebottle (Physalia utriculus, a close relative of the Portuguese man o' war) is common on beaches of eastern Australia, New Zealand, and South Africa. Stings cause immediate intense burning pain along the contact line of the tentacle. The current first-aid consensus for bluebottle stings in Australia: remove tentacles (with tweezers, not fingers), then immerse the sting area in hot water (as hot as can be tolerated without burning, typically 42 to 45 degrees Celsius) for 20 minutes. Research published in the MJA (Medical Journal of Australia) from 2016 onwards has established that hot water is significantly more effective than cold for pain relief in bluebottle stings. Do not apply vinegar — this may cause remaining nematocysts to fire.

The Portuguese man o' war (Physalia physalis), which is larger than the Pacific bluebottle and found in the Atlantic, Gulf of Mexico, and Indian Ocean, is treated similarly: remove tentacles, apply hot water.

## Lion's mane jellyfish

The lion's mane jellyfish (Cyanea capillata), found in cold northern Atlantic and Pacific waters, is one of the world's largest jellyfish with tentacles that can extend many metres. Stings cause a burning, stinging pain and raised red weals on the skin. Treatment: remove tentacles with tweezers or a card, rinse with seawater (not fresh water), apply a cold pack for pain relief. Lion's mane stings are painful but rarely medically serious for healthy adults. Allergic reactions are possible; monitor for systemic symptoms.

## Common temperate jellyfish

The moon jellyfish (Aurelia aurita), barrel jellyfish (Rhizostoma pulmo), and compass jellyfish (Chrysaora hysoscella) commonly encountered at European beaches cause mild stings or no reaction at all in most adults. Treatment is simple: remove any tentacles, rinse with seawater, apply a cold pack if desired. These species do not warrant vinegar, hot water, or medical attention in most cases.

## The vinegar debate resolved

Vinegar (acetic acid) is effective for box jellyfish of the class Cubozoa because it deactivates unfired nematocysts and prevents additional venom from being injected during tentacle removal. For Physalia (bluebottles, man o' war), vinegar causes nematocyst firing and increases venom delivery — it should not be used. For Chrysaora (compass, sea nettle), evidence on vinegar is mixed. For Aurelia (moon jellyfish), stings are rarely painful enough to require treatment.

The practical beach bag response: for known box jellyfish regions (northern Australia, Indo-Pacific coastal waters in summer), carry vinegar. For Atlantic or Pacific temperate beaches, a cold pack and tweezers cover the common species.

## When to seek medical help

Seek emergency medical care if: the sting is from a confirmed or suspected box jellyfish; the victim is a child; breathing difficulties, chest pain, altered consciousness, or severe systemic symptoms develop; the sting covers a large body area; or the victim has a known allergic or anaphylactic history.

For mild stings from common temperate species with no systemic symptoms, medical care is not necessary.

## Sea lice and other minor stings

Sea lice — actually the larvae of thimble jellyfish (Linuche unguiculata) or seabather's eruption caused by certain anemone larvae — are common in warm Caribbean and Gulf of Mexico waters from April through August. They are too small to see, and the sting is not felt while swimming. A rash develops hours later on skin covered by swimwear (where the larvae become trapped against the skin). Treatment is hydrocortisone cream and antihistamines; the rash typically resolves in a few days. Recognising sea lice season at a destination — ask at beach facilities, or note when other swimmers report it — allows the decision to wear a rash guard rather than just a swimsuit, which reduces contact.

Coral contact is not a jellyfish sting but produces similar symptoms: abrasive cuts from the coral structure itself plus possible stinging from nematocysts in coral surface tissue. Treatment is to flush the wound with clean water, not seawater (which maintains pathogen presence), and apply antiseptic. Coral cuts are notoriously slow to heal in tropical heat; keeping them clean and covered is more important than any topical treatment.

Fire coral (Millepora), despite looking like coral, is a colonial hydrozoan related to jellyfish and produces a painful burning sting on contact. It is common on reefs throughout the Indo-Pacific, Caribbean, and Red Sea. Treatment: rinse with seawater, apply vinegar or hydrocortisone. The sting is painful but not medically serious for healthy adults.

## Beach warning signs

Australian beaches in box jellyfish season (October through May in the Top End) post warning signs and maintain stinger suits and vinegar stations. Patrolled beaches in northern Australia often have stinger nets enclosing a small swimming area. These signs and nets are not decorative — they exist because box jellyfish are invisible in the water and can be lethal.

## Seasonal variation

Jellyfish populations are seasonal and location-specific. In the Mediterranean, the Mauve stinger (Pelagia noctiluca) blooms in summer and early autumn when warmer surface water concentrates it near shore. Years with severe blooms — which are driven by oceanographic conditions and affected by the warming of surface waters — close some Mediterranean beaches temporarily.

In the Irish Sea and North Sea, lion's mane season is late summer when the jellyfish reaches maximum size and drifts inshore with prevailing currents. Norwegian and Scottish swimmers are accustomed to encountering lion's mane in July and August. Moon jellyfish blooms in northern European waters are less painful but create the impression — common to untrained observers — that the sea is full of jellyfish while most of what they are seeing is the harmless Aurelia.

In the Pacific tropics, the pattern reverses seasonally from the southern hemisphere: Australian box jellyfish season peaks in the northern summer of the southern hemisphere (October through April), which is the time when European and North American tourists are most likely to visit the Great Barrier Reef. Checking the specific species risk for the destination and season before travel — rather than applying generic jellyfish advice — produces a much more accurate risk assessment.
