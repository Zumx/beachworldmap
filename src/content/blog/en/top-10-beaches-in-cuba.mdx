Cuba has more than 5,700 kilometres of coastline and over 290 beaches spread across its long main island and thousands of outlying cays and keys. The northern coast, facing the Atlantic and the Straits of Florida, has some of the most famous beaches — Varadero and the cays of the Jardines del Rey archipelago among them. The southern Caribbean coast tends to be calmer, shallower, and quieter, with fewer visitors and more intact reef systems. The island's particular political and economic history has paradoxically protected much of its coastline from the intensive resort development that has transformed many other Caribbean islands, and genuinely remote beaches remain accessible to those willing to travel beyond the main resort zones.

## 1. Playa Santa Isabel, eastern Cuba

Playa Santa Isabel sits on the eastern end of Cuba, in a region that receives fewer visitors than the resort zones of Varadero and Cayo Coco. Eastern Cuba — the Oriente region — has the highest mountains, the strongest Afro-Cuban cultural traditions, and generally wilder, less developed coastline. The beach is characteristic of Cuban undeveloped strands: pale sand, clear water, and a backdrop of mangrove and coastal scrub vegetation rather than hotel towers. Access requires planning and is typically done by renting a car from Santiago de Cuba, the eastern region's major city, about two to three hours away by road.

## 2. Playa Blanca, Holguín Province

Playa Blanca in Holguín Province on the northern coast is one of several white-sand beaches in this region that are developing slowly as Holguín's airport receives increasing international charter traffic. The beach has the fine pale sand and clear water characteristic of north-coast Cuba, and the reef system offshore supports snorkelling and diving. The Holguín region has been attracting beach resort development since the 1990s but remains notably less built-up than Varadero, and the local Cuban infrastructure around the towns of Guardalavaca and Rafael Freyre retains a more authentic character.

## 3. Playa Las Tumbas, Granma Province

Playa Las Tumbas sits in the Granma Province near the Gulf of Guacanayabo, in one of the remotest coastal areas of Cuba. The Granma Province is historically significant as the landing point of Fidel Castro's expedition from Mexico in December 1956, and the Desembarco del Granma National Park covers much of the nearby coast. This national park status has protected the coastal zone from development and the beach is largely wild, accessible via unpaved roads that require a 4WD vehicle. The water along this southern coast is exceptionally calm, shallow, and warm, sitting as it does on the sheltered inner side of the Gulf.

## 4. Playa Pinca, Camagüey Province

Playa Pinca occupies the northern coast of Camagüey Province, a large central Cuban province whose coast faces the Cayo Romano area of the Jardines del Rey archipelago. The Camagüey coast is a patchwork of bays, mangroves, and sandy cays accessed primarily by boat from the mainland. This low-lying, complex coastal geography protects the inner beaches from heavy Atlantic swell, creating calm, shallow waters that are excellent for swimming and snorkelling. The area is significantly less visited than the better-known cay resorts further east and west.

## 5. Playa del Oro, Santiago de Cuba Province

Playa del Oro — Gold Beach — is located in the Santiago de Cuba Province, near the city that is considered the cultural capital of Cuba and the heartland of son, salsa, and the rumba. The eastern beaches in this province benefit from the Windward Passage geography: the channel between Cuba and Haiti creates particular water conditions, and the beaches here have a slightly different character from the north-coast resort strands. Santiago de Cuba is directly accessible by air from several international gateways and the city itself — with its Casa de la Trova, the Cuartel Moncada, and the remarkable Trinidad-era colonial architecture — provides a rich urban complement to beach time.

## 6. El Esterito, Santiago de Cuba

El Esterito sits within the wider Santiago de Cuba metropolitan area, on the coastline near the city. It is a local beach used by Santiago residents rather than a tourist destination, and its appeal lies precisely in this character: a genuine Cuban beach experience, with families, music, food sellers, and the particular atmosphere of Cuban coastal life. The Santiago coast faces south into the Caribbean, and the water here is calm, warm, and clear. ## 7. Playa Castro, Holguín Province

Playa Castro is one of the less-publicised beaches in the Holguín resort zone, away from the main hotel concentrations at Guardalavaca. The Holguín northern coast has an abundance of smaller coves and strands between the main resort areas, and Playa Castro represents the quieter end of what this coast offers. The water clarity here is excellent, and the reef formations accessible by snorkelling from the beach have seen less pressure than the reefs immediately adjacent to the main resort zones. The combination of good reef access and relative quiet makes it worth the extra effort to reach.

## 8. Playa de Nibujon, Holguín Province

Playa de Nibujon sits in the Banes area of Holguín Province, a municipality that holds particular historical significance as the birthplace of Fulgencio Batista. The beach is on the Bay of Banes, a large, sheltered inlet that creates exceptionally calm conditions. The bay area has a scattering of archaeological sites relating to the pre-Columbian Taíno people, and the Museo Indocubano Baní in Banes town has an impressive collection of indigenous artefacts. The combination of history, calm water, and absence of resort development makes this stretch of the Holguín coast interesting for visitors who want more than beach time alone.

## 9. Playa Girón, Bay of Pigs

Playa Girón sits on the eastern shore of the Bay of Pigs — Bahía de Cochinos — in Matanzas Province and is known internationally as the landing point of the CIA-sponsored April 1961 invasion force. The beach has a small museum documenting the battle from the Cuban government perspective, and the town retains a modest working character. The diving and snorkelling along this stretch of coast is among the best in Cuba: the Zapata Peninsula to the north is Cuba's largest protected wetland, and the underwater wall that drops sharply off the bay shore supports impressive reef life accessible directly from the beach. The beach is sandy, the water clear, and the surrounding Zapata landscape extraordinary.

## 10. Playa Sirena, Cayo Largo del Sur

Playa Sirena on Cayo Largo del Sur is widely regarded as one of the finest beaches in Cuba and one of the best in the entire Caribbean. The cay sits 177 kilometres south of Havana in the Gulf of Canhey, an isolated stretch of white sand and turquoise water accessible only by air or boat from the mainland. Cayo Largo is essentially a beach resort island, with a small cluster of hotels and an airstrip receiving charter flights from Havana and some international gateways. Playa Sirena, on the western tip of the island, has particularly fine, powder-soft sand and water that graduates from pale turquoise to deep blue with exceptional clarity throughout.
