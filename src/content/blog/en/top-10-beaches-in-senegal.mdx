Senegal has about 700 kilometres of Atlantic coastline running from the Casamance region in the south to the border of Mauritania in the north. The country's beach geography divides into distinct zones: the Cap Vert Peninsula, which juts westward into the Atlantic and contains Dakar, the capital; the Petite Côte immediately south of Dakar, where most tourist beach development is concentrated; and the Casamance region in the far south, separated from the main body of the country by The Gambia. The Atlantic here is not the calm, sheltered water of the Mediterranean or the Caribbean — currents are strong, swell can be significant, and the upwelling of cold water from the deep Atlantic creates a refreshing sea temperature even in the tropical heat of West Africa. The combination of dramatic surf, fishing culture, and extraordinary food makes Senegal a beach destination that is genuinely different from anywhere else.

## 1. Anse Bernard, Dakar

Anse Bernard is a small cove on the northern coast of the Cap Vert Peninsula, in the upmarket Plateau neighbourhood of Dakar. The beach is compact and sheltered within the rocky shoreline of the peninsula's northern tip, and it is surrounded by the city — apartment buildings, embassies, and government offices loom on the heights above. Despite or because of this urban setting, it has a particular character: Dakarois families, fishermen launching pirogues, and the occasional diplomat all share a small stretch of Atlantic-facing sand. The cove faces north, which provides some shelter from the dominant southwesterly swells.

## 2. Plage des Tortues, Rufisque

Plage des Tortues — Turtle Beach — sits south of Dakar near the town of Rufisque, which was one of the first French colonial settlements in West Africa and retains colonial architecture alongside a working port. The beach is named for the sea turtles that historically nested here, and conservation efforts along this stretch of the Petite Côte focus on protecting the remaining nesting sites. The Rufisque coast gives a glimpse of working Senegalese coastal life — fishing pirogues drawn up on the sand, women processing the catch, the smell of drying fish — alongside the beach itself.

## 3. Plage de la Pointe des Almadies, Dakar

The Pointe des Almadies is the westernmost point of continental Africa — a cape that projects into the Atlantic at the tip of the Cap Vert Peninsula. The beach at the point is rocky and wave-battered on its western face, with a surf break used by Dakar's small but growing surf community. The significance of standing at the westernmost point of Africa gives the site a particular draw beyond its beach qualities. The surrounding neighbourhood of Les Almadies is one of Dakar's wealthiest districts, with a concentration of restaurants, beach clubs, and the Ngor Island landing point nearby.

## 4. Baie de Mermoz, Dakar

Baie de Mermoz is named for Jean Mermoz, the pioneering French aviator who crossed the South Atlantic in 1930 and used the coast near Dakar as part of the Aéropostale mail route. The bay is a sheltered cove in the Mermoz-Sacré-Coeur neighbourhood of Dakar, popular with residents of the surrounding middle-class suburb. The beach is sandy and the bay's orientation provides protection from the dominant Atlantic swell. The area has good access to the Corniche Ouest, Dakar's famous seaside boulevard, and the uphill cliff walk along the Corniche provides views back over the bay and the Atlantic horizon. ## 5. Plage Ouakam, Dakar

Plage Ouakam sits in the Ouakam neighbourhood on the western shore of the Cap Vert Peninsula, facing into the full force of the Atlantic swells arriving from the southwest. The beach is popular with local fishermen and with the surf community — the waves here can be substantial and the beach attracts bodyboarders and surfers alongside the fishing boats. The African Renaissance Monument, a 49-metre bronze statue completed in 2010 and designed to rival the Statue of Liberty in scale, stands on the hill directly above Ouakam and is visible from the beach, creating a peculiar juxtaposition of Atlantic surf and monumental public art.

## 6. Plage Somone, Mbour Department

Plage Somone sits at the mouth of the Somone Lagoon on the Petite Côte, south of Dakar between Saly and Mbour. The lagoon is a classified nature reserve supporting flamingos, pelicans, and dozens of migratory bird species, and the beach at its mouth separates the brackish lagoon waters from the open Atlantic. The town of La Somone has developed into a moderate beach resort serving French and European visitors who prefer a quieter alternative to the larger Saly resort strip. The combination of lagoon birdwatching and Atlantic swimming makes it one of the more ecologically interesting beach destinations on the Petite Côte.

## 7. Plage de Ngor, Dakar

Plage de Ngor sits opposite Ngor Island, a small inhabited island about 300 metres offshore from the northern tip of the Cap Vert Peninsula. The beach is one of Dakar's most popular, backed by a fishing village that has survived within the expanding city, and the Ngor Island crossing is made by pirogue for a few hundred francs. The island has a small beach of its own, a surf break, several restaurants, and an entirely different pace of life from the mainland city 300 metres away. The main Ngor beach has consistent surf; competitions and informal surf sessions happen here regularly, and the Ngor surf club is one of the oldest in West Africa.

## 8. Monaco Plage, Dakar

Monaco Plage is one of several small urban beaches along the Corniche Ouest in Dakar, occupying a cove between the rocky outcrops that characterise this stretch of the Cap Vert Peninsula. The name has no connection to the principality; it was given by local residents, likely aspirationally, in the mid-20th century. The beach is a working class local beach, popular with young people and families from the surrounding Médina and Parcelles Assainies neighbourhoods. The Corniche cliffs provide a natural grandstand from which Dakar residents watch the surf and socialise in the late afternoon as the Atlantic sun descends.

## 9. Plage du Virage, Dakar

Plage du Virage sits at a bend in the Corniche Ouest — the virage, or turn, where the coastal road changes direction — and is one of the more accessible of Dakar's Atlantic beaches from the city centre. The beach is part of the continuous coastal experience of the Corniche, where the road above and the beach below operate in tandem: people running on the promenade, fishermen below, and the constant backdrop of Atlantic swell breaking on the rocky coastline. The beach clubs and restaurants along this stretch serve excellent thiéboudienne — the Senegalese national dish of rice and fish — and the evening atmosphere when the day's heat softens is relaxed.

## 10. Plage de Saly, Mbour Department

Saly is the principal beach resort town of the Petite Côte, a concentrated strip of hotels, restaurants, and beach facilities about 80 kilometres south of Dakar. It was developed specifically for European beach tourism beginning in the 1970s and now has a well-established infrastructure. The beach itself is wide and sandy, backed by the hotel zone, with watersports operations offering jet skiing, sailing, and deep-sea fishing. The Atlantic at Saly is cooled by upwelling currents, which keeps the water temperature refreshingly lower than the Caribbean at the same latitude. Mbour market town, five kilometres north, provides access to authentic local fish markets and the real economy of the Petite Côte fishing communities.
