Sleeping on a beach — waking to the sound of waves, the shift of light at dawn over flat water, the smell of salt air — is among the most distinctive camping experiences available. It is also among the most technically demanding, because the coastal environment subjects camping gear to stresses that inland camping does not: salt corrosion, condensation and dew, exposed wind, and the tidal zone that can rearrange or submerge a campsite between dusk and dawn. Getting the practical details right transforms beach camping from an uncomfortable ordeal into something genuinely memorable.

## Permits and Legal Access

The first question for beach camping in any jurisdiction is whether it is legal. This varies enormously and depends on both the legal access framework of the country (see the separate discussion of beach access law) and the specific management of the particular coastal area. In the United States, most federally protected beaches — within National Seashores, National Recreation Areas, and some State Parks — require a permit for camping. The permits at popular sites like the Fire Island National Seashore wilderness camping area on Long Island, or the walk-in beach camps at Point Reyes National Seashore in California, fill up months in advance.

In England and Wales, there is no right to camp on a beach or any other private land without permission. Wild camping is legal in Scotland under the Land Reform Act 2003 as long as it is done responsibly, but below the high tide line the public right is for passage and reasonable recreation, not indefinite residence. In Nordic countries (Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland), Everyman's Right typically permits camping for one to two nights in any location, including beaches, provided you are not within sight of a residence and are not in a nature reserve where different rules apply.

The practical advice is to check the specific rules for any beach you plan to camp on before arriving with a tent. Ignorance of local regulations is not a defence against removal or fines, and many coastal areas have experienced enough damage from unregulated camping that enforcement has been tightened.

## Tide Timing: The Non-Negotiable

Pitching a tent in the tidal zone is the most common practical error made by novice beach campers, and it ranges from inconvenient (wet gear) to dangerous (tent submerged). The tidal range on beaches varies enormously by location: less than 30 centimetres on the Mediterranean, 2 to 3 metres on a typical Atlantic beach, up to 16 metres in the Bay of Fundy in Canada. Arriving at low tide and pitching above what appears to be the high tide mark can be deceptive if the high tide mark visible is from a neap tide and the night's tide is a spring tide.

The practical approach is to verify the high tide level from a reliable tide table for the specific beach, add a comfortable margin above the predicted high, and pitch there. Most tidal prediction apps (Tides Planner, Windfinder, NOAA Tides and Currents) provide predictions at specific locations. The physical markers of the actual high tide line — wrack (seaweed) and debris deposited by the previous high tide — are always more reliable than visual estimates from below.

Some beaches, particularly in estuaries and on barrier islands, flood from the landward side during extreme high tides, not just from the seaward face. This requires understanding the topography of the full site rather than just the seaward gradient.

## Managing Wind and Condensation

Coastal campsites are almost always exposed to more wind than inland sites, and the wind works against camping comfort in several ways. A tent pitched broadside to the prevailing wind will flex and flap loudly all night and can fail at its pegs or fabric seams if the wind is strong. Always orient the tent so the smallest profile faces into the wind — the foot of a mountaineering tent, the curved end of a dome tent. Use all available pegs and guylines from the first minute; beach camping is not the environment for pitching half the peg points.

Condensation and dew are the other constant of coastal camping. The temperature differential between the warm sea and the cold land at night, combined with salt moisture in the air, produces condensation on tent fabric that can be significant even on dry nights. Double-wall tents with a separate flysheet and inner perform far better than single-wall tents in this environment. Pitching the flysheet with tension rather than allowing it to sag reduces contact between the flysheet and inner when dew forms.

## Food Storage: Wildlife Considerations

The food storage requirements for beach camping depend on location. In bear country — Pacific coast of the United States and Canada, coastal Alaska, and certain parts of Scandinavia and Russia — certified hard-sided bear canisters or park-approved bear boxes are required by regulation at most permit camping sites and strongly recommended everywhere else. Bears are attracted to salt-dried fish and seafood odours particularly strongly, which makes beach camping in bear habitat higher-risk than forested inland camping in terms of food odour management.

In locations without bears, coastal camping still requires careful food storage because of smaller wildlife — raccoons on the Atlantic US coast, foxes, corvids (crows and ravens), and sea birds that will investigate any accessible food. Hanging food is less reliable near the coast where trees are absent or small; a sealed, weighted dry bag submerged in a tidal pool or held below water is an option. Hard-sided locking containers are the simplest solution.

## Leave No Trace on Coastal Land

The Leave No Trace principles that govern responsible camping apply in amplified form to beach camping because beaches are often more visible, more fragile, and more shared than forest campsites. On sand, tent peg holes, fire rings, and compressed sand marks from sleeping mats are slow to recover compared to forest leaf litter. The principle of camping on durable surfaces means using bare sand rather than dune vegetation, and never positioning a tent or fire on dune grass, which can be killed by compression and heat.

Beach fires require particular care. Above-tide-line fires leave charcoal deposits that can persist for years and disrupt the sand chemistry. The best practice in many coastal systems is a fire pan — an elevated metal pan that contains the fire above the sand surface — with the ash carried out with the rest of rubbish. Many permit camping systems in the United States require fire pans for this reason.

Human waste management requires more planning on a beach than in a forest, because beaches are shared public spaces and the burial option — used in forest settings — is inappropriate on sand that is used by other people. Most permit coastal camping areas provide vault toilets or require pack-out systems.
