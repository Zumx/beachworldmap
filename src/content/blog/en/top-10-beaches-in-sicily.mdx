Sicily is the largest island in the Mediterranean, with a coastline that exceeds 1,000 kilometres and changes character roughly every 50 kilometres. The western and southern coasts face Africa and receive the warmest water in Italy, with August sea temperatures frequently reaching 28°C at the southernmost points. The eastern coast, dominated by the vast presence of Etna, offers volcanic black sand beaches within sight of snow-capped craters. The northern coast, backing onto the Tyrrhenian Sea, has a more rugged profile, with headlands and coves between the cities of Palermo and Messina. Cultural layering is impossible to ignore at the coast: Greek columns stand above beaches at Agrigento and Selinunte, Arab-Norman architecture towers over Palermo's port, and Carthaginian salt flats at Marsala mirror the sky at sunset. The island's beach season runs from May through October, and the best weather — warm water without summer crowds — arrives in September. Ferries connect mainland Italy via Messina, or flights arrive at Palermo and Catania.

## 1. Scala dei Turchi, Realmonte

Scala dei Turchi — the Staircase of the Turks — is a white marl cliff that descends to the sea in natural terraced steps, producing one of the most visually arresting beaches on any Mediterranean coast. The cliff's bright white colour comes from the high calcium carbonate content of the Tripoli marl formation, and it contrasts almost theatrically with the deep turquoise of the water below. The beach at the base is pebbly rather than sandy, but swimming from the natural platforms is excellent. Access is from the small resort of Realmonte, about 20 kilometres west of Agrigento, via a 15-minute walk. The cliff glows orange-gold at sunset. Summer crowds have increased significantly since the location went viral on international travel platforms after 2015.

## 2. Cala Rossa, Favignana Island

Cala Rossa is on the island of Favignana, part of the Egadi Archipelago off the western tip of Sicily, reachable by hydrofoil from Trapani in about 30 minutes. The name — Red Cove — refers to the Punic-era naval battle fought just offshore in 241 BC, in which Rome decisively defeated Carthage and ended the First Punic War. Today the cove is a pristine rocky inlet with extraordinary water clarity. There is almost no sand — access to the water is over flat tuff stone — but the turquoise depth and the silence make it compelling. The surrounding quarried tuff stone walls, which provided building material for centuries, enclose the cove like a natural amphitheatre.

## 3. Riserva Naturale dello Zingaro, San Vito Lo Capo

The Zingaro Nature Reserve was established in 1981 after a local protest blocked a coast road, and it remains the first protected natural area in Sicily. Stretching seven kilometres along the north-western coast between San Vito Lo Capo and Scopello, it is accessible only on foot — there are no roads within the reserve. Seven small beaches punctuate the walking trail: Cala della Disa, Cala Berretta, Cala dell'Uzzo, and others, each reachable within 30 to 120 minutes of hiking. The water is among the clearest in Italy, the trail passes through dense Mediterranean maquis and Aleppo pine forest, and the absence of motorised access keeps all seven coves quiet even in August.

## 4. Playa di Catania, Catania

Catania's city beach, the Playa, stretches for about five kilometres south of the port, a long grey-black sand beach backed by a flat coastal plain. The colour comes from eroded basalt, the volcanic rock that underlies most of eastern Sicily. It is an urban beach by any measure — backed by apartments, beach clubs, and food kiosks — but it functions as an important social space for the city's residents. The backdrop of Etna rising above the port to the north gives the setting a dramatic quality absent from more manicured resort beaches. Catania itself rewards time: its baroque historic centre, largely built from black lava stone after the 1693 earthquake, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 5. Isola Bella, Taormina

Isola Bella is a tiny island connected to the mainland just south of Taormina by a narrow gravel bar that appears and disappears with the tide, creating one of the most photographed spots in Sicily. The island itself is a protected nature reserve covered in dense subtropical vegetation and surrounded by an astonishing variety of sea life in its rocky shallows. The beaches on either side of the connecting bar are small and rocky rather than sandy, but the snorkelling around the island is exceptional: moray eels, sea breams, and occasional octopuses are visible in water rarely more than three metres deep. Taormina above, with its Greek theatre, sits at 200 metres and is accessible by cable car from the coast.

## 6. Cala Azzurra, Favignana

Cala Azzurra on Favignana's south-eastern coast is the counterpart to Cala Rossa — where Cala Rossa is rugged and dark, Cala Azzurra is sandy and brilliant. The name directly describes the water: a spectrum of azure from the shoreline out through emerald to deep cobalt. The beach is sandy and wide enough to spread out even in summer, and is reachable on Favignana by bicycle — the island is entirely flat and well-suited to cycling. The ferry connections from Trapani mean it is feasible as a day trip from the Sicilian mainland, though spending a night on the island is more rewarding.

## 7. Punta Bianca, Agrigento Province

Punta Bianca is a white limestone headland and its associated beach south of Agrigento, set in an area of rural coast between Realmonte and Porto Empedocle that remains largely undeveloped. The beach is accessible only via a rough track from the coastal road; the last kilometre requires a vehicle with reasonable ground clearance or a 30-minute walk. The effort pays off with a stretch of near-empty white sand, water of striking clarity, and occasional views of the African coastline on clear winter days — the Sicilian Channel narrows to about 140 kilometres at this point. The proximity to the Valley of the Temples makes Punta Bianca a logical addition to any Agrigento cultural visit.

## 8. San Vito Lo Capo, Trapani Province

San Vito Lo Capo is a resort town at the base of a dramatic limestone promontory jutting north into the Tyrrhenian Sea from Sicily's north-western corner. The town beach is arguably the finest sandy beach on the island — 1.5 kilometres of fine white sand in a bay sheltered by the Monte Monaco headland, with water so clear that the sandy bottom is visible at five metres' depth. The Cous Cous Fest held each September reflects the town's position midway between Italy and North Africa, bringing chefs and cultural events to the beach town. The reserve of Monte Cofano to the east provides walking country with coastal caves and rock-art sites.

## 9. Cala Tonnarella, Castellammare del Golfo

Cala Tonnarella sits at the base of Castellammare's castle promontory, next to the restored remains of a historic tonnara — the tuna-trap fishing establishment that once made this coast famous. The cove is small, protected, and completely enclosed by the castle rock and the old tonnara buildings, giving it a theatrical intimacy. The water is calm and clear, the bottom a mix of sand and posidonia. The tonnara buildings have been converted to accommodation, making it possible to sleep directly above the beach. Castellammare del Golfo itself has a well-preserved historic centre and a working port, and serves as the gateway to the Zingaro Reserve to the west.

## 10. Cefalù Beach, Cefalù

Cefalù has one of the most recognizable beach settings in Sicily: a compact Norman cathedral rising above medieval streets, with the La Rocca cliff towering behind and a sandy beach at the front. The beach is crowded in July and August but retains genuine appeal because the backdrop is simply extraordinary — nowhere else in the Mediterranean can you swim in front of a Norman cathedral built in 1131. The sand is fine and pale, the water clear, and there is enough protected bay to keep conditions calm on most summer days. Cefalù is 70 kilometres east of Palermo by A19 motorway, or 45 minutes by train. It fills up fast; arrive early.
