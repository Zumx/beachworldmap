Greek beaches range from chalk-white Cycladic coves to pink-sand
Mediterranean lagoons to dramatic cliff-walled fjords. With six thousand
islands competing, the standard is high. These ten earn their place through
water clarity, setting, and the fact that Greeks themselves still travel to
them.

## 1. Navagio (Shipwreck Beach), Zakynthos

The white-sand cove with a rusted freighter and vertical limestone cliffs,
boat-access only. The viewpoint above gives the famous shot; the swim
below gives the day. Subject to closures for rockfall — check before you
book a boat.

## 2. Balos Lagoon, Crete

A turquoise lagoon between Crete and the Gramvousa peninsula, with pink-
tinged sand from crushed shells and water so shallow you can wade for
hundreds of metres. Boat from Kissamos or a rough drive plus a long walk
down.

## 3. Elafonissi, Crete

Western Crete's pink-sand beach, a shallow lagoon you can walk across to a
protected islet of dunes and cedar. Heavily visited — go early or
shoulder-season.

## 4. Myrtos Beach, Kefalonia

A long white-pebble crescent under sheer cliffs, with the deep blue you
only get on steeply shelving Ionian beaches. The viewpoint on the road
above is itself a destination.

## 5. Sarakiniko, Milos

Not sand at all — a lunar landscape of wind-sculpted white volcanic rock
with deep clear inlets to swim in. Closest thing to swimming on the moon
that Earth offers.

## 6. Porto Katsiki, Lefkada

A long strip of white pebbles at the foot of a 100-metre limestone cliff
on Lefkada's west coast. Reached by a steep staircase from the car park;
the descent is part of the experience.

## 7. Voutoumi, Antipaxos

The small island next to Paxos in the Ionian, where the water is so clear
boats appear to float on air. A short beach in a sheltered bay — the
appeal is entirely the colour and the calm.

## 8. Simos Beach, Elafonisos

A double-sided sand spit on the small island off the Peloponnese, with
dunes, juniper, and shallow turquoise water on both sides. Quieter than
the Cycladic icons and arguably as good.

## 9. Lalaria, Skiathos

A boat-access beach of smooth white pebbles backed by white cliffs in the
Sporades, with a natural rock arch at one end. No facilities — bring
everything, leave nothing.

## 10. Red Beach, Santorini

A short crescent of red volcanic sand under crumbling red cliffs near
Akrotiri. Subject to rockfall closures and best viewed from the headland
path above as well as from the sand. Pair it with nearby White Beach by
boat.

## How Greek beaches work

Many top beaches are organised — pay for a sunbed and umbrella pair from
a beach bar, typically EUR 15–40 per pair per day, with free sections at
either end. Blue Flag beaches meet EU water-quality and facility standards
and are a reliable shortlist. Lifeguards exist on the busiest beaches in
peak season only.

## Plan around the regions

Crete for the largest variety and the pink-sand standouts; the Cyclades
(Milos, Santorini, Naxos) for volcanic drama and white cubist towns; the
Ionian (Zakynthos, Kefalonia, Lefkada) for cliff-backed beaches and clear
deep water; the Sporades and Peloponnese for quieter, less-photographed
coves. Inter-island ferries are the backbone — book ahead in July and
August.

## Season, wind, and hazards

May, June, and September are the best months: warm sea, fewer crowds,
lower prices, and the meltemi wind (the strong northerly that blows
through the Aegean in midsummer) is weaker. Sea urchins on rocky entries
are the most common hazard — reef shoes solve it. Many famous beaches now
have visitor caps or seasonal access rules to protect dunes and reef;
respect them.
